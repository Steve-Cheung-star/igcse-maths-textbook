We now move away from polynomials (like lines and parabolas) and introduce a new family of curves where the independent variable, $x$, appears in the denominator of a fraction. This fundamental shift introduces "breaks" in our graphs, leading to the concept of **asymptotes**—invisible boundary lines that a curve approaches infinitely closely but never actually touches.

## 1. The Reciprocal Function

The simplest form of a rational function is the **reciprocal function**:
$$
f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}, \quad x \neq 0
$$

### Key Features of $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$
* **The Shape:** The graph is a **rectangular hyperbola** consisting of two separate branches in opposite quadrants.
* **Vertical Asymptote (VA):** Because we cannot divide by zero, $x$ cannot equal $0$. The $y$-axis ($x = 0$) forms a vertical barrier.
* **Horizontal Asymptote (HA):** As $x$ gets infinitely large (e.g., $1/1000000$), $y$ gets infinitely close to $0$. The $x$-axis ($y = 0$) forms a horizontal barrier.
* **Self-Inverse Nature:** An interesting property of the reciprocal function is that it is its own inverse. If you reflect the graph of $y = \dfrac{1}{x}$ across the line $y = x$, it lands exactly on itself.
    * *Algebraic proof:* Let $y = \dfrac{1}{x}$. Swap $x$ and $y$ to find the inverse: $x = \dfrac{1}{y}$. Rearranging for $y$ gives $y = \dfrac{1}{x}$. Therefore, $f^{-1}(x) = f(x)$.

## 2. Rational Functions

A rational function is a ratio of two linear polynomials. The general form you need to know is:
$$
f(x) = \dfrac{ax + b}{cx + d}
$$

Because the denominator cannot be zero, these functions will feature translations and stretches of the basic $1/x$ hyperbola. The IB expects you to be able to state the equations of the asymptotes directly from the function parameters.

**Equations of Asymptotes:**
* **Vertical Asymptote:** Occurs where the denominator is zero. Set $cx + d = 0$ to get $x = -\dfrac{d}{c}$.
* **Horizontal Asymptote:** Found by comparing the leading coefficients of $x$ as $x$ becomes extremely large. The horizontal asymptote is $y = \dfrac{a}{c}$.

### Finding the Intercepts
To sketch the graph accurately, you also need the points where the curve crosses the axes:
* **$y$-intercept:** Set $x = 0$. The equation simplifies instantly to $y = \dfrac{b}{d}$.
* **$x$-intercept:** Set $y = 0$. For a fraction to equal zero, its *numerator* must equal zero. Set $ax + b = 0$ to get $x = -\dfrac{b}{a}$.

## 3. Technology & Sketching: Using the TI-Nspire

When solving rational function problems on Paper 2 or Paper 3, your TI-Nspire is a powerful tool to verify your algebraic work and visualize the asymptotes.

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire Tips for Rational Functions">
* **Entering the Function:** Always use the fraction template (`Ctrl` + `÷`) when entering rational functions to avoid order-of-operations errors.
* **Finding the $x$-intercept:** Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero** and set your bounds around the intercept.
* **Spotting Vertical Asymptotes:** Look for the visual break in the graph. You can confirm it by pressing `Ctrl` + `T` to bring up the Table. Scroll to the suspected $x$-value; the table will say **#UNDEF**.
* **Finding Intersections:** If asked to find where a rational function meets a line, graph both. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection** to let the calculator find the exact coordinates.
</SteveTip>

To sketch a rational function by hand, follow a strict checklist:
1.  **Draw the Asymptotes:** Calculate the VA and HA. Draw them as **dashed lines** on your axes and label them with their equations (e.g., $x = 2$, $y = -3$).
2.  **Plot the Intercepts:** Calculate the $x$-intercept and $y$-intercept. Mark their coordinates clearly.
3.  **Draw the Curves:** Smoothly draw the two hyperbolic branches approaching the asymptotes, ensuring you pass through your marked intercepts.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Algebraic Intersection & GDC Verification">
    Let $h(x) = \dfrac{3x + 1}{x - 2}$ and $k(x) = x + 4$.
    
    a) Find the exact coordinates of the points of intersection of the graphs of $h$ and $k$ algebraically.  
    b) Explain the sequence of steps to verify your answer using a TI-Nspire calculator.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** Set the equations equal to each other to find the intersection points:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \dfrac{3x + 1}{x - 2} &= x + 4 \\
    3x + 1 &= (x + 4)(x - 2) \\
    3x + 1 &= x^2 + 2x - 8 \\
    0 &= x^2 - x - 9
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    This does not factorise easily, so we apply the quadratic formula ($a = 1, b = -1, c = -9$):
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x &= \dfrac{-(-1) \pm \sqrt{(-1)^2 - 4(1)(-9)}}{2(1)} \\
    x &= \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    Substitute back into the simpler linear equation, $k(x) = x + 4$, to find the $y$-coordinates:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    y &= \left( \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2} \right) + 4 \\
    y &= \dfrac{1 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2} + \dfrac{8}{2} \\
    y &= \dfrac{9 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    The exact coordinates are **$(\frac{1 + \sqrt{37}}{2}, \frac{9 + \sqrt{37}}{2})$** and **$(\frac{1 - \sqrt{37}}{2}, \frac{9 - \sqrt{37}}{2})$**.

    **b)** To verify on the TI-Nspire:
    1. Open a **Graph** page.
    2. Enter $f_1(x) = \dfrac{3x + 1}{x - 2}$ (use `Ctrl` + `÷` for the fraction template) and $f_2(x) = x + 4$.
    3. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**.
    4. Click and drag the shaded region to cover the first intersection point, then repeat for the second. The GDC will output the decimal equivalents of your exact surd coordinates.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Contextual Modelling (Average Cost)">
    A manufacturer determines that the average cost, $A$ in dollars, of producing $x$ reusable water bottles is modelled by the rational function:
    
    $$
    A(x) = \dfrac{1500 + 3.5x}{x}, \quad x > 0
    $$
    
    a) Calculate the exact average cost of producing $50$ bottles.  
    b) Find the number of bottles that must be produced for the average cost to drop to $\$4.00$.  
    c) Write down the equation of the horizontal asymptote for the graph of $y = A(x)$.  
    d) Interpret the meaning of the horizontal asymptote in the context of this manufacturing model.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Substitute $x = 50$ into the function:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    A(50) &= \dfrac{1500 + 3.5(50)}{50} \\
    A(50) &= \dfrac{1500 + 175}{50} \\
    A(50) &= \dfrac{1675}{50} \\
    A(50) &= 33.5
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The average cost is **$\$33.50$**.

    **b)** Set the function equal to $4$ and solve for $x$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    4 &= \dfrac{1500 + 3.5x}{x} \\
    4x &= 1500 + 3.5x \\
    0.5x &= 1500 \\
    x &= 3000
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    They must produce **$3000$ bottles**. *(You can also solve this quickly using `nSolve(4 = (1500 + 3.5x)/x, x)` on your TI-Nspire).*

    **c)** Rewrite the equation in the standard $\frac{ax+b}{cx+d}$ format:
    $$
    A(x) = \dfrac{3.5x + 1500}{1x + 0}
    $$
    The horizontal asymptote is $y = \frac{a}{c}$, so the equation is **$y = 3.5$**.

    **d)** As the number of bottles produced ($x$) becomes extremely large, the average cost approaches $\$3.50$. In context, this means the absolute minimum average cost per bottle is **$\$3.50$**. This likely represents the fixed raw material cost to make one bottle, which remains after the initial $\$1500$ overhead is diluted across thousands of units.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
