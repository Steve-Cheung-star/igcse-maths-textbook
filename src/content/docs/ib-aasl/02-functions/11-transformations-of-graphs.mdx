Functions are not static; they can be moved and resized. By applying specific algebraic changes to a base function $f(x)$, we can transform its graph. This is a vital skill for modelling real-world phenomena, such as shifts in Economic supply and demand curves or the phase shifts in Physics for simple harmonic motion.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections & TOK">
**Cross-curricular Links:** In Economics, a change in consumer income might result in a horizontal translation of the demand curve. In Physics, the vertical stretch of a sine wave represents an increase in amplitude (volume or brightness).

**Mathematical Language:** We often use the word "transformation" in everyday life to mean a total change. In mathematics, transformations are rigid and predictable. Does the precision of mathematical language help or hinder our ability to describe a messy, non-linear world?
</Aside>

## 1. Translations and Reflections

Translations move the graph without changing its shape or orientation, while reflections "flip" the graph across an axis.

### Translations
* **Vertical Translation:** $y = f(x) + b$  
  Shifts the graph **up** by $b$ units (or down if $b$ is negative).
* **Horizontal Translation:** $y = f(x - a)$  
  Shifts the graph **right** by $a$ units. 
  *Note: The sign is counter-intuitive. $f(x - 3)$ moves 3 units to the right.*

### Reflections
* **Reflection in the $x$-axis:** $y = -f(x)$  
  The $y$-values change sign. The graph flips upside down.
* **Reflection in the $y$-axis:** $y = f(-x)$  
  The $x$-values change sign. The graph flips horizontally.

---

## 2. Stretches

Stretches pull the graph away from or push it toward the axes.

* **Vertical Stretch:** $y = p \cdot f(x)$  
  The graph is stretched vertically by a **scale factor of $p$**. 
  * Points on the $x$-axis (where $y=0$) stay fixed.
* **Horizontal Stretch:** $y = f(q \cdot x)$  
  The graph is stretched horizontally by a **scale factor of $\frac{1}{q}$**.
  * Points on the $y$-axis (where $x=0$) stay fixed.
  * *Note: Like horizontal translations, this is counter-intuitive. $f(2x)$ is a horizontal compression by a factor of $0.5$.*



---

## 3. Composite Transformations and Order

When multiple transformations are applied to a base function, the **order matters**. Generally, you should follow the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) or work from the "inside" out:

1.  **Horizontal Transformations** (inside the brackets).
2.  **Stretches/Reflections.**
3.  **Vertical Translations** (outside the brackets).

**Example:** To transform $y = x^2$ into $y = 3x^2 + 2$:
1.  **Vertical Stretch** by scale factor 3.
2.  **Vertical Translation** up by 2 units.

*Note: For the SL syllabus, you are not required to perform transformations of the form $f(ax + b)$—you only need to handle them one at a time or in clear composite steps.*

    The graph of $y = f(x)$ is transformed to the graph of $g(x) = -2f(x + 4)$.
    
    Describe the three transformations that have been applied to $f(x)$ to obtain $g(x)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    Following the order of operations and the transformation rules:

    1.  **Horizontal Translation:** The $(x + 4)$ term indicates a shift **4 units to the left**.
    2.  **Reflection:** The negative sign in front of the 2 indicates a **reflection in the $x$-axis**.
    3.  **Vertical Stretch:** The coefficient 2 indicates a **vertical stretch with a scale factor of 2**.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Transforming Coordinates">
    A function $f(x)$ passes through the point $(3, 10)$. 
    
    Find the new coordinates of this point after the following transformations:
    a) $y = f(x - 5) - 2$  
    b) $y = f(2x) + 7$  
    c) $y = -3f(x)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** $y = f(x - 5) - 2$
    * Horizontal shift: $x \to 3 + 5 = 8$
    * Vertical shift: $y \to 10 - 2 = 8$
    * New Point: **$(8, 8)$**

    **b)** $y = f(2x) + 7$
    * Horizontal stretch (SF $\frac{1}{2}$): $x \to 3 \times 0.5 = 1.5$
    * Vertical shift: $y \to 10 + 7 = 17$
    * New Point: **$(1.5, 17)$**

    **c)** $y = -3f(x)$
    * Vertical stretch (SF 3): $y \to 10 \times 3 = 30$
    * Reflection in $x$-axis: $y \to -30$
    * New Point: **$(3, -30)$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Transforming Asymptotes">
    The rational function $h(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$ has a vertical asymptote at $x = 0$ and a horizontal asymptote at $y = 0$.
    
    Find the equations of the new asymptotes for the function $g(x) = \dfrac{4}{x - 3} + 5$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    The function $g(x)$ is a transformed version of $h(x)$:
    $$ g(x) = 4 \cdot h(x - 3) + 5 $$

    **Vertical Asymptote:**
    * The original VA is $x = 0$.
    * The horizontal translation $(x - 3)$ moves the graph 3 units right.
    * New VA: **$x = 3$**.

    **Horizontal Asymptote:**
    * The original HA is $y = 0$.
    * The vertical stretch (factor 4) affects $y$, but $0 \times 4 = 0$, so it remains at 0.
    * The vertical translation $(+ 5)$ moves the graph up 5 units.
    * New HA: **$y = 5$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    The graph of $f(x) = x^2$ is stretched vertically by a scale factor of $0.5$, reflected in the $y$-axis, and then translated **3 units down**.
