A graph is the visual soul of an algebraic function. Across various disciplines—from sketching supply and demand curves in economics, to interpreting topographical profiles in geography, to mapping velocity in physics—graphs allow us to instantly comprehend the behavior of a system. 

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Rigour and Representation:** Does studying the graph of a function contain the same level of mathematical rigour as studying the function algebraically? Some argue that a graph is just a "picture" and algebra is the pure truth, yet graphs can instantly reveal global behaviors (like asymptotes or limits) that are easily hidden in messy algebra. What are the advantages and disadvantages of having these different forms of symbolic language?
</Aside>

## 1. "Draw" vs "Sketch" (The IB Command Terms)

One of the most common ways students lose marks in Paper 1 and Paper 2 is by misunderstanding what the examiner is asking them to physically put on the page. You must know the strict difference between these two command terms.

### The "Draw" Command
When the IB asks you to **draw** a graph, they expect high precision. 
* You **must** use graph paper (or the provided grid).
* You **must** use a ruler for straight lines and axes.
* You **must** plot points accurately to scale.
* *Analogy:* An architectural blueprint.

### The "Sketch" Command
When the IB asks you to **sketch** a graph, they are testing your understanding of the graph's *features*, not your artistic precision.
* You do **not** need graph paper; an unruled box or blank space is fine.
* It does **not** need to be perfectly to scale, but proportions should be reasonable.
* You **must** label all key features algebraically.
* *Analogy:* A caricature that exaggerates and highlights the most important traits.

## 2. Transferring from Screen to Paper

In Paper 2, you will frequently be given a complex function and asked to sketch it. You must use your TI-Nspire to view the graph, and then accurately reproduce it on your exam paper.

<Aside type="danger" title="The Golden Rules of Sketching">
If you miss any of these, you will drop marks, even if the general shape of your curve is perfect:
1. **Label the Axes:** Always write $x$ and $y$ (or $t$ and $h$, depending on the context).
2. **Label the Intercepts:** Clearly write the numerical coordinates of where the graph crosses the axes.
3. **Label Turning Points:** Use your GDC's **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 2: Minimum** (or Maximum) tool to find the exact coordinates of the peaks and valleys, and label them.
4. **Label Asymptotes:** If the graph levels out or avoids a certain line, draw it as a dotted line and write its equation (e.g., $y = 2$ or $x = -1$).
</Aside>

## 3. Sums and Differences of Functions

Sometimes you will be asked to graph or evaluate the result of adding or subtracting two entirely different functions. 
For example, if $h(x) = f(x) + g(x)$, the $y$-value of $h(x)$ at any given point is simply the $y$-value of $f(x)$ plus the $y$-value of $g(x)$.

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire Mastery: Graphing Sums and Differences">
You do not always need to combine the algebra by hand! Your TI-Nspire can graph the sum or difference of two functions dynamically, which is a massive time-saver on Paper 2 and prevents manual arithmetic errors.

<Steps>
1. Open a **Graphs** page.
2. In the `f1(x)` entry line, type your first function (e.g., `50x - x^2`) and press **Enter**.
3. Press **Tab** to open the entry line again. In `f2(x)`, type your second function (e.g., `10x + 100`) and press **Enter**.
4. Press **Tab** again. In `f3(x)`, simply type `f1(x) - f2(x)` and press **Enter**. The GDC will instantly plot the combined function!
5. *Clean up your screen:* If the screen is too cluttered, press **Menu > 3: Graph Entry/Edit > 1: Function**, arrow up to `f1(x)` and `f2(x)`, and uncheck the boxes next to them to hide the original curves, leaving only your final result.
</Steps>
</SteveTip>

**Worked Example: Subtracting Functions (Profit Model)**
In business, Profit $P(x)$ is defined as Revenue $R(x)$ minus Cost $C(x)$.
Given a revenue function $R(x) = 50x - x^2$ and a cost function $C(x) = 10x + 100$:
<Steps>
1. **Form the new function algebraically:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   P(x) &= R(x) - C(x) \\
   P(x) &= (50x - x^2) - (10x + 100)
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Simplify the expression:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   P(x) &= 50x - x^2 - 10x - 100 \\
   P(x) &= -x^2 + 40x - 100
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Analyze:**
   Whether you graphed $y = -x^2 + 40x - 100$ manually or used $f_3(x) = f_1(x) - f_2(x)$ on your TI-Nspire, you can now analyze the parabola to find the break-even points (roots) and the maximum possible profit (the vertex).
</Steps>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: GDC Sketching & Key Features">
    Let $f(x) = x^3 - 4x^2 + x + 6$.
    
    **a)** Sketch the graph of $y = f(x)$ for the domain $-2 \le x \le 5$.
    
    **b)** Write down the coordinates of the $y$-intercept.
    
    **c)** Write down the coordinates of all $x$-intercepts.
    
    **d)** Write down the coordinates of the local minimum, correct to 3 significant figures.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a (Sketching Strategy):** Enter the equation into your TI-Nspire. Press **Menu > 4: Window / Zoom > 1: Window Settings** and adjust $XMin = -2$ and $XMax = 5$. Draw a set of axes on your paper, label them $x$ and $y$, and draw the smooth curve mimicking the screen.
       
    2. **Part b (y-intercept):**
       Substitute $x = 0$ into the equation (or use **Menu > 5: Trace > 1: Graph Trace** and type `0`):
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       f(0) &= (0)^3 - 4(0)^2 + 0 + 6 \\
       f(0) &= 6
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       The coordinate is $(0, 6)$. Label this on your sketch!

    3. **Part c (x-intercepts):**
       Use **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero**. Click to the left and right of each crossing point.
       The coordinates are $(-1, 0)$, $(2, 0)$, and $(3, 0)$.

    4. **Part d (Local Minimum):**
       Use **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 2: Minimum**. 
       The GDC gives $x = 2.535...$ and $y = -0.879...$.
       Rounding to 3 sig figs, the coordinate is $(2.54, -0.879)$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Unfamiliar Functions">
    Let $g(x) = \frac{2^x}{x}$ for $x > 0$.
    *(Note: This type of mixed function is fully expected on Paper 2!)*
    
    **a)** Sketch the graph of $g(x)$ on the domain $0 < x \le 5$.
    
    **b)** Find the coordinates of the local minimum point.
    
    **c)** The line $y = 3$ intersects the graph of $g(x)$ at two distinct points. Find the $x$-coordinates of these points.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a (Sketching Strategy):**
       Type `f1(x) = (2^x) / x` into your GDC. Notice that as $x$ gets closer to $0$, the graph shoots upwards to infinity (there is a vertical asymptote at $x = 0$). Sketch this characteristic "U-shape" curve on your paper.
       
    2. **Part b (Local Minimum):**
       Use the Analyze Graph Minimum tool.
       The tool outputs $x = 1.44269...$ and $y = 1.88416...$
       Correct to 3 significant figures, the local minimum is $(1.44, 1.88)$.

    3. **Part c (Intersection):**
       Press **Tab** and add a second equation: `f2(x) = 3`. 
       Use **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection** to find where the horizontal line cuts through the curve.
       The GDC provides two $x$-values:
       $x_1 = 0.458$ (to 3 s.f.)
       $x_2 = 3.31$ (to 3 s.f.)
    </Steps>

    <SteveTip title="GDC Mastery: Finding Intersections">
    Never try to solve mixed equations like $\frac{2^x}{x} = 3$ by hand in SL. Algebraically isolating $x$ when it appears as both a base and an exponent is incredibly difficult and often impossible without advanced university math. If it's a Paper 2, immediately graph both sides as separate functions and use the intersection tool!
    </SteveTip>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Sum of Two Functions (Algebraic & Graphical)">
    Consider the functions $f(x) = x^2 - 4$ and $g(x) = -x + 2$.
    Let $h(x) = f(x) + g(x)$.
    
    **a)** Find an expression for $h(x)$ in the form $ax^2 + bx + c$.
    
    **b)** Find the $x$-intercepts of $h(x)$.
    
    **c)** State the coordinates of the $y$-intercept of $h(x)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a:** Add the two functions algebraically.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       h(x) &= (x^2 - 4) + (-x + 2) \\
       h(x) &= x^2 - x - 2
       \end{aligned}
       $$

    2. **Part b:** To find the $x$-intercepts, set $h(x) = 0$ and factorize.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       0 &= x^2 - x - 2 \\
       0 &= (x - 2)(x + 1)
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       The solutions are $x = 2$ and $x = -1$.
       The coordinates are $(2, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$.

    3. **Part c:** To find the $y$-intercept, set $x = 0$.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       h(0) &= (0)^2 - (0) - 2 \\
       h(0) &= -2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       The coordinate is $(0, -2)$. 
       *(Notice this is exactly $f(0) + g(0) \implies -4 + 2 = -2$. The sum of the functions holds true at every individual coordinate!)*
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
