While some equations can be manipulated algebraically into familiar forms (like quadratics), others mix incompatible functions and strictly require technology to solve.

## 1. Analytic Methods: Disguised Quadratics

Sometimes, an equation looks exponential or logarithmic but is actually a quadratic equation in disguise. The key to solving these is **substitution**.

Consider the syllabus example: $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 4 = 0$

Notice that $e^{2x} = (e^x)^2$. If we let a dummy variable, say $u$, equal $e^x$, the equation transforms into something highly familiar:

$$
\begin{aligned}
u^2 - 5u + 4 &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

From here, you factorise and solve for $u$, and then swap $e^x$ back in to find the final values for $x$. *(You will see the full working for this in Question 1 below).*

---

## 2. Using Technology: When Algebra Fails

Algebra has limits. If an equation mixes a polynomial with an exponential, or an exponential with a trigonometric function, there is generally **no analytic approach** to solve it. 

Examples of equations requiring a GDC:
* $e^x = \sin(x)$
* $x^4 + 5x - 6 = 0$ (while some higher-order polynomials can be factored, most require tech).
* $2^x = x + 3$

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire Tips for Solving Equations">
There are two main ways to solve complex equations on your GDC:

**Method 1: The Graphing Intersection Method (Highly Recommended)**
1. Open a **Graph** page.
2. Enter the left side of the equation as $f_1(x)$.
3. Enter the right side of the equation as $f_2(x)$.
4. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**.
5. The $x$-coordinates of the intersection points are your solutions.

**Method 2: Numerical Solve**
1. Open a **Calculator** page.
2. Press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)**.
3. Type your equation followed by a comma and the variable: `nSolve(e^x = sin(x), x)`.
*Note: nSolve only gives ONE solution at a time. If there are multiple solutions, Method 1 is much safer to ensure you don't miss any.*
</SteveTip>

## Practice Questions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Disguised Quadratics">
    Solve the equation $e^{2x} - 5e^x + 4 = 0$ analytically. Show all your working.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    Recognise that $e^{2x}$ is the square of $e^x$. We can use a substitution.
    
    Let $u = e^x$.
    
    Substitute $u$ into the equation:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u^2 - 5u + 4 &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    Factorise the quadratic:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    (u - 4)(u - 1) &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    This gives two possible values for $u$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u &= 4 \quad \text{or} \quad u &= 1
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    Substitute $e^x$ back in for $u$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    e^x &= 4 \quad &\text{or} \quad e^x &= 1
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    Take the natural logarithm ($\ln$) of both sides for each case:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x &= \ln(4) \quad &\text{or} \quad x &= \ln(1)
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    Since $\ln(1) = 0$, the exact solutions are:
    **$x = \ln(4)$** and **$x = 0$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Real-Life Application (Finance)">
    Two competing investments are being monitored over time, $t$, in years. 
    
    * **Investment A** starts with a high capital but grows linearly: $V_A(t) = 15000 + 1200t$
    * **Investment B** starts with lower capital but grows continuously: $V_B(t) = 8000 e^{0.08t}$
    
    a) Find the initial value of Investment B.  
    b) Calculate the value of Investment A after $10$ years.  
    c) Using your GDC, find the exact time it takes for the value of Investment B to catch up and equal Investment A. State the time in years (to 3 s.f.) and the value at which they are equal.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Set $t = 0$ for Investment B:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    V_B(0) &= 8000 e^{0.08(0)} \\
    V_B(0) &= 8000 e^0 \\
    V_B(0) &= 8000 \times 1
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The initial value is **8000 EUR**.

    **b)** Set $t = 10$ for Investment A:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    V_A(10) &= 15000 + 1200(10) \\
    V_A(10) &= 15000 + 12000 \\
    V_A(10) &= 27000
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    After 10 years, Investment A is worth **27000 EUR**.

    **c)** We need to solve $V_A(t) = V_B(t)$, which means:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    15000 + 1200t &= 8000 e^{0.08t}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    This mixes a linear polynomial with an exponential, so it requires a GDC.
    
    1. Graph $f_1(x) = 15000 + 1200x$.
    2. Graph $f_2(x) = 8000 \cdot e^{0.08x}$.
    3. Adjust your window. We know from part (b) that at 10 years, the values are around 27000. Try setting $x$ from $0$ to $30$, and $y$ from $0$ to $50000$.
    4. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**.
    
    The GDC yields the coordinate point $(17.808..., 36369.94...)$.
    
    * It will take **17.8 years**.
    * The investments will be equal at **36400 EUR** (rounded to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A drone's vertical displacement, $s$, in metres above a launch pad at time $t$ seconds is modelled by the polynomial:
    $$
    s(t) = t^4 - 6t^3 + 11t^2 - 6t
    $$
    
    Use your GDC to find all the times during the flight when the drone is exactly at ground level ($s = 0$).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    We need to solve the equation:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    t^4 - 6t^3 + 11t^2 - 6t &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    While you *could* factorise an $t$ out, the resulting cubic is difficult to solve by hand. This is a perfect candidate for the GDC.
    
    1. Graph $f_1(x) = x^4 - 6x^3 + 11x^2 - 6x$.
    2. We are looking for the $x$-intercepts (roots).
    3. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero**.
    
    The graph crosses the horizontal axis multiple times. By repeating the "Zero" function for each intercept, the GDC gives:
    
    **$t = 0$, $t = 1$, $t = 2$, and $t = 3$**.
    
    The drone is at ground level exactly at **0, 1, 2, and 3 seconds**.
  </TabItem>
