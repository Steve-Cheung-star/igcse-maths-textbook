## 1. Constructing Diagrams from Text

The most critical skill in Topic 3.3 is drawing a well-labelled diagram. If your diagram is wrong, the subsequent math will be wrong, even if your algebra is flawless.

<SteveTip title="Drawing the Perfect Diagram">
1. **Read the entire problem first** before drawing anything, to understand the scale and layout.
2. **Draw large.** A cramped diagram makes it difficult to label angles and see geometric relationships.
3. **Add one piece of information at a time.** Label vertices with capital letters (A, B, C) and clearly mark right angles.
4. **Identify the goal.** Highlight the specific side or angle you are trying to find so you know which rule to apply.
</SteveTip>

## 2. Angles of Elevation and Depression

When an observer looks at an object, their line of sight forms an angle with the perfectly horizontal line extending from their eyes.

* **Angle of Elevation:** The angle formed when looking **up** from the horizontal.
* **Angle of Depression:** The angle formed when looking **down** from the horizontal.

**Crucial Geometric Property:** Because the horizontal line at the observer's eye level and the horizontal line at the object's level are parallel, the angle of elevation from observer A to object B is exactly equal to the angle of depression from object B to observer A (they are alternate interior "Z" angles).

## 3. Bearings and Navigation

Bearings are used to define direction, primarily in navigation for ships and aircraft. To properly interpret and draw a bearing, you must follow three absolute rules:

1.  **Always measure from North.** Draw a vertical North line pointing "up" at the starting position.
2.  **Always measure clockwise.**
3.  **Always use three digits.** For example, an angle of 45° measured clockwise from North is written as a bearing of 045°. Due East is 090°, South is 180°, and West is 270°.

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Wheelchair Ramp (Right Angled)">
    A building requires a wheelchair ramp to be installed to reach a door that is 1.2 m above the flat ground. The ramp begins at a point 14 m horizontally away from the base of the door.
    
    **a)** Calculate the length of the ramp surface.  
    **b)** Calculate the angle of elevation of the ramp.  
    **c)** Local safety regulations state that a wheelchair ramp must have an angle of elevation no greater than 4.8°. Does this ramp meet the safety regulations? Justify your answer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a) Length of the ramp**
    This forms a right-angled triangle. We can use Pythagoras' Theorem.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    c^2 &= a^2 + b^2 \\
    c^2 &= 14^2 + 1.2^2 \\
    c^2 &= 196 + 1.44 = 197.44 \\
    c &= \sqrt{197.44} \approx 14.051...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The ramp surface is **14.1 m** long (to 3 s.f.).

    **b) Angle of elevation**
    We know the opposite (1.2 m) and adjacent (14 m) sides. We use TOA.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \tan(\theta) &= \frac{\text{Opp}}{\text{Adj}} = \frac{1.2}{14} \\
    \theta &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1.2}{14}\right) \\
    \theta &\approx 4.899...^\circ
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The angle of elevation is **4.90°** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c) Safety verification**
    No, the ramp **does not** meet safety regulations. The calculated angle of 4.90° is greater than the maximum allowed 4.8°.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: The Lighthouse & Two Boats">
    An observer stands at the top of a lighthouse, 65 m above sea level. She spots two boats, A and B, situated directly in line with the lighthouse on the same side. 
    The angle of depression to Boat A is 32°.
    The angle of depression to Boat B is 19°.
    
    **a)** Calculate the horizontal distance from the base of the lighthouse to Boat A.  
    **b)** Calculate the horizontal distance from the base of the lighthouse to Boat B.  
    **c)** Calculate the exact distance between Boat A and Boat B.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    *Remember: Angle of depression from the top = Angle of elevation from the boat.*

    **a) Distance to Boat A**
    Using the right-angled triangle formed by the lighthouse and Boat A. The opposite side is 65 m, and the angle is 32°.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \tan(32^\circ) &= \frac{65}{d_A} \\
    d_A &= \frac{65}{\tan(32^\circ)} \approx 104.02...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Boat A is **104 m** away (to 3 s.f.).

    **b) Distance to Boat B**
    Using the right-angled triangle formed by the lighthouse and Boat B. The angle is 19°.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \tan(19^\circ) &= \frac{65}{d_B} \\
    d_B &= \frac{65}{\tan(19^\circ)} \approx 188.77...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Boat B is **189 m** away (to 3 s.f.).

    **c) Distance between the boats**
    Subtract the unrounded horizontal distances.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Distance} &= d_B - d_A \\
    \text{Distance} &= 188.77... - 104.02... = 84.75...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The boats are **84.8 m** apart (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Non-Right Bearings (Helicopter Rescue)">
    A rescue helicopter leaves a hospital (H) and flies 45 km on a bearing of 080° to pick up a patient at point P. 
    It then flies 60 km on a bearing of 210° to drop the patient at a specialized clinic (C).
    
    **a)** Show that the interior angle $\angle HPC$ is 50°.  
    **b)** Calculate the direct distance from the hospital (H) to the clinic (C).  
    **c)** Calculate the area of the triangle formed by H, P, and C.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a) Show the interior angle is 50°**
    * Draw a North line at H. The bearing to P is 080°.
    * Draw a North line at P. The co-interior angle looking back at H is $180^\circ - 080^\circ = 100^\circ$.
    * The bearing from P to C is 210°. 
    * The angles around point P must equal 360°.
    * Interior $\angle HPC = 360^\circ - 100^\circ - 210^\circ = 50^\circ$.

    **b) Distance from H to C**
    We have SAS ($HP = 45$, $PC = 60$, included angle = 50°). Use the Cosine Rule.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    HC^2 &= 45^2 + 60^2 - 2(45)(60)\cos(50^\circ) \\
    HC^2 &= 2025 + 3600 - 5400(0.6427...) \\
    HC^2 &= 5625 - 3471.05... \\
    HC^2 &= 2153.94... \\
    HC &= \sqrt{2153.94...} \approx 46.41...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The direct distance is **46.4 km** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c) Area of the triangle**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2}(45)(60)\sin(50^\circ) \\
    \text{Area} &= 1350 \times 0.7660... \approx 1034.1...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The area is **1030 km²** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Triangulation & The Sine Rule">
    Two fire lookout towers, Tower X and Tower Y, are located 12 km apart on a straight East-West road, with Tower Y directly East of Tower X. 
    Both towers spot a forest fire at point F. 
    The bearing of the fire from Tower X is 055°.
    The bearing of the fire from Tower Y is 315°.
    
    **a)** Calculate the interior angles of triangle XYF.  
    **b)** Calculate the distance from Tower X to the fire.  
    **c)** Calculate the shortest (perpendicular) distance from the fire to the road.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a) Interior angles**
    * The road is East-West, which corresponds to bearings of 090° and 270°.
    * At Tower X, the fire is at 055°. The angle from the North line down to the East road is 090°. So, $\angle FXY = 90^\circ - 55^\circ = 35^\circ$.
    * At Tower Y, the road points West (270° from North). The fire is at 315°. The angle inside the triangle is $\angle FYX = 315^\circ - 270^\circ = 45^\circ$.
    * The angle at the fire: $\angle XFY = 180^\circ - 35^\circ - 45^\circ = 100^\circ$.
    **Angles: 35°, 45°, 100°.**

    **b) Distance from Tower X to the fire ($YF$)**
    We have a matching pair: $\angle F = 100^\circ$ and side $XY = 12$. We want side $XF$ (opposite the 45° angle at Y).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \frac{XF}{\sin(45^\circ)} &= \frac{12}{\sin(100^\circ)} \\
    XF &= 12 \times \frac{\sin(45^\circ)}{\sin(100^\circ)} \\
    XF &\approx 12 \times \frac{0.7071...}{0.9848...} \approx 8.616...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The distance is **8.62 km** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c) Shortest distance to the road**
    This creates a right-angled triangle with the road as the adjacent, the perpendicular distance ($h$) as the opposite, and $XF$ as the hypotenuse. We use the 35° angle at X.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \sin(35^\circ) &= \frac{h}{8.616...} \\
    h &= 8.616... \times \sin(35^\circ) \\
    h &\approx 4.942...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The shortest distance is **4.94 km** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Composite Structures">
    A communications mast is mounted vertically on top of a flat-roofed building. 
    From point M on the ground, 50 m from the building's base, the angle of elevation to the top of the building is 28°, and the angle of elevation to the top of the mast is 42°.
    
    **a)** Calculate the height of the building.  
    **b)** Calculate the height of the mast itself.  
    **c)** A supporting cable is attached from point M to the very top of the mast. Calculate the length of this cable.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a) Height of the building ($h_B$)**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \tan(28^\circ) &= \frac{h_B}{50} \\
    h_B &= 50\tan(28^\circ) \approx 26.58...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The building is **26.6 m** tall.

    **b) Height of the mast**
    First, find the total height to the top of the mast ($h_T$).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \tan(42^\circ) &= \frac{h_T}{50} \\
    h_T &= 50\tan(42^\circ) \approx 45.02...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Mast height = $h_T - h_B = 45.02... - 26.58... = 18.43...$
    The mast is **18.4 m** tall.

    **c) Length of the cable**
    This is the hypotenuse of the large right triangle (angle 42°, adjacent 50).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \cos(42^\circ) &= \frac{50}{\text{Cable}} \\
    \text{Cable} &= \frac{50}{\cos(42^\circ)} \approx 67.28...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The cable is **67.3 m** long.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Finding a Return Bearing (Advanced)">
    A drone maps a forest area. It flies from base station S for 14 km on a bearing of 110° to point A. 
    It then flies 22 km from point A on a bearing of 260° to point B.
    
    **a)** Calculate the distance the drone must fly to return directly from point B to the base station S.  
    **b)** Calculate the bearing the drone must take to fly from point B back to base station S.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a) Distance to return (SB)**
    * Interior angle at A: North line S to A is 110°. Co-interior angle at A looking back to S is $180^\circ - 110^\circ = 070^\circ$.
    * Bearing of B from A is 260°. 
    * The angle inside the triangle $\angle SAB = 360^\circ - 260^\circ - 070^\circ = 30^\circ$.
    * Use the Cosine Rule (SAS): $SA = 14$, $AB = 22$, included angle = 30°.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    SB^2 &= 14^2 + 22^2 - 2(14)(22)\cos(30^\circ) \\
    SB^2 &= 196 + 484 - 616(0.8660...) \\
    SB^2 &= 680 - 533.47... = 146.52... \\
    SB &= \sqrt{146.52...} \approx 12.10...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The return distance is **12.1 km**.

    **b) Return Bearing from B to S**
    * First, find the interior angle at B ($\angle SBA$) using the Sine Rule.
      $$\frac{\sin B}{14} = \frac{\sin 30^\circ}{12.10...} \Rightarrow \sin B \approx 0.578 \Rightarrow B \approx 35.3^\circ$$
    * Now, set up the North line at B.
    * The line BA comes from A (bearing 260°). The co-interior angle at B looking back to A is $260^\circ - 180^\circ = 080^\circ$. (Or use parallel North lines: $180^\circ - (360^\circ - 260^\circ) = 080^\circ$).
    * The bearing to S is measured clockwise from North. We go 080° to the line BA, then subtract the interior angle $35.3^\circ$ to get to the line BS.
    * $\text{Bearing} = 080^\circ - 35.3^\circ = 044.7^\circ$.
    The bearing is **045°** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Fencing and Area Costs">
    A farmer owns a triangular plot of land bounded by three markers, P, Q, and R. 
    The distance from P to Q is 120 m. The distance from P to R is 150 m. The angle at corner P is 68°.
    
    The farmer wants to enclose the entire plot with fencing that costs $12.50 per metre.
    He also needs to buy fertilizer for the area, which costs $0.40 per square metre.
    
    **a)** Calculate the total perimeter of the plot.  
    **b)** Calculate the total cost of the fencing.  
    **c)** Calculate the total cost of the fertilizer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a) Total Perimeter**
    We need side $QR$. Use Cosine Rule.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    QR^2 &= 120^2 + 150^2 - 2(120)(150)\cos(68^\circ) \\
    QR^2 &= 14400 + 22500 - 36000(0.3746...) \\
    QR^2 &= 36900 - 13485.8... = 23414.1... \\
    QR &= \sqrt{23414.1...} \approx 153.01...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    $$\text{Perimeter} = 120 + 150 + 153.01... = 423.01...$$
    The perimeter is **423 m** (to 3 s.f.).

    **b) Fencing Cost**
    $$\text{Cost} = 423.01... \times 12.50 = 5287.6...$$
    The fencing costs **$5288** (to nearest dollar).

    **c) Fertilizer Cost**
    First, find the area using $A = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2}(120)(150)\sin(68^\circ) \\
    \text{Area} &= 9000 \times 0.9271... = 8344.6... \text{ m}^2
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Now find the cost.
    $$\text{Cost} = 8344.6... \times 0.40 = 3337.8...$$
    The fertilizer costs **$3338** (to nearest dollar).
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Multi-Step Speeds & Dynamics">
    A cargo ship leaves Port A and travels on a bearing of 135° at a constant speed of 24 km/h for 2.5 hours until it drops anchor at point B.
    A patrol boat leaves Port A at the same time and travels on a bearing of 045° at a constant speed of 35 km/h for 2.5 hours to point C.
    
    **a)** Calculate the distance each vessel has travelled.  
    **b)** Calculate the distance between the two vessels when they stop.  
    **c)** What bearing would the patrol boat at point C need to take to sail directly to the cargo ship at point B?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a) Distances Travelled**
    * Cargo Ship ($AB$): $24 \times 2.5 = 60 \text{ km}$.
    * Patrol Boat ($AC$): $35 \times 2.5 = 87.5 \text{ km}$.

    **b) Distance between vessels ($BC$)**
    * The angle between their paths at Port A is $135^\circ - 045^\circ = 090^\circ$. 
    * Since it is exactly 90°, we can use Pythagoras!
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    BC^2 &= 60^2 + 87.5^2 \\
    BC^2 &= 3600 + 7656.25 = 11256.25 \\
    BC &= \sqrt{11256.25} = 106.09...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The vessels are **106 km** apart.

    **c) Bearing from C to B**
    * We need the interior angle at C ($\angle ACB$). Use basic SOH CAH TOA because it's a right triangle.
    $$\tan(C) = \frac{\text{Opp}}{\text{Adj}} = \frac{60}{87.5} \Rightarrow C \approx 34.4^\circ$$
    * Set up a North line at C. The bearing from A to C was 045°. The co-interior "look back" angle at C to A is $180^\circ - 045^\circ = 135^\circ$.
    * The bearing to A is 135° clockwise, plus the interior angle of 34.4° down to the line CB.
    * $\text{Bearing} = 135^\circ + 34.4^\circ = 169.4^\circ$.
    The required bearing is **169°**.
  </TabItem>
