## 1. The Unit Circle Definition of Sine and Cosine

Imagine a circle with a radius of exactly $1$ unit, centred at the origin $(0,0)$ of a Cartesian coordinate system. If we draw a line from the origin to a point on the circle, making an angle $\theta$ with the positive x-axis, the coordinates of that point are precisely defined by cosine and sine.

* The **x-coordinate** is $\cos\theta$.
* The **y-coordinate** is $\sin\theta$.

This definition explains the behaviour of angles across different quadrants, often remembered using the **CAST diagram**:

| Quadrant | Angle Range | CAST Letter | Positive Ratios | Negative Ratios |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st** | $0 < \theta < \dfrac{\pi}{2}$ | **A** (All) | $\sin$, $\cos$, $\tan$ | None |
| **2nd** | $\dfrac{\pi}{2} < \theta < \pi$ | **S** (Sine) | $\sin$ | $\cos$, $\tan$ |
| **3rd** | $\pi < \theta < \dfrac{3\pi}{2}$ | **T** (Tangent) | $\tan$ | $\sin$, $\cos$ |
| **4th** | $\dfrac{3\pi}{2} < \theta < 2\pi$ | **C** (Cosine) | $\cos$ | $\sin$, $\tan$ |

Because of the circle's symmetry, we can deduce important equivalent identities:
* $\cos x = \cos(-x)$ (cosine is positive in the 4th quadrant, mirroring the 1st)
* $\sin(\pi + x) = -\sin x$ (moving into the 3rd quadrant makes the y-coordinate negative)
* $\tan(3\pi - x) = -\tan x$ (equivalent to $\pi - x$, which is in the 2nd quadrant where tangent is negative)

### Worked Example 1: Unit Circle Symmetries
**Question:** Given that $\sin x = 0.6$ and $x$ is acute, find the value of $\sin(\pi - x)$ and $\sin(-x)$.

**Solution:**
The angle $(\pi - x)$ is in the second quadrant. Since sine corresponds to the y-coordinate, and y is positive in both the first and second quadrants:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin(\pi - x) &= \sin x \\
&= 0.6
\end{aligned}
$$

The angle $-x$ is in the fourth quadrant. The y-coordinate here is negative:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin(-x) &= -\sin x \\
&= -0.6
\end{aligned}
$$

## 2. The Tangent Function and Straight Lines

The tangent of an angle is fundamentally linked to the slope (gradient) of a line.

Identity for $\tan \theta$
<FormulaBox>
  $$\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$$
</FormulaBox>


Because slope is defined as "change in $y$ over change in $x$" ($\dfrac{y}{x}$), and on the unit circle $y = \sin\theta$ and $x = \cos\theta$, the tangent of $\theta$ is exactly the slope of the line passing through the origin!

Therefore, the equation of a straight line through the origin can be written as:
$$
\begin{aligned}
y &= x\tan\theta
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\theta$ is the angle formed between the line and the positive x-axis.

### Worked Example 2: Straight Lines
**Question:** A straight line passes through the origin and makes an angle of $\dfrac{3\pi}{4}$ radians with the positive x-axis. Find the exact equation of this line.

**Solution:**
Using the relationship $y = x\tan\theta$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
y &= x\tan\left(\dfrac{3\pi}{4}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$
The angle $\dfrac{3\pi}{4}$ is in the second quadrant, where tangent is negative. The reference angle is $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$, and $\tan\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right) = 1$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\tan\left(\dfrac{3\pi}{4}\right) &= -1 \\
y &= -1x \\
y &= -x
\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Exact Values of Trigonometric Ratios

You are expected to memorise the exact trigonometric ratios for standard angles across different quadrants.

| Angle (radians) | Angle (degrees) | $\sin\theta$ | $\cos\theta$ | $\tan\theta$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0$ | $0^\circ$ | $0$ | $1$ | $0$ |
| $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$ | $30^\circ$ | $\dfrac{1}{2}$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$ |
| $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ | $45^\circ$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $1$ |
| $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$ | $60^\circ$ | $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\dfrac{1}{2}$ | $\sqrt{3}$ |
| $\dfrac{\pi}{2}$ | $90^\circ$ | $1$ | $0$ | *Undefined* |

<SteveTip title="The Left-Hand Trick">
  To easily memorise exact values in the first quadrant, use your left hand. Assign $0, \dfrac{\pi}{6}, \dfrac{\pi}{4}, \dfrac{\pi}{3}, \dfrac{\pi}{2}$ to your thumb through to your pinky. Fold down the finger of the angle you want. 
  * $\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{\text{fingers below}}}{2}$
  * $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{\text{fingers above}}}{2}$
</SteveTip>

### Worked Example 3: Exact Values
**Question:** Find the exact value of $\sin\left(\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right)$.

**Solution:**
The angle $\dfrac{4\pi}{3}$ is in the third quadrant, where sine is negative. The reference angle is $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin\left(\dfrac{4\pi}{3}\right) &= -\sin\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) \\
&= -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 4. The Ambiguous Case of the Sine Rule

When you are given two sides and a non-included angle (SSA - Side, Side, Angle), there is a possibility that **two different triangles** can be drawn with those exact measurements. This is known as the **ambiguous case**.

Sine rule
<FormulaBox>
  $$\dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B} = \dfrac{c}{\sin C}$$
</FormulaBox>


<Aside type="danger" title="Calculator Limitations">
  When solving for a missing angle using the inverse sine function ($\arcsin$ or $\sin^{-1}$) on your calculator, it will only ever return the **acute** angle (1st quadrant). Because $\sin\theta = \sin(180^\circ - \theta)$, you must manually check if the obtuse angle ($180^\circ - \theta$) is also a valid mathematical possibility for your triangle!
</Aside>

### Worked Example 4: The Ambiguous Case
**Question:** In triangle $PQR$, $PQ = 10 \text{ cm}$, $PR = 14 \text{ cm}$, and angle $PQR = 45^\circ$. Find all possible values for angle $PRQ$.

**Solution:**
Set up the Sine Rule:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dfrac{14}{\sin(45^\circ)} &= \dfrac{10}{\sin R} \\
\sin R &= \dfrac{10 \times \sin(45^\circ)}{14} \\
\sin R &\approx 0.50507...
\end{aligned}
$$

Find the acute angle ($R_1$):
$$
\begin{aligned}
R_1 &= \arcsin(0.50507...) \\
R_1 &\approx 30.3^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Check for the obtuse ambiguous angle ($R_2$):
$$
\begin{aligned}
R_2 &= 180^\circ - 30.33...^\circ \\
R_2 &\approx 149.7^\circ
\end{aligned}
$$

Verify if $R_2$ is valid: If $R = 149.7^\circ$ and $Q = 45^\circ$, the sum is $194.7^\circ$, which exceeds $180^\circ$. Therefore, there is no ambiguous case here. The only valid angle is $30.3^\circ$.

       &= p
       \end{aligned}

       &= -\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi}{7}\right) \\
       &= -p
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
    1. **Identify the quadrant for $\dfrac{8\pi}{7}$.**
       This is slightly more than $\pi$, putting it in the third quadrant where cosine is also negative.
    2. **Express using the reference angle.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \cos\left(\dfrac{8\pi}{7}\right) &= \cos\left(\pi + \dfrac{\pi}{7}\right) \\
       &= -\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi}{7}\right) \\
       &= -p
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>

    In a triangle $ABC$, $AB = 7 \text{ cm}$, $BC = 9 \text{ cm}$, and angle $BAC = 65^\circ$.

       \begin{aligned}

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \sin C &= \dfrac{7 \times \sin(65^\circ)}{9} \\
       \sin C &\approx 0.7049...
       \end{aligned}
       $$

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       C_1 &= \arcsin(0.7049...) \\

       \end{aligned}
       $$

<Steps>

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       C_2 &= 180^\circ - 44.82...^\circ \\
       C_2 &= 135.17...^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A + C_2 &= 65^\circ + 135.17...^\circ \\
       &= 200.17...^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$

       Since the sum of just two angles exceeds $180^\circ$, a triangle cannot be formed with an obtuse angle $C$.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Triggering the Ambiguous Case">
    In triangle $DEF$, side $DE = 12 \text{ cm}$, side $DF = 15 \text{ cm}$, and angle $DEF = 40^\circ$.
    
    **a)** Find the valid value(s) for angle $DFE$.
    
    **b)** Hence, find the possible value(s) for angle $EDF$.
    
    **c)** Calculate the possible length(s) of side $EF$.

       \sin F &= \dfrac{12 \times \sin(40^\circ)}{15} \\
       \sin F &\approx 0.5142...
       \end{aligned}

       \begin{aligned}

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       F_2 &= 180^\circ - 30.94...^\circ \\
       F_2 &\approx 149.1^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}

</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Calculate the single valid angle $EDF$.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       D &= 180^\circ - (40^\circ + 30.94...^\circ) \\
       D &\approx 109.1^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$

    **c)**
<Steps>

       $$
       \begin{aligned}

       EF &= \dfrac{15 \times \sin(109.05...^\circ)}{\sin(40^\circ)} \\

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: A True Ambiguous Triangle">
    A surveyor measures a triangular plot $XYZ$. Side $XY = 18 \text{ m}$, side $YZ = 14 \text{ m}$, and angle $YXZ = 42^\circ$.
    
    **a)** Show that there are two possible values for angle $XZY$.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
    1. **Apply the Sine Rule for angle $Z$.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \dfrac{18}{\sin Z} &= \dfrac{14}{\sin(42^\circ)} \\
       \sin Z &= \dfrac{18 \times \sin(42^\circ)}{14} \\
       \sin Z &\approx 0.8603...
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Find the acute angle ($Z_1$).**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       Z_1 &= \arcsin(0.8603...) \\
       Z_1 &\approx 59.4^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Find the obtuse angle ($Z_2$).**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       Z_2 &= 180^\circ - 59.35...^\circ \\
       Z_2 &\approx 120.6^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Verify both are valid triangles.**
       Triangle 1: $42^\circ + 59.4^\circ = 101.4^\circ < 180^\circ$.
       Triangle 2: $42^\circ + 120.6^\circ = 162.6^\circ < 180^\circ$.
       Since both sums are under $180^\circ$, there are two valid triangles.
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Find the two possible angles for $Y$.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       Y_1 &= 180^\circ - 101.35...^\circ = 78.64...^\circ \\
       Y_2 &= 180^\circ - 162.64...^\circ = 17.35...^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$

       Area of a triangle
       <FormulaBox>
         $$A = \dfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$$
       </FormulaBox>
      
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Area}_1 &= \dfrac{1}{2}(18)(14)\sin(Y_1) \\
       &= 126 \times \sin(78.64...^\circ) \\
       &\approx 123.5 \text{ m}^2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Calculate Area 2.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Area}_2 &= \dfrac{1}{2}(18)(14)\sin(Y_2) \\
       &= 126 \times \sin(17.35...^\circ) \\
       &\approx 37.6 \text{ m}^2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Find the difference.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Difference} &= 123.5 - 37.6 \\
       &= 85.9 \text{ m}^2 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>

<Steps>
    1. **Analyse the signs.**
       We are given $\sin\theta > 0$. Sine is positive in the 1st and 2nd quadrants.
       We are given $\tan\theta < 0$. Tangent is negative in the 2nd and 4th quadrants.
    2. **Find the intersection.**
       The only quadrant where both conditions are true is the **second quadrant**.
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Substitute into the Pythagorean identity.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \left(\dfrac{3}{5}\right)^2 + \cos^2\theta &= 1 \\
       \dfrac{9}{25} + \cos^2\theta &= 1 \\
       \cos^2\theta &= 1 - \dfrac{9}{25} \\
       \cos^2\theta &= \dfrac{16}{25}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Solve for $\cos\theta$ applying the quadrant condition.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \cos\theta &= \pm\sqrt{\dfrac{16}{25}} \\
       \cos\theta &= \pm\dfrac{4}{5}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       Since $\theta$ is in the second quadrant, cosine must be negative.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \cos\theta &= -\dfrac{4}{5}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
    1. **Use the tangent identity.**
       Identity for $\tan \theta$
       <FormulaBox>
         $$\tan\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$$
       </FormulaBox>
      
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \tan\theta &= \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute exact values.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \tan\theta &= \dfrac{\dfrac{3}{5}}{-\dfrac{4}{5}} \\
       &= \dfrac{3}{5} \times -\dfrac{5}{4} \\
       &= -\dfrac{3}{4}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
