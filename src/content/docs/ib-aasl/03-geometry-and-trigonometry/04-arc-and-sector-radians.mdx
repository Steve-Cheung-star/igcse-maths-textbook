</Aside>

## 1. Introducing the Radian

A **radian** is defined by taking the radius of a circle and wrapping it along the circle's edge. The angle created at the centre when the arc length exactly equals the radius is exactly 1 radian.

Because the circumference of a full circle is $2\pi r$, there are exactly $2\pi$ radians in a full $360^\circ$ turn. This gives us our golden conversion rule:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\pi \text{ radians} = 180^\circ
\end{aligned}
$$

<Aside type="tip" title="Converting Between Degrees and Radians">
  * **Degrees to Radians:** Multiply by $\dfrac{\pi}{180}$
  * **Radians to Degrees:** Multiply by $\dfrac{180}{\pi}$

  *Note: Radian measure is often expressed as exact multiples of $\pi$ (e.g., $\dfrac{\pi}{2}$, $\dfrac{3\pi}{4}$), but can also be written as plain decimals (e.g., $1.57 \text{ rad}$).*
</Aside>

### Worked Example 1: Conversions
**Question:** Convert $120^\circ$ to exact radians, and convert $\dfrac{5\pi}{4}$ radians to degrees.

**Solution:**
For degrees to radians:
$$
\begin{aligned}
120 \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} &= \dfrac{120\pi}{180} \\
&= \dfrac{2\pi}{3} \text{ rad}
\end{aligned}
$$

For radians to degrees:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dfrac{5\pi}{4} \times \dfrac{180}{\pi} &= \dfrac{5 \times 180}{4} \\
&= 5 \times 45 \\
&= 225^\circ
\end{aligned}
$$

## 2. Length of an Arc and Area of a Sector

Working in radians dramatically simplifies the formulae for circular geometry. Instead of calculating a fraction of 360 degrees, the formulae become beautifully streamlined.

<FormulaBox title="Length of an arc">
  $$l = r\theta$$
</FormulaBox>

where $r$ is the radius, $\theta$ is the angle measured in radians

<FormulaBox title="Area of a sector">
  $$A = \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$$
</FormulaBox>

where $r$ is the radius, $\theta$ is the angle measured in radians

<SteveTip title="Calculator Mode Check">
  Before you type any trigonometric functions ($\sin$, $\cos$, $\tan$) into your calculator during an exam, check the top of your screen! If your angle is in radians, your calculator must be in **RAD** mode. If it's in degrees, it must be in **DEG** mode.
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="danger" title="The Golden Rule of Sector Formulae">
  The formulae $l = r\theta$ and $A = \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ **only work if $\theta$ is in radians**. If you plug $45^\circ$ directly into $l = r\theta$, you are telling the formula you have an angle of 45 radians (which is over 7 full circles!). Always convert first.
</Aside>

### Worked Example 2: Arc Length
**Question:** A sector has a radius of $6 \text{ cm}$ and a central angle of $0.8 \text{ radians}$. Find the arc length and the total perimeter of the sector.

**Solution:**
Using the arc length formula:
$$
\begin{aligned}
l &= r\theta \\
&= 6 \times 0.8 \\
&= 4.8 \text{ cm}
\end{aligned}
$$

The perimeter includes the arc and the two radii straight edges:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Perimeter} &= l + 2r \\
&= 4.8 + 2(6) \\
&= 16.8 \text{ cm}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Worked Example 3: Sector Area
**Question:** A garden bed is a sector of a circle with radius $4 \text{ m}$ and an angle of $75^\circ$. Calculate its area.

**Solution:**
First, convert the angle to radians because the formula requires it:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\theta &= 75 \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} \\
&= \dfrac{5\pi}{12} \text{ rad}
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, apply the area formula:
$$
\begin{aligned}
A &= \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta \\
&= \dfrac{1}{2}(4)^2\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{12}\right) \\
&= 8 \left(\dfrac{5\pi}{12}\right) \\
&= \dfrac{10\pi}{3} \text{ m}^2
\end{aligned}
$$

       \end{aligned}

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \dfrac{240\pi}{180} &= \dfrac{4\pi}{3} \text{ rad}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Multiply the radian value by $\dfrac{180}{\pi}$**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \dfrac{11\pi}{6} \times \dfrac{180}{\pi}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Cancel $\pi$ and simplify**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       11 \times \dfrac{180}{6} &= 11 \times 30 \\
       &= 330^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>

       $$
       \begin{aligned}

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Basic Arc Length and Area">
    A sector of a circle has a radius of $12 \text{ cm}$ and an internal angle of $1.5 \text{ radians}$.
    
    **a)** Calculate the length of the arc.
    
    **b)** Calculate the area of the sector.
    
    **c)** Calculate the total perimeter of the sector.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
    1. **Apply the arc length formula**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       l &= r\theta
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute known values**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       l &= 12 \times 1.5 \\
       &= 18 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Apply the sector area formula**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A &= \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute known values and solve**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A &= \dfrac{1}{2}(12)^2(1.5) \\
       &= \dfrac{1}{2}(144)(1.5) \\
       &= 72 \times 1.5 \\
       &= 108 \text{ cm}^2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
    1. **Identify the components of a sector's perimeter**
       The perimeter consists of the arc length plus two radii.
    2. **Calculate the total**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Perimeter} &= l + 2r \\
       &= 18 + 2(12) \\
       &= 18 + 24 \\
       &= 42 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Working Backwards">
    A slice of cake (a sector of a circle) has an area of $54 \text{ cm}^2$. The radius of the cake is $9 \text{ cm}$.
    
    **a)** Find the angle of the cake slice in radians.
    
    **b)** Calculate the length of the curved edge (the arc length) of this slice.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
    1. **Set up the area formula**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A &= \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute the known area and radius**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       54 &= \dfrac{1}{2}(9)^2 \theta
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Simplify and solve for $\theta$**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       54 &= \dfrac{1}{2}(81) \theta \\
       54 &= 40.5 \theta \\
       \theta &= \dfrac{54}{40.5} \\
       \theta &= \dfrac{4}{3} \text{ rad}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Use the arc length formula with the newly found angle**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       l &= r\theta
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute and solve**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       l &= 9 \times \dfrac{4}{3} \\
       &= 12 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Segment Area (Composite Shapes)">
    A chord connects two points $A$ and $B$ on a circle of radius $10 \text{ cm}$. The angle subtended at the centre is $1.2 \text{ radians}$.
    
    **a)** Calculate the area of the entire sector.
    
    **b)** Calculate the area of the triangle formed by the two radii and the chord.
    
    **c)** Hence, find the area of the minor segment (the region bounded by the chord and the arc).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
    1. **Calculate sector area**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A_{\text{sector}} &= \dfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta \\
       &= \dfrac{1}{2}(10)^2(1.2) \\
       &= \dfrac{1}{2}(100)(1.2) \\
       &= 60 \text{ cm}^2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
    1. **Use the triangle area formula**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A_{\text{triangle}} &= \dfrac{1}{2}ab\sin(C)
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Substitute $a=10, b=10$, and $C=1.2 \text{ rad}$**
       Make sure your calculator is in **RADIAN** mode!
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A_{\text{triangle}} &= \dfrac{1}{2}(10)(10)\sin(1.2) \\
       &= 50 \times 0.9320... \\
       &\approx 46.60... \text{ cm}^2
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
    1. **Subtract the triangle area from the sector area**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Area}_{\text{segment}} &= A_{\text{sector}} - A_{\text{triangle}} \\
       &= 60 - 46.60... \\
       &= 13.39...
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       The area of the minor segment is **$13.4 \text{ cm}^2$** (to 3 s.f.).
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
