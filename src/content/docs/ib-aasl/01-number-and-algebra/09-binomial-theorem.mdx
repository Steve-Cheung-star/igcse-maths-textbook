Expanding $(a+b)^2$ is a fundamental algebra skill: $a^2 + 2ab + b^2$. Expanding $(a+b)^3$ takes a bit of manual multiplication but is manageable. But what if an exam question asks you to expand $(a+b)^7$? Or to find the specific coefficient of $x^{12}$ in a massive expansion? 

Manually multiplying brackets is highly inefficient and prone to error. Enter the **Binomial Theorem**: an elegant shortcut for expanding any expression in the form $(a+b)^n$.

### Combinations: The Formula Method

Drawing Pascal's Triangle is great for expanding powers up to $n=5$, but it becomes tedious for large exponents. Instead, we can calculate these numbers directly using **Combinations**, specifically the formula for "n choose r". 

In IB Mathematics, the combination coefficient is usually written as $\binom{n}{r}$.

<FormulaBox title="1.9 Binomial coefficient">

$$
\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}
$$

</FormulaBox>

*(Note: $n!$ means "$n$ factorial", which is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to $n$. For example, $4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24$. Factorials are evaluated first in the order of operations).*

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire Mastery: Solving for n or r">
The syllabus specifically requires you to use your GDC to solve equations like $\binom{n}{3} = 56$. On the TI-Nspire:
1. Open a **Graphs** page.
2. Enter the function `f1(x) = nCr(x, 3)`. (You can type `nCr` directly on the keypad or find it via **Menu > Probability > Combinations**).
3. Press `Ctrl + T` to open the Table view.
4. Scroll down the $x$ column until the `f1(x)` column reads 56. You will see this happens when $x = 8$. Therefore, $n = 8$.
</SteveTip>

## 2. The Binomial Theorem

The Binomial Theorem puts everything together: the coefficients from combinations and the shifting powers of the terms $a$ and $b$.

<FormulaBox title="1.9 Binomial theorem">

$$
(a+b)^n = a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \dots + \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r + \dots + b^n
$$

</FormulaBox>

### The Three Golden Rules of Expansion
When you expand $(a+b)^n$:
1. The powers of the first term ($a$) start at $n$ and **decrease** to $0$.
2. The powers of the second term ($b$) start at $0$ and **increase** to $n$.
3. In any single term of the expansion, **the sum of the powers always equals $n$**.

### The Quick Expansion of $(1+x)^n$ (Paper 1 Lifesaver)

Many students panic on Paper 1 (non-calculator) when asked to expand a binomial algebraically because the full combination formula looks intimidating. However, if the first term is a $1$, the expansion simplifies dramatically. By expanding the factorials inside $\binom{n}{r}$, we get a much faster, direct sequence:

<Aside type="tip" title="The Quick (1+x) Formula">

$$
(1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \dots
$$

If your binomial is $(a+x)^n$, you can factor out the $a^n$ to force a $1$ inside the bracket: $a^n\left(1 + \frac{x}{a}\right)^n$, and then use this quick expansion!
</Aside>

## 3. Finding Specific Terms

You will rarely be asked to expand a massive binomial like $(x-2)^{12}$ completely. Instead, exam questions will ask you to find a single, specific term, like "the coefficient of $x^4$".

For this, we isolate the general term from the Binomial Theorem:
$$
\text{Term}_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r
$$

<Aside type="danger" title="The Tricky Definition of r">
Remember that $r$ starts at $0$. Therefore:
* The 1st term occurs when $r=0$.
* The 2nd term occurs when $r=1$.
* The **$(r+1)$th term** occurs when you use the value $r$. 

If a question asks for the "4th term", you must use **$r = 3$**, not $r = 4$!
</Aside>

## Practice Questions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Manual Calculation">
    Evaluate the binomial coefficient $\binom{6}{2}$ without using a calculator.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    Use the combination formula from the formula booklet:
    
    $$
    \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}
    $$

    Substitute $n=6$ and $r=2$:

    $$
    \binom{6}{2} = \frac{6!}{2!(6-2)!} = \frac{6!}{2!4!}
    $$

    Expand the factorials, stopping the top one at $4!$ to easily cancel it out with the denominator:

    $$
    \binom{6}{2} = \frac{6 \times 5 \times 4!}{2 \times 1 \times 4!}
    $$

    Cancel the $4!$:

    $$
    \binom{6}{2} = \frac{30}{2} = 15
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    **a)** Expand $(2x - 1)^4$ using the Binomial Theorem. Simplify your final answer.
    
    **b)** Hence, find the exact value of $1.9^4$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Part a:** Here, $a = 2x$, $b = -1$, and $n = 4$.
    *Caution: You must include the negative sign with the $b$ term, and keep $2x$ in brackets!*

    Write out the expansion using the formula. The coefficients for $n=4$ from Pascal's Triangle are 1, 4, 6, 4, 1.

    $$
    (2x-1)^4 = 1(2x)^4(-1)^0 + 4(2x)^3(-1)^1 + 6(2x)^2(-1)^2 + 4(2x)^1(-1)^3 + 1(2x)^0(-1)^4
    $$

    Simplify the individual powers carefully:

    $$
    = 1(16x^4)(1) + 4(8x^3)(-1) + 6(4x^2)(1) + 4(2x)(-1) + 1(1)(1)
    $$

    Multiply it all out to get the final simplified polynomial:

    $$
    = 16x^4 - 32x^3 + 24x^2 - 8x + 1
    $$

    **Part b:**
    The phrase "Hence" means we must use our previous answer. We want to evaluate $1.9^4$ using the expansion of $(2x-1)^4$.
    First, find the value of $x$ that makes the bracket equal to $1.9$:
    
    $$
    2x - 1 = 1.9 \implies 2x = 2.9 \implies x = 1.45
    $$
    
    This is too complex. Let's find an easier $x$. What if we want $1.9$ inside the bracket? Oh wait, let's use an $x$ that makes the calculation easy without a calculator. 
    Let $x = 1$. Then $2(1) - 1 = 1$. No, we want $1.9$. 
    Let's set $x = 1.45$. 
    *(Alternatively, if the question was $19^4$, we would set $x=10$. In this case, we just substitute $x = 1.45$ into the expanded polynomial).*

    $$
    16(1.45)^4 - 32(1.45)^3 + 24(1.45)^2 - 8(1.45) + 1 = 13.0321
    $$
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Using the Quick Expansion">
    Find the first three terms, in ascending powers of $x$, in the expansion of $(1 + 3x)^8$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    Because the first term is a $1$, we can use the quick expansion formula:
    
    $$
    (1 + X)^n \approx 1 + nX + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}X^2 + \dots
    $$

    Here, $n = 8$ and our "$X$" is $(3x)$. Substitute these in:

    $$
    (1 + 3x)^8 = 1 + 8(3x) + \frac{8 \times 7}{2}(3x)^2 + \dots
    $$

    Simplify the terms:
    * The second term: $8 \times 3x = 24x$
    * The third term: $\frac{56}{2} \times 9x^2 = 28 \times 9x^2 = 252x^2$

    The first three terms are:
    **$1 + 24x + 252x^2$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Finding a Specific Coefficient">
    Find the coefficient of $x^5$ in the expansion of $(x - 2)^8$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    We do not need to expand the whole thing. We will use the general term:
    $$
    \text{Term} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r
    $$

    We know: $a = x$, $b = -2$, and $n = 8$.
    We need the term with $x^5$. Looking at $a^{n-r} \implies x^{8-r}$, we want $8-r = 5$. 
    Therefore, **$r = 3$**.

    Substitute $r=3$ into the general term:

    $$
    \binom{8}{3}(x)^5(-2)^3
    $$

    Calculate the parts:
    * $\binom{8}{3} = 56$ (Using TI-Nspire `nCr(8,3)` or formula)
    * $(-2)^3 = -8$

    Multiply the numbers together:

    $$
    = 56 \times (-8) \times x^5 = -448x^5
    $$

    The question asks specifically for the *coefficient* (the number in front), so the final answer is **$-448$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Finding an Unknown Constant">
    **a)** Find the general term in the expansion of $(ax + 2)^5$.
    
    **b)** Given that the coefficient of the $x^3$ term is $1080$, find the exact value of $a$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a:** Set up the general term formula with $n=5$:
    $$
    \text{Term} = \binom{5}{r}(ax)^{5-r}(2)^r
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We want the $x^3$ term. The power of the first term ($ax$) must be 3. Since the powers must add up to $n=5$, the power of the second term ($2$) must be 2.
    This means $5 - r = 3 \implies r = 2$.

    Substitute $r=2$ into the general term:

    $$
    \binom{5}{2}(ax)^3(2)^2
    $$

    Evaluate the known parts:
    * $\binom{5}{2} = 10$
    * $(2)^2 = 4$

    Substitute and simplify:

    $$
    10 \times (a^3 x^3) \times 4 = 40a^3 x^3
    $$

    We are told the coefficient is $1080$. Therefore:

    $$
    40a^3 = 1080
    $$

    Divide by 40:

    $$
    a^3 = 27
    $$

    Cube root both sides:

    $$
    a = 3
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Finding the Constant Term">
    Find the constant term (the term independent of $x$) in the expansion of $\left(2x^2 - \dfrac{1}{x}\right)^6$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    The constant term is the term where the power of $x$ is $0$. 
    Let's set up the general term formula without knowing $r$ yet. 
    *Note: write $\dfrac{1}{x}$ as $x^{-1}$ to make index laws easier.*

    $$
    \binom{6}{r}(2x^2)^{6-r}(-1x^{-1})^r
    $$

    We only care about the $x$ variables right now to find $r$. Let's pull out just the $x$ parts and multiply them:

    $$
    (x^2)^{6-r} \times (x^{-1})^r
    $$

    Apply exponent laws (multiply powers):

    $$
    x^{12-2r} \times x^{-r}
    $$

    Apply exponent laws (add powers when bases are the same):

    $$
    x^{12-3r}
    $$

    For a constant term, the overall power must be $0$:

    $$
    12 - 3r = 0 \implies 3r = 12 \implies r = 4
    $$

    Now substitute $r=4$ back into the *entire* general term formula:

    $$
    \binom{6}{4}(2x^2)^{2}(-1x^{-1})^4
    $$

    $$
    15 \times (4x^4) \times (1x^{-4})
    $$

    $$
    = 60
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Multiplying with a Binomial">
    Find the coefficient of $x^3$ in the expansion of $(1 + 2x)(x - 1)^4$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    This is a compound expansion. The expression on the right, $(x-1)^4$, will expand into multiple terms. Those terms will then be multiplied by both the $1$ and the $2x$ from the first bracket.

    How can we get an $x^3$ term from this?
    * **Path 1:** The $1$ multiplies with the **$x^3$ term** from $(x-1)^4$.
    * **Path 2:** The $2x$ multiplies with the **$x^2$ term** from $(x-1)^4$.

    Let's find those specific terms from $(x-1)^4$ (where $n=4$).

    **Finding the $x^3$ term of $(x-1)^4$:**
    Here $r = 1$ (so the second term $-1$ has a power of 1, and $x$ has a power of 3).
    $$
    \binom{4}{1}(x)^3(-1)^1 = 4(x^3)(-1) = -4x^3
    $$

    **Finding the $x^2$ term of $(x-1)^4$:**
    Here $r = 2$.
    $$
    \binom{4}{2}(x)^2(-1)^2 = 6(x^2)(1) = 6x^2
    $$

    Now, multiply these by their respective counterparts from the first bracket $(1 + 2x)$:
    * Path 1: $1 \times (-4x^3) = -4x^3$
    * Path 2: $2x \times (6x^2) = 12x^3$

    Add them together to get the total $x^3$ term:
    $$
    -4x^3 + 12x^3 = 8x^3
    $$

    The coefficient of $x^3$ is **$8$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Algebraic Coefficients (Paper 1 Classic)">
    In the expansion of $(1 + x)^n$, where $n \geq 3$, the coefficient of $x^3$ is exactly $5$ times the coefficient of $x$. 
    
    By setting up an algebraic equation, find the exact value of $n$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    Using the quick expansion formula for $(1+x)^n$:
    * The coefficient of $x$ is simply $n$, since $\binom{n}{1} = n$.
    * The coefficient of $x^3$ is $\binom{n}{3}$, which algebraically expands to $\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}$.

    The question states:
    $$
    \text{Coefficient of } x^3 = 5 \times (\text{Coefficient of } x)
    $$

    Substitute our algebraic expressions:
    $$
    \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{6} = 5n
    $$

    Since $n \geq 3$ (it cannot be zero), we can divide both sides by $n$ to cancel it:
    $$
    \frac{(n-1)(n-2)}{6} = 5
    $$

    Multiply by 6 to clear the fraction:
    $$
    (n-1)(n-2) = 30
    $$

    Expand the brackets to form a quadratic equation:
    $$
    n^2 - 3n + 2 = 30
    $$

    Bring everything to one side:
    $$
    n^2 - 3n - 28 = 0
    $$

    Factorise the quadratic:
    $$
    (n - 7)(n + 4) = 0
    $$

    This gives possible solutions of $n = 7$ or $n = -4$. 
    Because $n$ must be a positive integer ($n \in \mathbb{N}$), we reject the negative solution.

    **$n = 7$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
