Until now, we have visualised the derivative or estimated it using tables and secant lines. However, calculus provides us with powerful algebraic rules that allow us to find the exact gradient function, $f'(x)$, instantly. The most foundational of these rules is the **power rule**.

</Aside>

## 1. The Power Rule for $f(x) = ax^n$

To differentiate a single term of the form $ax^n$, we use a remarkably simple two-step process:
1. **Multiply** the entire term by the current exponent ($n$).
2. **Subtract** $1$ from the exponent.

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $x^n$">
$$ f(x) = x^n \Rightarrow f'(x) = nx^{n-1} $$
</FormulaBox>

If there is already a coefficient ($a$) in front of the $x$, the power simply multiplies with it.
Therefore, if $f(x) = ax^n$, then $f'(x) = anx^{n-1}$.

<Aside type="caution" title="Important Condition">
  For this section of the syllabus, we are focusing strictly on functions where $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ (all exponents are integers). This includes positive integers ($x^2, x^3$) and negative integers ($x^{-1}, x^{-4}$), but excludes fractional exponents like $x^{1/2}$ for now.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Differentiating Single Terms
**Question:** Find the derivative of the following functions:
**a)** $f(x) = 4x^3$
**b)** $y = 7x$
**c)** $g(x) = 5$

**Solution:**
**a)**
<Steps>
  1. Multiply the coefficient ($4$) by the power ($3$).
     $$ 4 \times 3 = 12 $$
  2. Subtract $1$ from the power.
     $$ 3 - 1 = 2 $$
  3. Combine the results.
     $$ f'(x) = 12x^2 $$
</Steps>

**b)**
<Steps>
  1. Recognise that $7x$ is actually $7x^1$.
  2. Multiply by the power ($1 \times 7 = 7$).
  3. Subtract $1$ from the power ($1 - 1 = 0$).
  4. Since $x^0 = 1$, the $x$ disappears entirely. The gradient of a straight line $y=mx+c$ is just its constant gradient $m$!
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 7 $$
</Steps>

**c)**
<Steps>
  1. Recognise that a constant like $5$ can be written as $5x^0$.
  2. Multiplying by the power means multiplying by $0$.
  3. Therefore, the derivative of *any* constant number is always zero. Graphically, the line $y=5$ is perfectly horizontal, so its gradient is zero everywhere.
     $$ g'(x) = 0 $$
</Steps>

## 2. Differentiating Polynomials (Sums and Differences)

When a function is made up of multiple terms added or subtracted together (a polynomial), we simply differentiate it **term by term**.

If $f(x) = ax^n + bx^{n-1} + \dots$, then $f'(x) = anx^{n-1} + b(n-1)x^{n-2} + \dots$

### Worked Example: Differentiating a Polynomial
**Question:** Differentiate $y = 3x^4 - 2x^2 + 8x - 11$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Differentiate each term individually according to the power rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{d}{dx}(3x^4) &= 12x^3 \\
     \dfrac{d}{dx}(-2x^2) &= -4x \\
     \dfrac{d}{dx}(8x) &= 8 \\
     \dfrac{d}{dx}(-11) &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Combine them back together to form the derivative function.
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 12x^3 - 4x + 8 $$
</Steps>

## 3. Preparing Functions for Differentiation

The power rule only works when the terms are strictly in the form $ax^n$. If the variable $x$ is trapped in a denominator or inside brackets, you **must algebraically manipulate** the function first before you differentiate.

### Dealing with $x$ in the Denominator
If $x$ is in the denominator, move it to the numerator by using negative indices: $\dfrac{1}{x^n} = x^{-n}$.

### Worked Example: Negative Indices
**Question:** Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = \dfrac{5}{x^2} - \dfrac{2}{x}$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Prepare:** Rewrite the fractions using negative exponents. Do not write $f'(x)$ yet, because you are not differentiating in this step!
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(x) &= 5x^{-2} - 2x^{-1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Differentiate:** Apply the power rule. Be careful with negative numbers: subtracting $1$ from $-2$ gives $-3$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= 5(-2)x^{-2-1} - 2(-1)x^{-1-1} \\
     &= -10x^{-3} + 2x^{-2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. **Format (Optional but recommended):** Rewrite with positive exponents.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= -\dfrac{10}{x^3} + \dfrac{2}{x^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="danger" title="Exam Trap: Differentiating Fractions">
  A very common mistake is differentiating the numerator and denominator separately. 
  For example, if $y = \dfrac{x^3 + 2}{x}$, you **cannot** just differentiate the top and bottom. You must divide both terms by $x$ to split the fraction first: $y = x^2 + 2x^{-1}$, and *then* differentiate.
</Aside>

    Find the derivative with respect to $x$ for the following functions:  
    **a)** $f(x) = x^8$  
    **b)** $y = 6x^3$  
    **c)** $g(x) = \dfrac{x^4}{2}$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Multiply by the power and subtract one from the power.
     $$ f'(x) = 8x^7 $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Multiply the coefficient by the power ($6 \times 3$).
  2. Subtract one from the power.
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 18x^2 $$
</Steps>

    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Recognise that dividing by $2$ is the same as multiplying by a half.
     $$ g(x) = \dfrac{1}{2}x^4 $$
  2. Apply the power rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     g'(x) &= 4 \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)x^3 \\
     &= 2x^3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Polynomials">
    Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for the following curves:  
    **a)** $y = -2x^5 + 7x^2 - 3x + 10$  
    **b)** $y = x(x^2 - 5)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Differentiate term by term. Remember the derivative of a constant ($10$) is zero.
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = -10x^4 + 14x - 3 $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. You must expand the brackets first to create a polynomial.
     $$ y = x^3 - 5x $$
  2. Now differentiate.
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 5 $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Negative Exponents">
    Differentiate the following functions:  
    **a)** $f(x) = \dfrac{3}{x^4}$  
    **b)** $h(t) = 5t^2 - \dfrac{1}{3t^3}$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Prepare the function by moving the $x$ to the numerator using a negative index.
     $$ f(x) = 3x^{-4} $$
  2. Differentiate. Be careful: $-4 - 1 = -5$.
     $$ f'(x) = -12x^{-5} $$
  3. Optionally, rewrite with positive indices.
     $$ f'(x) = -\dfrac{12}{x^5} $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Pay attention to the variable. It is $t$, so we will find $h'(t)$.
  2. Prepare the function. Ensure only the variable moves up, not the constant $3$ in the denominator.
     $$ h(t) = 5t^2 - \dfrac{1}{3}t^{-3} $$
  3. Differentiate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     h'(t) &= 10t - (-3)\left(\dfrac{1}{3}\right)t^{-4} \\
     &= 10t + 1t^{-4} \\
     &= 10t + \dfrac{1}{t^4}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Splitting Fractions">
    Find the derivative of $y = \dfrac{x^4 - 2x^2 + 8}{x^2}$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
<Steps>
  1. Divide each term in the numerator by the denominator ($x^2$). Use index laws ($x^a \div x^b = x^{a-b}$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= \dfrac{x^4}{x^2} - \dfrac{2x^2}{x^2} + \dfrac{8}{x^2} \\
     &= x^2 - 2 + 8x^{-2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate the prepared polynomial.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{dy}{dx} &= 2x - 0 - 16x^{-3} \\
     &= 2x - \dfrac{16}{x^3}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Evaluating the Gradient">
    A curve has the equation $f(x) = x^3 - 4x^2 + x$.  
    **a)** Find $f'(x)$.  
    **b)** Calculate the gradient of the curve at the point where $x = 2$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Apply the power rule.
     $$ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 8x + 1 $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $x = 2$ into the derivative function $f'(x)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(2) &= 3(2)^2 - 8(2) + 1 \\
     &= 3(4) - 16 + 1 \\
     &= 12 - 16 + 1 \\
     &= -3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. The gradient of the curve at $x=2$ is $-3$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Finding Coordinates from Gradients">
    The curve $y = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 12x + 5$ has two stationary points.  
    Find the $x$-coordinates of these stationary points.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
<Steps>
  1. Recall that a stationary point occurs where the gradient is zero ($\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$).
  2. Find the derivative $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.
     $$ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6x^2 - 6x - 12 $$
  3. Set the derivative equal to zero and solve the resulting quadratic equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     6x^2 - 6x - 12 &= 0 \\
     6(x^2 - x - 2) &= 0 \\
     x^2 - x - 2 &= 0 \\
     (x - 2)(x + 1) &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. State the $x$-coordinates.
     $$ x = 2 \text{ and } x = -1 $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Real-world Application (Physics)">
    The displacement, $s$ metres, of a particle moving in a straight line after $t$ seconds is modelled by the equation $s(t) = \dfrac{1}{3}t^3 - 2t^2 + 3t$, for $t \geq 0$.  
    **a)** Find an expression for the particle's velocity, $v(t)$, given that velocity is the rate of change of displacement ($v = \dfrac{ds}{dt}$).  
    **b)** Find the time(s) when the particle is momentarily at rest.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Differentiate the displacement function with respect to $t$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     v(t) = s'(t) &= 3\left(\dfrac{1}{3}\right)t^2 - 4t + 3 \\
     &= t^2 - 4t + 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. The particle is momentarily at rest when its velocity is zero ($v(t) = 0$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     t^2 - 4t + 3 &= 0 \\
     (t - 3)(t - 1) &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $t$. Both answers are valid since $t \geq 0$.
     $$ t = 1 \text{ second and } t = 3 \text{ seconds} $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Economics Application">
    The total cost $C$, in EUR, to produce $x$ units of a product is given by $C(x) = 0.05x^2 + 10x + 500$.  
    **a)** Find the marginal cost function, $C'(x)$.  
    **b)** Calculate the marginal cost when $100$ units are produced, and interpret what this number means in context.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Differentiate the cost function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     C'(x) &= 2(0.05)x + 10 + 0 \\
     &= 0.1x + 10
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $x = 100$ into the marginal cost function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     C'(100) &= 0.1(100) + 10 \\
     &= 10 + 10 \\
     &= 20 \text{ EUR}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Interpret the result. When $100$ units are currently being produced, the rate of change of the cost is 20 EUR per unit. This means producing the 101st unit will cost approximately 20 EUR.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
