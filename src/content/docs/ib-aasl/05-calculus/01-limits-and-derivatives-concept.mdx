You could find the gradient of a straight line, but what about the gradient of a curve that is constantly changing? Calculus is the mathematics of change, and it all begins with one remarkably powerful idea: **the limit**.

## 1. Introduction to the Concept of a Limit

A limit asks a simple question: **"As the $x$-value gets closer and closer to a certain number, what value does the $y$-value get closer and closer to?"**

Sometimes, you cannot directly substitute an $x$-value into a function because it breaks the rules of maths (like dividing by zero). Limits allow us to see what the function is *approaching* right up to that broken point.

We denote the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ as:
$$ \lim\limits_{x \to a} f(x) $$

### Estimating Limits Numerically (Tables)
Consider the function $f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$. If we try to find $f(3)$, we get $\dfrac{0}{0}$, which is undefined. Let's see what happens as $x$ gets extremely close to $3$ using a table.

| $x$ approaches from left $\rightarrow$ | $2.9$ | $2.99$ | $2.999$ | **$3$** | $3.001$ | $3.01$ | $3.1$ | $\leftarrow$ $x$ approaches from right |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :--- |
| **$f(x)$ approaches $\rightarrow$** | $5.9$ | $5.99$ | $5.999$ | **Undefined** | $6.001$ | $6.01$ | $6.1$ | **$\leftarrow$ $f(x)$ approaches** |

Even though the function does not exist at exactly $x = 3$, the table clearly shows that as $x$ approaches $3$, $f(x)$ is heading directly toward $6$. Therefore, we say $\lim\limits_{x \to 3} f(x) = 6$.

### Estimating Limits Graphically
We can also look at a graph to determine a limit. Look at the $y$-value the curve is aiming for as you trace your finger toward the $x$-value from both the left and the right.

## 2. The Derivative as a Gradient Function

Finding the gradient of a straight line is easy: $m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$. 
But curves don't have a constant gradient. Their steepness changes at every single point.

To find the gradient of a curve at a specific point $P$, we want to find the gradient of the **tangent line** at $P$. A tangent line touches the curve at exactly one local point.

### The Informal Limit of the Gradient
We can estimate the tangent's gradient using a **secant line**—a line that cuts through the curve at two points, $P$ and $Q$.
As we move point $Q$ closer and closer to point $P$, the secant line becomes a better and better estimation of the tangent line. 

When the distance between $P$ and $Q$ shrinks towards zero (a limit!), the secant line *becomes* the tangent line.

## 3. The Derivative as a Rate of Change and Notation

The gradient of a graph tells us how much $y$ changes for every $1$ unit change in $x$. Because of this, the derivative is fundamentally a **rate of change**. 

When we calculate the derivative, we are finding the *instantaneous* rate of change at one specific split-second, rather than an *average* rate of change over a long period.

### Common Notations
Depending on the context, we use different symbols to represent the first derivative.

* **$f'(x)$**: Pronounced "f prime of x". This is Lagrange's notation. It clearly shows that the derivative is itself a function of $x$. If the original curve is $f(x)$, the gradient function is $f'(x)$.
* **$\dfrac{dy}{dx}$**: Pronounced "dee-y by dee-x". This is Leibniz's notation. It comes from the gradient formula $\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$ (change in $y$ over change in $x$). As the change ($\Delta$) becomes infinitesimally small (a limit!), it becomes a $d$.
* **$\dfrac{dV}{dr}$**: The rate of change of volume ($V$) with respect to the radius ($r$). E.g., How fast a balloon's volume grows as its radius increases.
* **$\dfrac{ds}{dt}$**: The rate of change of displacement ($s$) with respect to time ($t$). In physics, this is the exact definition of **velocity**.

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Mistake: Fractions">
  Even though $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ looks like a fraction, it is **not** a fraction. You cannot "cancel out" the $d$s. It is a single mathematical symbol representing the derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Interpreting Notation
**Question:** The cost, $C$, in USD, of producing $x$ pairs of shoes in a factory is modelled by the function $C(x)$. Explain the real-world meaning of the statement $C'(500) = 45$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Identify what the prime notation $C'(x)$ represents in this context. It is the rate of change of cost with respect to the number of shoes produced ($x$). In economics, this is called the **marginal cost**.
  2. Interpret the number inside the bracket ($500$). This is the current $x$-value (number of shoes currently being produced).
  3. Interpret the output value ($45$). This is the rate of change.
  4. Combine these into a real-world sentence:
     "When the factory is producing $500$ pairs of shoes, the cost of producing one additional pair of shoes is approximately 45 USD."
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Average vs Instantaneous">
    The distance travelled by a cyclist, $s$ in metres, after $t$ seconds is given by $s(t) = 2t^2$.  
    **a)** Calculate the average velocity of the cyclist between $t = 3$ and $t = 5$.  
    **b)** Explain how you could use average velocities to estimate the *instantaneous* velocity exactly at $t = 3$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Find the total distance travelled at $t=3$ and $t=5$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s(3) &= 2(3)^2 = 18\text{ m} \\
     s(5) &= 2(5)^2 = 50\text{ m}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Use the gradient formula (change in distance over change in time) to find average velocity.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Average Velocity} &= \dfrac{s(5) - s(3)}{5 - 3} \\
     &= \dfrac{50 - 18}{2} \\
     &= \dfrac{32}{2} \\
     &= 16\text{ m s}^{-1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
    You could calculate the average velocity over increasingly smaller time intervals starting at $t=3$. For example, calculate the average velocity between $t=3$ and $t=3.1$, then between $t=3$ and $t=3.01$. The limit that these average velocities approach, $\lim\limits_{t \to 3} \dfrac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$, is the instantaneous velocity at $t=3$.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Gradient Limit Concept">
    The points $A(1, 1)$ and $B(1+h, y_B)$ lie on the curve $y = x^2$.  
    **a)** Write down an expression for $y_B$ in terms of $h$.  
    **b)** Write down an expression for the gradient of the secant line $AB$.  
    **c)** Explain what happens to your expression in part (b) as $h \to 0$, and what this represents.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**

    $$ y_B = (1+h)^2 $$

  1. Use the gradient formula $m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
  2. Substitute the coordinates of $A(1, 1)$ and $B(1+h, (1+h)^2)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_{AB} &= \dfrac{(1+h)^2 - 1}{(1+h) - 1} \\
