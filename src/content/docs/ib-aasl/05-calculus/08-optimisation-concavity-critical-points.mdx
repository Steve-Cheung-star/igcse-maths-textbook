## 1. Local Maximum and Minimum Points

The first derivative, $f'(x)$, represents the gradient (slope) of the curve. When $f'(x) = 0$, the tangent to the curve is horizontal. These points are called **stationary points**. A stationary point can be a local maximum, a local minimum, or a horizontal point of inflexion.

To classify a stationary point, we can use the **First Derivative Test**:
* **Local Maximum:** $f'(x)$ changes from positive (increasing) to negative (decreasing).
* **Local Minimum:** $f'(x)$ changes from negative (decreasing) to positive (increasing).

Alternatively, we can use the **Second Derivative Test**, which evaluates the concavity at the stationary point:
* If $f''(x) > 0$, the curve is concave-up, implying a **local minimum**.
* If $f''(x) < 0$, the curve is concave-down, implying a **local maximum**.

</SteveTip>

### Worked Example 1
Find the coordinates of the local maximum and minimum points of $f(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 12x + 5$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Find the first derivative and set it to zero to locate stationary points:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= 6x^2 - 6x - 12 \\
     0 &= 6(x^2 - x - 2) \\
     0 &= 6(x - 2)(x + 1)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     So, $x = 2$ or $x = -1$.
  2. Use the second derivative to classify the points:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f''(x) &= 12x - 6
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Test each $x$-value:
     * At $x = 2$: $f''(2) = 12(2) - 6 = 18 > 0$ (Local Minimum).
     * At $x = -1$: $f''(-1) = 12(-1) - 6 = -18 < 0$ (Local Maximum).
  4. Find the corresponding $y$-coordinates by substituting back into $f(x)$:
     * $f(2) = 2(8) - 3(4) - 12(2) + 5 = -15 \implies (2, -15)$ is the local minimum.
     * $f(-1) = 2(-1) - 3(1) - 12(-1) + 5 = 12 \implies (-1, 12)$ is the local maximum.
</Steps>

## 2. Points of Inflexion and Concavity

Concavity refers to the "bend" of the curve, governed by the second derivative, $f''(x)$.
* **Concave-up:** $f''(x) > 0$. The curve looks like a smile. The gradient $f'(x)$ is increasing.
* **Concave-down:** $f''(x) < 0$. The curve looks like a frown. The gradient $f'(x)$ is decreasing.

A **point of inflexion** is where the concavity changes (e.g., from concave-up to concave-down). 

<Aside type="danger" title="Crucial Condition">
  At a point of inflexion, $f''(x) = 0$ **AND** $f''(x)$ must change sign. Simply setting $f''(x) = 0$ is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition!
</Aside>

Consider $y = x^4$. The second derivative is $y'' = 12x^2$. Setting $y'' = 0$ gives $x = 0$. However, checking the sign of $12x^2$ slightly before and slightly after $x = 0$ shows it is positive on both sides. The concavity does not change, so $(0,0)$ is **not** a point of inflexion; it's just a very flat local minimum.

### Worked Example 2
Find the coordinates of the point of inflexion for $f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Find the first and second derivatives:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= 3x^2 - 12x + 9 \\
     f''(x) &= 6x - 12
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Set the second derivative to zero to find potential points of inflexion:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0 &= 6x - 12 \\
     x &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Verify the sign change of $f''(x)$ around $x = 2$:
     * Test $x = 1$: $f''(1) = 6(1) - 12 = -6$ (Negative)
     * Test $x = 3$: $f''(3) = 6(3) - 12 = 6$ (Positive)
     * Since $f''(x)$ changes sign, a point of inflexion exists at $x = 2$.
  4. Find the $y$-coordinate:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(2) &= (2)^3 - 6(2)^2 + 9(2) + 2 \\
     f(2) &= 8 - 24 + 18 + 2 = 4
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The point of inflexion is $(2, 4)$.
</Steps>

## 3. Optimisation

Optimisation involves translating a real-world problem into a mathematical function and finding its maximum or minimum value. This usually requires linking two variables, substituting one into the other to create a single-variable function, and applying the calculus techniques above.

<Aside type="tip" title="Domain Restrictions">
  Always check the physical domains. If you are optimising the side length of a box, $x$ must be strictly greater than $0$. Sometimes the global maximum or minimum occurs at the boundary of the domain rather than at a turning point.
</Aside>

### Worked Example 3
A manufacturer produces cylindrical cans with a volume of $250 \text{ cm}^3$. Find the radius $r$ that minimises the surface area of the can, and calculate that minimum surface area.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Set up the volume and surface area equations:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     V &= \pi r^2 h = 250 \\
     A &= 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Express $h$ in terms of $r$ using the volume equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     h &= \dfrac{250}{\pi r^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute $h$ into the surface area equation to create a function of one variable:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     A(r) &= 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r \left(\dfrac{250}{\pi r^2}\right) \\
     A(r) &= 2\pi r^2 + 500r^{-1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Differentiate with respect to $r$ and set to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     A'(r) &= 4\pi r - 500r^{-2} \\
     0 &= 4\pi r - \dfrac{500}{r^2} \\
     4\pi r^3 &= 500 \\
     r^3 &= \dfrac{125}{\pi} \implies r = \dfrac{5}{\sqrt[3]{\pi}} \approx 3.41 \text{ cm}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  5. Verify it's a minimum using the second derivative:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     A''(r) &= 4\pi + 1000r^{-3}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     Since $r > 0$, $A''(r)$ is strictly positive, confirming a minimum. Minimum Area $\approx A(3.41) \approx 219.69 \text{ cm}^2$.
</Steps>

## 4. Connections & Contexts

<Aside type="note" title="Cross-Subject Links & TOK">
  **Physics (Kinematics & SHM):** In a displacement-time graph, local extrema represent points where velocity is zero (the object turns around). A point of inflexion corresponds to a point of maximum or minimum velocity (acceleration is zero). In Simple Harmonic Motion, zero acceleration (inflexion) occurs precisely at the equilibrium position.
  
  **Economics:** Optimisation is the backbone of marginal analysis. Profit is maximised when Marginal Revenue equals Marginal Cost ($MR = MC$), which is conceptually identical to setting the derivative of the Profit function to zero. Allocative efficiency is achieved where the price equals the marginal cost, optimising societal surplus.

  **Theory of Knowledge:** *When mathematicians and historians say that they have explained something, are they using the word “explain” in the same way?* In mathematics, an "explanation" is a rigorously deduced proof stemming from axioms. It is absolute and objective. In history, "explanation" involves interpreting evidence to construct a narrative of cause and effect. It is subjective and open to debate.
</Aside>

    Given the function $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{3}x^3 - 4x + 2$:  
    **a)** Find the coordinates of the local maximum and local minimum points.  
    **b)** Determine the intervals where the function is concave-down.

    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Find $f'(x)$ and set it to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= x^2 - 4 \\
     0 &= (x-2)(x+2) \implies x = 2, x = -2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Find the $y$-coordinates:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(2) &= \dfrac{8}{3} - 8 + 2 = -\dfrac{10}{3} \\
     f(-2) &= -\dfrac{8}{3} + 8 + 2 = \dfrac{22}{3}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Use the second derivative to classify:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f''(x) &= 2x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     * $f''(2) = 4 > 0 \implies (2, -\dfrac{10}{3})$ is a local minimum.
     * $f''(-2) = -4 < 0 \implies (-2, \dfrac{22}{3})$ is a local maximum.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. A function is concave-down when $f''(x) < 0$. Set up the inequality:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2x &< 0 \\
     x &< 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The interval is $x < 0$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A company’s profit, $P$, measured in currency, from selling $x$ thousand units of a product is modelled by $P(x) = -2x^3 + 15x^2 + 84x - 50$.

     \end{aligned}

  3. Verify it's a maximum using the second derivative:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P''(x) &= -12x + 30 \\
     P''(7) &= -84 + 30 = -54 < 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The profit is maximised when $7000$ units are sold.
</Steps>

     P(7) &= -2(343) + 15(49) + 84(7) - 50 \\
     P(7) &= -686 + 735 + 588 - 50 = 587
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Attach the appropriate units: $587 \text{ USD}$.

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Product Rule & Extrema">
    Consider $g(x) = x \text{e}^{-x}$.  
    **a)** Find the exact coordinates of the stationary point and determine its nature.  
    **b)** Find the exact coordinates of the point of inflexion.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Use the product rule to find $g'(x)$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     g'(x) &= (1)\text{e}^{-x} + x(-\text{e}^{-x}) \\
     g'(x) &= \text{e}^{-x}(1 - x)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Set $g'(x) = 0$. Since $\text{e}^{-x} \neq 0$, we have $1 - x = 0 \implies x = 1$.
  3. Find the $y$-coordinate: 
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     g(1) &= 1\text{e}^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{\text{e}}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Determine nature using $g''(x)$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     g''(x) &= -\text{e}^{-x}(1-x) + \text{e}^{-x}(-1) = \text{e}^{-x}(x - 2)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     $g''(1) = \text{e}^{-1}(1 - 2) = -\text{e}^{-1} < 0$. Therefore, $(1, \dfrac{1}{\text{e}})$ is a local maximum.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. Set $g''(x) = 0$ to find potential points of inflexion:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{e}^{-x}(x - 2) &= 0 \implies x = 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Verify the sign change for $g''(x)$:
     * For $x < 2$ (e.g., $x=1$), $g''(1) < 0$.
     * For $x > 2$ (e.g., $x=3$), $g''(3) > 0$.
     Because concavity changes, it is a point of inflexion.
  3. Find the $y$-coordinate: 
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     g(2) &= 2\text{e}^{-2} = \dfrac{2}{\text{e}^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The point is $(2, \dfrac{2}{\text{e}^2})$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A rectangular plot of land is to be enclosed by a fence. One side of the plot lies along a straight river, so no fencing is required along that side. The farmer has $800 \text{ m}$ of fencing available.  
    **a)** Show that the area of the enclosure can be written as $A(x) = 800x - 2x^2$, where $x$ is the width of the rectangle.

    **a)**

  1. Let the width of the rectangle be $x$ and the length parallel to the river be $y$. The fencing is only used for three sides:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2x + y &= 800
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $y$: 
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 800 - 2x
     \end{aligned}
     $$

     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     A(x) &= x(800 - 2x) \\
     A(x) &= 800x - 2x^2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>

     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     A'(x) &= 800 - 4x \\
     0 &= 800 - 4x \implies x = 200
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Verify it's a maximum: $A''(x) = -4 < 0$, confirming a maximum.
  3. Calculate the corresponding length $y$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 800 - 2(200) = 400
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The dimensions are $200 \text{ m}$ by $400 \text{ m}$.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: The Inflexion Trap">
    Let $h(x) = x^4 - 8x^3 + 24x^2 - 10$.  
    **a)** Find all stationary points of $h(x)$.  
    **b)** Show that $h(x)$ does not have any points of inflexion, despite having a point where $h''(x) = 0$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Find $h'(x)$ and set to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     h'(x) &= 4x^3 - 24x^2 + 48x \\
     0 &= 4x(x^2 - 6x + 12) 
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. The quadratic $x^2 - 6x + 12$ has a discriminant $\Delta = 36 - 48 = -12 < 0$, so it yields no real roots. The only stationary point occurs at $x = 0$.
  3. Find the $y$-coordinate:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     h(0) &= -10 
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The only stationary point is $(0, -10)$.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. Find the second derivative and set it to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     h''(x) &= 12x^2 - 48x + 48 \\
     0 &= 12(x^2 - 4x + 4) \\
     0 &= 12(x - 2)^2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     There is a potential point of inflexion at $x = 2$.
  2. Verify the sign change of $h''(x)$ around $x = 2$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Since } (x - 2)^2 \ge 0 \text{ for all real } x, \text{ } h''(x) \ge 0 \text{ everywhere.}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Because $h''(x)$ never changes from positive to negative, the concavity does not change. Therefore, there are no points of inflexion.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Kinematics Context">
    The displacement $s$ metres of a particle at time $t$ seconds is given by $s(t) = t^3 - 9t^2 + 15t + 2$ for $t \geq 0$.  
    **a)** Find the times when the particle is momentarily at rest.  
    **b)** Find the maximum velocity of the particle in the interval $0 \leq t \leq 4$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Velocity is the first derivative of displacement. Find $v(t)$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     v(t) &= s'(t) = 3t^2 - 18t + 15
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Set $v(t) = 0$ to find when the particle is at rest:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0 &= 3(t^2 - 6t + 5) \\
     0 &= 3(t - 1)(t - 5)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The particle is at rest at $t = 1 \text{ s}$ and $t = 5 \text{ s}$.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. To find critical points for velocity, set acceleration $a(t)$ to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     a(t) &= v'(t) = 6t - 18 \\
     0 &= 6t - 18 \implies t = 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Verify if $t=3$ is a maximum or minimum for velocity.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     a'(t) = v''(t) = 6 > 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     This means $t=3$ is a *local minimum* for velocity.
  3. Since the only turning point is a minimum, the maximum velocity must occur at the boundaries of the interval $0 \leq t \leq 4$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     v(0) &= 15 \text{ m/s} \\
     v(4) &= 3(16) - 18(4) + 15 = -9 \text{ m/s}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. The maximum velocity is $15 \text{ m/s}$, occurring at $t = 0$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A solid glass paperweight consists of a hemisphere of radius $r$ mounted on top of a cylinder of radius $r$ and height $h$. The total volume of the paperweight is $360 \text{ cm}^3$.  
    **a)** Express the total surface area $S$ in terms of $r$ only.  
    **b)** Find the value of $r$ that minimises the total surface area.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Write the formulas for the total volume and total surface area.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     V &= \dfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3 + \pi r^2 h = 360 \\
     S &= 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h + \pi r^2 \\
     S &= 3\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     *(Note: Surface area includes the hemisphere curved surface, cylinder curved surface, and the circular base).*
  2. Isolate $h$ from the volume equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \pi r^2 h &= 360 - \dfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3 \\
     h &= \dfrac{360}{\pi r^2} - \dfrac{2r}{3}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute $h$ into the surface area equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     S(r) &= 3\pi r^2 + 2\pi r \left( \dfrac{360}{\pi r^2} - \dfrac{2r}{3} \right) \\
     S(r) &= 3\pi r^2 + \dfrac{720}{r} - \dfrac{4\pi r^2}{3} \\
     S(r) &= \dfrac{5\pi r^2}{3} + 720r^{-1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. Differentiate $S(r)$ and set to zero:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     S'(r) &= \dfrac{10\pi r}{3} - 720r^{-2} \\
     0 &= \dfrac{10\pi r}{3} - \dfrac{720}{r^2} \\
     \dfrac{10\pi r^3}{3} &= 720 \\
     r^3 &= \dfrac{2160}{10\pi} = \dfrac{216}{\pi}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $r$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     r &= \dfrac{6}{\sqrt[3]{\pi}} \approx 4.09 \text{ cm}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

    Consider the function $f(x) = \ln(x^2 + 1)$.  
    **a)** Find the coordinates of the local minimum point.  
    **b)** Determine the exact values of $x$ for which the concavity of the graph changes.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**

<Steps>
  1. Find $f'(x)$ using the chain rule:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= \dfrac{1}{x^2 + 1} \cdot (2x) = \dfrac{2x}{x^2 + 1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Set $f'(x) = 0$ to find stationary points:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{2x}{x^2 + 1} &= 0 \implies x = 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Verify it's a minimum using the first derivative test:
     * For $x < 0$, $f'(x) < 0$
     * For $x > 0$, $f'(x) > 0$
     Since the gradient changes from negative to positive, it's a local minimum.
  4. Find the $y$-coordinate: 
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(0) &= \ln(1) = 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The minimum is $(0, 0)$.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
  1. Concavity changes at points of inflexion. Find $f''(x)$ using the quotient rule:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f''(x) &= \dfrac{(2)(x^2 + 1) - (2x)(2x)}{(x^2 + 1)^2} \\
     f''(x) &= \dfrac{2x^2 + 2 - 4x^2}{(x^2 + 1)^2} = \dfrac{2 - 2x^2}{(x^2 + 1)^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Set $f''(x) = 0$:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2 - 2x^2 &= 0 \\
     x^2 &= 1 \implies x = 1, x = -1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Verify the sign change of $f''(x)$. The denominator is always positive, so the sign depends on $2 - 2x^2$:
     * $x < -1 \implies f''(x) < 0$
     * $-1 < x < 1 \implies f''(x) > 0$
     * $x > 1 \implies f''(x) < 0$
     Concavity changes at exactly $x = 1$ and $x = -1$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
