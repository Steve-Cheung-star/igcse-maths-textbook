Now that we have established the concept of a derivative and the power rule, we can expand our mathematical toolkit. Real-world phenomena rarely follow simple polynomial paths. To model complex behaviours—from the oscillation of sound waves to the compounding of continuous interest—we must know how to differentiate trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions, as well as functions that are combined or nested together.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: Physics and TOK">
  **Links to Physics:** Uniform circular motion and induced electromotive force (emf) rely heavily on these rules. For instance, an object moving in a circle has a position modelled by $x = r \cos(\omega t)$ and $y = r \sin(\omega t)$. To find its velocity, we must differentiate these trigonometric functions using the chain rule. Similarly, Faraday's Law of Induction states that induced emf is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux ($\varepsilon = -N \dfrac{\text{d}\Phi}{\text{d}t}$).
  
  **TOK:** *What is the role of convention in mathematics?* In calculus, we use specific notations like $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$ (Leibniz notation) or $f'(x)$ (Lagrange notation). Are these merely arbitrary choices, or do they actively shape how we think about the concepts? The chain rule, for instance, looks like fractions cancelling out in Leibniz notation ($\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}u} \times \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}$). Does this convention make the math easier to discover, or just easier to remember?
</Aside>

## 1. Standard Derivatives

The IB formula booklet provides the derivatives for several fundamental functions. You do not need to derive these from first principles in the exam, but you must recognise and apply them fluently.

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $x^n$">
$$
f(x) = x^n \implies f'(x) = nx^{n-1}
$$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $\sin x$">
$$
f(x) = \sin x \implies f'(x) = \cos x
$$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $\cos x$">
$$
f(x) = \cos x \implies f'(x) = -\sin x
$$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $\text{e}^x$">
$$
f(x) = \text{e}^x \implies f'(x) = \text{e}^x
$$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of $\ln x$">
$$
f(x) = \ln x \implies f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}
$$
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="danger" title="Radians are Required">
  Whenever you differentiate or integrate trigonometric functions ($\sin x$, $\cos x$, etc.) in calculus, **$x$ must be measured in radians**. The standard derivative formulas are invalid if degrees are used.
</Aside>

### Linearity: Sums and Multiples
If you have a function multiplied by a constant, the constant simply multiplies the derivative. If functions are added or subtracted, you differentiate them term by term. Roots (square roots, cube roots) and rational terms must first be rewritten as fractional and negative exponents using index laws.

### Worked Example: Standard Rules
**Question:** Differentiate $f(x) = 4\text{e}^x - \sqrt{x} + \dfrac{5}{x}$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Rewrite the square root and fraction using exponent rules.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(x) &= 4\text{e}^x - x^{\frac{1}{2}} + 5x^{-1}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate each term individually, keeping the constant multipliers.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= 4(\text{e}^x) - \dfrac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}} - 5x^{-2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Simplify the expression back into surds and fractions.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= 4\text{e}^x - \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} - \dfrac{5}{x^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 2. The Chain Rule (Composite Functions)

The **Chain Rule** is used when dealing with composite functions—functions "nested" inside other functions, like $y = (3x + 1)^4$ or $y = \sin(x^2)$. 

<FormulaBox title="Chain rule">
$$
y = g(u), \text{ where } u = f(x) \implies \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}u} \times \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}
$$
</FormulaBox>

Informally, we can think of this as: **Derivative of the OUTSIDE function (leaving the inside alone) $\times$ Derivative of the INSIDE function.**

### Worked Example: Chain Rule
**Question:** Differentiate $y = \text{e}^{5x^2 - 3x}$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Identify the outside function and inside function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Outside: } y &= \text{e}^u \\
     \text{Inside: } u &= 5x^2 - 3x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate both parts with respect to their variables.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}u} &= \text{e}^u \\
     \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} &= 10x - 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Multiply them together according to the chain rule and substitute $u$ back in.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}u} \times \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} \\
     &= \text{e}^u \times (10x - 3) \\
     &= (10x - 3)\text{e}^{5x^2 - 3x}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 3. The Product Rule

The **Product Rule** is used when a function is the exact multiplication of two other functions of $x$, such as $y = x^2 \sin x$.

<FormulaBox title="Product rule">
$$
y = uv \implies \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = u\dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x} + v\dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}
$$
</FormulaBox>

### Worked Example: Product Rule
**Question:** Find $f'(x)$ given $f(x) = x^3 \ln x$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Define $u$ and $v$ as the two separate functions being multiplied.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     u &= x^3 \\
     v &= \ln x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate $u$ and $v$ individually.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} &= 3x^2 \\
     \dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{1}{x}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Apply the product rule formula and simplify.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) &= u\dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x} + v\dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} \\
     &= (x^3)\left(\dfrac{1}{x}\right) + (\ln x)(3x^2) \\
     &= x^2 + 3x^2 \ln x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 4. The Quotient Rule

The **Quotient Rule** is used when a function is dividing another function of $x$, such as $y = \dfrac{\cos x}{2x}$. 

<FormulaBox title="Quotient rule">
$$
y = \dfrac{u}{v} \implies \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = \dfrac{v\dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} - u\dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x}}{v^2}
$$
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="caution" title="Order Matters!">
  Unlike the product rule, which has a plus sign and is commutative, the quotient rule uses subtraction in the numerator. You **must** start with $v \times \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x}$ first. A helpful mnemonic is: "Bottom d-Top minus Top d-Bottom, over Bottom squared."
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Quotient Rule
**Question:** Differentiate $y = \dfrac{\text{e}^x}{x^2 + 1}$.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Define $u$ (numerator) and $v$ (denominator).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     u &= \text{e}^x \\
     v &= x^2 + 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate $u$ and $v$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}x} &= \text{e}^x \\
     \dfrac{\text{d}v}{\text{d}x} &= 2x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute into the quotient rule formula.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(x^2 + 1)(\text{e}^x) - (\text{e}^x)(2x)}{(x^2 + 1)^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Factor out common terms in the numerator to present a simplified answer.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{\text{e}^x(x^2 - 2x + 1)}{(x^2 + 1)^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **d)** $y = \dfrac{\ln x}{x}$  

    **e)** $y = \dfrac{2x+3}{x-1}$  
    
    **f)** $y = \dfrac{\text{e}^{2x}}{x^2+1}$  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Apply quotient rule: $\dfrac{vu' - uv'}{v^2}$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(x+1)(2x) - (x^2)(1)}{(x+1)^2}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. Expand and simplify numerator.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{2x^2 + 2x - x^2}{(x+1)^2} = \dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Apply quotient rule.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(x^2)(\cos x) - (\sin x)(2x)}{(x^2)^2}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. Simplify and factorise out $x$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{x(x\cos x - 2\sin x)}{x^4} = \dfrac{x\cos x - 2\sin x}{x^3}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Apply quotient rule. Watch the negative from differentiating $\cos x$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(\cos x)(\text{e}^x) - (\text{e}^x)(-\sin x)}{(\cos x)^2}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. Simplify.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{\text{e}^x(\cos x + \sin x)}{\cos^2 x}
         \end{aligned}

    <Steps>
      1. Apply quotient rule.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(x)\left(\dfrac{1}{x}\right) - (\ln x)(1)}{x^2}
         \end{aligned}

         \begin{aligned}

    </Steps>

    <Steps>

    </Steps>

         \begin{aligned}

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Mixed Rules (Chain inside Product)">
    Find $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$ for the function $y = x^2 \text{e}^{-3x}$. Give your answer in fully factorised form.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
<Steps>
  1. This is a product of $u = x^2$ and $v = \text{e}^{-3x}$. The $v$ term requires the chain rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     u' &= 2x \\
     v' &= -3\text{e}^{-3x}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Apply the product rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= (2x)(\text{e}^{-3x}) + (x^2)(-3\text{e}^{-3x}) \\
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= 2x\text{e}^{-3x} - 3x^2\text{e}^{-3x}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Factor out the common terms ($x$ and $\text{e}^{-3x}$) as requested.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= x\text{e}^{-3x}(2 - 3x)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Tangents and Normals">
    Consider the curve with equation $y = \dfrac{\ln x}{x}$ for $x > 0$.  
    **a)** Find $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$.  
    **b)** Hence, find the exact coordinates of the point on the curve where the tangent is horizontal.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the quotient rule with $u = \ln x$ and $v = x$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     u' &= \dfrac{1}{x} \\
     v' &= 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Substitute into the formula.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{(x)\left(\dfrac{1}{x}\right) - (\ln x)(1)}{x^2} \\
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= \dfrac{1 - \ln x}{x^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. A horizontal tangent implies the gradient is equal to zero. Set the derivative to $0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{1 - \ln x}{x^2} &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. A fraction equals zero when its numerator is zero (and denominator is non-zero).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     1 - \ln x &= 0 \\
     \ln x &= 1 \\
     x &= \text{e}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute $x = \text{e}$ back into the original equation to find the $y$-coordinate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= \dfrac{\ln(\text{e})}{\text{e}} \\
     y &= \dfrac{1}{\text{e}}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The coordinates are $\left(\text{e}, \dfrac{1}{\text{e}}\right)$.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Kinematics Context">
    A particle's displacement $s$ (in metres) from a fixed origin at time $t$ (in seconds) is given by $s(t) = 4\cos(2t)$.  
    **a)** Find the velocity function $v(t)$.  
    **b)** Find the first time $t > 0$ when the particle is momentarily at rest.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement, so we differentiate $s(t)$ using the chain rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     v(t) &= s'(t) \\
     v(t) &= 4 \times (-\sin(2t)) \times 2 \\
     v(t) &= -8\sin(2t) \text{ m/s}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. "Momentarily at rest" means velocity is zero.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     -8\sin(2t) &= 0 \\
     \sin(2t) &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $2t$. The sine function is zero at multiples of $\pi$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2t &= \pi, 2\pi, 3\pi \dots
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Find the first time $t > 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2t &= \pi \\
     t &= \dfrac{\pi}{2} \text{ seconds}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
