title: 4.10 The Regression Line of x on y
description: Understand how to find the regression line of x on y, when to use it for predictions, and the limitations of rearranging regression equations.
---

In the previous sections, we explored the regression line of $y$ on $x$, which we use to predict a $y$-value for a given $x$-value. However, in statistics, the relationship between two variables is not perfectly symmetrical when it comes to regression. If we want to predict an $x$-value given a $y$-value, we cannot simply algebraically rearrange our $y$ on $x$ equation. We must calculate a completely new line: the **regression line of $x$ on $y$**.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: TOK & Knowledge of the Future">
  **Theory of Knowledge (TOK): Is it possible to have knowledge of the future?**
  When we use regression lines to predict values outside of our current data set (extrapolation), we are essentially trying to model the future or the unknown. But is a mathematical prediction the same as "knowledge"? Statistics provides probabilistic models, not deterministic certainties. A regression line tells us what the trend *suggests* will happen, but black swan events, changing variables, and errors in data collection mean the future remains fundamentally uncertain. How does the language of mathematics give us a false sense of certainty about the future?
</Aside>

## 1. Minimising Horizontal vs Vertical Distances

The line of best fit for $y$ on $x$ is found by minimising the square of the **vertical distances** (residuals) between the data points and the line. 

Conversely, the regression line of $x$ on $y$ is found by minimising the square of the **horizontal distances**. Because the distances being minimised are different, the two lines are completely different equations, unless the correlation coefficient $r$ is exactly $1$ or $-1$.

The equation of the regression line of $x$ on $y$ is written in the form:
$$ x = cy + d $$
where $c$ is the gradient and $d$ is the $x$-intercept. Both regression lines always intersect at the mean point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

<Aside type="danger" title="A Common Exam Trap">
  You **cannot** reliably make a prediction of $y$ from a value of $x$ by rearranging the $x$ on $y$ equation. 
  Similarly, you **cannot** reliably predict $x$ by substituting a $y$ value into the $y = ax + b$ equation and solving for $x$. 
  
  **Rule of thumb:**
  * To predict $y$ given $x$, calculate and use the $y$ on $x$ line.
  * To predict $x$ given $y$, calculate and use the $x$ on $y$ line.
</Aside>

## 2. Using Technology to Find x on y

Finding the $x$ on $y$ line on your Graphic Display Calculator simply requires you to swap the order of your input lists.

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire: Regression line of x on y">
  1. Enter your data into a `Lists & Spreadsheet` page. Name the columns (e.g., `xdata` and `ydata`).
  2. Go to `Menu` > `4: Statistics` > `1: Stat Calculations` > `3: Linear Regression (mx+b)`.
  3. **Crucial Step:** For the `X List`, select your **y-data**. For the `Y List`, select your **x-data**.
  4. The calculator will output $m$ and $b$. Remember, your equation is $x = my + b$.
</SteveTip>

### Worked Example: Predicting x from y
**Question:** An economist records the advertising budget ($x$, in thousands of USD) and the resulting sales ($y$, in thousands of USD) for 5 local businesses.

| Advertising ($x$) | 2.5 | 3.0 | 4.5 | 5.0 | 6.5 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sales ($y$)** | 15.0 | 18.5 | 22.0 | 25.0 | 32.5 |

Find the equation of the regression line of $x$ on $y$, and use it to estimate the required advertising budget for a business aiming for 28.0 thousand USD in sales.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Enter data into the GDC.**
     Enter the $x$ values into one list and $y$ values into another.
  2. **Run Linear Regression, swapping the lists.**
     Set `X List` to the sales ($y$) data.
     Set `Y List` to the advertising ($x$) data.
     The calculator gives $m \approx 0.222$ and $b \approx -0.835$.
  3. **Write the equation of $x$ on $y$.**
     $$ x = 0.222y - 0.835 $$
  4. **Substitute the given $y$ value to predict $x$.**
     We are given $y = 28.0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x &= 0.222(28.0) - 0.835 \\
     &= 6.216 - 0.835 \\
     &= 5.381
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  5. **State the final answer in context.**
     The estimated required advertising budget is approximately 5.38 thousand USD (or 5380.00 USD).
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Basic x on y Calculation">
    A researcher measures the length ($x$ cm) and the mass ($y$ kg) of 6 newborn animals. The data is as follows:
    
    | Length ($x$) | 35.0 | 38.5 | 42.0 | 45.5 | 49.0 | 52.5 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Mass ($y$)** | 2.1 | 2.6 | 3.2 | 3.5 | 4.1 | 4.8 |
    
    **a)** Find the equation of the regression line of $x$ on $y$.  
    **b)** Use your equation to estimate the length of a newborn animal that has a mass of 3.8 kg.  
    **c)** State whether your estimate in part (b) is reliable. Justify your answer.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Enter the data into the GDC.
  2. Run linear regression with $y$ as the independent variable list and $x$ as the dependent variable list.
  3. $m \approx 6.30$ and $b \approx 22.0$.
  4. The equation is:
     $$ x = 6.30y + 22.0 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $y = 3.8$ into the regression equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x &= 6.30(3.8) + 22.0 \\
     x &\approx 45.94
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. The estimated length is 45.9 cm (3 s.f.).
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. The value $y = 3.8$ lies within the given data range of mass ($2.1$ to $4.8$).
  2. Therefore, this is **interpolation**, and assuming a strong linear correlation, the estimate is considered **reliable**.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Comparing the Two Lines">
    Consider the following bivariate data regarding hours studied ($x$) and test score out of 100 ($y$):
    
    | Hours ($x$) | 1 | 3 | 4 | 7 | 8 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Score ($y$)** | 40 | 55 | 65 | 80 | 95 |
    
    **a)** Find the equation of the regression line of $y$ on $x$.  
    **b)** Find the equation of the regression line of $x$ on $y$.  
    **c)** Calculate the mean point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.  
    **d)** Show algebraically that both regression lines pass through the mean point.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Using the GDC with `X List` as Hours and `Y List` as Score.
     $$ y = 7.35x + 33.2 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Using the GDC with `X List` as Score and `Y List` as Hours.
     $$ x = 0.133y - 4.39 $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Calculate the mean of $x$ and mean of $y$ using 2-Variable Statistics on the GDC.
     $$ \bar{x} = \dfrac{1+3+4+7+8}{5} = 4.6 $$
     $$ \bar{y} = \dfrac{40+55+65+80+95}{5} = 67 $$
  2. The mean point is $(4.6, 67)$.
</Steps>
    **d)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $\bar{x} = 4.6$ into the $y$ on $x$ equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 7.35(4.6) + 33.2 \\
     y &= 33.81 + 33.2 = 67.01 \approx 67 = \bar{y}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     *(Note: The slight difference is due to rounding the gradient to 3 s.f. Using exact values yields exactly 67).*
  2. Substitute $\bar{y} = 67$ into the $x$ on $y$ equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x &= 0.133(67) - 4.39 \\
     x &= 8.911 - 4.39 = 4.521 \approx 4.6 = \bar{x}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     Both lines intersect at $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Real-World Context (Temperatures)">
    A café owner tracks the average daily temperature ($x$, in $^\circ\text{C}$) and hot chocolate sales ($y$, in GBP) over winter.
    She calculates the regression line of $y$ on $x$ to be:
    $$ y = -15.5x + 350 $$
    She also calculates the regression line of $x$ on $y$ to be:
    $$ x = -0.060y + 21.0 $$
    
    **a)** On a day where the café sold 120.00 GBP of hot chocolate, estimate the average daily temperature.  
    **b)** The owner decides to use the equation $y = -15.5x + 350$, substitutes $y = 120$, and solves for $x$. State why this method is mathematically flawed for this context.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Since we are given a $y$-value (sales) and asked to predict an $x$-value (temperature), we must use the regression line of **$x$ on $y$**.
  2. Substitute $y = 120$ into the correct equation:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x &= -0.060(120) + 21.0 \\
     x &= -7.2 + 21.0 \\
     x &= 13.8
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. The estimated average daily temperature is $13.8^\circ\text{C}$.
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. The line $y = -15.5x + 350$ was constructed by minimising the **vertical residuals** (the errors in $y$). 
  2. It is strictly designed to predict sales given a temperature. 
  3. Rearranging this equation to solve for $x$ does not give the line that minimises the horizontal residuals, meaning the prediction for temperature would be unreliable and statistically invalid.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A marine biologist measures the wingspan ($w$, in cm) and the weight ($m$, in kg) of 8 adult albatrosses.
    
    | Wingspan ($w$) | 280 | 295 | 310 | 325 | 340 | 350 | 360 | 370 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Weight ($m$)** | 7.1 | 7.6 | 8.4 | 8.9 | 9.7 | 10.1 | 10.5 | 11.2 |
    
    **a)** Find the equation of the regression line of $w$ on $m$.  
    **b)** An albatross feather is found, and DNA analysis estimates the bird's weight to be 12.5 kg. Use your equation to estimate the wingspan of this bird.  
    **c)** State, with a reason, whether your estimate in part (b) is reliable.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. We are looking for $w$ on $m$. This means $m$ is our independent variable (acting like $x$) and $w$ is our dependent variable (acting like $y$).
  2. Input data into GDC: `X List` = Weight ($m$), `Y List` = Wingspan ($w$).
  3. Calculate linear regression. $m \approx 21.5$ and $b \approx 130$.
  4. The equation is:
     $$ w = 21.5m + 130 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $m = 12.5$ into the equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     w &= 21.5(12.5) + 130 \\
     w &\approx 268.75 + 130 \\
     w &\approx 398.75
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. The estimated wingspan is 399 cm (3 s.f.).
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. The value $m = 12.5$ kg is significantly outside the given data range for weight ($7.1$ kg to $11.2$ kg).
  2. This is an example of **extrapolation**. Therefore, the estimate is **unreliable**, as we do not know if the linear relationship continues beyond the measured data set.
</Steps>
