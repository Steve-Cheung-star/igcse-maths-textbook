## 1. Measures of Central Tendency

Measures of central tendency give us a single value that attempts to describe a set of data by identifying the central position within that set. 

* **Mean ($\bar{x}$):** The sum of all values divided by the total number of values.
* **Median ($Q_2$):** The middle value when the data is ordered by size.
* **Mode:** The most frequently occurring value. For grouped data, we refer to the **modal class** (only for equal class intervals).

<FormulaBox title="Mean, \bar{x}, of a set of data">
  $$ \bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^k f_i x_i}{n} \text{, where } n = \sum_{i=1}^k f_i $$
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="caution" title="Grouped Data">
  When data is given in class intervals (e.g., $10 \le x < 20$), we do not know the exact raw values. Therefore, we must use the **mid-interval value** ($x_i$) to estimate the mean.
</Aside>

### Worked Example 1: Estimating the Mean
**Question:** The weekly wages of $50$ employees are recorded in the table below. Estimate the mean weekly wage.
* $200 \le w < 400$: $12$ employees
* $400 \le w < 600$: $25$ employees
* $600 \le w < 800$: $13$ employees

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Find the mid-interval value ($x_i$) for each class.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     200 \le w < 400 &\implies x_1 = 300 \\
     400 \le w < 600 &\implies x_2 = 500 \\
     600 \le w < 800 &\implies x_3 = 700
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Apply the formula for the mean.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \bar{x} &= \dfrac{\sum f_i x_i}{n} \\
     &= \dfrac{(12 \times 300) + (25 \times 500) + (13 \times 700)}{50} \\
     &= \dfrac{3600 + 12500 + 9100}{50} \\
     &= \dfrac{25200}{50} \\
     &= 504 \text{ GBP}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 2. Measures of Dispersion

While central tendency tells us about the middle, **measures of dispersion** tell us how spread out the data is. 

* **Interquartile Range (IQR):** Measures the spread of the middle $50\%$ of the data. 

<FormulaBox title="Interquartile range">
  $$ \text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 $$
</FormulaBox>

* **Standard Deviation ($\sigma$ or $s$):** Measures the average distance of each data point from the mean. A small standard deviation means the data is clustered closely around the mean.
* **Variance ($\sigma^2$ or $s^2$):** The square of the standard deviation.

<Aside type="tip" title="Technology Requirement">
  For the IB exams, you are **only required to calculate standard deviation and variance using technology** (your GDC). You do not need to memorise or use the complex hand-calculation formulae for variance, though understanding how they work conceptually can be helpful.
</Aside>

### Quartiles of Discrete Data
When finding quartiles for discrete data, there are several different mathematical methods (e.g., inclusive vs exclusive methods). Because of this, the quartiles you find by hand might sometimes slightly differ from the values given by your GDC. **Always rely on the values obtained using technology** for IB assessments, but possess an awareness that these different methods exist.

## 3. Effect of Constant Changes on Data

Often, data undergoes a transformation. For example, a teacher might add $5$ marks to every student's test score, or all salaries might be increased by $10\%$. You need to know how these transformations affect the mean and standard deviation.

Let the original dataset have a mean of $\bar{x}$ and a standard deviation of $\sigma$. If we transform this data to create a new dataset, let its mean be $\bar{x}_{\text{new}}$ and its standard deviation be $\sigma_{\text{new}}$.

| Transformation | Effect on Mean ($\bar{x}_{\text{new}}$) | Effect on Standard Deviation ($\sigma_{\text{new}}$) | Effect on Variance ($\sigma^2_{\text{new}}$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Add/Subtract $k$** | $\bar{x}_{\text{new}} = \bar{x} \pm k$ | $\sigma_{\text{new}} = \sigma$ (Unchanged) | $\sigma^2_{\text{new}} = \sigma^2$ (Unchanged) |
| **Multiply/Divide by $k$** | $\bar{x}_{\text{new}} = \bar{x} \times k$ | $\sigma_{\text{new}} = \sigma \times \lvert k \rvert$ | $\sigma^2_{\text{new}} = \sigma^2 \times k^2$ |

<Aside type="danger" title="Why is standard deviation unchanged by addition?">
  If you add $3$ to every value in a dataset, the entire distribution simply shifts to the right by $3$ units. The data points do not get any closer together or further apart. Therefore, the spread (standard deviation and variance) remains exactly the same!
</Aside>

### Worked Example 2: Constant Changes
**Question:** A set of test scores has a mean of $45$ and a standard deviation of $4$. The teacher decides to multiply every student's score by $1.2$ and then add $5$ bonus marks. Find the new mean and the new standard deviation.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Calculate the new mean.**
     The mean is affected by both multiplication and addition.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \bar{x}_{\text{new}} &= (45 \times 1.2) + 5 \\
     &= 54 + 5 \\
     &= 59
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Calculate the new standard deviation.**
     Standard deviation is affected by multiplication, but *not* by addition.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \sigma_{\text{new}} &= 4 \times 1.2 \\
     &= 4.8
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    The ages of $7$ children at a party are: $5, 7, 7, 8, 10, 11, 15$.
    
    **a)** State the mode.
    
    **b)** Find the median age.
    
    **c)** Calculate the mean age.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** The mode is the most frequent value. The mode is $7$.
    
    **b)** The data is already ordered. The middle value is the 4th item. The median is $8$.
    
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. **Sum the values and divide by $n$.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \bar{x} &= \dfrac{5 + 7 + 7 + 8 + 10 + 11 + 15}{7} \\
     &= \dfrac{63}{7} \\
     &= 9
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Grouped Data Estimation">
    The time, $t$ minutes, taken by $40$ students to run $5 \text{ km}$ is shown below.
    * $20 \le t < 25$: $8$ students
    * $25 \le t < 30$: $14$ students
    * $30 \le t < 35$: $12$ students
    * $35 \le t < 40$: $6$ students
    
    **a)** State the modal class.
    
    **b)** Estimate the mean time taken.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** The highest frequency is $14$. The modal class is $25 \le t < 30$.
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Find the mid-interval values ($x_i$).**
     $x_1 = 22.5$, $x_2 = 27.5$, $x_3 = 32.5$, $x_4 = 37.5$
  2. **Apply the mean formula.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \bar{x} &= \dfrac{(8 \times 22.5) + (14 \times 27.5) + (12 \times 32.5) + (6 \times 37.5)}{40} \\
     &= \dfrac{180 + 385 + 390 + 225}{40} \\
     &= \dfrac{1180}{40} \\
     &= 29.5 \text{ mins}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Adding a Constant">
    A factory measures the daily output of its machines. The mean output is $150$ items with a standard deviation of $12$ items. The factory upgrades its power supply, which reliably adds exactly $15$ items to every machine's daily output.
    
    **a)** State the new mean.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Multiplying by a Constant">
    The temperatures of $10$ reactions are recorded in Celsius. The variance is calculated to be $25$. The scientist decides to convert all measurements to a new scale where every value is multiplied by $3$.
    
    **a)** Find the standard deviation of the original Celsius data.
    
    **b)** Calculate the new variance after the transformation.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)** Standard deviation is the square root of variance. 
    $$ \sigma = \sqrt{25} = 5 $$
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Apply the rule for variance.**
     When data is multiplied by $k$, variance is multiplied by $k^2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \sigma^2_{\text{new}} &= \sigma^2 \times 3^2 \\
     &= 25 \times 9 \\
     &= 225
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Combined Transformations">
    A dataset has a mean of $10$ and a standard deviation of $2$. Every value in the dataset is first multiplied by $-4$, and then $7$ is added to each result.
    
    **a)** Find the new mean.
    
    **b)** Find the new standard deviation.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Apply both transformations to the mean.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \bar{x}_{\text{new}} &= (10 \times -4) + 7 \\
     &= -40 + 7 \\
     &= -33
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Apply the transformation to the standard deviation.**
     Standard deviation is only affected by the absolute value of the multiplier, not by the addition.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \sigma_{\text{new}} &= 2 \times \lvert -4 \rvert \\
     &= 2 \times 4 \\
     &= 8
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
