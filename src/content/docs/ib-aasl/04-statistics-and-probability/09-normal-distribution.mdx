We now move from discrete data (countable outcomes) to **continuous random variables**. A continuous random variable can take any real numerical value within a given range (for example, time, height, mass, or volume). The most important continuous probability distribution is the **normal distribution**. 

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: History, Aim 8 & TOK">
  **International-mindedness:** Abraham de Moivre, a French mathematician, first introduced the normal distribution in 1733 as an approximation to the binomial distribution. Later, the Belgian astronomer and sociologist Adolphe Quetelet applied it to human characteristics, creating the concept of *l'homme moyen* (the average man).
  
  **Links to other subjects:** You will see the normal distribution constantly in the sciences, psychology, and Environmental Systems and Societies (ESS) when analysing natural measurements, survey data, and descriptive statistics.
  
  **Aim 8:** Why might the misuse of the normal distribution lead to dangerous inferences? Assuming financial markets follow a strict normal distribution underestimates the likelihood of extreme events ("fat tails"). This misuse of the model has contributed to major global financial crises, as analysts mistakenly believed catastrophic crashes were statistically "impossible".
  
  **Theory of Knowledge (TOK):** To what extent can we trust mathematical models such as the normal distribution? The normal curve goes to infinity in both directions, implying humans with negative heights or infinite masses are mathematically possible, albeit with near-zero probability. How can we know what to include and exclude in a model for it to be useful in reality?
</Aside>

## 1. Properties of the Normal Distribution

If a continuous random variable $X$ is normally distributed with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, we write:
$$ X \sim \text{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) $$

The normal distribution has several defining properties:
1. **Bell-shaped and Symmetrical:** The curve is perfectly symmetrical around its centre.
2. **Central Measures:** The mean, median, and mode are all equal and located exactly at the centre ($\mu$).
3. **Asymptotic:** The tails of the curve approach the $x$-axis as $x \to \infty$ and $x \to -\infty$, but never actually touch it.
4. **Total Area is 1:** Because it is a probability distribution, the total area under the curve is exactly $1$.
5. **Exact Values:** For any continuous distribution, the probability of obtaining an exact value is zero. That is, $\text{P}(X = a) = 0$. Therefore, $\text{P}(X < a)$ is identical to $\text{P}(X \le a)$.

## 2. Sketching and The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7%)

In examinations, you are highly expected to **sketch the normal curve** and label the shaded area under the curve based on probability expressions. Visualising the area helps prevent errors when using your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC).

For any normally distributed variable, a predictable percentage of data falls within certain distances from the mean, measured in standard deviations ($\sigma$). This is known as the **Empirical Rule** or the **68-95-99.7% Rule**.

* Approximately **68%** of the data lies between $\mu - \sigma$ and $\mu + \sigma$.
* Approximately **95%** of the data lies between $\mu - 2\sigma$ and $\mu + 2\sigma$.
* Approximately **99.7%** of the data lies between $\mu - 3\sigma$ and $\mu + 3\sigma$.

### Worked Example: Using the Empirical Rule
**Question:** The lifespans of a specific type of lightbulb are normally distributed with a mean of 1200 hours and a standard deviation of 50 hours. Without using a calculator, estimate the percentage of lightbulbs that last between 1150 hours and 1300 hours.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Identify how many standard deviations the bounds are from the mean.**
     Lower bound: 1150 is $1200 - 50 = \mu - 1\sigma$.
     Upper bound: 1300 is $1200 + 2(50) = \mu + 2\sigma$.
  2. **Sketch the distribution to visualise the area.**

         <polyline points={Array.from({ length: 201 }, (_, i) => i * 2).map(x => x + "," + (180 - 140 * Math.exp(-Math.pow(x - 200, 2) / 3200))).join(' ')} fill="none" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="3" />
         <line x1="10" y1="180" x2="390" y2="180" stroke="#0f172a" stroke-width="2" />
         <line x1="200" y1="40" x2="200" y2="180" stroke="#64748b" stroke-width="1.5" stroke-dasharray="4" />
         <text x="200" y="200" font-family="sans-serif" font-size="12" fill="#0f172a" text-anchor="middle">1200</text>
         <text x="160" y="200" font-family="sans-serif" font-size="12" fill="#ef4444" text-anchor="middle">1150</text>
         <text x="280" y="200" font-family="sans-serif" font-size="12" fill="#ef4444" text-anchor="middle">1300</text>
         <text x="220" y="120" font-family="sans-serif" font-size="14" font-weight="bold" fill="#1d4ed8" text-anchor="middle">81.5%</text>
       </svg>
     </div>
  3. **Use the symmetry of the empirical rule percentages.**
     The area from $\mu - 1\sigma$ to $\mu$ is half of 68%, which is 34%.
     The area from $\mu$ to $\mu + 2\sigma$ is half of 95%, which is 47.5%.
  4. **Sum the percentages.**
     $$ \text{Percentage} = 34\% + 47.5\% = 81.5\% $$
</Steps>

## 3. Calculating Normal Probabilities

For boundaries that do not land exactly on integer multiples of $\sigma$, we must use technology. A given area under the normal curve represents the probability that a random value falls within that interval.

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire: normCdf">
  To find the probability (area) between two values:
  1. Open a Calculator page.
  2. Press `Menu` > `5: Probability` > `5: Distributions` > `2: Normal Cdf`.
  3. Enter `Lower Bound`, `Upper Bound`, `μ` (mean), and `σ` (standard deviation).
  
  *If there is no lower bound (e.g., $X < 20$), enter `-9E999` for the lower bound.*
  *If there is no upper bound (e.g., $X > 30$), enter `9E999` for the upper bound.*
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="danger" title="Variance vs. Standard Deviation">
  The notation $X \sim \text{N}(50, 16)$ gives the mean and the **variance** ($\sigma^2 = 16$). Your calculator requires the **standard deviation** ($\sigma$). Always remember to take the square root of the variance before entering it into your GDC! In this case, $\sigma = 4$.
</Aside>

## 4. Inverse Normal Calculations

Sometimes, you are given the probability (the area under the curve) and you need to work backwards to find the data value ($x$) that corresponds to that boundary. This is called an **inverse normal** calculation.

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire: invNorm">
  To find a boundary value given a probability:
  1. Press `Menu` > `5: Probability` > `5: Distributions` > `3: Inverse Normal`.
  2. Enter the **Area** (this is *always* the area to the **LEFT** of the unknown value on older OS versions, though newer ones let you select left/center/right). Default to calculating the area to the left if unsure.
  3. Enter `μ` and `σ`.
</SteveTip>

     The area to the left of $a$ is $0.10$.
     `invNorm(0.10, 48, 8)`
     $$ a \approx 37.7 \text{ months} $$
  3. **Find the upper bound $b$.**
     The area to the left of $b$ is $0.10 + 0.80 = 0.90$.
     `invNorm(0.90, 48, 8)`
     $$ b \approx 58.3 \text{ months} $$
  4. **State the interval.**
     The symmetric interval is between 37.7 months and 58.3 months.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Conditional Probability with Normal">
    Scores on a university admissions test are normally distributed with a mean of 60 and a standard deviation of 15. A student must score above 70 to be considered for an interview.
    
    Given that a randomly selected student is considered for an interview, find the probability that they scored above 85.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    Let $X \sim \text{N}(60, 15^2)$.
<Steps>
  1. **Set up the conditional probability formula.**
     We want $\text{P}(X > 85 | X > 70)$.
     $$ \text{P}(X > 85 | X > 70) = \dfrac{\text{P}((X > 85) \cap (X > 70))}{\text{P}(X > 70)} $$
  2. **Simplify the intersection.**
     If a score is greater than 85 AND greater than 70, it is simply greater than 85.
     $$ \text{Numerator} = \text{P}(X > 85) $$
  3. **Calculate the numerator and denominator using `normCdf`.**
     Numerator: `normCdf(85, 9E999, 60, 15)` $\approx 0.04779$
     Denominator: `normCdf(70, 9E999, 60, 15)` $\approx 0.25249$
  4. **Evaluate the fraction.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Probability} &= \dfrac{0.04779}{0.25249} \\
     &\approx 0.189 \text{ (3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Using Symmetry Properties">
    The time, $T$ minutes, taken by mechanics to service a car is normally distributed with a mean of 45 minutes. 
    It is known that $\text{P}(T < 35) = 0.15$.
    
    Without calculating the standard deviation, find:
    
    **a)** $\text{P}(T > 55)$
    
    **b)** $\text{P}(35 < T < 55)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Analyse the distances from the mean.**
     The mean is $\mu = 45$.
     The value 35 is $10$ units below the mean ($45 - 10 = 35$).
     The value 55 is $10$ units above the mean ($45 + 10 = 55$).
  2. **Apply symmetry.**
     Because 35 and 55 are equidistant from the mean, the areas in the tails are equal.
     $$ \text{P}(T > 55) = \text{P}(T < 35) = 0.15 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Use the total probability property.**
     The total area under the curve is 1.
     $$ \text{P}(35 < T < 55) = 1 - \text{P}(T < 35) - \text{P}(T > 55) $$
  2. **Substitute the known areas.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(35 < T < 55) &= 1 - 0.15 - 0.15 \\
     &= 1 - 0.30 \\
     &= 0.70
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
