Once data has been collected, presenting it clearly is essential for meaningful analysis. In this section, you will learn how to visually represent both discrete and continuous data. These visual tools allow us to quickly identify trends, calculate key measures like the median and interquartile range, and compare different datasets.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections & TOK">

Raw data consists of unorganised facts and figures, whereas information is data that has been processed, organised, and structured (like in a histogram or box plot) to give it meaning. Does "data" mean the same thing in Mathematics as it does in History or Biology?
  
  **Links to other subjects:** The presentation of data is fundamental across the sciences (e.g., graphing experimental results in Physics or Biology) and individuals and societies (e.g., presenting demographic distributions in Geography or Economics).
  
  **International Mindedness:** Different countries and educational systems sometimes use varied formulae or notations for the same statistical measure. However, visual representations like histograms and box plots provide a universal language that transcends these regional differences.
</Aside>

## 1. Frequency Distributions and Histograms

When dealing with continuous data (or large sets of discrete data), we group the data into **class intervals** to summarise it in a frequency distribution table. 

In the IB syllabus, class intervals are given as inequalities without gaps, such as $10 \le x < 20$. This means a value of exactly $20$ goes into the next class interval ($20 \le x < 30$).

### Histograms

A **histogram** is a visual representation of grouped continuous data. 
* The $x$-axis represents the continuous variable (the class intervals).
* The $y$-axis represents the frequency.
* Unlike bar charts, there are **no gaps** between the bars because the data is continuous.
* For SL, you only need to work with **equal class intervals** (frequency density is not required).

### Worked Example 1: Creating a Histogram
**Question:** The time taken, $t$ minutes, for $40$ students to travel to school is recorded.
* $0 \le t < 10$: $8$ students
* $10 \le t < 20$: $15$ students
* $20 \le t < 30$: $12$ students
* $30 \le t < 40$: $5$ students

State the modal class and determine how many students take $20$ minutes or more to travel to school.

**Solution:**

<Steps>
1. **Identify the modal class.**
   The modal class is the interval with the highest frequency.
   Looking at the frequencies, $15$ is the highest.
   Therefore, the modal class is $10 \le t < 20$.
2. **Calculate the number of students taking $\ge 20$ minutes.**
   Add the frequencies for the intervals $20 \le t < 30$ and $30 \le t < 40$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \text{Total} &= 12 + 5 \\
   &= 17 \text{ students}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 2. Cumulative Frequency

**Cumulative frequency** is a running total of frequencies. A cumulative frequency graph (also known as an ogive) plots the cumulative frequency on the $y$-axis against the **upper boundary** of each class interval on the $x$-axis. It forms an S-shaped curve.

From a cumulative frequency graph, you can estimate:
* **Median ($Q_2$):** The value at $50\%$ of the total frequency.
* **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** The value at $25\%$ of the total frequency.
* **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** The value at $75\%$ of the total frequency.
* **Percentiles:** The $p$-th percentile is the value below which $p\%$ of the observations fall.

The Interquartile Range (IQR) measures the spread of the middle $50\%$ of your data.

<FormulaBox title="Interquartile range">
$$ \text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 $$
</FormulaBox>

### Worked Example 2: Finding Quartiles from a Table
**Question:** The cumulative frequencies for the wages of $80$ workers are recorded. If $Q_1$ is approximately $350 \text{ GBP}$ and $Q_3$ is approximately $520 \text{ GBP}$, find the Interquartile Range.

**Solution:**
Use the formula for the Interquartile Range.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{IQR} &= Q_3 - Q_1 \\
&= 520 - 350 \\
&= 170 \text{ GBP}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Box and Whisker Diagrams

A **box and whisker diagram** (or box plot) is a fantastic tool for visualising the spread and skewness of data. It is constructed using the **five-number summary**:
1. Minimum value
2. Lower Quartile ($Q_1$)
3. Median ($Q_2$)
4. Upper Quartile ($Q_3$)
5. Maximum value

<Aside type="danger" title="Important: Outliers">
  When drawing a box and whisker diagram, **outliers must be indicated with a cross** ($\times$). The whisker then extends only to the lowest or highest data value that is *not* an outlier.
</Aside>

### Comparing Distributions
You can use box plots to compare two sets of data by discussing:
* **Central Tendency:** Compare the medians.
* **Spread:** Compare the Interquartile Ranges (IQR) and the overall ranges.
* **Symmetry:** Determine if the data is skewed or roughly symmetrical.

If the median is perfectly in the centre of the box, and the whiskers are of equal length, the data is symmetrical. **A highly symmetrical box and whisker diagram suggests the data may be normally distributed.**

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Histogram Interpretation">
    A histogram displays the ages of $50$ people attending a concert. The bars have equal widths of $10$ years ($0 \le x < 10$, $10 \le x < 20$, etc.). The frequencies are:
    * $10 \le x < 20$: $12$
    * $20 \le x < 30$: $25$
    * $30 \le x < 40$: $8$
    * $40 \le x < 50$: $5$
    
    **a)** State the modal class.
    
    **b)** Calculate the percentage of attendees who are under $30$ years old.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** The highest frequency is $25$. Therefore, the modal class is **$20 \le x < 30$**.
    
    **b)** 
    <Steps>
    1. **Calculate the total number of people under 30.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Total} &= 12 \text{ (from 10-20)} + 25 \text{ (from 20-30)} \\
       &= 37
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Calculate the percentage.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Percentage} &= \dfrac{37}{50} \times 100 \\
       &= 74\%
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Constructing Cumulative Frequency">
    The weights, $w$ in kg, of $40$ parcels are given below:
    * $0 \le w < 2$: $5$
    * $2 \le w < 4$: $14$
    * $4 \le w < 6$: $16$
    * $6 \le w < 8$: $5$
    
    **a)** Complete a cumulative frequency table for this data.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    The cumulative frequency is the running total at the upper bound of each class.
    * $w < 2$: **$5$**
    * $w < 4$: $5 + 14 = $ **$19$**
    * $w < 6$: $19 + 16 = $ **$35$**
    * $w < 8$: $35 + 5 = $ **$40$**
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Reading an Ogive (CF Graph)">
    A cumulative frequency graph for the time taken to complete a puzzle by $120$ students shows the following key points on the curve:
    * at $y = 30$, $x = 12 \text{ mins}$
    * at $y = 60$, $x = 18 \text{ mins}$
    * at $y = 90$, $x = 26 \text{ mins}$
    
    **a)** Estimate the median time taken.
    
    **b)** Calculate the interquartile range (IQR).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**

<Steps>
    1. **Find the median position.**
       The median is at $50\%$ of the total frequency.
       $50\% \text{ of } 120 = 60$.
    2. **Read the corresponding $x$ value.**
       At $y = 60$, $x = 18 \text{ mins}$.
       The median is **$18 \text{ mins}$**.
</Steps>

    **b)**

<Steps>
    1. **Find $Q_1$ and $Q_3$.**
       $Q_1$ is at $25\%$ of $120 = 30$. At $y = 30$, $x = 12$. So, $Q_1 = 12 \text{ mins}$.
       $Q_3$ is at $75\%$ of $120 = 90$. At $y = 90$, $x = 26$. So, $Q_3 = 26 \text{ mins}$.
    2. **Calculate IQR.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{IQR} &= Q_3 - Q_1 \\
       &= 26 - 12 \\
       &= 14 \text{ mins}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Box and Whisker Outliers">
    A dataset of test scores has a minimum value of $15$, $Q_1 = 45$, Median = $60$, $Q_3 = 75$, and a maximum value of $98$. 
    An outlier is defined as any value less than $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ or greater than $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$.
    
    **a)** Calculate the IQR.
    
    **b)** Determine mathematically if the minimum value ($15$) or the maximum value ($98$) should be marked as an outlier with a cross ($\times$) on a box plot.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    Use the IQR formula.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{IQR} &= Q_3 - Q_1 \\
    &= 75 - 45 \\
    &= 30
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    **b)**

<Steps>
    1. **Calculate the outlier boundaries.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Lower boundary} &= 45 - 1.5(30) = 45 - 45 = 0 \\
       \text{Upper boundary} &= 75 + 1.5(30) = 75 + 45 = 120
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Evaluate the minimum and maximum.**
       The minimum is $15$. Since $15 > 0$, it is **not** an outlier.
       The maximum is $98$. Since $98 < 120$, it is **not** an outlier.
       Neither point should be marked with a cross.
</Steps>

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Symmetry and Normal Distribution">
    A researcher draws a box and whisker diagram for the weights of a sample of apples. The diagram shows:
    Min = $90\text{ g}$, $Q_1 = 110\text{ g}$, Median = $130\text{ g}$, $Q_3 = 150\text{ g}$, Max = $170\text{ g}$.
    
    **a)** Calculate the difference between the Median and $Q_1$, and the difference between $Q_3$ and the Median.
    
    **b)** Based on the symmetry of the box and whiskers, suggest what type of distribution this data might follow.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**

<Steps>
    1. **Calculate differences.**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Median} - Q_1 &= 130 - 110 = 20\text{ g} \\
       Q_3 - \text{Median} &= 150 - 130 = 20\text{ g}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
    Because the median is exactly in the middle of $Q_1$ and $Q_3$ (both distances are $20\text{ g}$), and the whiskers are of equal length (both $20\text{ g}$ long), the box plot is perfectly symmetrical. This suggests the data may be **normally distributed**.
  </TabItem>
