## 1. Core Terminology and Sample Spaces

To study probability mathematically, we must first define our terms precisely:
* **Trial:** A single action or experiment that results in an outcome (e.g., flipping a coin, rolling a die).
* **Outcome:** A single possible result of a trial (e.g., getting a "Heads").
* **Sample Space ($U$):** The set of *all* possible outcomes of an experiment. It is often represented as a list, a table, or a 2D grid.
* **Event ($A$):** A specific outcome or a set of outcomes we are interested in (e.g., rolling an even number).
* **Equally Likely Outcomes:** When every outcome in the sample space has the exact same chance of occurring (e.g., a perfectly fair, balanced die).

## 2. Theoretical Probability and Complementary Events



When outcomes are equally likely, we calculate the theoretical probability of an event $A$ occurring by dividing the number of favourable outcomes by the total number of outcomes in the sample space.

<FormulaBox title="Probability of an event A">
$$ \text{P}(A) = \dfrac{n(A)}{n(U)} $$
</FormulaBox>

The probability of an event *not* happening is called the **complementary event**, denoted as $A'$. Because an event must either happen or not happen, their probabilities always sum to 1.

<FormulaBox title="Complementary events">
$$ \text{P}(A) + \text{P}(A') = 1 $$
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="tip" title="Rearranging the Complement Formula">
  It is frequently much faster to calculate the probability of something *not* happening by rearranging the formula: $\text{P}(A') = 1 - \text{P}(A)$. If a question asks for the probability of rolling "at least a 2" on a standard die, it is faster to calculate $1 - \text{P}(\text{rolling a } 1)$.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Basic Probability
**Question:** A single fair 6-sided die is rolled. Find the probability of rolling a prime number.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Define the sample space and favourable outcomes.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     U &= \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\} \Rightarrow n(U) = 6 \\
     A &= \{2, 3, 5\} \Rightarrow n(A) = 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Calculate $\text{P}(A)$.**
     $$ \text{P}(A) = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2} $$
</Steps>

## 3. Experimental Probability (Relative Frequency)

We do not always know if a coin, die, or system is fair. **Experimental probability** (or relative frequency) is determined by conducting trials and recording the outcomes. 

$$ \text{Experimental Probability} = \dfrac{\text{Number of times event occurs}}{\text{Total number of trials}} $$

As you increase the number of trials, the experimental probability will gradually approach the theoretical probability. This fundamental concept is known as the *Law of Large Numbers*.

## 4. Expected Number of Occurrences

If you know the probability of an event, you can predict how many times it will occur over a series of repeated trials.

$$ \text{Expected occurrences} = \text{Total trials} \times \text{P}(A) $$

**Worked Example:** If there are 128 students in a year group and the historical probability of a student being absent on any given Friday is 0.1, the expected number of absent students is $128 \times 0.1 = 12.8$. (Note: Even though we cannot have 0.8 of a student, we leave expected values as exact decimals unless asked to round to the nearest integer contextually).

    A fair octagonal spinner is numbered 1 to 8. It is spun once. Let $A$ be the event "the spinner lands on a multiple of 3".
    
    **a)** Find $\text{P}(A)$.
    
    **b)** Find $\text{P}(A')$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Identify the sample space and favourable outcomes.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     U &= \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8\} \Rightarrow n(U) = 8 \\
     A &= \{3, 6\} \Rightarrow n(A) = 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Apply the probability formula.**
     $$ \text{P}(A) = \dfrac{2}{8} = \dfrac{1}{4} $$
</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Use the complementary events formula.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A') &= 1 - \text{P}(A) \\
     &= 1 - \dfrac{1}{4} = \dfrac{3}{4}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: 2D Sample Space">
    Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled simultaneously. 
    
    **a)** Find the probability that the sum of the dice is exactly 9.
    
    **b)** Find the probability that the sum is greater than 9.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Use the sample space grid (from section 1).**
     There are 36 total outcomes. The outcomes resulting in a sum of 9 are $(3,6), (4,5), (5,4),$ and $(6,3)$.
     $$ \text{P}(\text{Sum is } 9) = \dfrac{4}{36} = \dfrac{1}{9} $$
</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Identify all outcomes greater than 9 (sums of 10, 11, or 12).**
     Outcomes: $(4,6), (5,5), (6,4), (5,6), (6,5), (6,6)$. That is 6 favourable outcomes.
     $$ \text{P}(\text{Sum } > 9) = \dfrac{6}{36} = \dfrac{1}{6} $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Playing Cards">
    A single card is drawn at random from a standard deck of 52 playing cards (no jokers). Let $B$ be the event "a black face card (Jack, Queen, or King) is drawn".
    
    **a)** Find $\text{P}(B)$.
    
    **b)** The card is replaced, the deck is shuffled, and a card is drawn again. Find the expected number of times event $B$ occurs if this process is repeated 260 times.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Identify the number of black face cards.**
     There are 2 black suits (Clubs, Spades). Each has 3 face cards. Total $n(B) = 6$.
     $$ \text{P}(B) = \dfrac{6}{52} = \dfrac{3}{26} $$
</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Calculate the expected value.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Expected occurrences} &= n \times \text{P}(B) \\
     &= 260 \times \left(\dfrac{3}{26}\right) \\
     &= 30
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Manufacturing Defects">
    A factory produces smartphone screens. The probability that any single screen passes quality control is 0.985.
    
    **a)** Find the probability that a screen is defective.
    
    **b)** If the factory produces 8000 screens in a week, calculate the expected number of defective screens.

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Calculate the expected value for defects.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Expected defects} &= 8000 \times 0.015 \\
     &= 120
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Experimental vs Theoretical">
    A student suspects a 4-sided die (tetrahedron) is biased. She rolls it 50 times and records the outcomes below:
    
    | Result | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | Frequency | 8 | 10 | 22 | 10 |
    
    **a)** Find the experimental probability of rolling a 3.
    
    **b)** Find the theoretical probability of rolling a 3 if the die were perfectly fair.
    
    **c)** State, with a reason, whether the data suggests the die is biased.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
<Steps>

</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Calculate the theoretical probability.**
     $$ \text{Theoretical P}(3) = \dfrac{1}{4} = 0.25 $$
</Steps>
    
    **c)** Yes, the data suggests bias. The experimental relative frequency (0.44) is significantly higher than the theoretical probability (0.25). A completely fair die would yield a frequency much closer to 12.5.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Genetics (Punnett Squares)">
    In pea plants, tall stem trait ($T$) is dominant over short stem trait ($t$). Two heterozygous plants ($Tt$) are crossed. The possible allele combinations for the offspring are $TT$, $Tt$, $tT$, and $tt$, which are all equally likely.
    
    **a)** Find the theoretical probability that an offspring plant will be tall (which requires at least one dominant $T$ allele).
    
    **b)** If a farmer grows 400 offspring plants, find the expected number of short plants.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Determine favourable outcomes for tall plants.**
     The sample space is $U = \{TT, Tt, tT, tt\}$. The combinations that result in a tall plant are $\{TT, Tt, tT\}$.
     $$ \text{P}(\text{Tall}) = \dfrac{3}{4} = 0.75 $$
</Steps>
    
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. **Find probability of short, then expected value.**
     A short plant requires the $tt$ combination. $\text{P}(\text{Short}) = \dfrac{1}{4}$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Expected short} &= 400 \times \dfrac{1}{4} \\
     &= 100
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
