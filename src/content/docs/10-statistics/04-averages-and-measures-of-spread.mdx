When analysing data, we usually want to find two things: a single value that represents the "centre" of the data (an **average**), and a value that tells us how stretched out or consistent the data is (a **measure of spread**).

<Aside type="note" title="The Three Averages">
* **Mode:** The most frequent value.
* **Median:** The middle value when the data is in order.
* **Mean:** The sum of all values divided by the total number of values.
</Aside>

---

## 1. Averages for Individual Data

Let's look at a raw list of data: $4, 7, 2, 7, 9, 10, 3$.

Before doing anything with median or quartiles, **always sort the data into ascending order:**
$2, 3, 4, 7, 7, 9, 10$

### The Mode
The **mode** is the number that appears most often. 
In our list, the number $7$ appears twice, while all others appear once. 
* **Mode = 7**

### The Median
The **median** is the exact middle value. Cross off numbers from both ends until you reach the centre.
$2, 3, 4, \mathbf{7}, 7, 9, 10$
* **Median = 7**

*(If there are two middle numbers, add them together and divide by 2).*

### The Mean
To find the **mean** (often represented by $\bar{x}$), add up all the numbers and divide by how many numbers there are.

$$
\text{Mean} = \frac{2 + 3 + 4 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 10}{7} = \frac{42}{7} = 6
$$

---

## 2. Measures of Spread: Range and Quartiles

Averages tell you about the centre, but they don't tell you how spread out the numbers are. For that, we use the **range** and the **interquartile range (IQR)**.

<Aside type="tip" title="Range vs. IQR">
* **Range:** The difference between the largest and smallest values. It is heavily affected by extreme outliers.
* **Interquartile Range (IQR):** The spread of the middle 50% of the data. It ignores extreme outliers, making it a more reliable measure of consistency.
</Aside>

### Calculating Quartiles
Quartiles split the data into four equal parts.
1. **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** The median of the lower half of the data.
2. **Median ($Q_2$):** The middle of the whole data set.
3. **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** The median of the upper half of the data.

Using our previous sorted list: $\mathbf{2}, 3, \mathbf{4}, 7, \mathbf{7}, 9, \mathbf{10}$
* **Minimum** = $2$
* **$Q_1$ (Lower Quartile)** = $3$ (middle of $2, 3, 4$)
* **Median** = $7$
* **$Q_3$ (Upper Quartile)** = $9$ (middle of $7, 9, 10$)
* **Maximum** = $10$

$$
\text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum} = 10 - 2 = 8
$$

$$
\text{Interquartile Range (IQR)} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 9 - 3 = 6
$$

## 3. Which Average is Best?

You must understand the purpose of each average, as examiners will ask you to justify your choice.

* **Mode:** Used for categorical (non-numerical) data or finding the most popular item (e.g., shoe size to stock in a shop).
* **Median:** The best average to use when the data contains extreme outliers (e.g., house prices or salaries), because it is not distorted by abnormally high or low values.
* **Mean:** Uses all the data values. Best when the data is fairly symmetrical without extreme outliers.

## 4. Grouped Data

When data is grouped into classes, we lose the exact original numbers. We only know how many numbers fall into a certain category.

### The Modal Class
Instead of finding a single mode, we find the **modal class**—the group with the highest frequency.

### Estimating the Mean
Because we don't know the exact values in each group, we use the **midpoint** ($x$) of each class as a representative value. 

We then multiply each midpoint by its frequency ($f$) to get an estimated total for that row, sum these totals, and divide by the total frequency.

$$
\text{Estimated Mean} = \frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}
$$

**Example:** The heights of 40 plants are recorded.

| Height, $h$ (cm) | Frequency ($f$) | Midpoint ($x$) | $f \times x$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0 < h \le 10$ | 8 | 5 | $8 \times 5 = 40$ |
| $10 < h \le 20$ | 20 | 15 | $20 \times 15 = 300$ |
| $20 < h \le 30$ | 12 | 25 | $12 \times 25 = 300$ |
| **Totals:** | **$\sum f = 40$** | | **$\sum fx = 640$** |

$$
\text{Estimated Mean} = \frac{640}{40} = 16\text{ cm}
$$

<SteveTip title="The 'Estimate' Trick Question">
Examiners love asking: *"Explain why your answer is only an estimate."*
**Perfect Answer:** "Because the exact values within each class interval are unknown, so we must assume they are evenly distributed around the midpoint."
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
When dividing to find the mean of a grouped table, always divide by the **Total Frequency** ($\sum f$). A very common mistake is dividing by the number of rows in the table!
</Aside>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Individual Data">
    The ages of 9 children in a club are: $12, 10, 15, 11, 10, 13, 14, 10, 16$.
    
    Calculate:
    1. The mode
    2. The median
    3. The mean
    4. The range
    5. The interquartile range (IQR)
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. **Sort the data:** $10, 10, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16$
      2. **Mode:** $10$ (appears three times).
      3. **Median:** The middle value of the 9 numbers is the 5th number. Median = $12$.
      4. **Mean:** Sum $= 10+10+10+11+12+13+14+15+16 = 111$. 
         Mean $= 111 \div 9 = 12.33$ (to 2 d.p.).
      5. **Range:** Max - Min $= 16 - 10 = 6$.
      6. **IQR:** Lower half is $10, 10, 10, 11$ -> $Q_1 = 10$. 
         Upper half is $13, 14, 15, 16$ -> $Q_3 = 14.5$. 
         IQR $= 14.5 - 10 = 4.5$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Grouped Data (Extended)">
    The table shows the time, $t$ minutes, taken by 50 students to travel to school.

    | Time ($t$ mins) | Frequency |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | $0 < t \le 10$ | $7$ |
    | $10 < t \le 20$ | $18$ |
    | $20 < t \le 30$ | $15$ |
    | $30 < t \le 40$ | $10$ |

    1. Write down the modal class.
    2. Calculate an estimate for the mean time taken.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. **Modal class:** This is the group with the highest frequency. The highest frequency is $18$. Therefore, the modal class is **$10 < t \le 20$**.
      2. **Estimated Mean:** Find midpoints ($x$) and multiply by frequencies ($f$).
         * $0 < t \le 10$: $x = 5$, $fx = 7 \times 5 = 35$
         * $10 < t \le 20$: $x = 15$, $fx = 18 \times 15 = 270$
         * $20 < t \le 30$: $x = 25$, $fx = 15 \times 25 = 375$
         * $30 < t \le 40$: $x = 35$, $fx = 10 \times 35 = 350$
      3. Total frequency ($\sum f$) = $50$.
      4. Total $fx$ ($\sum fx$) = $35 + 270 + 375 + 350 = 1030$.
      5. Estimated Mean = $1030 \div 50 = \mathbf{20.6}$ minutes.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
