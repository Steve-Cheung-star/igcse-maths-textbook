When we want to see if there is a relationship between two different variables (known as **bivariate data**), we plot them on a **scatter diagram**. For example, we might want to see if there is a link between the hours a student spends studying and the score they get on a test.

* **Positive Correlation:** As one variable increases, the other variable also increases (e.g., Height and Shoe Size). The points slope upwards from left to right.
* **Negative Correlation:** As one variable increases, the other variable decreases (e.g., Age of a car and its Value). The points slope downwards from left to right.
* **Zero Correlation:** There is no relationship at all between the variables (e.g., Number of pets owned and Score on a maths test). The points are scattered randomly.

<Aside type="note" title="Strong vs. Weak">
Correlation can also be described as **strong** or **weak**. 
If the points are clustered tightly together in a straight line, the correlation is **strong**. If the points follow a general trend but are widely spread out, the correlation is **weak**.
</Aside>

## 2. Drawing Scatter Diagrams

When asked to draw a scatter diagram in the exam, there are strict rules you must follow to secure your plotting marks.

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rules of Plotting">
1. **Use Crosses:** You **MUST** plot points using small, neat crosses ($\times$). Do not use dots or filled circles! Examiners will deduct marks if your points are not clear crosses.
2. **Read the Scale Carefully:** Do not blindly assume that one little square on the grid equals $1$ unit. Common values for one small square are $1, 2, 5, 0.1, 0.2,$ or $0.5$. 
   *Pro-tip:* To find the exact value of one small square, locate two major number labels on the axis, find their difference (e.g., $40 - 20 = 20$), count the number of small squares between them (e.g., $10$ squares), and divide the difference by the number of squares ($20 \div 10 = 2$ units per square). Be mindful of this!
</SteveTip>

## 3. The Line of Best Fit (By Eye)

A line of best fit (LOBF) is a straight line drawn through the center of the data points that best represents the trend. 

If an exam question asks you to draw a line of best fit "by eye", you must follow these four exact syllabus criteria:

<Steps>
  1. **Find the Mean Point:** You must calculate the mean of the $x$-values ($\bar{x}$) and the mean of the $y$-values ($\bar{y}$). 
  2. **Plot the Mean Point:** Plot the coordinate $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ on your scatter diagram.
  3. **Draw a Single Ruled Line:** Use a ruler! The line **must** pass perfectly through your plotted mean point.
  4. **Balance the Points:** Ensure the line extends across the *full* data set, and that there is a roughly even number of points on either side of the line over its entire length.
</Steps>

### Using the Line of Best Fit
Once you have drawn the line of best fit, you can use it to estimate values. 

<Aside type="caution" title="Interpolation vs. Extrapolation">
* **Interpolation:** Estimating a value *inside* the range of your plotted data. This is generally **reliable**.
* **Extrapolation:** Extending the line to estimate a value *outside* the range of your plotted data. This is **unreliable** because we don't know if the trend continues beyond the data we have measured. Examiners love asking why an estimate is unreliable!
</Aside>

## 4. Linear Regression on a Calculator (GDC)

While drawing by eye is a useful skill, the IGCSE 0607 syllabus heavily tests your ability to use a Graphic Display Calculator to find the exact mathematical equation of the line of best fit. This is called the **equation of linear regression**, written in the form $y = mx + c$.

### Steps for the TI-Nspire

Imagine you are given this data representing Hours Studied ($x$) vs. Test Score ($y$):

| Hours ($x$) | 2 | 3 | 5 | 6 | 8 | 9 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Score ($y$)** | 45 | 55 | 70 | 80 | 90 | 95 |

<Steps>
  1. Open a new **Lists & Spreadsheet** document.
  2. Label the top of column A with your $x$-variable name (e.g., `hours`).
  3. Label the top of column B with your $y$-variable name (e.g., `score`).
  4. Enter all your data points down the columns. Ensure the pairs line up exactly across the rows!
  5. Press **Menu** > **4: Statistics** > **1: Stat Calculations** > **3: Linear Regression (mx+b)**.
  6. A box will pop up. Set your **X List** to `hours` and your **Y List** to `score`.
  7. Press **OK**.
</Steps>

The calculator will output a list of variables. The ones you need to write the equation are:
* **$m$:** The gradient (slope) of the line.
* **$b$:** The $y$-intercept (the syllabus writes this as $c$, but the calculator calls it $b$).

For the data above, the calculator gives $m \approx 7.15$ and $b \approx 33.2$, meaning your linear regression equation is:

$$
y = 7.15x + 33.2
$$

    The scatter graph shows the heights and weights of some dogs. 
    The x-axis (Height) has 1 large square representing 10 cm, split into 10 small squares.
    The y-axis (Weight) has 1 large square representing 5 kg, split into 10 small squares.
    
    A new dog is 44 cm tall and weighs 21.5 kg. 
    Explain exactly how many small squares along and up from the origin you would plot this cross.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. **X-axis (Height):** 10 small squares = 10 cm. 
         Therefore, 1 small square = $10 \div 10 = 1$ cm.
         To plot 44 cm, move **44 small squares** along the x-axis.
      2. **Y-axis (Weight):** 10 small squares = 5 kg. 
         Therefore, 1 small square = $5 \div 10 = 0.5$ kg.
         To plot 21.5 kg, divide the weight by the value of one square:
         $21.5 \div 0.5 = 43$.
         Move **43 small squares** up the y-axis.
      3. Plot a small, neat cross ($\times$) at this exact intersection.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Calculating the Mean Point">
    A botanist measures the temperature ($x ^\circ\text{C}$) and the growth of a plant ($y$ mm) over 5 days.
    The results are:
    $(15, 20), (18, 26), (20, 30), (22, 35), (25, 44)$.
    
    Calculate the coordinates of the mean point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. Calculate the mean of the $x$-values:
         
         $$
         \bar{x} = \frac{15 + 18 + 20 + 22 + 25}{5} = \frac{100}{5} = 20
         $$
         
      2. Calculate the mean of the $y$-values:
         
         $$
         \bar{y} = \frac{20 + 26 + 30 + 35 + 44}{5} = \frac{155}{5} = 31
         $$
         
      3. **Mean Point:** $(20, 31)$
