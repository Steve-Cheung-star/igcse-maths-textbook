The metric system is elegantly designed around the number 10. To convert between units, you simply multiply or divide by powers of 10 (like 10, 100, or 1000). The secret to never making a mistake is understanding *why* these numbers change, especially when dealing with area (2D) and volume (3D).

---

## 1. Length and Mass (1D)

Length and mass are straightforward 1-dimensional measurements. 

* **Length:** Kilometres (km), metres (m), centimetres (cm), millimetres (mm).
* **Mass:** Kilograms (kg), grams (g).

<Aside type="tip" title="The Golden Rule of Conversion">
* **Large to Small:** Multiply (You will have *more* of the smaller units).
* **Small to Large:** Divide (You will have *fewer* of the larger units).
</Aside>

## 2. Area Conversions (2D)

<Aside type="caution" title="The Most Common Exam Mistake">
Many students think that because $1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm}$, then $1 \text{ m}^2$ must equal $100 \text{ cm}^2$. **This is completely wrong!**
</Aside>

Area measures two dimensions: length and width. Imagine a square patio that is exactly $1 \text{ m}$ long and $1 \text{ m}$ wide. 
The area is:
$$
1 \text{ m} \times 1 \text{ m} = 1 \text{ m}^2
$$

Now, convert those side lengths into centimetres. The patio is $100 \text{ cm}$ long and $100 \text{ cm}$ wide.
The area in $\text{cm}^2$ is:
$$
100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} = 10,000 \text{ cm}^2
$$

**Rule:** When converting area, you must **square** the conversion factor.

## 3. Volume and Capacity (3D)

Volume represents 3D space. Using the exact same logic as area, when converting volume, you must **cube** the conversion factor. 

For example, a cube that is $1 \text{ m}$ on each side has a volume of $1 \text{ m}^3$. In centimetres, that is $100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} \times 100 \text{ cm} = 1,000,000 \text{ cm}^3$.

### Capacity vs. Volume

**Volume** measures the physical space an object takes up (e.g., cubic metres). 
**Capacity** measures how much fluid a container can hold (e.g., litres). 



You must know how to build the "bridge" between them. Memorise these key connections:
* $1 \text{ cm}^3$ is exactly equal to $1 \text{ ml}$.
* $1 \text{ litre}$ is equal to $1000 \text{ ml}$ (and therefore $1000 \text{ cm}^3$).
* $1 \text{ m}^3$ is massive—it holds exactly $1000 \text{ litres}$!

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Basic Conversions (Core)">
    1. Convert $4.5 \text{ km}$ to $\text{cm}$.
    2. Convert $850 \text{ g}$ to $\text{kg}$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. **km to cm:** First convert to metres (multiply by 1000), then to centimetres (multiply by 100).
       $$
       4.5 \times 1000 = 4500 \text{ m}
       $$
       $$
       4500 \times 100 = 450,000 \text{ cm}
       $$
    2. **g to kg:** Divide by 1000.
       $$
       850 \div 1000 = 0.85 \text{ kg}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Area Conversion Application (Core)">
    A rectangular room measures $4 \text{ m}$ by $3 \text{ m}$. It needs to be tiled completely. Each square tile has an area of $400 \text{ cm}^2$. 
    How many tiles are needed to cover the floor?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
    1. **Calculate the room's area in m²:** 
       $$
       4 \text{ m} \times 3 \text{ m} = 12 \text{ m}^2
       $$
    2. **Convert the area to cm²:** Remember to multiply by $100^2$ (which is $10,000$).
       $$
       12 \times 10,000 = 120,000 \text{ cm}^2
       $$
    3. **Calculate the number of tiles:** Divide the total floor area by the area of one tile.
       $$
       120,000 \div 400 = 300 \text{ tiles}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Volume to Capacity (Extended)">
    A rectangular swimming pool is $10 \text{ m}$ long, $5 \text{ m}$ wide, and $2 \text{ m}$ deep. 
    Calculate the capacity of the swimming pool in litres.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
    1. **Calculate the volume in m³:**
       $$
       10 \times 5 \times 2 = 100 \text{ m}^3
       $$
    2. **Convert m³ to litres:** Look at the capacity bridge. $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1000 \text{ litres}$. Multiply by 1000.
       $$
       100 \times 1000 = 100,000 \text{ litres}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Multi-Step Rate Problem (Extended)">
    Water flows from a pipe at a steady rate of $250 \text{ ml}$ per second into an empty rectangular tank. The tank has a volume of $0.45 \text{ m}^3$. 
    How many minutes will it take to completely fill the tank?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
    1. **Convert the tank's volume into litres:**
       $$
       0.45 \text{ m}^3 \times 1000 = 450 \text{ litres}
       $$
    2. **Convert litres into ml to match the flow rate:**
       $$
       450 \text{ litres} \times 1000 = 450,000 \text{ ml}
       $$
    3. **Calculate total time in seconds:** Divide total volume by flow rate per second.
       $$
       450,000 \div 250 = 1800 \text{ seconds}
       $$
    4. **Convert seconds to minutes:** Divide by 60.
       $$
       1800 \div 60 = 30 \text{ minutes}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
