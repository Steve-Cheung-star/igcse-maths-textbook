## 1. Inquiry: Precision and Tools
**Inquiry Question:** *How do calculators help us explore numbers that aren't "perfect"?*

While we can calculate $3^2$ or $\sqrt{25}$ in our heads, most numbers in the real world are not perfect squares. To work with these, we must master the specific functions of a scientific calculator.

### Key Calculator Buttons
* **The Square Key ($x^2$):** Multiplies the number by itself.
* **The Square Root Key ($\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$):** Finds the side length of a square area.
* **The Power Key ($x^y$ or $\wedge$):** Allows you to calculate any power (e.g., $2^5$).



---

## 2. Estimating vs. Calculating
When you find the square root of a non-perfect square, like $\sqrt{20}$, the calculator will give you a long decimal.

**Estimation:** We know $\sqrt{16} = 4$ and $\sqrt{25} = 5$. 
Since 20 is between 16 and 25, $\sqrt{20}$ must be between 4 and 5.

**Calculation:**
Using a calculator: $\sqrt{20} \approx 4.4721...$

<Aside type="caution" title="Rounding">
In Year 7, unless told otherwise, it is standard practice to round your calculator answers to **2 decimal places**.
*Example: 4.4721 becomes 4.47*
</Aside>
