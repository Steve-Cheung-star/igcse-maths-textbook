## 1. Inquiry: The Third Dimension
**Inquiry Question:** *How does "cubing" a number differ from "squaring" it in terms of growth?*

While a square number represents an area (2D), a **cube number** represents volume (3D). 

### Cube Numbers ($n^3$)
A cube number is the result of multiplying an integer by itself, and then by itself again.
* $1^3 = 1 \times 1 \times 1 = 1$
* $2^3 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$
* $3^3 = 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 27$



### Cube Roots ($\sqrt[3]{x}$)
The cube root is the inverse operation. It asks: "What number multiplied by itself three times gives this total?"
$$\sqrt[3]{64} = 4 \text{ because } 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$$

## 2. Advanced HCF and LCM
In Lesson 2.1, we listed factors to find the HCF. For very large numbers, we use **Prime Factorization**.

**Example: Find the HCF and LCM of 60 and 72**
1. **Prime Factorization:**
   * $60 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5$
   * $72 = 2^3 \times 3^2$

2. **Finding HCF:** Take the *lowest* power of each common prime.
   * $2^2$ and $3^1 \rightarrow 4 \times 3 =$ **12**

3. **Finding LCM:** Take the *highest* power of every prime present.
   * $2^3$, $3^2$, and $5 \rightarrow 8 \times 9 \times 5 =$ **360**

### Q3: Advanced HCF (The Prime Method)
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Use the prime factorizations below to find the **HCF**:
    
    Number A: $2^3 \times 3 \times 5$
    
    Number B: $2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    Look for common bases and choose the smallest exponent:
    Common bases are 2 and 3.
    Smallest power of 2 is $2^2$ (4).
    Smallest power of 3 is $3^1$ (3).
    $4 \times 3 =$ **12**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q4: Advanced LCM (The Prime Method)
<Tabs>

    Number B: $2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    Take the highest power of every base present:
    $2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5 \times 7$
    
    $8 \times 9 \times 5 \times 7 = 2520$
  </TabItem>
