## 1. Inquiry: The Geometry of Our World
**Inquiry Questions:** *How does geometry help us look at the world differently?*  *How does geometry shape our world?* 

An angle is formed when two lines meet at a point called a vertex. We classify angles based on their size in degrees:
* **Acute:** Less than 90°.
* **Right:** Exactly 90°.
* **Obtuse:** Between 90° and 180°.
* **Straight:** Exactly 180°.
* **Reflex:** Between 180° and 360°.

## 2. Classifying Triangles
Triangles are classified by both their **sides** and their **angles**:
* **Equilateral:** 3 equal sides and 3 equal angles (60°).
* **Isosceles:** 2 equal sides and 2 equal angles.
* **Scalene:** No equal sides or angles.
* **Right-angled:** Contains one 90° angle.

---

## 3. The Triangle Sum Rule
A critical geometric property is that the sum of the interior angles of any triangle is always **180°**.

[Image showing the interior angles of a triangle being torn off and placed together to form a straight line of 180 degrees]

**Example:**
If a triangle has angles of 50° and 70°, what is the third angle?
1.  Add the known angles: $50 + 70 = 120°$.
2.  Subtract from 180: $180 - 120 = 60°$.
3.  **Result:** The third angle is 60°.

### Q2: Missing Angles in Triangles
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Find the missing angle $x$ in these triangles:
    
    a) A triangle with 40° and 60°.
    
    b) A right-angled triangle with one angle of 30°.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    a) $180 - (40 + 60) = \mathbf{80°}$.
    
    b) $180 - (90 + 30) = \mathbf{60°}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q3: Isosceles Logic
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    An isosceles triangle has one angle of 100°. If this is the "top" angle (not one of the equal ones), what are the other two angles?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    1.  $180 - 100 = 80°$ remaining.
    2.  Divide by 2 (since they are equal): $80 \div 2 = \mathbf{40°}$ each.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
