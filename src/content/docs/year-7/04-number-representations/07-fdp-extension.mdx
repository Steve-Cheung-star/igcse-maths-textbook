## 1. Inquiry: Measuring Change
**Inquiry Question:** *If a price goes up by 10% and then down by 10%, do we end up back at the original price?*

In the real world, values are rarely static. We use percentages to describe how much a value has grown or shrunk relative to its starting point.

### The Percentage Change Formula
When you need to find the percentage of a change that has already happened:
$$\text{Percentage Change} = \frac{\text{Change}}{\text{Original Amount}} \times 100$$

---

## 2. Increase and Decrease

### Strategy A: The Two-Step Method
1. Calculate the percentage of the amount.
2. Add it to (for increase) or subtract it from (for decrease) the original amount.

### Strategy B: The Multiplier Method
This is the fastest way to calculate a new total, especially when using a calculator.
* **To increase by 15%:** Multiply by 1.15 (100% + 15% = 115%).
* **To decrease by 15%:** Multiply by 0.85 (100% - 15% = 85%).

### Q3: Finding the Percentage Change
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Find the percentage change in these scenarios:
    
    a) A toy was 10 dollars and is now 12 dollars.
    
    b) A phone battery was at 80% and is now at 60%.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    a) Change is 2. (2 ÷ 10) × 100 = 20% increase.
    
    b) Change is 20. (20 ÷ 80) × 100 = 25% decrease.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q4: Real-World Finance
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    A laptop costs 500 dollars. The price increases by 10% for a year, but then there is a "10% off" sale. What is the final price?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    1. Increase: 500 + 50 = 550 dollars.
    2. Decrease: 10% of 550 is 55.
    3. Final: 550 - 55 = 495 dollars.
    
    **Note:** You do not end up back at 500 because the 10% decrease was calculated on the new, larger number!
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
