## 1. Inquiry: Infinite Patterns
**Inquiry Question:** *What happens when a division never ends, and how do we represent that infinity on paper?*

Most fractions we use are "terminating" decimals, meaning they stop (like $1/4 = 0.25$). However, some fractions create a "recurring" decimal, where a digit or a sequence of digits repeats forever.

### Representing Recurring Decimals
Instead of writing an infinite string of numbers, we use **dot notation**.
* **One repeating digit:** Put a dot over the digit. 
  * $1/3 = 0.3333... = 0.\dot{3}$
* **A repeating block:** Put a dot over the start and the end of the repeating sequence.
  * $1/7 = 0.142857142857... = 0.\dot{1}4285\dot{7}$

## 2. Negative Fractions
Fractions follow the exact same "sign rules" as integers. 
* A negative divided by a positive is **negative**: $-\frac{1}{2}$
* A negative divided by a negative is **positive**: $\frac{-1}{-2} = \frac{1}{2}$

### Adding and Subtracting with Negatives
When calculating, treat the numerator as the "directed number" and keep the common denominator.

**Example:**
$-\frac{3}{5} + \frac{1}{5}$
Think of this as $(-3 + 1)$ over $5$.
Result: $-\frac{2}{5}$
