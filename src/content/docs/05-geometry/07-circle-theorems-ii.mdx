While the first set of circle theorems focuses heavily on inscribed angles, this second set is all about **symmetry and distance**. Circles are perfectly symmetrical shapes, which means that lines, chords, and tangents interact with them in highly predictable, proportional ways.

<SteveTip title="Reasons are Still Mandatory">
Just like the angle theorems, you must memorise the exact wording of these symmetry properties. Examiners will ask you to "give a reason for your answer" when you use them to find missing lengths or angles. 
</SteveTip>

## 1. Equal Chords are Equidistant from the Centre

A chord is a straight line drawn between any two points on the circumference. Because a circle is perfectly round, chords that are exactly the same length will always be the exact same distance from the centre.

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 1: Equal Chords">
**Equal chords are equidistant from the centre**.
Conversely, if two chords are the same distance from the centre, they must be equal in length.
</Aside>

## 2. The Perpendicular Bisector of a Chord

If you draw a line from the centre of a circle so that it hits a chord at exactly $90^\circ$ (perpendicular), it will always cut that chord perfectly in half (bisect it). 

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 2: Perpendicular Bisector">
**The perpendicular bisector of a chord passes through the centre**.
* **Rule A:** A line from the centre perpendicular to a chord bisects the chord.
* **Rule B:** A line from the centre that bisects a chord is perpendicular to the chord.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="The Pythagoras Connection">
This theorem is almost *always* paired with **Pythagoras' Theorem** in exams. The radius, half the chord, and the perpendicular distance to the centre form a perfect right-angled triangle.
</Aside>

## 3. Tangents from an External Point

If you pick a random point anywhere outside a circle and draw straight lines to the circle, exactly two lines will act as tangents (touching the edge perfectly). Because of the circle's symmetry, these two lines will be a mirror image of each other.

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 3: Tangents from a Point">
**Tangents from an external point are equal in length**.
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="The Tangent Kite">
When you draw two tangents from an external point and connect their touching points to the centre with radii, you create a **kite**.
* The tangents are equal.
* The radii are equal.
* The angle between the tangent and radius is $90^\circ$ (from Theorem 2).
* A line drawn from the centre to the external point slices the kite into **two identical right-angled triangles**.
</Aside>

## 4. Structured Worked Examples

Examiners will blend these symmetry theorems with Pythagoras' Theorem, trigonometry (SOH CAH TOA), and basic angle properties.

**Example Task:** A chord of length $30\text{cm}$ is drawn in a circle with centre $O$. The perpendicular distance from $O$ to the chord is $8\text{cm}$. Calculate the radius of the circle, $r$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
1. **Apply the theorem:** The perpendicular line from the centre bisects the chord. 
   Half of the chord $= 30\text{cm} \div 2 = 15\text{cm}$.
   *Reason:* The perpendicular bisector of a chord passes through the centre.
2. **Set up Pythagoras:** A right-angled triangle is formed by the radius (hypotenuse), the $8\text{cm}$ distance, and the $15\text{cm}$ half-chord.
   $$
   r^2 = 8^2 + 15^2
   $$
3. **Solve:**
   $$
   r^2 = 64 + 225
   $$
   $$
   r^2 = 289
   $$
   $$
   r = \sqrt{289} = 17\text{cm}
   $$
</Steps>

    A circle has a radius of $13\text{cm}$. A chord is drawn at a distance of $5\text{cm}$ from the centre. 
    Calculate the total length of the chord.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. Draw a quick sketch. The radius ($13\text{cm}$), the distance from the centre ($5\text{cm}$), and *half* of the chord form a right-angled triangle.
    2. Use Pythagoras' Theorem to find half the chord ($x$):
       $$
       13^2 = 5^2 + x^2
       $$
       $$
       169 = 25 + x^2
       $$
       $$
       x^2 = 144
       $$
       $$
       x = 12\text{cm}
       $$
    3. The theorem states a line from the centre perpendicular to a chord bisects it. Therefore, the total length is double the half-chord:
       $$
       \text{Total Length} = 12 \times 2 = 24\text{cm}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Tangents are drawn from an external point $P$ to a circle with centre $O$. The straight line $OP$ connects the point to the centre. The angle between one tangent and the line $OP$ is $21^\circ$. 
    Calculate the total reflex angle $y$ at the centre of the circle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
    1. A tangent and radius meet at $90^\circ$. So, the triangle formed by $O$, $P$, and the top touch point is a right-angled triangle.
    2. The angle at $O$ inside that top right-angled triangle is:
       $$
       180^\circ - (90^\circ + 21^\circ) = 180^\circ - 111^\circ = 69^\circ
       $$
    3. The line $OP$ acts as a line of symmetry, splitting the kite into two identical triangles. 
       The angle inside the bottom triangle is also $69^\circ$.
    4. The total internal (obtuse) angle at the centre is:
       $$
       69^\circ + 69^\circ = 138^\circ
       $$
    5. The question asks for the **reflex angle** $y$, which completes the $360^\circ$ circle at the centre:
       $$
       y = 360^\circ - 138^\circ = 222^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Angles from Chords (Extended)">
    A chord $AB$ of length $12\text{cm}$ is drawn inside a circle of radius $10\text{cm}$. $O$ is the centre of the circle.
    Calculate the angle subtended by the chord at the centre of the circle (Angle $AOB$).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
    1. The perpendicular bisector from the centre $O$ splits the triangle $AOB$ into two identical right-angled triangles.
    2. The bisector cuts the chord in half. So the opposite side of our right-angled triangle is $12 \div 2 = 6\text{cm}$.
    3. The hypotenuse is the radius ($10\text{cm}$).
    4. Let the angle at $O$ for one of these small triangles be $x$. Use SOH CAH TOA:
       $$
       \sin(x) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{6}{10} = 0.6
       $$
    5. Find $x$ using inverse sine:
       $$
       x = \sin^{-1}(0.6) = 36.87^\circ
       $$
    6. The total angle $AOB$ is double this value:
       $$
       \text{Angle } AOB = 2 \times 36.87^\circ = 73.7^\circ \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Tangent Kite Area (Extended)">
    Tangents are drawn from an external point $T$ to touch a circle at points $P$ and $Q$. The radius of the circle is $5\text{cm}$ and the straight-line distance from the centre $O$ to point $T$ is $13\text{cm}$.
    Calculate the total area of the kite $OPTQ$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    <Steps>
    1. The kite $OPTQ$ is formed by two identical right-angled triangles: Triangle $OPT$ and Triangle $OQT$.
    2. Focus on Triangle $OPT$. The angle at $P$ is $90^\circ$ (tangent meets radius).
    3. Use Pythagoras' Theorem to find the length of the tangent $PT$:
       $$
       OT^2 = OP^2 + PT^2
       $$
       $$
       13^2 = 5^2 + PT^2
       $$
       $$
       169 = 25 + PT^2
       $$
       $$
       PT^2 = 144
       $$
       $$
       PT = 12\text{cm}
       $$
    4. Calculate the area of Triangle $OPT$:
       $$
       \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 12 = 30\text{cm}^2
       $$
    5. The kite consists of two such triangles, so the total area is:
       $$
       \text{Total Area} = 30 \times 2 = 60\text{cm}^2
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
