## 1. Inquiry: The Multiplicative Inverse
**Inquiry Question:** *How can fractions be used to communicate ideas? What happens when you multiply a number by its "flip"?* 

A **reciprocal** is the "multiplicative inverse" of a number. This means that any non-zero number multiplied by its reciprocal equals **1**. 
* To find the reciprocal of a fraction, simply "flip" the numerator and denominator.
* **Example:** The reciprocal of $\frac{3}{4}$ is $\frac{4}{3}$. 
* **Note:** Zero has no reciprocal because division by zero is not defined.



---

## 2. Adding and Subtracting with Unlike Denominators
In Year 8, you must be able to add and subtract fractions where the bottom numbers (denominators) are different.

**The Process:**
1. Find a **Common Denominator** (use the LCM of the denominators).
2. Convert both fractions into equivalent fractions with that denominator.
3. Add or subtract the numerators only.
4. Simplify the resulting fraction if possible.

**Example:** $\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3}$
* LCM of 2 and 3 is 6.
* $\frac{3}{6} + \frac{2}{6} = \frac{5}{6}$

---

## 3. Multiplying and Dividing Fractions
Multiplying and dividing are often easier than adding because you don't need a common denominator.

### Multiplication
Simply multiply the numerators together and the denominators together.
* **Example:** $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{4}{5} = \frac{8}{15}$

### Division (Keep, Change, Flip)
To divide by a fraction, multiply by its **reciprocal**.
* **Example:** $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{1}{4}$
* Keep $\frac{2}{3}$, Change $\div$ to $\times$, Flip $\frac{1}{4}$ to $\frac{4}{1}$.
* $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{4}{1} = \frac{8}{3}$
