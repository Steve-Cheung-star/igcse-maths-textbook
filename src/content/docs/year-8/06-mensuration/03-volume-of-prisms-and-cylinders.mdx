## 1. Inquiry: Filling the Space
**Inquiry Questions:** *How do architects and engineers determine how much a container can hold?  Why is the volume of a cylinder calculated using the same logic as a rectangular prism? *

Volume is the measure of how much space an object occupies. For any **Right Prism** (a 3-D shape with a constant cross-section), the volume is found by "stacking" the base area up to the total height of the object.

---

## 2. The Universal Volume Formula
For any prism or cylinder, the volume is calculated by:
$$Volume = \text{Area of the Base} \times \text{height}$$




### Volume of a Cylinder
A cylinder is just a prism with a circular base. 
1. **Find the Base Area:** Use the circle formula $\pi \times r^2$.
2. **Multiply by Height:** $V = (\pi \times r^2) \times h$.

**Example:** Calculate the volume of a cylinder with a radius of $4\text{ cm}$ and a height of $10\text{ cm}$.
* $Area_{base} = \pi \times 4^2 \approx 50.27\text{ cm}^2$ 
* $Volume = 50.27 \times 10 = \mathbf{502.7\text{ cm}^3}$ 

---

## 3. Comparing Pyramids and Prisms (Extension)
Through investigation, you will find that a pyramid (or cone) is much "emptier" than a prism with the same base and height. In fact, the volume of a pyramid is exactly **one-third** ($1/3$) of the volume of its matching prism.
$$V_{pyramid} = \frac{1}{3} \times V_{prism}$$

### Q1: Rectangular Prism Capacity
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    A rectangular water tank has a base area of $15\text{ m}^2$ and a height of $2\text{ m}$. What is its volume? 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    $Volume = 15 \times 2 = \mathbf{30\text{ m}^3}$ 
  </TabItem>

### Q2: Cylinder Calculation
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    A soda can has a radius of $3\text{ cm}$ and a height of $12\text{ cm}$. Find the volume. (Use $\pi \approx 3.14$). 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    1. Base Area = $3.14 \times 3^2 = 28.26\text{ cm}^2$ 
    2. Volume = $28.26 \times 12 = \mathbf{339.12\text{ cm}^3}$ 
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q3: Units of Volume
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    How many milliliters ($\text{mL}$) are in $1.5$ liters ($\text{L}$)? 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    Since $1\text{ L} = 1000\text{ mL}$, $1.5 \times 1000 = \mathbf{1500\text{ mL}}$. 
  </TabItem>
