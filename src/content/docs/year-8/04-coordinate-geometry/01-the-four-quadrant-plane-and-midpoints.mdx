## 2. Navigating the Four Quadrants
The coordinate plane is divided by two axes: the **x-axis** (horizontal) and the **y-axis** (vertical). They meet at a center point called the **origin** $(0,0)$.



**Plotting Conventions:**
* **Coordinates** are written as $(x, y)$.
* **Quadrant 1:** $(+, +)$ — Top Right.
* **Quadrant 2:** $(-, +)$ — Top Left.
* **Quadrant 3:** $(-, -)$ — Bottom Left.
* **Quadrant 4:** $(+, -)$ — Bottom Right.

> **Student Tip:** Remember the phrase "Over the hall and up the stairs"—always move along the x-axis first, then the y-axis.

---

## 3. Finding the Midpoint
If you have a line segment between two points, $A$ and $B$, the **midpoint** is the exact center of that line. To find it, you simply calculate the average of the x-values and the average of the y-values.

**The Midpoint Formula:**
$$\text{Midpoint} = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)$$



**Example:** Find the midpoint of $A(2, 4)$ and $B(6, 10)$.
1.  **Average the x's:** $(2 + 6) \div 2 = 4$
2.  **Average the y's:** $(4 + 10) \div 2 = 7$
3.  **Midpoint:** $(4, 7)$

### Q1: Quadrant Identification

  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    State which quadrant the following points are located in:
    
    a) $(5, -2)$
    
    b) $(-3, -7)$
    
    c) $(-1, 4)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    a) **Quadrant 4** (Positive x, Negative y)
    
    b) **Quadrant 3** (Negative x, Negative y)
    
    c) **Quadrant 2** (Negative x, Positive y)
  </TabItem>

### Q2: Calculating Midpoints
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Find the midpoint of the line segment $AB$ for the following points:
    
    a) $A(1, 2)$ and $B(5, 8)$
    
    b) $A(-4, 3)$ and $B(2, 5)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    a) $x = \frac{1+5}{2} = 3$; $y = \frac{2+8}{2} = 5$. Midpoint: **$(3, 5)$**
    
    b) $x = \frac{-4+2}{2} = -1$; $y = \frac{3+5}{2} = 4$. Midpoint: **$(-1, 4)$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q3: Plotting Challenge
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    If the origin $(0,0)$ is the midpoint of a line, and one end of the line is at $(4, 6)$, where is the other end of the line?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    To get back to 0 from 4, you must go -4. To get back to 0 from 6, you must go -6.
    **Result:** The other end is at **$(-4, -6)$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
