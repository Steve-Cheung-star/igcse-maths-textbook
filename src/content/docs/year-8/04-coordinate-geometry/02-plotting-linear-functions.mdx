## 1. Inquiry: From Equations to Lines
**Inquiry Questions:** *How do equations and graphs help us solve problems? How do we model relations in real-life?* 

An algebraic equation like $y = 2x + 3$ isn't just a math problem—it’s a set of instructions for a line. In this lesson, we use a **Table of Values** to find specific points and connect them to see the "shape" of the equation.

---

## 2. The Table of Values Method
To plot a graph where $y$ is given in terms of $x$ (e.g., $y = 2x + 3$), follow these steps:

1.  **Choose x-values:** Usually simple integers like $-2, -1, 0, 1, 2$.
2.  **Substitute:** Plug each $x$ into the equation to find the matching $y$.
3.  **Coordinate Pairs:** Write them as $(x, y)$.
4.  **Plot & Connect:** Place the dots on your grid and draw a straight line through them.

**Example Table for $y = 2x + 3$:**

| $x$ | $-1$ | $0$ | $1$ | $2$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Substitution** | $2(-1) + 3$ | $2(0) + 3$ | $2(1) + 3$ | $2(2) + 3$ |
| **$y$** | $1$ | $3$ | $5$ | $7$ |
| **Coordinate** | $(-1, 1)$ | $(0, 3)$ | $(1, 5)$ | $(2, 7)$ |



---

## 3. Recognizing Linear Equations ($y = mx + c$)
Equations that result in a straight line typically look like $y = mx + c$.
* **$m$ (The Gradient):** Tells you the steepness of the line.
* **$c$ (The Intercept):** Tells you where the line crosses the y-axis.

If you see an equation in this form, you automatically know it will be a straight line on your coordinate plane.

## 4. Practice & Homework (20 Minutes)

### Q1: Completing the Table
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Complete the table of values for the function $y = x - 4$.
    
    | $x$ | $0$ | $2$ | $4$ | $6$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **$y$** | ? | ? | ? | ? |
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    * If $x=0$, $y=0-4 = \mathbf{-4}$
    * If $x=2$, $y=2-4 = \mathbf{-2}$
    * If $x=4$, $y=4-4 = \mathbf{0}$
    * If $x=6$, $y=6-4 = \mathbf{2}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q2: Identifying the Intercept
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Look at the equation $y = 3x + 5$. Based on the $y = mx + c$ convention, what is the value of the y-intercept? 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    The constant $c$ is the intercept. In $y = 3x + 5$, the intercept is **$5$**. 
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q3: Plotting Logic
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    True or False: The equation $y = x^2$ will result in a straight-line graph. 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    **False.** Equations with $x^2$ are quadratic functions and create curves, not straight lines. 
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
