## 1. Inquiry: The Limits of Prediction
**Inquiry Questions:** *How does one event affect another?  How can we calculate the probability that something will NOT occur? *

### Independent Events
Two events are **independent** if the outcome of the first event does not affect the outcome of the second. For example, flipping a coin twice; the first flip doesn't change the odds of the second.

### Complementary Events
Every event has a "complement"—the event **not** happening. 
* The sum of an event and its complement is always **1**.
* **Formula:** $P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)$.

---

## 2. Representing Outcomes
To find the theoretical probability of two independent events, we first need to list all possible outcomes.

### Method A: Systematic Lists
If flipping a coin (H, T) and rolling a 4-sided die (1, 2, 3, 4), the outcomes are:
(H,1), (H,2), (H,3), (H,4), (T,1), (T,2), (T,3), (T,4).

### Method B: Tree Diagrams
Tree diagrams help visualize "branches" of possibilities for sequential events.



---

## 3. Calculating "And" Probabilities
To find the probability of two independent events both happening, **multiply** their individual probabilities.

**Example:**
What is the probability of flipping "Heads" **and** rolling a "6" on a standard die?
1. $P(\text{Heads}) = \frac{1}{2}$
2. $P(\text{6}) = \frac{1}{6}$
3. Calculation: $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{12}$.

### Q2: Two Independent Events
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    You spin a spinner with 3 equal sections (A, B, C) and flip a coin. Use a list or tree diagram to find the probability of getting **(A, Heads)**. 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    1. Total outcomes: $3 \times 2 = 6$ (A-H, A-T, B-H, B-T, C-H, C-T).
    2. Favorable outcomes: 1 (A-H).
    3. **Result:** $\frac{1}{6}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Q3: Comparing Data
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    A bag has a $\frac{1}{2}$ theoretical probability of giving a red marble. In an experiment of 100 pulls, you get 42 red marbles. Compare the experimental data to the theoretical probability. 
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    The **Theoretical Probability** was 50 pulls (0.5), but the **Experimental Data** showed 42. This difference is common in small sample sizes; experimental results usually get closer to theoretical ones as you do more trials.
  </TabItem>
