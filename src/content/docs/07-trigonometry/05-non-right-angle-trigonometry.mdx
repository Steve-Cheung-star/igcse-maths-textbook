SOH CAH TOA and standard Pythagoras' theorem are fantastic tools, but they **only work for right-angled triangles**. When a triangle doesn't have a $90^\circ$ angle, we need a different set of rules. 

For IGCSE Extended, you need to master three key formulas: the Area of a Triangle, the Sine Rule, and the Cosine Rule. 

<Aside type="note" title="Good News!">
The main versions of these formulas are provided on the IGCSE formula sheet in your exam! You do not need to memorise them, but you *do* need to know **when** and **how** to use them.
</Aside>

## Formula Cheat Sheet

| Rule | Formula (to find a Side) | Formula (to find an Angle) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Area** | $\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C$ | $\sin C = \frac{2 \times \text{Area}}{ab}$ |
| **Sine Rule** | $\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$ | $\frac{\sin A}{a} = \frac{\sin B}{b} = \frac{\sin C}{c}$ |
| **Cosine Rule** | $a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A$ | $A = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}\right)$ |

## 1. Area of a Triangle

To find the area of *any* triangle, you need two sides and the angle squished exactly between them (the "included" angle).

**Formula:**
$$
\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C
$$

* **When to use it:** When you have **SAS** (Side-Angle-Side).

### The Proof
Why does this work? Imagine dropping a perpendicular line ($h$) down from angle $B$ to side $b$. 

<div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
  <svg viewBox="0 0 300 150" width="100%" maxWidth="300px" style={{ background: 'white', borderRadius: '8px', border: '1px solid #e2e8f0' }}>
    <polygon points="20,130 280,130 100,20" fill="#f8fafc" stroke="#1e293b" stroke-width="2" />
    <line x1="100" y1="20" x2="100" y2="130" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" stroke-dasharray="4,4" />
    <text x="105" y="80" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold">h</text>
    <text x="10" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">A</text>
    <text x="280" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">C</text>
    <text x="95" y="15" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">B</text>
    <text x="45" y="65" fill="#3b82f6" font-style="italic" text-anchor="middle">c</text>
    <text x="200" y="65" fill="#3b82f6" font-style="italic" text-anchor="middle">a</text>
    <text x="150" y="145" fill="#3b82f6" font-style="italic" text-anchor="middle">b</text>
    {/* Right angle square */}
    <polyline points="100,120 110,120 110,130" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" />
  </svg>
</div>

Using basic SOH CAH TOA on the left-hand right-angled triangle:
$$ \sin A = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{h}{c} $$

Rearranging this gives us the height: 
$$ h = c \sin A $$

The normal area of a triangle is $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$. 

Substitute our base ($b$) and our new height formula ($c \sin A$):
$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times b \times (c \sin A) = \frac{1}{2}bc \sin A $$

*(This is the exact same formula as $\frac{1}{2}ab \sin C$, just using different sides!)*

### Example: Finding the Area
Find the area of triangle $ABC$ where $a = 7\text{ cm}$, $b = 10\text{ cm}$, and the angle $C$ between them is $42^\circ$.

    <text x="220" y="75" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">7 cm</text>

<Steps>
1. **Identify the rule:** We have Side-Angle-Side (SAS).
2. **Substitute values:**
   $$

   \text{Area} &= 23.4\text{ cm}^2 \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 2. The Sine Rule

The Sine Rule relates the lengths of the sides to the sines of their opposite angles.

**Formulas:**
To find a **side**, put the sides on top:
$$
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}
$$

To find an **angle**, flip it upside down (it makes the algebra easier):
$$
\frac{\sin A}{a} = \frac{\sin B}{b} = \frac{\sin C}{c}
$$

* **When to use it:** When you have a **"Known Pair"** (an angle and its opposite side) plus one other piece of information.

### The Proof
Using the exact same height $h$ from the Area proof above, we can look at both sides of the triangle.

From the left triangle: 
$$ h = c \sin A $$

From the right triangle: 
$$ h = a \sin C $$

Since both expressions equal $h$, we can set them equal to each other:
$$ c \sin A = a \sin C $$

Divide both sides by $(\sin A \sin C)$ to isolate the ratios:
$$ \frac{c}{\sin C} = \frac{a}{\sin A} $$

### Example: Finding a missing side
In triangle $PQR$, angle $P = 65^\circ$, angle $R = 40^\circ$, and side $p = 12\text{ cm}$. Find the length of side $r$.

    <text x="270" y="85" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">12 cm (p)</text>
    <text x="100" y="85" fill="#10b981" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">r = ?</text>
    
    <path d="M 60 150 A 30 30 0 0 0 54.5 132.7" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="70" y="140" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">65°</text>
    
    <path d="M 179.6 44.4 A 25 25 0 0 0 217.7 47.7" fill="none" stroke="#10b981" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="190" y="75" fill="#10b981" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">40°</text>
  </svg>

<Steps>
1. **Check for a Known Pair:** We know angle $P$ ($65^\circ$) and side $p$ ($12\text{ cm}$). We want side $r$, and we know angle $R$ ($40^\circ$). Perfect for the Sine Rule!
2. **Set up the equation (sides on top):**
   $$ \frac{r}{\sin(40^\circ)} = \frac{12}{\sin(65^\circ)} $$
3. **Rearrange and solve:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   r &= \frac{12 \times \sin(40^\circ)}{\sin(65^\circ)} \\
   r &= \frac{7.713...}{0.906...} \\
   r &= 8.51\text{ cm} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 3. The Cosine Rule

The Cosine Rule is basically a super-powered version of Pythagoras' theorem that works for any angle.

**Formulas:**
To find a **side**:
$$
a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A
$$

To find an **angle** (this is just the above formula rearranged):
$$
A = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}\right)
$$

* **When to use it:** When you **do NOT** have a known pair. Specifically, when you have **SSS** (all three sides) or **SAS** (two sides and the trapped angle).

### The Proof
We drop a perpendicular line $h$ from the top vertex $C$. This splits the bottom base $c$ into two parts: let's call the left part $x$, making the right part $c - x$. 

<div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>

    <polygon points="30,150 320,150 120,30" fill="#f8fafc" stroke="#1e293b" stroke-width="2" />
    <line x1="120" y1="30" x2="120" y2="150" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" stroke-dasharray="4,4" />
    <text x="125" y="90" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">h</text>
    <text x="15" y="165" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">A</text>
    <text x="325" y="165" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">B</text>
    <text x="115" y="20" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">C</text>
    
    <text x="70" y="145" fill="#3b82f6" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">x</text>
    <text x="210" y="145" fill="#3b82f6" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">c - x</text>
    <text x="50" y="85" fill="#1e293b" font-style="italic" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">b</text>
    <text x="230" y="85" fill="#1e293b" font-style="italic" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">a</text>
    
    {/* Right angle squares */}
    <polyline points="120,140 130,140 130,150" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" />
    <polyline points="120,140 110,140 110,150" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" />
    
    {/* Base bracket */}
    <path d="M 30 160 L 30 170 L 320 170 L 320 160" fill="none" stroke="#94a3b8" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="175" y="185" fill="#64748b" font-style="italic" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">c</text>
  </svg>
</div>

Using Pythagoras' theorem on the **right-hand** right-angled triangle:
$$ a^2 = h^2 + (c - x)^2 $$
$$ a^2 = h^2 + c^2 - 2cx + x^2 $$

Using Pythagoras on the **left-hand** triangle gives us:
$$ b^2 = h^2 + x^2 $$

We can substitute $b^2$ into the equation above wherever we see $(h^2 + x^2)$:
$$ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2cx $$

Finally, from the left triangle, basic trigonometry tells us:
$$ \cos A = \frac{x}{b} \implies x = b \cos A $$

Substitute that in for $x$:
$$ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A $$

### Example: Finding a missing side
In triangle $XYZ$, $y = 8\text{ cm}$, $z = 11\text{ cm}$, and the included angle $X = 115^\circ$. Calculate the length of side $x$.

<div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
  <svg viewBox="0 0 350 150" width="100%" maxWidth="300px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>
    <polygon points="120,130 330,130 30,30" fill="#eff6ff" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="110" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">X</text>
    <text x="335" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">Y</text>
    <text x="15" y="25" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">Z</text>
    
    <text x="210" y="145" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">11 cm (z)</text>
    <text x="50" y="95" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">8 cm (y)</text>
    <text x="180" y="70" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">x = ?</text>
    
    <path d="M 103.3 111.4 A 25 25 0 0 0 145 130" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="135" y="115" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">115°</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Identify the rule:** We have SAS, but we need a side. No known pairs exist. We must use the Cosine Rule.
2. **Substitute values:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   x^2 &= 8^2 + 11^2 - 2(8)(11)\cos(115^\circ) \\
   x^2 &= 64 + 121 - 176(-0.4226...) \\
   x^2 &= 185 + 74.38... \\
   x^2 &= 259.38...
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Square root to find $x$:**
   $$ x = \sqrt{259.38...} = 16.1\text{ cm} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)} $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="note" title="Obtuse Angles & Cosine">
Unlike the Sine Rule, the Cosine Rule is perfectly well-behaved with obtuse angles. Notice in the example above that $\cos(115^\circ)$ naturally became a negative number, which correctly added length to the opposite side.
</Aside>

If you use the Sine Rule to find angle $B$, your calculator will only give you the acute angle at $B_1$. If the question specifies that the angle is obtuse (meaning it's the triangle formed by $B_2$), you must manually find the second angle using the symmetry rule from the graphs:
$$
\text{Obtuse Angle} = 180^\circ - \text{Calculator Angle}
$$

<SteveTip>
If you are using `nSolve()` on your TI-Nspire to find an angle with the Sine Rule, and the question specifies the angle is obtuse, you can tell the calculator to look in the right place by adding bounds! Type: `nSolve(sin(x)/7 = sin(30)/12, x) | 90 < x < 180`. This saves you doing the $180^\circ - x$ step manually.
</SteveTip>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Working Backward with Area">
    In triangle $PQR$, $PQ = 8\text{ cm}$, $QR = 10\text{ cm}$, and the area of the triangle is $20\text{ cm}^2$. Given that angle $PQR$ is an acute angle, calculate the size of angle $PQR$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
      <svg viewBox="0 0 300 150" width="100%" maxWidth="250px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>
        <polygon points="30,130 250,130 150,30" fill="#eff6ff" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="20" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">Q</text>
        <text x="255" y="145" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="end">R</text>
        <text x="145" y="20" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">P</text>
        <text x="130" y="145" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">10 cm</text>
        <text x="70" y="75" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">8 cm</text>
        <text x="120" y="90" fill="#3b82f6" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">Area = 20 cm²</text>
      </svg>
    </div>
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the rule:** We are dealing with Area, two sides, and the included angle. Use Area $= \frac{1}{2}ab \sin C$.
    2. **Substitute known values:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       20 &= \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \times 10 \times \sin(PQR) \\
       20 &= 40 \times \sin(PQR)
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Solve for the angle:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \frac{20}{40} &= \sin(PQR) \\
       0.5 &= \sin(PQR) \\
       PQR &= \sin^{-1}(0.5) \\
       PQR &= 30^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: The Ambiguous Case">
    In triangle $ABC$, $AB = 8\text{ cm}$, $BC = 12\text{ cm}$, and angle $BAC = 35^\circ$. Angle $ACB$ is obtuse. Calculate the size of angle $ACB$ to 1 decimal place.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
      <svg viewBox="0 0 350 180" width="100%" maxWidth="300px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>
        <polygon points="30,150 140,150 220,30" fill="#eff6ff" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="15" y="165" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">A</text>
        <text x="145" y="165" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">C</text>
        <text x="225" y="25" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">B</text>
        
        <text x="180" y="100" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">12 cm</text>
        <text x="100" y="80" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">8 cm</text>
        
        <path d="M 60 150 A 30 30 0 0 0 55.4 134.0" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="70" y="145" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">35°</text>
        
        <path d="M 120 150 A 20 20 0 0 1 151.1 133.4" fill="none" stroke="#f59e0b" stroke-width="2" />
      </svg>
    </div>
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the rule:** We have a "known pair" (side $BC = 12$ and opposite angle $BAC = 35^\circ$). We need an angle, so put sines on top.
    2. **Set up the Sine Rule:**
       $$ \frac{\sin(ACB)}{8} = \frac{\sin(35^\circ)}{12} $$
    3. **Solve for the principal value (PV) using your GDC:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \sin(ACB) &= \frac{8 \times \sin(35^\circ)}{12} \\
       \sin(ACB) &= 0.3823... \\
       ACB_{PV} &= \sin^{-1}(0.3823...) \approx 22.47^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Apply the ambiguous case rule:** The question states angle $ACB$ is **obtuse**. Our calculator gave an acute angle.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Obtuse } ACB &= 180^\circ - 22.47...^\circ \\
       &= 157.5^\circ \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Finding an Angle with Cosine Rule">
    A triangle has sides of length $5\text{ cm}$, $7\text{ cm}$, and $9\text{ cm}$. Calculate the size of the **largest** angle in the triangle to 1 decimal place.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
      <svg viewBox="0 0 350 150" width="100%" maxWidth="250px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>
        <polygon points="30,120 280,120 120,30" fill="#eff6ff" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="140" y="140" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">9 cm (Longest side)</text>
        <text x="50" y="70" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">5 cm</text>
        <text x="210" y="70" fill="#1e293b" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="middle">7 cm</text>
        
        <path d="M 102.3 47.7 A 25 25 0 0 0 141.8 42.3" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="110" y="70" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">Largest ∠</text>
      </svg>
    </div>
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the largest angle:** The largest angle is always opposite the longest side. Therefore, we want the angle opposite the $9\text{ cm}$ side. Let's call this side $a = 9$, meaning we want to find angle $A$. The other sides are $b = 5$ and $c = 7$.
    2. **Identify the rule:** We know all three sides (SSS) and need an angle. Use the rearranged Cosine Rule.
    3. **Substitute values:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \cos A &= \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} \\
       \cos A &= \frac{5^2 + 7^2 - 9^2}{2(5)(7)} \\
       \cos A &= \frac{25 + 49 - 81}{70} \\
       \cos A &= \frac{-7}{70} = -0.1
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Solve using your GDC's inverse cosine:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       A &= \cos^{-1}(-0.1) \\
       A &= 95.7^\circ \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: The Mega Bearings Problem">
    A ship leaves Port $A$ and sails $12\text{ km}$ on a bearing of $050^\circ$ to Port $B$. 
    It then sails $18\text{ km}$ from Port $B$ on a bearing of $110^\circ$ to reach Port $C$.
    
    **(a)** Calculate the distance from Port $A$ directly to Port $C$.
    **(b)** Calculate the bearing of Port $C$ from Port $A$.
    **(c)** Calculate the area of the triangle $ABC$.
    **(d)** Calculate the shortest distance from Port $B$ to the path $AC$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **First, draw a diagram and find the inside angle at B:**
    <div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
      <svg viewBox="0 0 350 250" width="100%" maxWidth="400px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '12px' }}>
        {/* North Lines */}
        <line x1="50" y1="200" x2="50" y2="50" stroke="#94a3b8" stroke-width="2" stroke-dasharray="4,4" />
        <text x="45" y="40" font-weight="bold" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="start">N</text>
        <line x1="150" y1="120" x2="150" y2="20" stroke="#94a3b8" stroke-width="2" stroke-dasharray="4,4" />
        <text x="145" y="15" font-weight="bold" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="start">N</text>
        
        {/* Triangle Sides */}
        <line x1="50" y1="200" x2="150" y2="120" stroke="#1e293b" stroke-width="3" />
        <text x="80" y="150" font-weight="bold" transform="rotate(-38 80 150)" text-anchor="middle">12 km</text>
        <line x1="150" y1="120" x2="300" y2="180" stroke="#1e293b" stroke-width="3" />
        <text x="210" y="140" font-weight="bold" transform="rotate(22 210 140)" text-anchor="middle">18 km</text>
        <line x1="50" y1="200" x2="300" y2="180" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="2" stroke-dasharray="5,5" />
        
        {/* Points */}
        <circle cx="50" cy="200" r="5" fill="#ef4444" />
        <text x="30" y="210" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">A</text>
        <circle cx="150" cy="120" r="5" fill="#ef4444" />
        <text x="145" y="105" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">B</text>
        <circle cx="300" cy="180" r="5" fill="#ef4444" />
        <text x="310" y="185" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">C</text>

        {/* Angles */}
        <path d="M 50 170 A 30 30 0 0 1 73.4 181.3" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="55" y="165" font-size="12" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">50°</text>
        
        <path d="M 150 90 A 30 30 0 0 1 177.8 131.1" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="2" />
        <text x="160" y="100" font-size="12" fill="#ef4444" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">110°</text>
      </svg>
    </div>
    *Co-interior angles mean the angle from South at $B$ back to $A$ is $50^\circ$. A full straight line North-to-South is $180^\circ$. So the interior angle $ABC = 180^\circ - 50^\circ (\text{top bit}) + (180^\circ - 110^\circ)(\text{bottom bit}) = 120^\circ$.*

    <Steps>
    1. **(a) Distance AC (Cosine Rule):**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       b^2 &= 12^2 + 18^2 - 2(12)(18)\cos(120^\circ) \\
       b^2 &= 144 + 324 - 432(-0.5) \\
       b^2 &= 468 + 216 = 684 \\
       b &= \sqrt{684} \approx 26.2\text{ km (3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **(b) Bearing of C from A (Sine Rule for angle A):**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \frac{\sin A}{18} &= \frac{\sin 120^\circ}{26.15...} \\
       \sin A &= \frac{18 \times \sin 120^\circ}{26.15...} \approx 0.596... \\
       A &= \sin^{-1}(0.596...) \approx 36.6^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$
       The overall bearing from North is $50^\circ + 36.6^\circ = \mathbf{086.6^\circ}$.
    3. **(c) Area of triangle ABC:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2}(12)(18)\sin(120^\circ) \\
       \text{Area} &= 108 \times 0.866... = \mathbf{93.5\text{ km}^2}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **(d) Shortest distance from B to AC:**
       The "shortest distance" from a point to a line is the perpendicular height ($h$).
       Use standard area: $\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       93.53... &= \frac{1}{2} \times 26.15... \times h \\
       93.53... &= 13.07... \times h \\
       h &= \frac{93.53...}{13.07...} \approx \mathbf{7.15\text{ km}}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A surveyor needs to find the distance across a wide river from point $A$ to point $B$. She measures a baseline from point $A$ to point $C$ as $50\text{ m}$. She then measures the angles from $A$ and $C$ to point $B$, finding angle $BAC = 68^\circ$ and angle $BCA = 45^\circ$. Calculate the distance $AB$ across the river to 1 decimal place.

      <svg viewBox="0 0 350 180" width="100%" maxWidth="300px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>

        {/* River Graphic */}

        <text x="35" y="165" font-weight="bold" text-anchor="start">A</text>

    <Steps>
    1. **Find the third angle:** To use the Sine Rule, we need a known pair. We have side $AC = 50$, so we need angle $B$ opposite to it.
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Angle } B &= 180^\circ - (68^\circ + 45^\circ) \\
       \text{Angle } B &= 67^\circ
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Set up the Sine Rule:** We want distance $AB$ (which is side $c$).
       $$ \frac{c}{\sin(45^\circ)} = \frac{50}{\sin(67^\circ)} $$
    3. **Solve for c:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       c &= \frac{50 \times \sin(45^\circ)}{\sin(67^\circ)} \\
       c &= \frac{35.355...}{0.920...} \\
       c &\approx 38.4\text{ m}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
