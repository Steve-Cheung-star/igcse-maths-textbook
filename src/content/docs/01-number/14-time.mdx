Time is one of the few areas in maths that doesn't operate on a base-10 or base-100 system, which makes it a frequent trap for students. You need to be confident converting between units, jumping across time zones, and reading real-world timetables.

---

## 1. Units of Time and Conversions

You must know the exact relationship between all standard units of time. For the purposes of your exams, leap years are ignored unless specifically mentioned.

* **1 minute** = 60 seconds
* **1 hour** = 60 minutes
* **1 day** = 24 hours
* **1 week** = 7 days
* **1 year** = 12 months
* **1 year** = 52 weeks
* **1 year** = 365 days

<Aside type="caution" title="Exam Tip: The Decimal Trap">
Remember from our calculator lesson: **time is not decimal!** A duration of `2.5 hours` is NOT 2 hours and 50 minutes. Because an hour is 60 minutes, `0.5` of an hour is half of 60, which is 30 minutes. Always multiply the decimal part of an hour by 60 to find the exact minutes!
</Aside>

---

## 2. The 12-Hour and 24-Hour Clock

Exam questions will frequently use the 24-hour clock, especially when dealing with timetables. 

* **12-Hour Clock:** Uses **a.m.** (morning) and **p.m.** (afternoon/evening).
* **24-Hour Clock:** Always uses **four digits** and counts continuously from midnight (`00 00`) to one minute to midnight (`23 59`).

### Conversion Rules:
* **a.m. times:** Keep the numbers the same, but ensure there are four digits. (e.g., 6.15 a.m. becomes `06 15`). *Exception: 12.xx a.m. (midnight) translates to `00 xx`.*
* **p.m. times:** Add 12 to the hours. (e.g., 3.15 p.m. becomes $3 + 12 = 15$, so it is written as `15 15`). *Exception: 12.xx p.m. (midday) stays as `12 xx`.*

<SteveTip title="Cambridge Formatting Quirk">
You are probably used to seeing digital time written with a colon (like `15:15`). However, the Cambridge syllabus officially denotes 24-hour time with a space instead of a colon, such as **`15 15`** or **`03 15`**. Get used to reading and writing it this way for your exam!
</SteveTip>

## 3. Calculating Durations and Reading Timetables

When calculating how long a journey takes, the safest method is **counting up** (or using a "time line"). Do not try to subtract times using standard column subtraction, because time operates in 60s, not 100s!

**Example:** A bus leaves at `08 45` and arrives at `11 20`. How long is the journey?
1. Jump to the next full hour: `08 45` to `09 00` is **15 minutes**.
2. Jump the full hours: `09 00` to `11 00` is **2 hours**.
3. Jump the remaining minutes: `11 00` to `11 20` is **20 minutes**.
4. Total duration: 2 hours + 15 mins + 20 mins = **2 hours 35 minutes**.

---

## 4. Time Zones, Speed, and Local Time



The world is split into different time zones. When it is morning in London, it is evening in Tokyo. Exam questions will often combine time zones with a speed calculation to make you find the flight duration first.



### Integrating Speed and Distance
To find the duration of a flight when given the distance and average speed, use the formula:
$Time = \frac{Distance}{Speed}$

This will usually give you a decimal hour (like 11.25 hours). Remember to multiply the decimal part by 60 to convert it into exact minutes before adding it to your departure time!

**The Golden Rule for Flights:**
Always calculate the arrival time in the **country of departure first**, then apply the time zone difference at the very end.

    A train departs at `14 35`. The journey takes 3 hours and 45 minutes. What time does the train arrive? Give your answer in the 24-hour clock.

    3. To make it easier, jump to the next hour first: `17 35` + 25 mins = `18 00`.
    4. You have 20 minutes left to add (45 - 25 = 20).
    5. `18 00` + 20 mins = `18 20`.
    **Answer:** `18 20`

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Time Zones">
    New York is 5 hours behind London. 
    A flight leaves New York at `21 30` local time and takes 7 hours and 10 minutes to reach London. 
    What is the local time in London when the flight arrives?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Step 1:** Calculate the arrival time in New York's local time.
    * Departure: `21 30`
    * Add 7 hours: `21 30` + 7 hours = `28 30`. 
    * Because `28 30` is past midnight (`24 00`), subtract 24. $28 - 24 = 4$. So it arrives at `04 30` the next day (New York time).
    * Add the 10 minutes: `04 30` + 10 mins = `04 40` (New York time).

    **Step 2:** Apply the time difference.
    * New York is 5 hours *behind* London, which means London is 5 hours *ahead* of New York.
    * Add 5 hours to the New York arrival time: `04 40` + 5 hours = `09 40`.
    
    **Answer:** `09 40`
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Speed and Time Zones">
    An airplane flies from London to Tokyo, a distance of 9000 km. The average speed of the plane is 800 km/h. 
    Tokyo is 9 hours ahead of London. 
    If the flight leaves London at `14 30` local time on Tuesday, what is the local time and day in Tokyo when it arrives?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Step 1: Calculate the flight duration.**
    * $Time = \frac{Distance}{Speed} = \frac{9000}{800} = 11.25$ hours.
    * Convert 0.25 hours into minutes: $0.25 \times 60 = 15$ minutes.
    * Total flight time = 11 hours 15 minutes.

    **Step 2: Find the arrival time in London's local time.**
    * Departure: `14 30`
    * Add 11 hours: `14 30` + 11 hours = `25 30`.
    * Add 15 minutes: `25 30` + 15 mins = `25 45`.
    * Subtract 24 hours to step into the next day: $25 - 24 = 1$. The plane lands at `01 45` on Wednesday (London time).

    **Step 3: Apply the time difference for Tokyo.**
    * Tokyo is 9 hours ahead, so we add 9 hours to the London arrival time.
    * `01 45` + 9 hours = `10 45`.
    
    **Answer:** `10 45` on Wednesday
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
