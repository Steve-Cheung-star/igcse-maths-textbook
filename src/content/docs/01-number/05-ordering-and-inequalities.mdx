In IGCSE Maths, you will frequently be asked to order a random mixture of fractions, decimals, percentages, and even powers. To do this successfully, you need a reliable strategy and a solid understanding of mathematical symbols.

---

## 1. The Language of Inequalities

Before you can order numbers, you need to know how to compare them mathematically. You must be completely familiar with these six symbols:

* **$=$** (Equal to): $4 = \frac{8}{2}$
* **$\neq$** (Not equal to): $5 \neq 3$
* **$>$** (Greater than): $7 > 2$
* **$<$** (Less than): $0.5 < 1$
* **$\geq$** (Greater than or equal to): $x \geq 10$ *(x is 10 or bigger)*
* **$\leq$** (Less than or equal to): $y \leq 0$ *(y is 0 or negative)*

<SteveTip title="The Banned Crocodile">
Please, I am begging you: forget the primary school "hungry crocodile" trick! Every time a student talks about a crocodile eating the bigger number, a secondary school maths teacher's hair turns grey. 

Look at the inequality symbols as **literal arrowheads** sitting on a number line:
* **$<$** points to the **left** (the direction of the smaller, negative numbers).
* **$>$** points to the **right** (the direction of the larger, positive numbers).

The pointy end *always* points exactly to where the smaller number lives on the number line. It is perfectly logical. No reptiles required!
</SteveTip>

## 2. Ordering Mixed Quantities

Examiners love to test your conversion skills (from Section 1.4) at the exact same time they test your ordering skills. You might be asked to order a list like: $\frac{3}{8}, \quad 38\%, \quad 0.3, \quad \frac{1}{3}$.

### The Foolproof 3-Step Strategy:
1. **Convert Everything to Decimals:** Decimals are by far the easiest format to compare.
2. **Pad with Zeroes:** Make sure every decimal has the same number of decimal places. This stops your brain from thinking $0.3$ is smaller than $0.15$ just because $3$ is smaller than $15$.
3. **Sort and Revert:** Order the numbers, but write your final answer using the **original forms** given in the question.

<SteveTip title="The 'Original Form' Trap">
This is where students lose the final mark! If the question asks you to order $\frac{1}{2}$ and $0.4$, and you write "$0.4, 0.5$" on the answer line, you will lose a mark. You **must** write "$0.4, \frac{1}{2}$". The examiner wants to see the numbers exactly as they were printed in the question.
</SteveTip>

## 3. The Negative Number Trap

When dealing with negative numbers, your brain has to work in reverse. The "bigger" a negative number looks, the smaller its actual value is. This is because it is further to the left on the number line.



$$ \xleftarrow{\text{ Smaller }} \quad -3 \quad -2 \quad -1 \quad 0 \quad 1 \quad 2 \quad 3 \quad \xrightarrow{\text{ Larger }} $$

### Visualising an Example
Let's place these four numbers on a number line to see how they relate:  
**$-2.5 \quad \dots \quad -0.5 \quad \dots \quad 1.5 \quad \dots \quad -1.2$**

If we map their approximate positions:

$$ \xleftarrow{} \quad -3 \quad \underset{\mathbf{-2.5}}{\mathbf{\uparrow}} \quad -2 \quad \underset{\mathbf{-1.2}}{\mathbf{\uparrow}} \quad -1 \quad \underset{\mathbf{-0.5}}{\mathbf{\uparrow}} \quad 0 \quad 1 \quad \underset{\mathbf{1.5}}{\mathbf{\uparrow}} \quad 2 \quad \xrightarrow{} $$

By reading the number line directly from **left to right**, we automatically have our **ascending order** (smallest to largest):
$$-2.5, \quad -1.2, \quad -0.5, \quad 1.5$$

And we can use our inequality arrows to state that:
$$-2.5 < -1.2 < -0.5 < 1.5$$

## 4. Practice Problems

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Using Symbols">
    Insert the correct symbol ($<, >,$ or $=$) between these pairs of numbers:
    1. $-4 \quad \dots \quad -7$
    2. $0.6 \quad \dots \quad \frac{3}{5}$
    3. $2^3 \quad \dots \quad 3^2$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    1. $-4 > -7$ *(Negative 4 is further to the right on the number line than negative 7).*
    2. $0.6 = \frac{3}{5}$ *($\frac{3}{5}$ converts exactly to 0.6).*
    3. $2^3 < 3^2$ *(Because $8 < 9$).*
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Ordering Mixed Formats">
    Write the following quantities in **ascending order** (smallest to largest):
    $$0.35, \quad \frac{3}{8}, \quad 30\%, \quad \frac{1}{3}$$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Step 1: Convert all to decimals.**
    * $0.35 \rightarrow 0.35$
    * $\frac{3}{8} \rightarrow 3 \div 8 = 0.375$
    * $30\% \rightarrow 0.3$
    * $\frac{1}{3} \rightarrow 0.333...$

    **Step 2: Pad with zeroes (to 3 decimal places).**
    * $0.350$
    * $0.375$
    * $0.300$
    * $0.333$

    **Step 3: Compare and revert to original forms.**
    Smallest is $0.300$, then $0.333$, then $0.350$, then $0.375$.
    
    **Final Answer:** $30\%, \quad \frac{1}{3}, \quad 0.35, \quad \frac{3}{8}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Ordering with Negatives (Extended)">
    Write the following in **descending order** (largest to smallest):
    $$-0.5, \quad -\frac{3}{4}, \quad -0.09, \quad -51\%$$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Step 1: Convert to decimals.**
    * $-0.5 \rightarrow -0.50$
    * $-\frac{3}{4} \rightarrow -0.75$
    * $-0.09 \rightarrow -0.09$
    * $-51\% \rightarrow -0.51$

    **Step 2: Sort from largest (closest to zero, furthest right) to smallest (most negative, furthest left).**
    Largest is $-0.09$, then $-0.50$, then $-0.51$, then $-0.75$.
    
    **Final Answer:** $-0.09, \quad -0.5, \quad -51\%, \quad -\frac{3}{4}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
