A **rate** is a way of comparing two different quantities (like distance and time, or money and hours). In mathematics, the word "per" almost always means **divide**.

---

## 1. Common Measures of Rate

A rate tells you how much of one thing happens for every one unit of another. The units themselves usually tell you the formula you need to use.

| Rate Type | Common Units | Calculation Logic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hourly Pay** | £/hr | Total Pay $\div$ Hours worked |
| **Fuel Consumption** | km/L | Distance $\div$ Fuel used |
| **Flow Rate** | L/sec | Volume $\div$ Time |
| **Density** | g/cm³ | Mass $\div$ Volume |

<Aside type="note" title="Reading the Units">
The forward slash ($/$) in units like **m/s** is literally a division sign. It tells you to take the **metres** and divide them by the **seconds**. If you forget a formula in the exam, just look at the units in the question!
</Aside>

---

## 2. Speed, Distance, and Time

Speed is the rate at which distance changes over time. You must be able to rearrange the relationship between these three variables.



### The Core Formulae
* $\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$
* $\text{Distance} = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$
* $\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$

### The $m/s$ to $km/h$ Shortcut
In the exam, you often need to convert between these two. Instead of doing three separate conversions (metres to km, seconds to mins, mins to hours), use the **3.6 Rule**:

* **To go from $m/s \rightarrow km/h$:** Multiply by **$3.6$**.
* **To go from $km/h \rightarrow m/s$:** Divide by **$3.6$**.

> **Why 3.6?** There are $3600$ seconds in an hour and $1000$ metres in a km. $\frac{3600}{1000} = 3.6$.

---

## 3. The "Time Trap" (Minutes vs. Decimals)

This is the #1 place students lose marks. You **cannot** calculate with minutes directly in a speed formula.

### Entering the Formula (Minutes $\rightarrow$ Decimal)
If the question gives you $2\text{ hours } 15\text{ mins}$, you must convert to a decimal before multiplying or dividing:
$$ 15 \div 60 = 0.25 \rightarrow \mathbf{2.25 \text{ hours}} $$

### Exiting the Formula (Decimal $\rightarrow$ Minutes)
If your calculation gives you a time like $4.3\text{ hours}$, this is **NOT** 4 hours and 3 minutes.
1. **The "Whole" is hours:** $4\text{ hours}$.
2. **The decimal is minutes:** Multiply the decimal part by 60.
3. $0.3 \times 60 = 18\text{ minutes}$.
**Answer:** $4\text{ hours and } 18\text{ minutes}$.



<SteveTip title="The 0.45 Illusion">
Students often write $1.45$ for $1\text{ hour } 45\text{ mins}$. This is a guaranteed way to lose the method mark. Always ask: "Is 45 minutes nearly an hour?" Yes. So the decimal must be $0.75$. If you find yourself writing the minutes exactly as the decimal, stop and divide by 60!
</SteveTip>

## 4. Time Zones and Flight Times

Examiners love to give you a flight that leaves at one local time and arrives at another. To solve these, always find the **Duration** in a neutral space.

**Example: A flight leaves London (GMT) at 10:00 and arrives in New York (GMT-5) at 13:00. How long was the flight?**

1.  **Bring to same Time Zone:** When it is 13:00 in New York, what time is it in London? New York is 5 hours behind, so London is 18:00.
2.  **Calculate Difference:** $18:00 - 10:00 = 8\text{ hours}$.
**Answer:** $8\text{ hours}$.

## 5. Speed Equations & Algebra

### Linear Speed Equations
Sometimes you are told a journey takes $20\text{ minutes}$ longer if the speed is reduced by $10\text{ km/h}$. You set this up by making the **Time** the subject:
$$ \text{Time}_1 - \text{Time}_2 = \frac{20}{60} $$

### Quadratic Speed Equations (Sneak Peek)
In harder Paper 4 questions, you might see something like: *"A car travels $100\text{ km}$. If the speed increases by $x$, the time taken decreases by $1\text{ hour}$."*

$$ \frac{100}{v} - \frac{100}{v+x} = 1 $$

Multiplying this out leads to a **Quadratic Equation**.
<Aside type="note" title="Coming Soon">
We will cover how to solve these equations in **Chapter 2: Algebra**. For now, focus on being able to write the initial fraction correctly!
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="The Difference Equation">
When setting up time difference equations, always do:
**[Slow Time] - [Fast Time] = [Time Difference]**
Since the slower speed takes *more* time, it must come first to get a positive result.
</SteveTip>

### Part B: Time Zones & Flights
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3">
    A flight leaves Dubai (GMT+4) at 08:30. The flight takes $7\text{ hours } 15\text{ minutes}$. If London is GMT, what is the local time of arrival?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    1. Departure in GMT: $08:30 - 4\text{ hours} = 04:30\text{ GMT}$.
    2. Add Duration: $04:30 + 7\text{ hrs } 15\text{ mins} = 11:45\text{ GMT}$.
    3. London is GMT: So local time is $11:45$.
    **Answer:** $11:45$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Return Flight">
    A plane flies from Singapore (GMT+8) to Sydney (GMT+11). Departure is 23:00 local time. Arrival is 09:30 local time the next day. How long was the flight?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    1. Convert Arrival to Singapore Time: $09:30 - 3\text{ hours} = 06:30$.
    2. Duration from 23:00 to 06:30: $1\text{ hour to midnight} + 6.5\text{ hours} = 7.5\text{ hours}$.
    **Answer:** $7\text{ hours } 30\text{ minutes}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Part C: Setting up Algebra Equations
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Linear Time Difference">
    A journey of $15\text{ km}$ takes $20\text{ minutes}$. Find the speed in **km/h**.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    1. Convert minutes to decimal: $20/60 = 1/3$.
    2. Speed = $15 \div (1/3) = 45$.
    **Answer:** $45\text{ km/h}$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: The Quadratic Setup">
    A car travels $200\text{ km}$ at an average speed of $x\text{ km/h}$. If the speed was $(x+10)\text{ km/h}$, the journey would take 1 hour less. Show that $x^2 + 10x - 2000 = 0$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    1. $\text{Time}_1 = \frac{200}{x}$, $\text{Time}_2 = \frac{200}{x+10}$.
    2. $\frac{200}{x} - \frac{200}{x+10} = 1$.
    3. Multiply by $x(x+10)$: $200(x+10) - 200x = x(x+10)$.
    4. $200x + 2000 - 200x = x^2 + 10x \rightarrow 2000 = x^2 + 10x$.
    **Result:** $x^2 + 10x - 2000 = 0$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Part D: Rates and Pay
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Annual Pay">
    An apprentice earns £8.50 per hour. They work 38 hours a week. Calculate their annual pay (52 weeks).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    1. Weekly pay: $8.50 \times 38 = £323$.
    2. Annual pay: $323 \times 52 = £16,796$.
    **Answer:** £16,796
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Flow Rate (Volume)">
    A tap fills a $500\text{ ml}$ jug in 4 seconds. How long will it take to fill a $12\text{ litre}$ bucket?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    1. Flow rate: $500 \div 4 = 125\text{ ml/sec}$.
    2. Volume: $12\text{ Litres} = 12,000\text{ ml}$.
    3. Time: $12,000 \div 125 = 96\text{ seconds}$.
    **Answer:** $1\text{ minute } 36\text{ seconds}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
