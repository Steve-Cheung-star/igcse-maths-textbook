Once raw data has been collected, it is often a chaotic list of numbers. To make sense of this information, we must organise and present it visually. The way we present data depends on whether it is discrete or continuous, and what specific characteristics (like the median, spread, or shape) we want to highlight.

This section covers the standard methods for visually summarising continuous data, reading percentiles from graphs, and comparing different data sets.

---

## 1. Frequency Distributions and Histograms

When dealing with large amounts of continuous data (or discrete data with a wide range), we group the data into **class intervals** to create a grouped frequency table. 

<Aside type="note" title="Class Intervals and Inequalities">
In IB Mathematics, class intervals for continuous data are given as inequalities **without gaps**. 
For example, if we are measuring the time ($t$ in minutes) students take to complete a test:
* $0 \le t < 10$
* $10 \le t < 20$
* $20 \le t < 30$

A student taking exactly 10 minutes falls into the second interval, not the first, because of the strict less-than sign ($<$) on the upper boundary of the first interval.
</Aside>

### Histograms

A histogram is the standard visual representation for grouped continuous data. Unlike a bar chart (which is used for categorical data and has gaps between the bars), a histogram has no gaps, representing the continuous flow of the number line.

**Crucial Rule for AI SL:** You are only required to work with frequency histograms that have **equal class intervals**. You do not need to calculate frequency density. The height of the bar directly represents the frequency.

## 2. Cumulative Frequency

Cumulative frequency is a running total of the frequencies. It tells us how many data points are *less than or equal to* a certain value. 

To plot a cumulative frequency graph:
1. Calculate the running total of frequencies.
2. Plot points using the **upper boundary** of each class interval on the x-axis, and the cumulative frequency on the y-axis.
3. Connect the points with a smooth curve (this often forms a characteristic elongated 'S' shape called an ogive).

<Aside type="tip" title="Using the Graph to Find Key Statistics">
The cumulative frequency graph is powerful because it allows you to estimate percentiles and quartiles without complex formulas. Simply find the target value on the y-axis (total frequency), draw a horizontal line to the curve, and drop a vertical line to read the estimate on the x-axis.
* **Median ($Q_2$):** Find $50\%$ of the total frequency.
* **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** Find $25\%$ of the total frequency.
* **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** Find $75\%$ of the total frequency.
* **$k$th Percentile:** Find $k\%$ of the total frequency.
</Aside>

The interquartile range measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data, making it resistant to extreme outliers. The range measures the total spread of the data set.

<FormulaBox title="Interquartile Range (IQR)">
$$
\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1
$$
</FormulaBox>

$$
\text{Range} = \text{Maximum Value} - \text{Minimum Value}
$$

## 3. Box and Whisker Diagrams

A box and whisker diagram (often simply called a box plot) is a standardised way of displaying the distribution of data based on a five-number summary: **Minimum, Lower Quartile ($Q_1$), Median ($Q_2$), Upper Quartile ($Q_3$), and Maximum.**

<Aside type="caution" title="Marking Outliers">
In the IB, any data point mathematically identified as an outlier (greater than $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ or less than $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$) **must be indicated with a cross ($\times$)**. 
The "whisker" then only extends to the lowest/highest data value that is *not* an outlier.
</Aside>

### Comparing Distributions and Determining Normalcy

Box plots are exceptionally useful for comparing two different datasets on the same scale. You can immediately compare their centres (using the median) and their spread (using the IQR or range).

Furthermore, you can evaluate the symmetry of the box plot to determine if the underlying data might be normally distributed:
* **Symmetrical:** The median is exactly in the centre of the box, and the whiskers are of equal length. This suggests the data *may be normally distributed*.
* **Positively Skewed (Right Skewed):** The right whisker is noticeably longer, and the median is closer to the left of the box. The data "tails off" to the right.
* **Negatively Skewed (Left Skewed):** The left whisker is noticeably longer, and the median is closer to the right of the box. The data "tails off" to the left.

           $$
           
      </Steps>

    **b)**
      <Steps>

           Since $116 > 115$, Haile's score is mathematically an **outlier**.
      </Steps>
