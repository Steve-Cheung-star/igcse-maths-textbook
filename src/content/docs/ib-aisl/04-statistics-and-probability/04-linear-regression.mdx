In previous chapters, we looked at single variables (like the heights of a group of students) in isolation. But the real power of statistics comes from exploring relationships between *two* variables. Does an increase in hours spent studying lead to higher exam scores? Does a country's GDP affect its life expectancy? 

When we measure two variables for each individual in a dataset, we call it **bivariate data**.

---

## 1. Scatter Diagrams and Correlation

The first step in analysing bivariate data is always to plot it on a **scatter diagram**. This allows us to visually inspect the relationship between the independent variable ($x$, plotted on the horizontal axis) and the dependent variable ($y$, plotted on the vertical axis).

We describe the relationship (or **correlation**) in terms of:
1. **Direction:** Positive (as $x$ increases, $y$ increases) or Negative (as $x$ increases, $y$ decreases).
2. **Strength:** Strong (points tightly clustered around a line), Weak (points loosely scattered but showing a trend), or No Correlation (random cloud of points).

## 2. Pearson's Product-Moment Correlation Coefficient ($r$)

While describing a scatter graph by eye is useful, we need a mathematical way to measure the exact strength of the linear relationship. We use **Pearson's Product-Moment Correlation Coefficient ($r$)**. 

The value of $r$ always falls within a specific range:

$$
-1 \le r \le 1
$$

<Aside type="tip" title="Interpreting r values">
* **$r = 1$**: Perfect positive linear correlation.
* **$0.75 \le r < 1$**: Strong positive correlation.
* **$0.5 \le r < 0.75$**: Moderate positive correlation.
* **$0 \le r < 0.5$**: Weak positive correlation.
* **$r = 0$**: No linear correlation.
* **Negative values** follow the exact same strength bands, just indicating a negative direction (downwards).
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire: Calculating r">
Never calculate $r$ by hand in the exam.
1. Enter your $x$ and $y$ data into a `Lists & Spreadsheet` page. Name the columns.
2. Press `Menu > Statistics > Stat Calculations > Linear Regression (mx+b)`.
3. The value for `r` will be displayed in the output. (If you don't see $r$, you may need to turn on Stat Diagnostics in your calculator settings).
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception: Non-Linear Data">
Pearson’s $r$ ONLY measures the strength of a **linear** (straight-line) relationship. If your data forms a perfect U-shape (a parabola), the $r$ value will be very close to $0$. This does not mean there is no relationship; it just means there is no *linear* relationship. Always look at the scatter diagram first!
</Aside>

## 3. Correlation vs. Causation

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge: Discovering Causes">
**Aim 8:** The correlation between smoking and lung cancer was initially “discovered” using mathematics and statistics. However, showing that two variables move together does not prove that one causes the other. Science had to step in with biological evidence to justify the physical cause.
</Aside>

In statistics, **correlation does not imply causation**. Just because two variables have a high $r$ value does not mean changes in $x$ cause changes in $y$. There could be a coincidence, or a third **lurking variable** causing both to change (e.g., ice cream sales and shark attacks are highly correlated, but only because both are caused by the lurking variable: hot summer weather).

## 4. The Line of Best Fit and Mean Point

A line of best fit can be drawn "by eye" to model the trend of the data. However, to earn marks for a by-eye line, it **must** pass exactly through the **mean point**.

<Steps>
1. Calculate the mean of the $x$-values ($\bar{x}$).
2. Calculate the mean of the $y$-values ($\bar{y}$).
3. Plot the coordinate $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ on your scatter diagram.
4. Draw a straight line using a ruler that balances the points on either side, ensuring it passes directly through the mean point.
</Steps>

## 5. Linear Regression ($y$ on $x$)

To make predictions, we need the exact equation of the line of best fit, known as the **linear regression line of $y$ on $x$**.

The equation is written in the form:

$$
y = ax + b
$$

The parameters $a$ and $b$ have critical real-world interpretations:
* **$a$ (Gradient):** The rate of change. It represents the predicted change in $y$ for every $1$ unit increase in $x$.
* **$b$ ($y$-intercept):** The predicted value of $y$ when $x = 0$.

<SteveTip title="Interpreting 'a' and 'b' in the Exam">
When asked to "interpret the meaning of parameter $a$", do not just write "it is the gradient". You must use the context of the question. 
*Example:* If $y$ is crop yield (kg) and $x$ is fertiliser (grams), and $a = 2.5$, write: "For every additional gram of fertiliser, the predicted crop yield increases by 2.5 kg."
</SteveTip>

---

## 6. Prediction and Extrapolation

We use the regression equation to predict a $y$-value for a given $x$-value. 

* **Interpolation:** Predicting a $y$-value for an $x$-value that falls **within** the original range of data. This is generally reliable.
* **Extrapolation:** Predicting a $y$-value for an $x$-value that falls **outside** the original range of data. 

<Aside type="caution" title="The Dangers of Extrapolation">
Extrapolating is highly dangerous because we have no evidence that the linear trend continues outside our measured data. A plant might grow linearly with temperature for a while, but eventually, if it gets too hot, it will wilt and the model breaks down. Always state that extrapolations are **unreliable**.
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rule of Prediction">
You can plug an $x$-value into the $y = ax + b$ line to predict $y$. **However, you cannot rearrange this equation to reliably predict $x$ from $y$.** The IB syllabus explicitly states you cannot reliably make a prediction of $x$ from a value of $y$ using a $y$ on $x$ line (because mathematical distance is minimised vertically, not horizontally). If a question asks you to do this, write down that the estimate is unreliable.
</SteveTip>

    A fitness coach records the number of weeks a client has been training ($x$) and their maximum deadlift weight ($y$, in kg). The data is recorded from week 2 to week 12.

    The coach uses the equation to make two predictions:
    * **Prediction A:** Client's deadlift at week 8.
    * **Prediction B:** Client's deadlift at week 52 (one year later).
    
    Classify each prediction as interpolation or extrapolation, and state which is more mathematically reliable.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">

    * **Prediction B (Week 52):** This is **extrapolation** because 52 is far outside the measured data range.
    
    **Prediction A is much more mathematically reliable.** A person's strength cannot increase linearly forever; they will eventually hit a biological plateau, rendering Prediction B invalid.
