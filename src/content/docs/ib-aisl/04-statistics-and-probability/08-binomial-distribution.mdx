While discrete random variables can have wildly different and unique probability distributions, many real-world scenarios follow a very specific, predictable pattern. When an experiment consists of a fixed number of repeated, independent trials, and each trial has only two possible outcomes (success or failure), we can model it using the **binomial distribution**. 

From quality control in manufacturing (is the product defective or not?) to medical testing (does the patient have the antibody or not?), the binomial distribution is one of the most powerful and frequently used tools in applied mathematics.

---

## 1. Situations Appropriate for a Binomial Model

Before calculating any probabilities, you must verify that the scenario actually fits the binomial model. You can memorise the four strict criteria using the acronym **B.I.N.S.**

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of a Binomial Model (B.I.N.S.)">
An experiment can be modelled using a binomial distribution if and only if it satisfies all four of these conditions:
* **B - Binary Outcomes:** There are only two possible outcomes for each trial, usually categorised as "Success" and "Failure".
* **I - Independent Trials:** The outcome of one trial has zero effect on the outcome of any other trial.
* **N - Number of Trials is Fixed:** The experiment is repeated a set, predetermined number of times (denoted as $n$).
* **S - Same Probability:** The probability of a "Success" (denoted as $p$) remains constant for every single trial.
</Aside>

If a random variable $X$ follows a binomial distribution, we write this mathematically as:

$$
X \sim \text{B}(n, p)
$$

Where $n$ is the number of trials and $p$ is the probability of success in a single trial. The probability of failure is often denoted as $q$, where $q = 1 - p$.

## 2. Calculating Probabilities with Technology

In the IB Mathematics AI SL course, you are **not required** to use the complex binomial algebraic formula (or formal proofs) to calculate probabilities. Instead, you are expected to efficiently use your Graphics Display Calculator (GDC). 

<Aside type="caution" title="The Inequality Trap">
Because the binomial distribution is **discrete** (you cannot have 3.5 successes), inequalities must be handled very carefully before typing them into your GDC. Your calculator needs inclusive lower and upper bounds.

* $P(X < 4)$ means $0, 1, 2, 3$. Your bounds are lower = $0$, upper = $3$.
* $P(X > 4)$ means $5, 6, 7 \dots n$. Your bounds are lower = $5$, upper = $n$.
* $P(X \le 4)$ means $0, 1, 2, 3, 4$. Your bounds are lower = $0$, upper = $4$.
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire: binomPdf vs binomCdf">
You must know the difference between the two binomial functions on your calculator (`Menu > 5: Probability > 5: Distributions`):
* **`binomPdf(n, p, x)`**: Probability Density Function. Use this when you need an **exact** number of successes. 
    * *Example:* "Find the probability of getting *exactly* 4 heads." $\implies P(X=4)$
* **`binomCdf(n, p, lower, upper)`**: Cumulative Distribution Function. Use this for **inequalities** or a range of successes.
    * *Example:* "Find the probability of getting *between* 2 and 5 successes." $\implies P(2 \le X \le 5)$
</SteveTip>

## 3. Mean and Variance of the Binomial Distribution

Because the binomial distribution is perfectly structured and predictable, we do not need to construct a massive table to find its expected value (mean) or its variance. We can calculate them directly from the parameters $n$ and $p$.

<FormulaBox title="Mean and Variance of a Binomial Distribution">
For a random variable $X \sim \text{B}(n, p)$:

**Mean (Expected Value):**
$$
\text{E}(X) = np
$$

**Variance:**
$$
\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)
$$
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="tip" title="Expected Number of Occurrences">
The expected value $\text{E}(X) = np$ tells you how many "successes" you should expect to see on average. For example, if a machine produces defective parts 5% of the time ($p=0.05$), and you check 200 parts ($n=200$), you expect $\text{E}(X) = 200 \times 0.05 = 10$ defective parts. This links directly to your prior knowledge of expected occurrences from SL 4.5!
</Aside>

## 4. Theory of Knowledge & International-Mindedness

<Aside type="note" title="Who really invented Pascal's Triangle?">
The coefficients used in the algebraic expansion of binomials (which form the foundation of binomial probability proofs) are famously arranged in a pattern known globally as **Pascal's Triangle**, named after the 17th-century French mathematician Blaise Pascal.

However, this mathematical discovery is heavily misattributed (Aim 8). The exact same triangle of numbers was documented by the Chinese mathematician **Yang Hui** nearly 400 years earlier in the 13th century, and by the Persian mathematician **Omar Khayyam** even earlier than that. This highlights how Eurocentric history has often shaped the naming conventions in modern mathematics, reminding us of the importance of International-Mindedness in acknowledging global contributions to human knowledge.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Evaluating Mathematical Models (TOK)">
**What criteria can we use to decide between different models?** A binomial distribution assumes probabilities remain perfectly constant and trials are completely independent. When modelling a penalty shootout in football using the binomial model, we ignore the psychological pressure on a player after a missed shot (meaning trials are *not* truly independent). A mathematical model is only ever an approximation of reality. As statisticians, we must critically decide whether a model simplifies reality usefully, or distorts it dangerously.
</Aside>

    $$
    P(X \le 8) = \text{binomCdf}(15, 0.65, 0, 8) \approx 0.164 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
    
    **d)** "More than 11" means $X > 11$. Because it is discrete, this means $12, 13, 14, 15$. 
    Use `binomCdf` with lower = 12, upper = 15:
    
    $$
    P(X \ge 12) = \text{binomCdf}(15, 0.65, 12, 15) \approx 0.173 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>

    A seed company guarantees that 85% of their tomato seeds will germinate. A gardener plants a packet of 40 seeds. Assume that the germination of each seed is independent. Let $X$ be the number of seeds that germinate.

    **a)** Calculate the expected number of seeds that germinate.

    $$
    
    $$
    \text{Var}(X) = 34 \times 0.15 = 5.1
    $$
    
    **c)** We need the probability of getting exactly 34 germinations.
    
    $$
    P(X = 34) = \text{binomPdf}(40, 0.85, 34) \approx 0.160 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Working Backwards from Mean and Variance">
    A random variable $X$ is binomially distributed such that $X \sim \text{B}(n, p)$.
    It is given that the mean is $4.8$ and the variance is $1.92$.

    **a)** Find the probability of success, $p$.
    
    **b)** Find the number of trials, $n$.
    
    **c)** Hence, calculate $P(X \ge 3)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** We are given a system of equations from the formulae:
    1) $np = 4.8$
    2) $np(1-p) = 1.92$
    
    Substitute the value of $np$ from equation (1) into equation (2):
    
    $$
    4.8(1 - p) = 1.92
    $$
    
    $$
    1 - p = \frac{1.92}{4.8}
    $$
    
    $$
    1 - p = 0.4
    $$
    
    $$
    p = 0.6
    $$
    
    **b)** Now substitute $p = 0.6$ back into equation (1):
    
    $$
    n(0.6) = 4.8
    $$
    
    $$
    n = \frac{4.8}{0.6}
    $$
    
    $$
    n = 8
    $$
    
    **c)** The distribution is $X \sim \text{B}(8, 0.6)$. We need $P(X \ge 3)$.
    Using the GDC (`binomCdf` with lower = 3, upper = 8):
    
    $$
    P(X \ge 3) = \text{binomCdf}(8, 0.6, 3, 8) \approx 0.950 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: The 'At Least One' Strategy (Finding n)">
    A student plays a mobile game where the probability of winning a rare item in a single attempt is $0.05$. The attempts are independent.

    **a)** Write down an expression for the probability that the student does not win a rare item in $n$ attempts.
    
    **b)** Determine the minimum number of attempts required so that the probability of winning *at least one* rare item is greater than $0.99$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** If $P(\text{Win}) = 0.05$, then $P(\text{Lose}) = 1 - 0.05 = 0.95$.
    The probability of losing $n$ times in a row is simply the loss probability raised to the power of $n$:
    
    $$
    P(X = 0) = (0.95)^n
    $$
    
    **b)** "At least one" is the complement of "none". 
    
    $$
    P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(X = 0)
    $$
    
    We want this probability to be greater than $0.99$:
    
    $$
    1 - (0.95)^n > 0.99
    $$
    
    Rearrange the inequality:
    
    $$
    0.01 > (0.95)^n
    $$
    
    <SteveTip title="Solving Exponential Inequalities">
    For IB AI SL, you can solve $0.95^n = 0.01$ using `nSolve(0.95^n = 0.01, n)` on your GDC. Alternatively, type the function $f(x) = 0.95^x$ into the graphing screen and use the table feature to see when the value drops below $0.01$.
    </SteveTip>
    
    Solving the equation yields:
    
    $$
    n \approx 89.78
    $$
    
    Since $n$ must be an integer, the minimum number of attempts required is $n = 90$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Conditional Binomial Probability">
    A fair coin is flipped 10 times. Let $X$ be the number of Heads obtained.
    
    **a)** Find the probability of obtaining at least 4 Heads.
    
    **b)** Given that at least 4 Heads were flipped, find the probability that exactly 6 Heads were flipped.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)** The coin is fair, so $X \sim \text{B}(10, 0.5)$. We need $P(X \ge 4)$.
    
    $$
    P(X \ge 4) = \text{binomCdf}(10, 0.5, 4, 10) \approx 0.828 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
    
    **b)** This is a conditional probability problem: $P(X=6 \mid X \ge 4)$.
    Using the conditional probability formula $P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$:
    
    The intersection of "getting exactly 6 Heads" and "getting at least 4 Heads" is simply "getting exactly 6 Heads".
    
    $$
    P(X=6 \mid X \ge 4) = \frac{P(X=6)}{P(X \ge 4)}
    $$
    
    Calculate the numerator $P(X=6)$:
    
    $$
    P(X=6) = \text{binomPdf}(10, 0.5, 6) = 0.205078...
    $$
    
    Now divide by the unrounded denominator from part a) ($0.828125$):
    
    $$
    P(X=6 \mid X \ge 4) = \frac{0.205078...}{0.828125} \approx 0.248 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Finding an Unknown Probability from an Equation">
    A random variable $Y \sim \text{B}(12, p)$. 
    It is given that the probability of zero successes is $0.05$.
    
    **a)** Write down an algebraic equation for $p$.
    
    **b)** Hence, solve for $p$.
    
    **c)** Calculate $\text{Var}(Y)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)** The probability of zero successes is the equivalent of getting 12 consecutive failures. Since the probability of failure is $(1-p)$, we can write:
    
    $$
    (1 - p)^{12} = 0.05
    $$
    
    **b)** To solve for $p$, take the 12th root of both sides (or use `nSolve` on your GDC):
    
    $$
    1 - p = \sqrt[12]{0.05}
    $$
    
    $$
    1 - p \approx 0.779
    $$
    
    $$
    p \approx 0.221 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
    
    **c)** Use the variance formula $\text{Var}(Y) = np(1-p)$ with the unrounded value of $p$:
    
    $$
    \text{Var}(Y) = 12 \times 0.221 \times (1 - 0.221)
    $$
    
    $$
    \text{Var}(Y) \approx 2.07 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>
