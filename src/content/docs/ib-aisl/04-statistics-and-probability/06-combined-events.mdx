Probability is the mathematical language of uncertainty. Whether you are predicting the weather, analysing risk in finance, or modelling the outcomes of a medical trial, understanding how different events interact is essential. In this chapter, we will formalise the rules of probability and explore powerful visual tools to solve complex problems without getting lost in abstract algebra.

## 1. Visualising Probability

Before relying entirely on formulae, it is often much easier to solve probability problems using visual representations of the sample space (the set of all possible outcomes). The IB strongly encourages solving problems with the aid of diagrams.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Probability Diagrams">
* **Venn Diagrams:** Ideal for showing overlapping events (combined events, intersections, and mutually exclusive events) and calculating conditional probability.
* **Tree Diagrams:** The best tool for sequential events, especially when dealing with probabilities "with and without replacement".
* **Sample Space Diagrams & Tables:** Useful for displaying all equally likely combinations of two simple events (e.g., rolling two dice or flipping a coin and spinning a spinner).
</Aside>

## 2. Combined Events and the Addition Rule

When we want to find the probability of Event A **or** Event B occurring, we are looking at **combined events** (the union, denoted as $A \cup B$).

<Aside type="caution" title="The Non-exclusivity of 'Or'">
In everyday language, "or" sometimes means "one or the other, but not both" (e.g., "Would you like tea or coffee?"). In mathematics, "or" is **inclusive**. $P(A \cup B)$ means Event A occurs, Event B occurs, **or both occur**. 
</Aside>

If we simply add the probability of A to the probability of B, we accidentally count the overlapping intersection ($A \cap B$) twice. Therefore, we must subtract the intersection once.

<FormulaBox title="Combined events">
$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

### Example 1: Using the Addition Rule (Visualised)

In a class of 40 students, 24 play football ($F$), 18 play basketball ($B$), and 8 play both sports. A student is chosen at random. Find the probability that they play football or basketball.

Notice how the 24 Football players are split into "16 who only play Football" and "8 who play both". If we just added 24 and 18, we would count the 8 overlapping students twice!

<Steps>

  1. **Identify the known probabilities:** $P(F) = \frac{24}{40}$
     $P(B) = \frac{18}{40}$
     $P(F \cap B) = \frac{8}{40}$
  2. **Apply the Combined Events formula:**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P(F \cup B) &= P(F) + P(B) - P(F \cap B) \\
     P(F \cup B) &= \frac{24}{40} + \frac{18}{40} - \frac{8}{40}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. **Calculate the final probability:**
     $$ P(F \cup B) = \frac{34}{40} = \frac{17}{20} $$

</Steps>

### Example 2: Solving for $x$ in a Venn Diagram

Sometimes the IB will give you the total but hide the intersection, forcing you to use algebra.

A group of 30 tourists were asked about their travel history. 15 have visited France ($F$) and 12 have visited Spain ($S$). 5 tourists have visited neither country. If $x$ tourists have visited **both** countries, find the value of $x$.

<Steps>

  1. **Set up expressions for the regions:** Let the intersection $F \cap S = x$.
     Those who visited *only* France = $15 - x$.
     Those who visited *only* Spain = $12 - x$.
     Those who visited neither = $5$.
  2. **Create a linear equation:** The sum of all regions must equal the universal total (30).
     $$ (15 - x) + x + (12 - x) + 5 = 30 $$
  3. **Simplify and solve for $x$:**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     32 - x &= 30 \\
     x &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. **Interpret the result:** 2 tourists have visited both France and Spain.

</Steps>

## 3. Mutually Exclusive Events

If two events cannot possibly happen at the same time, they are called **mutually exclusive**. For example, rolling a standard die and getting a '2' and a '5' on the same roll is impossible. 

Because they cannot happen simultaneously, their intersection is zero ($P(A \cap B) = 0$). This simplifies the combined events formula.

<FormulaBox title="Mutually exclusive events">
$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

### Example 3: Mutually Exclusive Events

A single card is drawn from a standard deck of 52 cards. What is the probability of drawing a Heart or a Spade?

<Steps>

  1. **Check for mutual exclusivity:** A card cannot be both a Heart and a Spade simultaneously. Therefore, $P(\text{Heart} \cap \text{Spade}) = 0$.
  2. **Identify individual probabilities:**
     $P(\text{Heart}) = \frac{13}{52}$
     $P(\text{Spade}) = \frac{13}{52}$
  3. **Add the probabilities together:**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P(\text{Heart} \cup \text{Spade}) &= \frac{13}{52} + \frac{13}{52} \\
     P(\text{Heart} \cup \text{Spade}) &= \frac{26}{52} = \frac{1}{2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$

</Steps>

## 4. Conditional Probability & Restricted Domains

Often, the probability of an event changes if you know that another event has already happened. This is called **conditional probability**. The notation $P(A|B)$ is read as "the probability of A, given that B has occurred".

<Aside type="tip" title="The Restricted Sample Space">
When calculating $P(A|B)$, you are essentially ignoring the entire universe $U$ and **restricting your sample space solely to Event B**. The "new total" is $P(B)$, and the "successful outcomes" are the parts of A that live entirely within B (which is $A \cap B$).
</Aside>

<FormulaBox title="Conditional probability">
$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$
</FormulaBox>

Using our sports class from Example 1 (40 students total, 24 play football, 18 play basketball, 8 play both). A student is chosen at random. Given that the student plays basketball, what is the probability they also play football?

Notice how the universe shrinks. We only care about the 18 students inside the Basketball circle now!

<Steps>

  1. **Identify the "given" condition (the new denominator):** We are *given* that the student plays basketball. 
     $P(B) = \frac{18}{40}$
  2. **Identify the intersection (the numerator):** The students who play football *and* basketball.
     $P(F \cap B) = \frac{8}{40}$
  3. **Apply the conditional formula:**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P(F|B) &= \frac{P(F \cap B)}{P(B)} \\
     P(F|B) &= \frac{\frac{8}{40}}{\frac{18}{40}}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. **Simplify:** The original 40 cancels out, leaving just our Target Area over our New Total Area.
     $$ P(F|B) = \frac{8}{18} = \frac{4}{9} $$

</Steps>

<SteveTip title="Rearranging the Formula">
The formula booklet provides the standard conditional probability formula above, but it can be extremely useful to rearrange it using algebraic multiplication to find intersections:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(B) \times P(A|B)
$$

This rearranged form is essentially the logic you use when moving along the branches of a tree diagram!
</SteveTip>

## 5. Independent Events

Two events are **independent** if the occurrence of one has zero effect on the probability of the other occurring. For example, flipping a coin and rolling a die are independent events. Getting a "Heads" does not change the probability of rolling a "6".

If events A and B are independent, then $P(A|B) = P(A)$. Substituting this into our rearranged conditional probability formula yields the test for independence:

<FormulaBox title="Independent events">
$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

### Example 5: Testing for Independence

For two events $C$ and $D$, $P(C) = 0.4$, $P(D) = 0.5$, and $P(C \cup D) = 0.7$. Determine if events $C$ and $D$ are independent.

<Steps>

  1. **Find the intersection $P(C \cap D)$:** Use the addition rule backwards.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0.7 &= 0.4 + 0.5 - P(C \cap D) \\
     0.7 &= 0.9 - P(C \cap D) \\
     P(C \cap D) &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Test the independence formula:** Does $P(C \cap D) = P(C) \times P(D)$?
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0.2 &\stackrel{?}{=} 0.4 \times 0.5 \\
     0.2 &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. **Conclude:** Since the equation holds true, events $C$ and $D$ **are independent**.

</Steps>

## 6. With and Without Replacement (Tree Diagrams)

When drawing multiple items (like cards from a deck or marbles from a bag), you must pay close attention to whether the items are replaced. This fundamentally changes the denominators in your probabilities.

* **With Replacement:** The sample space resets. Events are **independent**. The denominator stays the same.
* **Without Replacement:** The sample space shrinks for each subsequent draw. Events are **dependent**, and you must adjust your fractions accordingly.

    <text x="130" y="20" font-weight="bold" text-decoration="underline">1st Draw</text>
    <text x="320" y="20" font-weight="bold" text-decoration="underline">2nd Draw</text>
  </svg>

**a) Find the probability of drawing two Red tokens.**
Multiply along the branches to find the probability of (Red AND Red).
$$ P(R, R) = \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{5}{8} = \frac{25}{64} $$

**b) Find the probability of drawing two tokens of the same colour.**
"Same colour" means (Red AND Red) OR (Blue AND Blue). We add the individual paths together.
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\text{Same}) &= P(R, R) + P(B, B) \\
P(\text{Same}) &= \left(\frac{5}{8} \times \frac{5}{8}\right) + \left(\frac{3}{8} \times \frac{3}{8}\right) \\
P(\text{Same}) &= \frac{25}{64} + \frac{9}{64} = \frac{34}{64} = \frac{17}{32}
\end{aligned}
$$

**c) Find the probability of drawing two tokens of different colours.**
We can calculate this by adding $P(R, B)$ and $P(B, R)$, but it is much faster to use the complement rule since "different colours" is the exact opposite of "same colours".
$$ P(\text{Different}) = 1 - P(\text{Same}) = 1 - \frac{17}{32} = \frac{15}{32} $$

**d) Find the probability of drawing at least one Red token.**
Again, using the 'Not' Rule saves us from adding three separate paths. The only scenario that does *not* contain a Red token is (Blue, Blue).
$$ P(\text{At least one Red}) = 1 - P(B, B) = 1 - \frac{9}{64} = \frac{55}{64} $$

**e) Given that the two tokens drawn are the same colour, what is the probability they are both Blue?**
This is a conditional probability question! Our "new total" is the probability they are the same colour (from part b), and our "target area" is the probability they are both blue.
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(B, B \mid \text{Same}) &= \frac{P(B, B \cap \text{Same})}{P(\text{Same})} \\
P(B, B \mid \text{Same}) &= \frac{P(B, B)}{P(\text{Same})} \\
P(B, B \mid \text{Same}) &= \frac{\frac{9}{64}}{\frac{34}{64}} = \frac{9}{34}
\end{aligned}
$$

    For two events $A$ and $B$, $P(A) = 0.50$, $P(A \cap B) = 0.10$, and $P(A \cup B) = 0.85$.

    P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)

    $$
    P(D|C) = \frac{P(C \cap D)}{P(C)} \implies P(C \cap D) = P(C) \times P(D|C)
    $$
    $$
    P(C \cap D) = 0.6 \times 0.8 = 0.48
    $$
  </TabItem>
