Probability is the mathematical language of uncertainty. Whether you are predicting the weather, analysing risk in finance, or modelling the outcomes of a medical trial, understanding how different events interact is essential. In this chapter, we will formalise the rules of probability and explore powerful visual tools to solve complex problems without getting lost in abstract algebra.

## 1. Visualising Probability

Before relying entirely on formulae, it is often much easier to solve probability problems using visual representations of the sample space (the set of all possible outcomes). The IB strongly encourages solving problems with the aid of diagrams.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Probability Diagrams">
* **Venn Diagrams:** Ideal for showing overlapping events (combined events, intersections, and mutually exclusive events) and calculating conditional probability.
* **Tree Diagrams:** The best tool for sequential events, especially when dealing with probabilities "with and without replacement".
* **Sample Space Diagrams & Tables:** Useful for displaying all equally likely combinations of two simple events (e.g., rolling two dice or flipping a coin and spinning a spinner).
</Aside>

## 2. Combined Events and the Addition Rule

When we want to find the probability of Event A **or** Event B occurring, we are looking at **combined events** (the union, denoted as $A \cup B$).

<Aside type="caution" title="The Non-exclusivity of 'Or'">
In everyday language, "or" sometimes means "one or the other, but not both" (e.g., "Would you like tea or coffee?"). In mathematics, "or" is **inclusive**. $P(A \cup B)$ means Event A occurs, Event B occurs, **or both occur**. 
</Aside>

If we simply add the probability of A to the probability of B, we accidentally count the overlapping intersection ($A \cap B$) twice. Therefore, we must subtract the intersection once.

<FormulaBox title="Combined events">
$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

---

## 3. Mutually Exclusive Events

If two events cannot possibly happen at the same time, they are called **mutually exclusive**. For example, rolling a standard die and getting a '2' and a '5' on the same roll is impossible. 

Because they cannot happen simultaneously, their intersection is zero ($P(A \cap B) = 0$). This simplifies the combined events formula.

<FormulaBox title="Mutually exclusive events">
$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

## 4. Conditional Probability & Restricted Domains

Often, the probability of an event changes if you know that another event has already happened. This is called **conditional probability**. The notation $P(A|B)$ is read as "the probability of A, given that B has occurred".

<Aside type="tip" title="The Restricted Sample Space">
When calculating $P(A|B)$, you are essentially ignoring the entire universe $U$ and **restricting your sample space solely to Event B**. The "new total" is $P(B)$, and the "successful outcomes" are the parts of A that live entirely within B (which is $A \cap B$).
</Aside>

<FormulaBox title="Conditional probability">
$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$
</FormulaBox>

<SteveTip title="Rearranging the Formula">
The formula booklet provides the standard conditional probability formula above, but it can be extremely useful to rearrange it using algebraic multiplication to find intersections:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(B) \times P(A|B)
$$

This rearranged form is essentially the logic you use when moving along the branches of a tree diagram!
</SteveTip>

## 5. Independent Events

Two events are **independent** if the occurrence of one has zero effect on the probability of the other occurring. For example, flipping a coin and rolling a die are independent events. Getting a "Heads" does not change the probability of rolling a "6".

If events A and B are independent, then $P(A|B) = P(A)$. Substituting this into our rearranged conditional probability formula yields the test for independence:

<FormulaBox title="Independent events">
$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)
$$
</FormulaBox>

### With and Without Replacement

When drawing multiple items (like cards from a deck or marbles from a bag), you must pay close attention to whether the items are replaced. This fundamentally changes the denominators in your probabilities.

* **With Replacement:** The sample space resets. Events are **independent**. The denominator stays the same.
* **Without Replacement:** The sample space shrinks for each subsequent draw. Events are **dependent**, and you must adjust your fractions accordingly.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Combined Events Formula">
    In a group of 50 students, the probability that a student studies History ($H$) is $0.6$, the probability they study Geography ($G$) is $0.5$, and the probability they study both is $0.3$. 
    
    Calculate the probability that a randomly chosen student studies History or Geography.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    We need to find the probability of the union, $P(H \cup G)$.
    Using the formula for combined events:
    
    $$
    P(H \cup G) = P(H) + P(G) - P(H \cap G)
    $$
    
    Substitute the given values:
    
    $$
    P(H \cup G) = 0.6 + 0.5 - 0.3
    $$
    
    $$
    P(H \cup G) = 0.8
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Testing for Independence">
    Events $A$ and $B$ are such that $P(A) = 0.4$, $P(B) = 0.25$, and $P(A \cup B) = 0.55$.
    
    **a)** Find $P(A \cap B)$.
    
    **b)** Determine, with mathematical reasoning, whether events $A$ and $B$ are independent.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** Rearrange the combined events formula to find the intersection.
    
    $$
    P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)
    $$
    
    $$
    0.55 = 0.4 + 0.25 - P(A \cap B)
    $$
    
    $$
    0.55 = 0.65 - P(A \cap B)
    $$
    
    $$
    P(A \cap B) = 0.1
    $$
    
    **b)** Use the independent events rule to test for independence. If independent, $P(A \cap B)$ must equal $P(A) \times P(B)$.
    
    $$
    P(A) \times P(B) = 0.4 \times 0.25 = 0.1
    $$
    
    Since $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$ ($0.1 = 0.1$), we conclude that events $A$ and $B$ **are independent**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Mutually Exclusive vs Independent">
    For two events $A$ and $B$, $P(A) = 0.35$, $P(B) = k$, and $P(A \cup B) = 0.61$.
    
    **a)** If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive, find the value of $k$.
    
    **b)** If $A$ and $B$ are independent, write down an equation in terms of $k$ and hence find its value.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** If mutually exclusive, $P(A \cap B) = 0$.
    
    $$
    P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)
    $$
    
    $$
    0.61 = 0.35 + k
    $$
    
    $$
    k = 0.26
    $$
    
    **b)** If independent, $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = 0.35k$.
    Substitute this into the combined events formula:
    
    $$
    P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)
    $$
    
    $$
    0.61 = 0.35 + k - 0.35k
    $$
    
    Now, simplify and solve for $k$ (or use `nSolve(0.61 = 0.35 + k - 0.35k, k)`):
    
    $$
    0.26 = 0.65k
    $$
    
    $$
    k = \frac{0.26}{0.65} = 0.4
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Missing Variables in Tables">
    A local sports club recorded the preferred training days of its members. 
    
    | | Weekdays | Weekends | Total |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Juniors** | 15 | $y$ | $15 + y$ |
    | **Seniors** | 22 | 18 | 40 |
    | **Total** | 37 | $y + 18$ | $y + 55$ |
    
    The probability that a randomly chosen member prefers Weekends, given that they are a Junior, is $0.6$.
    
    **a)** Write down an equation for $y$.
    
    **b)** Find the total number of members in the club.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)** We are given a conditional probability: $P(\text{Weekends} | \text{Juniors}) = 0.6$.
    The restricted domain is the total number of Juniors ($15 + y$).
    The number of Juniors who prefer Weekends is $y$.
    
    $$
    \frac{y}{15 + y} = 0.6
    $$
    
    **b)** Solve the linear equation for $y$.
    
    $$
    y = 0.6(15 + y)
    $$
    
    $$
    y = 9 + 0.6y
    $$
    
    $$
    0.4y = 9
    $$
    
    $$
    y = 22.5
    $$
    
    To find the total number of members, substitute $y = 22.5$ into the expression for the grand total:
    
    $$
    \text{Total} = 22.5 + 55 = 77.5
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    **b)** We want the total probability of a defective bolt. There are two paths that lead to a defect:
    (Machine A AND Defective) OR (Machine B AND Defective)
    
    $$
    P(D) = P(A \cap D) + P(B \cap D)
    $$
    
    $$
    P(D) = (0.7 \times 0.04) + (0.3 \times 0.06)
    $$
    
    $$
    P(D) = 0.028 + 0.018 = 0.046
    $$
    
    **c)** We need $P(A | D)$. We use the conditional probability formula.
    Our new restricted total is the probability of a defect: $P(D) = 0.046$.
    Our target outcome is the intersection: $P(A \cap D) = 0.028$.
    
    $$
    P(A | D) = \frac{P(A \cap D)}{P(D)}
    $$
    
    $$
    P(A | D) = \frac{0.028}{0.046} \approx 0.609 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
