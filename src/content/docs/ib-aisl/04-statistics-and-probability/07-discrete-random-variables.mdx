Whether evaluating the long-term profitability of a casino game or analysing risk in insurance, we use **random variables** to link outcomes to probabilities.

## 1. Discrete Random Variables and Distributions

A **random variable** (usually denoted by a capital letter like $X$) is a quantity whose value depends on chance. It is **discrete** if it can only take specific, separate values (often integers), such as the number of heads when flipping three coins, or the winning payout of a lottery ticket.

We use a lowercase letter ($x$) to represent the specific values that $X$ can take. Therefore, $P(X = x)$ reads as "the probability that the random variable $X$ takes the value $x$".

A **probability distribution** completely describes a random variable by listing all its possible values and their corresponding probabilities. The IB syllabus presents these in two main ways:

### Method B: Probability Functions
Sometimes the distribution is given as an algebraic rule. You must substitute the allowed values of $x$ into the function to generate the probabilities.

For example, given $P(X=x) = \frac{1}{18}(4+x)$ for $x \in \{1, 2, 3\}$:
* $P(X=1) = \frac{1}{18}(4+1) = \frac{5}{18}$
* $P(X=2) = \frac{1}{18}(4+2) = \frac{6}{18}$
* $P(X=3) = \frac{1}{18}(4+3) = \frac{7}{18}$

*(Notice that $\frac{5}{18} + \frac{6}{18} + \frac{7}{18} = \frac{18}{18} = 1$, confirming it is a valid probability distribution).*

## 2. Expected Value $\mathrm{E}(X)$

The **expected value**, denoted as $\mathrm{E}(X)$, is the theoretical mean (average) of the random variable. It tells you what you would expect the average outcome to be if you ran the experiment an infinite number of times.

<FormulaBox title="Expected value of a discrete random variable X">
$$
\mathrm{E}(X)=\sum_{i=1}^{k} x_i \mathrm{P}(X=x_i)
$$
</FormulaBox>

To calculate it manually, you multiply each outcome ($x$) by its probability ($P(X=x)$) and add all the results together.

## 3. Applications: Games of Chance and Fairness

A classic application of expected value is analysing games of chance. In mathematics, the definition of a "fair" game is very strict.

<Aside type="tip" title="Definition of a Fair Game">
A game is considered **fair** if the expected gain of a player is exactly zero. 

$$
\mathrm{E}(X) = 0
$$

where $X$ represents the **financial gain** (Total Prize Winnings $-$ Cost to Play). If $\mathrm{E}(X) < 0$, the game favours the organiser (like a casino). If $\mathrm{E}(X) > 0$, the game favours the player.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK) & Aim 8">
**Is it fair that casinos should make a profit?** Mathematical "fairness" implies $\mathrm{E}(X)=0$, but casinos are businesses with overheads and employees. They rely on an $\mathrm{E}(X)$ that is slightly negative for the player (the "house edge") to guarantee long-term profit. What do we mean by a "fair" game in a societal context versus a mathematical one? 

**The Casino Fallacy:** Why has it been argued that theories based on the calculable probabilities found in casinos are pernicious when applied to everyday life (for example, economics)? Casinos operate in "closed systems" with known, fixed probabilities. Real-world economics involve rare outlier events and human irrationality that no simple discrete model can fully capture, making casino-style risk models dangerous if relied upon exclusively.
</Aside>

    A random variable $X$ has a probability distribution given by the function:

    $$
    P(X=x) = \frac{x^2}{14} \text{ for } x \in \{1, 2, 3\}

    **a)** Write down a probability distribution table for $X$.

    **b)** Calculate the expected value, $\mathrm{E}(X)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** First, generate the probability for each outcome by substituting the allowed values of $x$:
    * $P(X=1) = \frac{1^2}{14} = \frac{1}{14}$
    * $P(X=2) = \frac{2^2}{14} = \frac{4}{14}$
    * $P(X=3) = \frac{3^2}{14} = \frac{9}{14}$

    | $x$ | $1$ | $2$ | $3$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $\frac{1}{14}$ | $\frac{4}{14}$ | $\frac{9}{14}$ |
    
    **b)** Now, apply the expected value formula: 
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = \sum x P(X=x)
    $$
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = \left(1 \times \frac{1}{14}\right) + \left(2 \times \frac{4}{14}\right) + \left(3 \times \frac{9}{14}\right)
    $$
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = \frac{1 + 8 + 27}{14} 
    $$

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = \frac{36}{14} = \frac{18}{7} \approx 2.57 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Modelling a Fair Game">
    A local charity runs a game where players spin a biased wheel. It costs £5 to play.
    * The probability of winning £15 is $0.2$.
    * The probability of winning £5 is $0.3$.
    * The probability of winning nothing (£0) is $0.5$.
    
    Let $X$ represent the player's **financial gain** from one game.
    
    **a)** Construct a probability distribution table for $X$.
    
    **b)** Determine whether the game is mathematically fair, giving a reason for your answer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** We must calculate the *gain* (Prize Winnings - Cost to Play) for each outcome.
    * Win £15: Gain = $£15 - £5 = £10$
    * Win £5: Gain = $£5 - £5 = £0$
    * Win £0: Gain = $£0 - £5 = -£5$
    
    | $x$ (Gain) | $10$ | $0$ | $-5$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $0.2$ | $0.3$ | $0.5$ |
    
    **b)** Calculate the expected value, $\mathrm{E}(X)$, of the gain.
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = (10 \times 0.2) + (0 \times 0.3) + (-5 \times 0.5)
    $$
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = 2 + 0 - 2.5
    $$
    
    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = -0.5
    $$
    
    Because $\mathrm{E}(X) = -0.5$ (which is not equal to 0), **the game is not fair**. On average, a player expects to lose £0.50 per game, meaning the game favours the charity.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Backwards Expected Value">
    A discrete random variable $X$ has the following probability distribution:
    
    | $x$ | $0$ | $2$ | $5$ | $10$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $0.1$ | $p$ | $q$ | $0.2$ |

    It is given that the expected value $\mathrm{E}(X) = 4.3$.
    
    **a)** Write down an equation connecting $p$ and $q$.

    **b)** Find the value of $p$ and the value of $q$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** The sum of all probabilities equals 1.
    
    $$
    0.1 + p + q + 0.2 = 1
    $$
    
    $$
    p + q = 0.7
    $$

    **b)** We use the expected value formula to create a second equation.

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = (0 \times 0.1) + (2 \times p) + (5 \times q) + (10 \times 0.2)
    $$
    
    $$
    4.3 = 0 + 2p + 5q + 2
    $$

    $$
    2.3 = 2p + 5q
    $$

    We now have a system of simultaneous equations. From part a), we can rearrange to get $p = 0.7 - q$. Substitute this into the second equation:

    $$
    2(0.7 - q) + 5q = 2.3
    $$

    $$
    1.4 - 2q + 5q = 2.3
    $$

    $$
    3q = 0.9
    $$

    $$
    q = 0.3
    $$

    Substitute $q = 0.3$ back into $p = 0.7 - q$:

    $$
    p = 0.7 - 0.3 = 0.4
    $$

    Therefore, $p = 0.4$ and $q = 0.3$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Inequalities and Conditional Probability">
    A quality control inspector records the number of faults found in a sample of mobile phone screens. The random variable $X$ represents the number of faults per screen.

    | $x$ | $0$ | $1$ | $2$ | $3$ | $4$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $0.45$ | $0.25$ | $0.15$ | $0.10$ | $0.05$ |

    **a)** Find $P(X < 2)$.

    **b)** Calculate $\mathrm{E}(X)$.

    **c)** Given that a screen has at least 1 fault, find the probability that it has exactly 2 faults.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** $P(X < 2)$ means strictly less than 2 faults, so we sum the probabilities for $x=0$ and $x=1$.

    $$
    P(X < 2) = 0.45 + 0.25 = 0.70
    $$

    **b)** Multiply each outcome by its probability and sum the results:

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = (0 \times 0.45) + (1 \times 0.25) + (2 \times 0.15) + (3 \times 0.10) + (4 \times 0.05)
    $$

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = 0 + 0.25 + 0.30 + 0.30 + 0.20 = 1.05 \text{ faults per screen}
    $$

    **c)** This is a conditional probability question: $P(X = 2 \mid X \ge 1)$.
    The restricted domain (our new denominator) is $P(X \ge 1)$.

    $$
    P(X \ge 1) = 1 - P(X=0) = 1 - 0.45 = 0.55
    $$

    The successful outcome within this domain is $P(X=2) = 0.15$.

    $$
    P(X = 2 \mid X \ge 1) = \frac{0.15}{0.55} = \frac{3}{11} \approx 0.273 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Expected Profit in Business Context">
    An insurance company offers a home protection policy that costs £500 per year. 
    Based on historical data, the probabilities of a customer making different sizes of claims in a year are modelled below:

    * Probability of a major claim (£10,000) is $0.01$.
    * Probability of a minor claim (£2,000) is $0.05$.
    * Probability of no claim (£0) is $0.94$.

    Let $X$ be the profit made by the insurance company per policy.

    **a)** Complete the probability distribution table for $X$.

    | $x$ (Profit in £) | \_\_\_\_\_\_ | \_\_\_\_\_\_ | \_\_\_\_\_\_ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $0.94$ | $0.05$ | $0.01$ |

    **b)** Calculate $\mathrm{E}(X)$.

    **c)** If the company sells 1,000 of these policies, estimate their total expected profit for the year.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)** Profit is calculated as (Money received from customer) $-$ (Money paid out for claim).
    * No claim: $500 - 0 = £500$
    * Minor claim: $500 - 2000 = -£1500$
    * Major claim: $500 - 10000 = -£9500$

    | $x$ (Profit in £) | $500$ | $-1500$ | $-9500$ |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $P(X=x)$ | $0.94$ | $0.05$ | $0.01$ |

    **b)** Multiply each profit outcome by its probability:

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = (500 \times 0.94) + (-1500 \times 0.05) + (-9500 \times 0.01)
    $$

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = 470 - 75 - 95
    $$

    $$
    \mathrm{E}(X) = 300
    $$

    The expected profit per policy is £300.

    **c)** The total expected profit is the expected profit per policy multiplied by the number of policies.

    $$
    \text{Total Profit} = 1000 \times 300 = £300,000
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
