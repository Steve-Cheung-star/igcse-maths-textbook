While the binomial distribution models discrete successes and failures, many variables in the real world are **continuous**. Heights, weights, exam scores, blood pressures, and measurement errors do not fall into neat, separated categories. Instead, they naturally form a continuous spread. 

When you collect a large amount of continuous data from nature, it almost always forms a symmetrical, bell-shaped curve where most values cluster around the average, and extreme values become increasingly rare. This is known as the **normal distribution**.

---

## 1. Properties of the Normal Curve

If a continuous random variable $X$ follows a normal distribution, we define it using two parameters: the mean ($\mu$) and the variance ($\sigma^2$).

$$
X \sim \text{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)
$$

<Aside type="note" title="Key Properties of the Normal Distribution">
* **Bell-shaped and Symmetrical:** The curve is perfectly symmetrical around its centre.
* **Central Measures are Equal:** The mean, median, and mode are all located at the exact highest point of the curve.
* **Asymptotic:** The tails of the curve get closer and closer to the x-axis but never touch it (meaning theoretically, there is a tiny probability of an infinitely large or small result).
* **Total Area = 1:** Like all probability distributions, the total area under the curve is exactly $1$ (or $100\%$).
</Aside>

## 2. The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

The standard deviation ($\sigma$) measures the spread of the data. Because the normal curve is perfectly mathematical, we know exactly what percentage of data falls within certain standard deviations from the mean.

Students are expected to know these approximate benchmarks by heart:

<Aside type="tip" title="The 68-95-99.7 Rule">
* Approximately **68%** of the data lies within **one** standard deviation of the mean ($\mu \pm \sigma$).
* Approximately **95%** of the data lies within **two** standard deviations of the mean ($\mu \pm 2\sigma$).
* Approximately **99.7%** of the data lies within **three** standard deviations of the mean ($\mu \pm 3\sigma$).
</Aside>

Because the curve is symmetrical, we can split these percentages in half to find the area of specific "slices". For example, the area between $\mu$ and $\mu + \sigma$ is strictly $34\%$.

## 3. Normal Probability Calculations

While the empirical rule is useful for exact standard deviations, we need technology to find probabilities for any other value. In the IB AI SL course, you are **not required** to standardise values to the $Z$-distribution formula ($z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$). You will find all probabilities directly using your GDC.

<Aside type="caution" title="Never Use normPdf!">
In the IB AI SL course, we deal with continuous normal variables. The probability of getting exactly one specific value on a continuous curve (like exactly $50.00000... \text{ kg}$) is technically $0$. We are only ever interested in the **area** between two values (ranges). 

Therefore, **you will NEVER use `normPdf`**. Always use `normCdf`!
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="GDC Function: normCdf">
To find the probability (area) between two values, use the Cumulative Distribution Function (`normCdf`).
* **Menu > 5: Probability > 5: Distributions > 2: Normal Cdf**
* **Lower Bound:** The smallest value. If asking for "less than $x$", use a massive negative number like `-9E99`.
* **Upper Bound:** The largest value. If asking for "greater than $x$", use a massive positive number like `9E99`.
* **$\mu$:** The mean.
* **$\sigma$:** The standard deviation.

*Caution:* The notation is $X \sim \text{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, which gives the **variance**. Your calculator asks for the **standard deviation** ($\sigma$). You may need to square root the given variance first!
</SteveTip>

---

## 4. Inverse Normal Calculations

Sometimes, a question will give you the probability (the area under the curve) and ask you to work backwards to find the boundary value ($x$) that corresponds to it.

<SteveTip title="GDC Function: invNorm">
Use the Inverse Normal function for backward calculations.
* **Menu > 5: Probability > 5: Distributions > 3: Inverse Normal**
* **Area:** The probability *to the left* of the unknown value $x$. 
* **$\mu$:** The mean.
* **$\sigma$:** The standard deviation.

*Important:* If a question says "the top 10% of students", the area *to the left* is 90%, so you must input `Area = 0.90`.
</SteveTip>

---

## 5. Linearity of Mean and Variance

Occasionally, you might need to adjust your normal distribution due to a change in units (e.g., converting Celsius to Fahrenheit) or adding a constant amount.

* **Adding a Constant ($+b$):** Shifts the mean by $b$, but the spread (variance/standard deviation) stays exactly the same.
* **Multiplying by a Constant ($\times a$):** Multiplies the mean by $a$. It multiplies the standard deviation by $a$, and the variance by $a^2$.

---

## 6. Theory of Knowledge & International-Mindedness

The normal distribution is remarkably powerful, but it relies on philosophical assumptions about how the universe works.

<Aside type="note" title="International-Mindedness: L'homme moyen">
The normal distribution's mathematical foundations were largely derived by the French mathematician Abraham de Moivre in 1733. Later, the Belgian astronomer and sociologist Adolphe Quetelet applied it to human characteristics (like height and chest size), introducing the concept of *l'homme moyen* (the "average man"). He believed the "average" was nature's ideal, and deviations were literal "errors" or imperfections.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Aim 8: Misuse of the Normal Curve">
**Why might the misuse of the normal distribution lead to dangerous inferences?** If we assume human intelligence or behaviour perfectly follows a normal distribution, we might improperly label people at the edges of the curve as "abnormal" or "defective" (as Quetelet originally implied). Furthermore, forcing data that is actually skewed (like global wealth distribution) into a normal model creates dangerous, inaccurate economic policies that underestimate severe poverty. **To what extent can we trust mathematical models?** A model is a simplification of reality; we must critically examine whether the continuous, symmetrical assumptions of a normal curve truly apply to the complex data we are studying.
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Inverse Normal (Finding a Value)">
    The volume of water in bottles filled by a machine is normally distributed with a mean of $502 \text{ ml}$ and a standard deviation of $3 \text{ ml}$.
    
    **a)** Find the volume of water exceeded by only the top 5% of bottles.
    
    **b)** The manufacturer rejects any bottle in the bottom 2% of volumes. Find the minimum acceptable volume for a bottle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    We need to find boundary values given probabilities, so we use `invNorm`.
    
    **a)** The "top 5%" means the area strictly to the *right* of our boundary is 0.05. 
    Because `invNorm` requires the area to the *left*, we input $1 - 0.05 = 0.95$.
    
    $$
    x = \text{invNorm}(0.95, 502, 3) \approx 506.93
    $$
    
    The volume exceeded by the top 5% is $507 \text{ ml}$ (to 3 s.f.).
    
    **b)** The "bottom 2%" means the area to the *left* is exactly 0.02.
    
    $$
    x = \text{invNorm}(0.02, 502, 3) \approx 495.84
    $$
    
    The minimum acceptable volume is $496 \text{ ml}$ (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Conditional Normal Probability">
    The scores on a university entrance exam are normally distributed, $X \sim \text{N}(55, 12^2)$.
    
    **a)** Find the probability that a student scores greater than 60.
    
    **b)** Given that a student scores greater than 60, find the probability that they score greater than 70.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)** We need $P(X > 60)$. 
    
    $$
    P(X > 60) = \text{normCdf}(60, 9E99, 55, 12) \approx 0.338
    $$
    
    **b)** This is a conditional probability statement. We are finding the probability of scoring greater than 70, *given* that the score is already greater than 60.
    Using the conditional probability formula:
    
    $$
    P(X > 70 \mid X > 60) = \frac{P(X > 70 \cap X > 60)}{P(X > 60)}
    $$
    
    The intersection of "scoring greater than 70" AND "scoring greater than 60" is simply "scoring greater than 70".
    
    $$
    P(X > 70 \mid X > 60) = \frac{P(X > 70)}{P(X > 60)}
    $$
    
    First, find the numerator $P(X > 70)$:
    
    $$
    P(X > 70) = \text{normCdf}(70, 9E99, 55, 12) \approx 0.10564...
    $$
    
    Now divide by the unrounded denominator from part a:
    
    $$
    P(X > 70 \mid X > 60) = \frac{0.10564}{0.33846} \approx 0.312 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Linearity of Mean and Variance">
    The daily temperature $C$ in a city, measured in Celsius, is normally distributed with a mean of $22^\circ\text{C}$ and a variance of $9$. 
    
    The temperature $F$ in Fahrenheit is calculated using the formula $F = 1.8C + 32$.
    
    **a)** Find the expected value (mean) of the temperature in Fahrenheit, $\text{E}(F)$.
    
    **b)** Find the variance of the temperature in Fahrenheit, $\text{Var}(F)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)** For expected value (mean), you apply the exact same transformation to the mean.
    
    $$
    \text{E}(F) = 1.8 \times \text{E}(C) + 32
    $$
    
    $$
    \text{E}(F) = 1.8(22) + 32 = 39.6 + 32 = 71.6^\circ\text{F}
    $$
    
    **b)** Adding a constant (+32) does *not* change the spread or variance. However, multiplying by a constant (1.8) scales the variance by the square of that constant.
    
    $$
    \text{Var}(F) = (1.8)^2 \times \text{Var}(C)
    $$
    
    $$
    \text{Var}(F) = 3.24 \times 9 = 29.16
    $$
    
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    $$
    P(X > 2500) = 0.15
    $$

    $$
    \text{normCdf}(2500, 9E99, \mu, 300) = 0.15

    </SteveTip>
    
    Solving this equation on the calculator gives:
    
    $$
    \mu \approx 2189.07...
    $$
    
    The mean daily calorie intake is approximately $2190 \text{ kcal}$ (to 3 s.f.).

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 10: Tying Normal to Binomial Probability">
    The weights of apples in an orchard are normally distributed with a mean of $150 \text{ g}$ and a standard deviation of $15 \text{ g}$.
    An apple is classified as "Premium" if it weighs more than $170 \text{ g}$.
    
    **a)** Find the probability that a randomly chosen apple is Premium.
    
    A grocer randomly selects a sample of 12 apples from the orchard.
    
    **b)** Find the probability that exactly 3 of these 12 apples are Premium.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 10">
    **a)** Let $W \sim \text{N}(150, 15^2)$ be the weight of an apple.
    
    $$
    P(W > 170) = \text{normCdf}(170, 9E99, 150, 15) \approx 0.09121...
    $$
    
    The probability is approximately $0.0912$ (to 3 s.f.).
    
    **b)** We are now dealing with a fixed number of independent trials (12 apples), each having a success/fail outcome (Premium or not). This is a **binomial distribution**.
    
    Let $Y$ be the number of Premium apples in the sample of 12. 
    $n = 12$, and $p = 0.09121...$ (use the unrounded answer from part a).
    
    $$
    Y \sim \text{B}(12, 0.09121...)
    $$
    
    We need exactly 3 successes: $P(Y = 3)$.
    
    $$
    P(Y = 3) = \text{binomPdf}(12, 0.09121..., 3) \approx 0.0716
    $$
    
    (to 3 s.f.)
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
