Once data is organised, we need ways to mathematically summarise it. We generally look for two things: the "typical" or middle value (central tendency) and how spread out the data is (dispersion). 

Understanding both is crucial. For example, knowing the average depth of a river is 1 metre is useful, but knowing the spread is large (meaning there are parts that are 5 metres deep) could save your life if you try to wade across it!

---

## 1. Measures of Central Tendency

Central tendency describes the centre of a data set. The three main measures are:

* **Mean ($\bar{x}$):** The arithmetic average. Found by adding all values and dividing by the total number of values. It uses all data points but is highly affected by outliers.
* **Median ($Q_2$):** The middle value when data is ordered by size. It is unaffected by extreme outliers.
* **Mode:** The most frequent value. For grouped continuous data with equal class intervals, we refer to the **modal class** (the interval with the highest frequency).

<FormulaBox title="Mean of a set of data">
$$
\bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i x_i}{n} \text{, where } n = \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i
$$
</FormulaBox>

### Estimating the Mean from Grouped Data

When data is grouped into intervals (e.g., $10 \le x < 20$), we lose the original individual data points. To estimate the mean, we must assume that all data points in a class are evenly spread out, meaning they average out to the exact middle of the interval.

<Aside type="note" title="Using Mid-Interval Values">
To estimate the mean of grouped data:
1. Find the **mid-interval value** for each class by averaging the upper and lower boundaries (e.g., for $10 \le x < 20$, the mid-interval is $15$).
2. Multiply each mid-interval value by its frequency ($f_i x_i$).
3. Sum these products and divide by the total frequency ($n$).
</Aside>

## 2. Measures of Dispersion

While central tendency tells us about the middle, dispersion tells us about consistency and spread. We have already looked at the Range and Interquartile Range (IQR). The most important measures of dispersion for continuous data are the Standard Deviation and Variance.

<Aside type="tip" title="Standard Deviation and Variance">
* **Standard Deviation ($\sigma$ or $s$):** A measure of the average distance of each data point from the mean. A small standard deviation means the data is highly consistent and clustered close to the mean. A large standard deviation means the data is widely spread out.
* **Variance ($\sigma^2$ or $s^2$):** Simply the square of the standard deviation.

**Note:** The relationship between variance and standard deviation is NOT in your formula booklet, but you must memorise it:

$$
\text{Variance} = \sigma^2
$$
</Aside>

## 3. The Effect of Constant Changes on Data

A highly tested concept in Paper 1 is understanding what happens to the mean and standard deviation when you apply a mathematical rule to every single piece of data in a set. 

### Adding or Subtracting a Constant
If you add $k$ to every data point, the entire distribution simply shifts up or down the number line. 
* The **mean** changes (it increases or decreases by $k$).
* The **standard deviation, variance, and IQR** remain exactly the **same**. The data hasn't stretched or squashed; the relative distance between points is perfectly preserved.

### Multiplying or Dividing by a Constant
If you multiply every data point by $k$, the data physically stretches outwards (or compresses if dividing).
* The **mean** is multiplied by $k$.
* The **standard deviation** is multiplied by $k$.
* The **variance** is multiplied by **$k^2$** (because variance is standard deviation squared).

    A researcher records the time ($t$, in minutes) it takes 40 participants to solve a puzzle. They create the following grouped frequency table.
    
    | Time ($t$ mins) | Frequency ($f$) |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | $12 \le t < 14$ | 8 |
    | $14 \le t < 16$ | 15 |
    | $16 \le t < 18$ | 12 |
    | $18 \le t < 20$ | 5 |

    **a)** State the modal class.
    
    **b)** Explain why the exact mean cannot be calculated from this table.
    
    **c)** Estimate the mean time taken to solve the puzzle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** The modal class is the interval with the highest frequency. This is **$14 \le t < 16$**.
    
    **b)** Because the data is grouped, the exact original times of the participants are lost. We can only *estimate* the mean by assuming every time is exactly the mid-interval value.
    
    **c)**
      <div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '1rem 0' }}>
        <svg viewBox="0 0 500 120" width="100%" maxWidth="500px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px' }}>
          <rect x="50" y="20" width="400" height="30" fill="rgba(59, 130, 246, 0.1)" stroke="#3b82f6" stroke-width="1" />
          <text x="250" y="40" font-size="14" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">Mid-interval: x = (Lower + Upper) / 2</text>
          <text x="100" y="80" font-size="14" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">12 to 14 → x=13</text>
          <text x="200" y="80" font-size="14" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">14 to 16 → x=15</text>
          <text x="300" y="80" font-size="14" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">16 to 18 → x=17</text>
          <text x="400" y="80" font-size="14" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">18 to 20 → x=19</text>
        </svg>
      </div>

      <Steps>
        1. Find the mid-interval value ($x$) for each class (as shown above).
        2. Multiply each mid-interval by its frequency ($f_i x_i$):
           * $8 \times 13 = 104$
           * $15 \times 15 = 225$
           * $12 \times 17 = 204$
           * $5 \times 19 = 95$
           
        3. Sum the products and divide by total frequency (40):
           
           $$
           \bar{x} = \frac{104 + 225 + 204 + 95}{40}
           $$
           
           $$
           \bar{x} = \frac{628}{40} = 15.7 \text{ minutes}
           $$
           
      </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Working Backwards from the Mean">
    A dataset of 5 numbers has a mean of 64. Four of the numbers are:
    $50, 60, 70, 75$
    
    Calculate the exact value of the fifth number.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. Use the mean formula to set up an equation. Let the missing score be $x$.
         
         $$
         \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n}
         $$
         
         $$
         64 = \frac{50 + 60 + 70 + 75 + x}{5}
         $$
         
      2. Multiply by 5 to find the total sum of all marks:
         
         $$
         64 \times 5 = 320
         $$
         
      3. Sum the known marks:
         
         $$
         50 + 60 + 70 + 75 = 255
         $$
         
      4. Subtract the known sum from the total to find the missing score:
         
         $$
         x = 320 - 255 = 65
         $$
         
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Finding a Missing Frequency">
    A dataset has the following frequency distribution:
    
    | Value ($x$) | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | Frequency ($f$) | 3 | 5 | $k$ | 2 |

    Given that the mean of the dataset is 5, find the value of $k$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    <Steps>
      1. Write an expression for the total frequency ($n$):
         
         $$
         n = 3 + 5 + k + 2 = 10 + k
         $$
         
      2. Write an expression for the sum of all values ($\sum f x$):
         
         $$
         \sum f x = (2 \times 3) + (4 \times 5) + (6 \times k) + (8 \times 2)
         $$
         
         $$
         \sum f x = 6 + 20 + 6k + 16 = 42 + 6k
         $$
         
      3. Substitute into the mean formula and solve for $k$:
         
         $$
         5 = \frac{42 + 6k}{10 + k}
         $$
         
         $$
         5(10 + k) = 42 + 6k
         $$
         
         $$
         50 + 5k = 42 + 6k
         $$
         
         $$
         8 = k
         $$
         
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    Selina is analysing a dataset of 15 integers. It has the following summary statistics:

    * Median = 38

    **c)** The Standard Deviation
