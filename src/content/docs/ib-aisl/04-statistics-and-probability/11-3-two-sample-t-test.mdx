While the Chi-Square test determines if there is an association between categorical variables, the **Two-Sample t-Test** is used to determine if there is a statistically significant difference between the **means** of two distinct, independent groups. 

For example, you might use a two-sample t-test to compare the average battery life of two different brands of smartphones, or the mean examination scores of two different classes taught using different methods. 

Because students taking standard level are not expected to manually calculate the pooled variance using complex algebraic formulas, this test relies entirely on processing raw data lists through your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC).

<Steps>
1. Formulate the null and alternative hypotheses in the context of the question.
2. Verify the underlying assumptions of the test.
3. Enter the raw data into your GDC to find the test statistic ($t$) and p-value.
4. Compare your p-value to the significance level to make a concluding statement.
</Steps>

---

## 1. Formulating Hypotheses in Context

In the Two-Sample t-Test, hypotheses must directly reference the **mean** of the two populations being compared. It is critical that you write these hypotheses in words that clearly describe the context, rather than just writing a mathematical $\mu$ statement.

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of t-Test Hypotheses">
* **The Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):** This always states that there is **no difference** between the two population means. 
  *(e.g., "$H_0$: The mean battery life of Brand A is equal to the mean battery life of Brand B.")*
* **The Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$):** This states what you are trying to prove. Depending on the question, it will be one of three options:
  * **Two-Tailed ($\neq$):** The means are simply different from one another.
  * **One-Tailed (&gt;):** The mean of the first group is strictly greater than the mean of the second group.
  * **One-Tailed (&lt;):** The mean of the first group is strictly less than the mean of the second group.
</Aside>

## 2. Assumptions of the Test

For a two-sample t-test to be mathematically valid, certain assumptions must be met. In IB Maths AI SL, you must know these constraints:
1. **Normal Distribution:** The underlying populations from which the samples are drawn must be normally distributed. 
2. **Independent Samples:** The two groups must be completely separate (unpaired). You cannot have the same subject or person in both Group 1 and Group 2.
3. **Equal Variances:** The population variance ($\sigma^2$) is always assumed to be unknown, but we assume the variance of both groups is equal. Therefore, you must **always use the POOLED two-sample t-test**.

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception: Pooling">
When setting up the test on your calculator, you will be asked "Pooled: Yes or No?". In AI SL examinations, because we assume equal variances, the answer is **always YES**. Selecting "No" will result in a slightly different p-value and cost you accuracy marks!
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip" title="Beyond the Syllabus: The Mann-Whitney U Test">
**Cross-Curricular Connection (Psychology):** The two-sample t-test strictly requires data to be normally distributed and continuous. However, in fields like Psychology, fieldwork often involves ordinal data (like ranking scales) or highly skewed distributions. 

In these cases, the **Mann-Whitney U Test** is used instead. It is a "non-parametric" test that compares the medians rather than the means, making it a much more reliable choice when the assumption of a normal distribution fails.
</Aside>

## 3. Technology Integration (TI-Nspire)

Because you do not use the raw algebraic formula for the pooled standard deviation, you must be perfectly fluent in typing raw data into your GDC to execute the test.

### Running the Test on the TI-Nspire

<Steps>
1. **Enter the Data:** Open a Lists & Spreadsheet page. Name column A `group1` and column B `group2`. Enter your raw data into these lists. 
2. **Open the Test Menu:** Go to **Menu** > **Statistics** > **Stat Tests** > **2-Sample t-Test...**
3. **Select Input Method:** Choose **Data** (since you have typed in raw lists).
4. **Configure the Test:** * Select `group1` for List 1 and `group2` for List 2.
   * Leave the Frequencies as 1.
   * Choose your Alternate Hypothesis correctly matching the context (`H1: μ1 ≠ μ2`, `μ1 > μ2`, or `μ1 < μ2`).
   * **CRITICAL:** Set **Pooled** to **Yes**.
5. **Execute:** Press OK. The calculator will output `stat.t` (the test statistic) and `stat.PVal` (the p-value).
</Steps>

<Aside type="note" title="TOK Connection: The Controversy of P-Values">
In theory, p-values help us decide whether to reject a null hypothesis objectively. But is saying a result is "statistically significant" the same as saying it is *true*? 

In reality, a p-value of $0.049$ yields a "significant" publication, while a p-value of $0.051$ is rejected, even though the practical difference is microscopic. Because of this artificial threshold, some major research journals have "banned" p-values entirely, arguing they encourage researchers to manipulate their data (p-hacking) just to cross the magic 5% line. How is the term “significant” used differently in mathematics compared to everyday language?
</Aside>

## 4. IA Enrichment: The Paired t-Test (Before & After)

<SteveTip title="IA Guide: Dependent vs Independent Samples">
Many students want to do a "Before and After" Internal Assessment (e.g., measuring students' resting heart rates, making them perform an exercise, and measuring their heart rates again).

**You CANNOT use the 2-Sample t-Test for this!** The 2-Sample test assumes the groups are independent, but these are the exact same students measured twice (dependent/paired samples).

**How to do a Paired t-Test on the TI-Nspire:**
1. Put your "Before" data in list A and "After" data in list B.
2. In list C, calculate the difference by typing `= B - A` in the grey formula cell.
3. Instead of the 2-Sample test, go to **Menu > Statistics > Stat Tests > t-Test...** (This is the standard 1-Sample t-test).
4. Run the test on List C, testing against a null hypothesis mean of $\mu_0 = 0$. If the p-value is significant, it proves the "Before" and "After" data are significantly different!
</SteveTip>

    | Method A | 72 | 75 | 81 | 68 | 74 | 88 | 70 | 76 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Method B** | 85 | 82 | 89 | 78 | 91 | 84 | 86 | |
    
    Assume the scores are normally distributed and population variances are equal. The researcher conducts a pooled two-sample t-test at the **5%** significance level.
    
    **a)** State the null and alternative hypotheses in context.
    
    **b)** Using your GDC, find the p-value for this test.
    
    **c)** State the conclusion of the test, giving a reason for your answer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. **State hypotheses in context:** $H_0$: The mean examination score for Method A is equal to the mean examination score for Method B.
       $H_1$: The mean examination score for Method A is not equal to the mean examination score for Method B.
    2. **Find p-value:** Enter the raw data into two lists on the GDC. Run a 2-Sample t-Test using 'Data', Alternative Hypothesis $\mu_1 \neq \mu_2$, and Pooled: Yes.
       p-value = 0.00330 (to 3 s.f.)
    3. **State conclusion:** Compare the p-value to the significance level (**5%** or 0.05). Since $0.00330 < 0.05$, we **reject $H_0$**. There is sufficient evidence at the 5% level to suggest a significant difference in the mean examination scores between the two methods.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
