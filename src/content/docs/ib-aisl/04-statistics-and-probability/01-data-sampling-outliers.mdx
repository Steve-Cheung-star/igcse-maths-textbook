Statistics is the science of learning from data. Before we can perform complex calculations or draw meaningful conclusions, we must first understand how to properly collect and categorise our data. If the foundation of your data collection is flawed, every subsequent analysis will be compromised. 

This sub-topic covers the key questions you must ask whenever you encounter a data set: Who was asked? How were they chosen? Is the data reliable?

## 1. Populations, Samples, and Data Types

In any statistical investigation, we must clearly define the group we are studying.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Statistical Groups">
* **Population:** The entire group of individuals or items that is the focus of the investigation (e.g., all students in your school).
* **Sample:** A smaller subset selected from the population used to estimate characteristics of the whole population.
* **Random Sample:** A sample in which every member of the population has an equal and independent chance of being selected.
</Aside>

### Categorising Data

Once you have established your sample, the data you collect will generally fall into one of two numerical categories:

* **Discrete Data:** Numerical values that can only take specific, distinct values. They are usually counted. For example, the number of siblings a person has, or the shoe size of a student. You cannot have 2.4 siblings.
* **Continuous Data:** Numerical values that can take any value within a given range. They are usually measured. For example, the height of a student, the time taken to run 100 metres, or the mass of an apple.

## 2. Sampling Techniques

Since it is rarely practical to survey an entire population (which would be a census), we rely on various sampling methods. The goal is always to create a representative sample, but real-world constraints often lead to compromise.

<Aside type="note" title="Classifying Sampling Techniques">
Understanding how sampling methods relate to one another helps you choose the correct procedure. Methods are broadly divided into **Probability (Random)** sampling, where everyone has a known chance of selection, and **Non-Probability** sampling, which relies on subjective human choices.

## 3. Reliability, Bias, and Missing Data

A sample is only useful if it accurately reflects the population. When it does not, we say the data is subject to **bias**. Bias is systematic error introduced by the sampling process itself, often due to non-probability methods like convenience sampling.

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Sources of Error and Bias">
Real-world data is rarely perfect. You must critically evaluate:
1. **Selection Bias:** Does the sampling method exclude a specific group? (e.g., surveying people at a gym about their exercise habits over-represents active people).
2. **Missing Data / Non-Response Bias:** Did a specific demographic refuse to answer the survey? 
3. **Errors in Recording:** Are there obvious typographical errors (e.g., a human height recorded as 18 metres instead of 1.8 metres)?
</Aside>

If a procedure calls for a representative sample but an error is committed (e.g., stopping the first 50 people who walk by), you must know how to **correct** it. To change convenience sampling to a proper probability technique, you must define the whole population, assign them identifiers, and use a random process to select participants.

## 4. Identifying Outliers

An outlier is a data item that lies noticeably outside the overall pattern of the other values. In IB Mathematics, we use a strict mathematical formula to identify them based on the Interquartile Range (IQR).

**Note: These outlier boundaries are NOT provided in your IB Formula Booklet. You must memorise them.**

A data value is formally considered an outlier if it is more than $1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ from the nearest quartile.

**Lower Boundary (Lower Fence):**
$$
\text{Lower Fence} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}
$$

**Upper Boundary (Upper Fence):**
$$
\text{Upper Fence} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}
$$

<SteveTip title="Dealing with Outliers in Context">
Once you have identified an outlier mathematically, you must interpret it in context. 
If a survey records an age of 150 years, this is an **error in recording** and should be discarded. 
However, if a survey of regional incomes includes one billionaire, this is a **valid part of the sample**. You must decide whether keeping or removing the outlier makes your statistical model more useful for your specific aim.
</SteveTip>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Outliers and Data Context">
    Lucas, an agricultural science student, is measuring the mass of 120 pumpkins grown in a test field. The masses are measured in kilograms to one decimal place. 
    The lower quartile ($Q_1$) of the data is 4.2 kg and the upper quartile ($Q_3$) is 7.8 kg.
    
    **a)** Calculate the interquartile range (IQR).
    
    **b)** Calculate the upper and lower fences used to determine outliers.
    
    **c)** During data entry, three pumpkins were recorded with the following masses: 
    * Pumpkin A: 0.5 kg
    * Pumpkin B: 13.0 kg
    * Pumpkin C: 45.0 kg
    
    Determine, with mathematical justification, which of these pumpkins are considered outliers.
    
    **d)** Lucas realises that Pumpkin C's mass was recorded without a decimal point and was meant to be 4.5 kg. Explain how classifying outliers helps in the data cleaning process.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    $$
    \text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 7.8 - 4.2 = 3.6 \text{ kg}
    $$
    
    **b)** 
    <Steps>
    1. Calculate the Lower Fence:
       $$
       \text{Lower Fence} = Q_1 - (1.5 \times \text{IQR}) = 4.2 - (1.5 \times 3.6) = 4.2 - 5.4 = -1.2 \text{ kg}
       $$
    2. Calculate the Upper Fence:
       $$
       \text{Upper Fence} = Q_3 + (1.5 \times \text{IQR}) = 7.8 + (1.5 \times 3.6) = 7.8 + 5.4 = 13.2 \text{ kg}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **c)** We compare the masses to our boundaries (must be $< -1.2$ or $> 13.2$ to be an outlier):
    * **Pumpkin A (0.5 kg):** Not an outlier (0.5 is greater than -1.2).
    * **Pumpkin B (13.0 kg):** Not an outlier (13.0 is less than 13.2).
    * **Pumpkin C (45.0 kg):** **Outlier** (45.0 is significantly greater than 13.2).
    
    **d)** Classifying outliers automatically highlights extreme, improbable values in a large data set. In this context, it alerted Lucas to an obvious typographical error, allowing him to correct the value to 4.5 kg before running his final statistical analysis.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A school librarian wants to know the average number of hours students spend doing homework each week. He decides to ask the first 40 students who enter the library on a Tuesday afternoon.

       \text{Upper Fence} = 8 + 1.5(5) = 8 + 7.5 = 15.5 \text{ hours}

    3. Compare the value:
