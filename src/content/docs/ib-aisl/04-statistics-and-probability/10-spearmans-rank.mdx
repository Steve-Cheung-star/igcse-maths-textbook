title: 4.10 Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient
description: Understand Spearman's rank correlation coefficient, handle tied ranks, compare it with Pearson's correlation, and analyse monotonic relationships for IB Maths AI SL.
---

When investigating the relationship between two variables, you are likely already familiar with Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient ($r$), which measures how well data fits a straight line. However, not all relationships in nature, economics, or human behaviour are perfectly linear. 

Sometimes, as one variable increases, the other strictly increases (or decreases), but it might do so on a curve. To measure these relationships, we use **Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient ($r_s$)**.

---

## 1. Monotonic Relationships

Spearman's correlation coefficient evaluates how well the relationship between two variables can be described by a **monotonic** function. 

<Aside type="note" title="What is a Monotonic Relationship?">
A monotonic relationship is one that consistently moves in one direction but does not have to be a straight line. 
* **Monotonic Increasing:** As $x$ increases, $y$ always increases (or stays flat), but never decreases.
* **Monotonic Decreasing:** As $x$ increases, $y$ always decreases (or stays flat), but never increases.
</Aside>



If data is strongly monotonic but curved (like exponential decay), Pearson's $r$ might mistakenly suggest a weaker correlation because it is strictly looking for a straight line. Spearman's $r_s$, however, will recognise the perfect underlying pattern and output a value close to $1$ or $-1$.

---

## 2. Pearson's vs. Spearman's

You must be able to identify the appropriateness and limitations of both correlation coefficients.

| Feature | Pearson's ($r$) | Spearman's ($r_s$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Measures** | Linearity (straight lines). | Monotonicity (consistent direction). |
| **Data Used** | Raw, actual data values. | The *ranks* of the data values. |
| **Outliers** | Highly sensitive. A single extreme outlier can drastically pull the line of best fit and ruin the $r$ value. | Less sensitive (robust). Because it only looks at order (ranks), an extreme numerical distance is ignored. |

---

## 3. The Ranking Process and Tied Ranks

To calculate Spearman's correlation, we throw away the actual data values and instead assign them **ranks** (1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.).

<SteveTip title="Exam Tip: Ranking Direction">
In IB exams, pay close attention to how the question asks you to rank data!
* **Standard Mathematical Ranking:** By default, the smallest value is usually **Rank 1** (ascending order).
* **Contextual Ranking:** For things like a 'Best Seller' list, 'Race Results', or 'Highest Test Scores', the "best" or "fastest" value is often given **Rank 1** (even if it's the largest number).
* **The Golden Rule:** Always look for the pattern specified in the prompt. If the question doesn't specify, you can choose, but you **must** be consistent for both variables (e.g., both must be ranked best-to-worst).
</SteveTip>

### Dealing with Tied Ranks
If two or more data items are identical, we cannot arbitrarily say one is "3rd" and the other is "4th". **If data items are equal, their ranks must be averaged.**

**Example:** Rank the following list of numbers: `[12, 15, 15, 18, 20]`
1. **12** is the lowest $\rightarrow$ **Rank 1**
2. The two **15s** are competing for Rank 2 and Rank 3. We average the ranks: $\frac{2 + 3}{2} = 2.5$. Both **15s** get **Rank 2.5**.
3. **18** is the next number. Since ranks 1, 2, and 3 are "used up", 18 gets **Rank 4**.
4. **20** is the highest $\rightarrow$ **Rank 5**.

## 4. Calculating Spearman's on the GDC

<SteveTip title="GDC Function: Spearman's Rank Correlation">
The IB syllabus explicitly states: *"In examinations, Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient ($r_s$) should be found using technology."* You are not required to memorise or prove the formula.

**How to do it on a TI-Nspire or similar GDC:**
1. Put your $x$-data into List 1 and your $y$-data into List 2.
2. Manually determine the ranks for the $x$-data and enter them into List 3.
3. Manually determine the ranks for the $y$-data and enter them into List 4.
4. Run a standard **Linear Regression (LinReg $mx+b$)** using **List 3 and List 4** (the ranked lists).
5. The $r$ value your calculator spits out for the ranked lists is exactly your Spearman's $r_s$!
</SteveTip>

## 5. Theory of Knowledge & Aim 8: Extrapolation

### Aim 8: The Danger of Extrapolation
The physicist Frank Oppenheimer famously wrote: *"Prediction is dependent only on the assumption that observed patterns will be repeated."* This highlights the extreme danger of **extrapolation**—using a line of best fit to predict values outside the range of your actual data. Even if Spearman's $r_s = 1$, assuming a pattern will continue infinitely has historically led to disastrous failures in modelling share prices, the spread of diseases, and climate change.

### Theory of Knowledge (TOK)
<Aside type="caution" title="Does correlation imply causation?">
If a town's ice cream sales and swimming pool drownings are highly correlated, does eating ice cream cause drowning? No. Both are driven by a third, hidden variable: hot summer weather. Mathematics can tell us two things move together perfectly, but it cannot tell us *why*. 
</Aside>

    A botanist measures the time a specific species of plant takes to grow to a height of $50 \text{ cm}$ at various controlled room temperatures. When plotting the data, she notices a clear trend: as the temperature increases, the growth time consistently decreases. However, the drop is rapid at first and then heavily levels out.
    
    **a)** State, with a reason, whether Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient or Spearman's rank correlation coefficient would be more appropriate to analyse this data.
    
    **b)** Explain why predicting the growth time at $50^\circ\text{C}$ (when her data only goes up to $30^\circ\text{C}$) might be unreliable.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** **Spearman's rank correlation coefficient ($r_s$)** would be more appropriate.
    **Reason:** The relationship described is consistently decreasing (monotonic) but it is not a straight line. Pearson's $r$ only tests for linearity, whereas Spearman's $r_s$ accurately measures any monotonic relationship.
    
    **b)** Predicting outside the range of the given data is called **extrapolation**. It is unreliable because we do not know if the observed mathematical pattern will continue at extreme temperatures.
  </TabItem>

    Seven students are graded on a poetry assignment out of 50. The teacher also records how many hours each student spent writing their poem.
    
    | Hours Spent ($x$) | 2 | 3 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 5 | 5 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Poetry Grade ($y$)** | 22 | 30 | 38 | 35 | 40 | 45 | 48 |
    
    **a)** Determine the ranks for the *Hours Spent* variable, starting with Rank 1 for the lowest time.
    
    **b)** Determine the ranks for the *Poetry Grade* variable, starting with Rank 1 for the lowest grade.
    
    **c)** Calculate $r_s$ and interpret its meaning in this context.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Ranking Hours Spent ($x$): 
    * $2$ is Rank 1. 
    * The two $3$s tie for Ranks 2 and 3. Average: $\frac{2 + 3}{2} = 2.5$. 
    * $4$ takes Rank 4. 
    * The three $5$s tie for Ranks 5, 6, and 7. Average: $\frac{5 + 6 + 7}{3} = 6$.
    **Ranks for $x$:** `1, 2.5, 2.5, 4, 6, 6, 6`
    
    **b)** Ranking Grades ($y$): Order from lowest to highest with no ties.
    **Ranks for $y$:** `1, 2, 4, 3, 5, 6, 7` (Careful to match back to the original table!)
    
    **c)** Running linear regression on the ranked lists gives:
    $$r_s = 0.932 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}$$
    **Interpretation:** There is a **strong, positive monotonic correlation**. As hours spent writing increase, the poetry grade generally increases.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Weak PMCC, Strong Spearman's">
    An environmentalist records the concentration of a pollutant in the soil ($y$) against the distance from an industrial factory ($x$). After processing the data through her GDC, she finds that Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient is $r = -0.42$, while Spearman's rank correlation coefficient is $r_s = -0.98$.
    
    **a)** Interpret what the value $r = -0.42$ suggests about the scatter plot of the raw data.
    
    **b)** Interpret what the value $r_s = -0.98$ suggests about the relationship.
    
    **c)** Explain how it is mathematically possible for these two coefficients to differ so significantly for the exact same dataset.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** A PMCC of $-0.42$ indicates a **weak, negative linear** correlation. The data points do not fit closely to a straight line.
    
    **b)** A Spearman's rank of $-0.98$ indicates a **very strong, negative monotonic** correlation. As distance increases, the pollutant concentration almost perfectly and consistently decreases.
    
    **c)** This happens when the relationship is highly non-linear but strictly follows a direction. For example, the pollutant might drop off exponentially. Pearson's $r$ strictly looks for a straight line (and is heavily penalised by the curve), whereas Spearman's $r_s$ only cares about the ranked order, correctly identifying the strong downward trend.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
