title: 2.5.5 Cubic Models
description: Discover how to model complex real-world scenarios with multiple turning points, such as packaging optimisation and wind turbine power generation, using cubic functions.
---

While linear models maintain a constant rate, and quadratic models have a single turning point, **cubic models** introduce even more flexibility. A cubic curve can have up to *two* turning points (a peak and a trough) and up to *three* $x$-intercepts. 

This characteristic "S-shape" (or "N-shape") makes cubic functions excellent for modelling phenomena that rise, fall, and rise again, or for 3D geometric models where three dimensions are multiplied together.

**Real-World Connections:**
* **Engineering:** The power produced by a wind turbine is directly proportional to the cube of the wind speed.
* **Packaging & Manufacturing:** Calculating the volume of a box with a fixed surface area to minimise cardboard waste, or calculating the amount of wasted empty space in a cylindrical can of tennis balls.
* **Economics:** Cost and profit functions that factor in varying scales of production efficiency.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Cubic Model

<FormulaBox title="Cubic Function">

$$
f(x) = ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d \quad (a \neq 0)
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of the Curve">
The defining parameters of a cubic model are its leading coefficient ($a$) and its constant ($d$):
* **The Leading Coefficient ($a$):** Determines the "end behaviour" of the model.
  * **If $a > 0$ (Positive):** The graph starts low on the left and ends high on the right ($\nearrow$).
  * **If $a < 0$ (Negative):** The graph starts high on the left and ends low on the right ($\searrow$).
* **The $y$-intercept ($0, d$):** The starting value of the model when $x = 0$.
</Aside>

When interpreting a cubic model in the exam, you will use your GDC to identify the key features. Because cubic equations are very difficult to solve algebraically, you are **expected** to rely heavily on technology:

<Steps>
1. **The Roots ($x$-intercepts):** The points where $f(x) = 0$. A cubic model will have 1, 2, or 3 real roots.
2. **The Local Maximum:** The "peak" of a turning point. Used for finding the greatest possible volume or maximum profit over a specific domain.
3. **The Local Minimum:** The "trough" of a turning point. Used for finding minimum costs or lowest values.
</Steps>

<SteveTip title="GDC Mastery: Hunting for Turning Points">
Never attempt to find the turning points of a cubic function by hand in AI SL. Once you graph your function $f_1(x)$, you can instantly find these crucial points:
* To find the peak: Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 3: Maximum**.
* To find the trough: Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 2: Minimum**.
* To find the $x$-intercepts: Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero**.
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception: Local vs. Global">
Notice we call them *local* maximums and minimums. The peak on a cubic graph is not the highest point on the entire line (because the line eventually shoots up to infinity). It is only the highest point *in its local area*. However, within the practical domain of a real-world question (e.g., cardboard size limits), the local maximum is exactly what you are looking for!
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Cubic Direct Variation (Wind Turbines)">
    In environmental engineering, the electrical power, $P$, generated by a specific wind turbine in kilowatts (kW) is directly proportional to the cube of the wind speed, $w$, in metres per second (m/s).
    
    This relationship is modelled by:
    
    $$
    P(w) = kw^3
    $$
    
    When the wind speed is **$10 \text{ m/s}$**, the turbine produces **$500 \text{ kW}$** of power.
    
    a) Find the exact value of the constant $k$.  
    b) Calculate the power generated when the wind speed increases to **$15 \text{ m/s}$**.  
    c) To power a small village, the turbine must generate at least **$1200 \text{ kW}$**. Use your GDC to find the minimum wind speed required.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** Substitute $w = 10$ and $P = 500$ into the model:
    
    $$
    500 = k(10)^3
    $$
    
    $$
    500 = 1000k
    $$
    
    Divide both sides by 1000:
    
    $$
    k = \frac{500}{1000} = 0.5
    $$

    **b)** The full model is $P(w) = 0.5w^3$. Substitute $w = 15$:
    
    $$
    P(15) = 0.5(15)^3
    $$
    
    $$
    P(15) = 0.5(3375) = 1687.5
    $$
    
    The power generated is **$1687.5 \text{ kW}$**.
    *(Notice the immense power of a cubic model: increasing the wind speed by just 50% more than tripled the power output!)*

    **c)** We need to find $w$ when $P = 1200$.
    
    $$
    1200 = 0.5w^3
    $$
    
    Press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** and enter `nSolve(1200 = 0.5 * w^3, w)`.
    
    $$
    w = 13.388...
    $$
    
    The minimum wind speed required is **$13.4 \text{ m/s}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    **a)** When the sides are folded up, the dimensions of the box become:
    * **Height:** $x$
    * **Length:** The original $20 \text{ cm}$, minus the two corners of size $x$ removed from each end $\implies 20 - 2x$.
    * **Width:** Also $20 - 2x$.
    
    The volume of a cuboid is $\text{Length} \times \text{Width} \times \text{Height}$:
    
    $$
    V(x) = (20 - 2x)(20 - 2x)(x)

    $$
    V(x) = (400 - 40x - 40x + 4x^2)(x)

    $$
    V(x) = (400 - 80x + 4x^2)(x)

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Wasted Space (Cubic Geometry)">
    A standard tennis ball has a radius of $r \text{ cm}$. A cylindrical tennis ball can is designed to hold exactly 3 balls perfectly stacked on top of one another.
    
    a) Express the height and the radius of the cylindrical can in terms of $r$.  
    b) Show that the volume of the wasted space inside the can (the air surrounding the balls) is a cubic model given by $V(r) = 2\pi r^3$.  
    c) If the total wasted space in a particular brand's can is measured at **$135 \text{ cm}^3$**, use your GDC to find the radius of their tennis balls.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** * The width of the can fits one ball perfectly, so the radius of the can is the same as the ball: **$r$**.
    * The can holds 3 balls. Each ball has a height (diameter) of $2r$. Therefore, the total height of the can is $3 \times 2r = \mathbf{6r}$.

    **b)** The volume of empty space is the volume of the cylinder minus the volume of the 3 spheres.
    
    * Volume of the Cylinder: $V_c = \pi r^2 h$. Substitute $h = 6r$:
      $$V_c = \pi r^2(6r) = 6\pi r^3$$
      
    * Volume of ONE Sphere: $V_s = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$.
    * Volume of THREE Spheres: $3 \times (\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3) = 4\pi r^3$.
    
    Subtract the volumes to find the wasted space:
    
    $$
    V(r) = 6\pi r^3 - 4\pi r^3
    $$
    
    $$
    V(r) = 2\pi r^3
    $$
    
    *(This is a direct cubic variation model!)*

    **c)** We are given $V(r) = 135$.
    
    $$
    135 = 2\pi r^3
    $$
    
    Press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** and enter `nSolve(135 = 2 * \pi * r^3, r)`.
    
    $$
    r = 2.779...
    $$
    
    The radius of the tennis balls is **$2.78 \text{ cm}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
