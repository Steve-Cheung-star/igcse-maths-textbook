Not all relationships between variables involve constant addition or percentage multipliers. Frequently, two quantities are connected by a proportional relationship where one variable scales in direct response to another. 

When you study science or economics, these variation models are everywhere:
* **Physics:** Boyle's Law states that the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume. Charles's Law states that volume is directly proportional to temperature.
* **Economics:** The laws of supply and demand often rely on inverse variation (as price goes up, demand goes down).
* **Life Sciences & Medicine:** The mass of an animal is proportional to the cube of its length, while blood flow through an artery is directly proportional to the fourth power of its radius!

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Variation Model

<FormulaBox title="Direct and Inverse Variation">

$$
f(x) = ax^n \quad (n \in \mathbb{Z})
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="note" title="Direct vs. Inverse Variation">
The value of the integer power $n$ completely changes the behaviour of the model. In all cases, $a$ is known as the **constant of variation**.

* **Direct Variation ($n > 0$):** If $n = 1, 2, 3 \dots$, the variables grow together. As $x$ increases, $f(x)$ increases. The graph will always pass exactly through the origin $(0,0)$. 
  *Example: $y = 5x^2$ (Directly proportional to the square of $x$)*

* **Inverse Variation ($n < 0$):**
  If $n = -1, -2 \dots$, the variables move in opposite directions. As $x$ increases, $f(x)$ decreases. We can also write this using positive indices in the denominator, for example: $f(x) = \frac{a}{x^2}$.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="The Vertical Asymptote Trap">
When $n < 0$, you are dealing with **inverse variation** (e.g., $f(x) = ax^{-1} = \frac{a}{x}$). 
Because you **cannot divide by zero**, $x$ can never equal $0$. 
This creates a **vertical asymptote at the y-axis** (the line $x = 0$). The curve will shoot upwards towards infinity as it gets closer to the y-axis, but it will never touch it.
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Boyle's Law (Inverse Variation)">
    The pressure, $P$, of a fixed mass of gas in a sealed syringe varies inversely with its volume, $V$. This can be modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    P(V) = aV^{-1} \quad \text{or} \quad P(V) = \frac{a}{V}
    $$
    
    When the volume is **$2.5 \text{ cm}^3$**, the pressure is recorded as **$120 \text{ kPa}$**.
    
    a) **Show that** the constant of variation $a$ is $300$.  
    b) Find the pressure of the gas when the syringe is pulled back to a volume of **$8 \text{ cm}^3$**.  
    c) A student claims that if the syringe is pushed all the way in so that $V = 0$, the pressure will reach a maximum limit of $1000 \text{ kPa}$. Mathematically justify why this claim is incorrect using the concept of asymptotes.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** "Show that" questions require full algebraic working. We are given the coordinate $V = 2.5$ and $P = 120$. 
    Substitute these values into the model:
    
    $$
    120 = a(2.5)^{-1}
    $$
    
    Recall that a negative exponent means division:
    
    $$
    120 = \frac{a}{2.5}
    $$
    
    Multiply both sides by $2.5$:
    
    $$
    a = 120 \times 2.5 = 300
    $$

    **b)** Our full model is now $P(V) = \frac{300}{V}$. Substitute $V = 8$:
    
    $$
    P(8) = \frac{300}{8} = 37.5
    $$
    
    The pressure is **$37.5 \text{ kPa}$**.

    **c)** The model is $P(V) = \frac{300}{V}$. As $V$ approaches $0$, the denominator gets infinitely small, which makes the pressure $P$ grow infinitely large. 
    Because we cannot divide by zero, there is a **vertical asymptote at the y-axis** ($V = 0$). Therefore, the pressure does not stop at a limit of $1000 \text{ kPa}$; mathematically, it approaches infinity. (In reality, the syringe would explode!).

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Direct Variation with a Square (Air Resistance)">
    The air resistance, $R$, measured in Newtons (N), experienced by a car is directly proportional to the square of its velocity, $v$, in m/s. 
    This is modelled by:
    
    $$
    R(v) = av^2
    $$
    
    When the car is travelling at **$15 \text{ m/s}$**, it experiences an air resistance of **$180 \text{ N}$**.
    
    a) Find the value of $a$.  
    b) Calculate the air resistance when the car is travelling at **$30 \text{ m/s}$**.  
    c) The maximum air resistance the car's engine can overcome is **$1200 \text{ N}$**. Using your GDC, find the absolute maximum velocity the car can reach.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** Substitute $v = 15$ and $R = 180$ into the model:
    
    $$
    180 = a(15)^2
    $$
    
    $$
    180 = 225a
    $$
    
    $$
    a = \frac{180}{225} = 0.8
    $$

    **b)** Our full model is $R(v) = 0.8v^2$. Substitute $v = 30$:
    
    $$
    R(30) = 0.8(30)^2 = 0.8(900) = 720
    $$
    
    The air resistance is **$720 \text{ N}$**. 
    *(Notice how doubling the speed from 15 to 30 caused the resistance to multiply by $2^2 = 4$, going from 180 to 720!)*

    **c)** We need to find $v$ when $R(v) = 1200$.
    
    $$
    1200 = 0.8v^2
    $$
    
    Use your GDC. Graph $f_1(x) = 0.8x^2$ and $f_2(x) = 1200$.
    Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**. 
    Alternatively, press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** and type `nSolve(1200 = 0.8v^2, v)`.
    
    $$
    v = 38.7298...
    $$
    
    The maximum velocity is **$38.7 \text{ m/s}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: The Inverse Square Law (Light Intensity)">
    The intensity of light, $I$, from a stage lamp is inversely proportional to the square of the distance, $d$, from the lamp. This is given by the function:
    
    $$
    I(d) = kd^{-2} \quad \text{or} \quad I(d) = \frac{k}{d^2}
    $$
    
    At a distance of **$4 \text{ metres}$**, the light intensity is measured at **$15 \text{ units}$**.
    
    a) Write down the value of $k$.  
    b) Find the distance at which the light intensity drops to **$5 \text{ units}$**.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Substitute $d = 4$ and $I = 15$ into the model:
    
    $$
    15 = \frac{k}{4^2}
    $$
    
    $$
    15 = \frac{k}{16}
    $$
    
    Multiply both sides by 16:
    
    $$
    k = 15 \times 16 = 240
    $$

    **b)** Our full model is $I(d) = \frac{240}{d^2}$. We need to find $d$ when $I = 5$:
    
    $$
    5 = \frac{240}{d^2}
    $$
    
    Press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** on your GDC and enter `nSolve(5 = 240 / d^2, d)`. Because distance must be positive, we take the positive root.
    
    $$
    d = 6.9282...
    $$
    
    The distance is **$6.93 \text{ metres}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    The daily demand, $D$, for a new brand of luxury sunglasses varies inversely with the selling price, $p$, in USD. The relationship is modelled by:
    
    $$
    D(p) = \frac{k}{p}
    $$
    
    When the sunglasses are priced at **$150**, the company sells exactly **40 pairs** a day.

    $$
    
    They must drop the price to **$100** to sell 60 pairs a day.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Distinguishing Direct Models from Data">
    A scientist records the following data relating variables $x$ and $y$:
    
    | $x$ | 2 | 4 | 8 |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | $y$ | 12 | 48 | 192 |
    
    The scientist knows the relationship is either a direct linear model $y = ax$ or a direct square variation model $y = ax^2$.
    
    a) By testing the coordinates, determine which model correctly represents the data.  
    b) State the exact equation of the model.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** To find the correct model, test a coordinate to find $a$, and then see if that $a$ value works for the other coordinates.
    
    *Let's test $y = ax^2$ using the first point (2, 12):*
    
    $$
    12 = a(2)^2 \implies 12 = 4a \implies a = 3
    $$
    
    If the model is $y = 3x^2$, let's check the second point $(x=4)$:
    
    $$
    y = 3(4)^2 = 3(16) = 48
    $$
    
    This matches the table perfectly! Let's check the third point $(x=8)$:
    
    $$
    y = 3(8)^2 = 3(64) = 192
    $$
    
    This also matches. Therefore, the relationship is a square direct variation.
    
    *(For completeness, if we had tested $y=ax$: $12 = a(2) \implies a = 6$. The model $y=6x$ would give $y = 6(4) = 24$ for the second point, which does not match our table).*

    **b)** Because the square model worked consistently, the exact equation is:
    
    $$
    y = 3x^2
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    In marine biology, the mass, $M$, in grams, of a certain species of fish is directly proportional to the cube of its length, $L$, in centimetres. This is modelled by:
    
    $$
    M(L) = aL^3
    $$
    
    A marine biologist measures a fish that is **$20 \text{ cm}$** long and has a mass of **$120 \text{ g}$**.
    
    a) Find the constant of variation, $a$.  
    b) Calculate the expected mass of a fish that is **$35 \text{ cm}$** long.  
    c) A local fishery considers a fish to be "market-sized" if its mass exceeds **$2 \text{ kg}$** (2000 g). Using your GDC, find the minimum length a fish must reach to be market-sized.
  </TabItem>

    120 = a(20)^3

    120 = 8000a
    $$

    a = \frac{120}{8000} = 0.015

    M(35) = 0.015(35)^3 = 0.015(42875) = 643.125

    The mass is **$643 \text{ g}$** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c)** We need to find $L$ when the mass reaches 2000 g:
    
    $$
    2000 = 0.015L^3
    $$

    $$
    L = 51.087...
    $$

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Medical Physics (Fourth Power Direct Variation)">
    According to Poiseuille's Law, the rate of blood flow, $F$, in millilitres per minute (ml/min) through a coronary artery is directly proportional to the **fourth power** of the artery's radius, $r$, in millimetres (mm).
    
    $$
    F(r) = kr^4
    $$
    
    A healthy artery with a radius of **$2 \text{ mm}$** has a blood flow rate of **$80 \text{ ml/min}$**.
    
    a) Find the exact value of the constant $k$.  
    b) Due to cholesterol plaque buildup, the radius of the artery decreases to **$1.5 \text{ mm}$**. Calculate the new blood flow rate.  
    c) Discuss what this mathematical result implies about the danger of even slightly narrowed arteries.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)** Substitute $r = 2$ and $F = 80$ into the model:
    
    $$
    80 = k(2)^4
    $$
    
    $$
    80 = 16k
    $$
    
    $$
    k = \frac{80}{16} = 5
    $$

    **b)** We use the full model $F(r) = 5r^4$. Substitute the new radius $r = 1.5$:
    
    $$
    F(1.5) = 5(1.5)^4
    $$
    
    $$
    F(1.5) = 5(5.0625) = 25.3125
    $$
    
    The new blood flow rate is **$25.3 \text{ ml/min}$** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c)** Even though the radius only decreased by $25\%$ (from 2 mm to 1.5 mm), the blood flow plummeted by nearly $68\%$ (from 80 down to 25.3). Because the variation depends on the *fourth power* ($r^4$), even a tiny reduction in the radius of an artery causes a massive and dangerous drop in blood flow to the heart!
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Engineering (Inverse Square Variation)">
    The electrical resistance, $R$, measured in Ohms ($\Omega$), of a specific type of copper wire is inversely proportional to the square of its diameter, $d$, in millimetres.
    
    $$
    R(d) = \frac{a}{d^2}
    $$
    
    A wire with a diameter of **$2 \text{ mm}$** has an electrical resistance of **$15 \ \Omega$**.
    
    a) Find the value of $a$.  
    b) Find the electrical resistance of a thicker wire that has a diameter of **$5 \text{ mm}$**.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)** Substitute $d = 2$ and $R = 15$ into the model:
    
    $$
    15 = \frac{a}{2^2}
    $$
    
    $$
    15 = \frac{a}{4}
    $$
    
    Multiply by 4:
    
    $$
    a = 15 \times 4 = 60
    $$

    **b)** The full model is $R(d) = \frac{60}{d^2}$. We need to find $R$ when $d = 5$:
    
    $$
    R(5) = \frac{60}{5^2} = \frac{60}{25} = 2.4
    $$
    
    The electrical resistance of the thicker wire is **$2.4 \ \Omega$**. 
    *(Notice that increasing the diameter greatly reduces the resistance, allowing electricity to flow more easily).*
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 9: Hooke's Law (Linear Direct Variation)">
    In physiotherapy, resistance bands are often used. According to Hooke's Law, the extension, $e$, in centimetres, of a specific elastic band is directly proportional to the force, $F$, in Newtons, applied to it.

    12 = 0.25F
    $$

    $$
    
    The required force is **$48 \text{ N}$**.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 10: Distinguishing Inverse Models from Data">
    An engineer tests the magnetic force of attraction, $F$ in Newtons, between two magnets at various distances, $x$ in centimetres. They record the following data:
    
    | $x$ | 2 | 4 | 5 |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | $F$ | 50 | 12.5 | 8 |
    
    The engineer knows the relationship is either a simple inverse model $F = \frac{a}{x}$ or an inverse square model $F = \frac{a}{x^2}$.
    
    a) By testing the coordinates, determine which model correctly represents the magnetic force.  
    b) Find the exact force of attraction when the magnets are **$10 \text{ cm}$** apart.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 10">
    **a)** We must test a coordinate to find $a$, then check the other points.
    
    *Let's test the simple inverse model $F = \frac{a}{x}$ using the first point (2, 50):*
    
    $$
    50 = \frac{a}{2} \implies a = 100
    $$
    
    If the model is $F = \frac{100}{x}$, let's check the second point $(x=4)$:
    
    $$
    F = \frac{100}{4} = 25
    $$
    
    But the table says $F = 12.5$. The simple inverse model is **incorrect**.
    
    *Now let's test the inverse square model $F = \frac{a}{x^2}$ using (2, 50):*
    
    $$
    50 = \frac{a}{2^2} \implies 50 = \frac{a}{4} \implies a = 200
    $$
    
    If the model is $F = \frac{200}{x^2}$, let's check the second point $(x=4)$:
    
    $$
    F = \frac{200}{4^2} = \frac{200}{16} = 12.5
    $$
    
    This matches perfectly! Let's verify with the third point $(x=5)$:
    
    $$
    F = \frac{200}{5^2} = \frac{200}{25} = 8
    $$
    
    This also matches. Therefore, the relationship is an **inverse square model**.

    **b)** The exact equation is $F(x) = \frac{200}{x^2}$. Substitute $x = 10$:
    
    $$
    F(10) = \frac{200}{10^2} = \frac{200}{100} = 2
    $$
    
    The force of attraction is **$2 \text{ N}$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
