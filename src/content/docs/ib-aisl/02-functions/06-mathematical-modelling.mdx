Mathematics is not just about abstract numbers; it is a powerful tool for making sense of the world around us. **Mathematical modelling** is the process of translating a real-world problem into a mathematical format, solving it, and then translating the results back into the real-world context to make predictions or decisions.

In Topic 2.5, we explored various theoretical models (linear, quadratic, exponential, and sinusoidal). Now, we will apply these functions to real data, finding their parameters and evaluating their reliability.

---

## 1. The Modelling Cycle

When tasked with modelling a scenario, you are generally expected to follow a structured cycle:

1. **Develop and Fit the Model:** * **Choose an appropriate model:** Look at the shape of the data (does it curve upwards? does it oscillate?) and consider the context (does it have a natural maximum? does it decay to zero?).
   * **Determine a reasonable domain:** Decide the range of $x$-values for which the model physically makes sense. (e.g., Time usually cannot be negative; populations cannot be negative).
   * **Find the parameters:** Use initial conditions, substitute points into the function, or set up simultaneous equations.
2. **Test and Reflect:**
   * Evaluate the model's appropriateness. Does the model output reasonable values?
   * Justify your choice of model based on mathematical properties and the context of the situation.
3. **Use the Model:**
   * Interpret the parameters in context (e.g., the $y$-intercept represents the starting temperature).
   * Make predictions by evaluating the function or finding intersections using your GDC.

## 3. Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

Once a model is built, you will often use it to estimate values. The reliability of your estimation depends heavily on where the predicted value lies in relation to your original data.

* **Interpolation:** Making predictions *within* the domain of your known data points. These estimates are generally considered reliable.
* **Extrapolation:** Making predictions *outside* the domain of your known data points. These estimates are fundamentally unreliable because we cannot guarantee the model's pattern continues indefinitely.

<Aside type="note" title="TOK Connection">
*What is it about models in mathematics that makes them effective? Is simplicity a desirable characteristic in models?* A simple model might ignore complex real-world variables, but it is often easier to use and understand. A highly complex model might fit historical data perfectly but fail catastrophically when extrapolating due to "overfitting". In modelling, there is always a tension between accuracy and simplicity.
</Aside>

    A biologist is studying the growth of a bacterial culture. The area, $A$, in cm$^2$, covered by the bacteria on a Petri dish $t$ hours after observation begins is recorded. 
    
    The biologist decides to model the growth using the quadratic function $A(t) = at^2 + bt + c$.
    
    She notes the following three data points:
    * At $t = 1$ hour, $A = 5 \text{ cm}^2$
    * At $t = 2$ hours, $A = 10 \text{ cm}^2$
    * At $t = 3$ hours, $A = 19 \text{ cm}^2$
    
    a) Set up a system of three linear equations in terms of $a$, $b$, and $c$.  
    b) Use your GDC to solve the system and find the values of $a$, $b$, and $c$.  
    c) Write down the final model for $A(t)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** Substitute each $(t, A)$ coordinate pair into the model $A = at^2 + bt + c$:
    
    For $(1, 5)$:
    $$a(1)^2 + b(1) + c = 5 \implies a + b + c = 5$$
    
    For $(2, 10)$:
    $$a(2)^2 + b(2) + c = 10 \implies 4a + 2b + c = 10$$
    
    For $(3, 19)$:
    $$a(3)^2 + b(3) + c = 19 \implies 9a + 3b + c = 19$$

    **b)** Enter this system into your GDC (using the *Solve System of Linear Equations* tool):
    
    * $a = 2$
    * $b = -1$
    * $c = 4$

    **c)** Substitute the parameters back into the general equation:
    
    $$A(t) = 2t^2 - t + 4$$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Using the biological model from Question 1, $A(t) = 2t^2 - t + 4$:
    
    a) Predict the area covered by the bacteria at $t = 2.5$ hours. State whether this is an example of interpolation or extrapolation.  
    b) The Petri dish has a total surface area of $150 \text{ cm}^2$. The biologist uses the model to predict the area covered after $t = 24$ hours. Calculate this prediction.

    **c)** The prediction of $1132 \text{ cm}^2$ is **unreasonable**. 
    
    **Limitation/Justification:** This prediction is a severe extrapolation. The physical constraint of the environment is that the Petri dish only has an area of $150 \text{ cm}^2$. The bacterial area cannot exceed the total area of the dish. A quadratic model is inappropriate for long-term growth because it assumes the area will increase towards infinity without any natural boundaries. A logistic model (which levels off) would be far more appropriate for this context.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Inverse Modelling (Worker Efficiency)">
    A construction company is analysing the time it takes to build a standard modular house. The project manager proposes that the number of days required, $D$, is inversely related to the number of workers, $w$, assigned to the site. 
    
    The manager uses the inverse model:
    
    $$
    D(w) = \frac{k}{w} + c
    $$
    
    where $k$ and $c$ are constants.
    
    Historical data from two recent builds shows:
    * A team of **5 workers** completed the house in **24 days**.
    * A team of **10 workers** completed the identical house in **14 days**.
    
    a) Set up a system of two linear equations in terms of $k$ and $c$. *(Hint: Substitute the data points and multiply by $w$ to remove the fraction!)* b) Solve the system to find the exact values of $k$ and $c$.  
    c) Write down the final model for $D(w)$ and interpret the real-world meaning of the parameter $c$.  
    d) Use the model to predict how long it would take if a massive team of **1000 workers** were assigned to the build. Comment on whether this prediction and the model itself are realistic at this extreme.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Substitute the $(w, D)$ coordinates into the model $D = \frac{k}{w} + c$:
    
    * For $w=5, D=24$:  
      $$24 = \frac{k}{5} + c \implies 120 = k + 5c$$
    * For $w=10, D=14$:  
      $$14 = \frac{k}{10} + c \implies 140 = k + 10c$$

    **b)** We now have a standard system of two linear equations:
    1) $k + 5c = 120$
    2) $k + 10c = 140$
    
    Subtracting equation (1) from equation (2):
    $$5c = 20 \implies c = 4$$
    
    Substitute $c = 4$ back into equation (1):
    $$k + 5(4) = 120 \implies k + 20 = 120 \implies k = 100$$

    **c)** The complete model is:
    $$D(w) = \frac{100}{w} + 4$$
    
    **Interpretation:** The parameter $c = 4$ represents the horizontal asymptote of the graph. In the real world, this means that no matter how many workers you add, there is a baseline minimum of **4 days** required to build the house (perhaps due to concrete needing to set, paint drying, or mandatory safety inspections that cannot be sped up by adding more hands).

    **d)** Substitute $w = 1000$ into the model:
    $$D(1000) = \frac{100}{1000} + 4 = 0.1 + 4 = 4.1 \text{ days}$$
    
    **Evaluation:** While the maths gives us 4.1 days, this prediction is **highly unrealistic**. This is a severe extrapolation. In reality, fitting 1000 workers into a single house build would cause extreme overcrowding, logistical chaos, and likely slow the project down rather than speed it up. The inverse model breaks down at extremely large values of $w$.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Choosing a Model & Extrapolation">
    A new electric car is purchased for **$£35,000$**. After 3 years, its value has dropped to **$£20,000$**. 
    An insurance company wants to model the car's depreciation, $V$, over time $t$ in years. They consider the simple exponential model:
    
    $$
    V(t) = ka^t
    $$
    
    a) Use the initial purchase price to find the value of $k$.  
    b) Use the value at year 3 to set up an equation and use your GDC to find the value of $a$ (to 4 decimal places).  
    c) The insurance company uses this model to predict the car's value after 25 years. Calculate this predicted value.  
    d) Critically evaluate the reasonableness of the model used in part (c). Suggest a modification to the model $V(t) = ka^t$ that would make it more realistic for long-term depreciation.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** At $t = 0$, $V = 35000$.
    $$35000 = ka^0 \implies 35000 = k(1) \implies k = 35000$$

    **b)** Our model is $V(t) = 35000a^t$. We know at $t = 3$, $V = 20000$.
    $$20000 = 35000a^3$$
    Use `nSolve(20000 = 35000 * a^3, a)` on your GDC.
    $$a = 0.8298$$ (This means the car retains roughly 83% of its value each year).

    **c)** The model is $V(t) = 35000(0.8298)^t$. Substitute $t = 25$:
    $$V(25) = 35000(0.8298)^{25} = 35000(0.00940...) = 329.13$$
    The predicted value is **$£329.13$**.

    **d)** **Evaluation:** Predicting 25 years into the future is a severe extrapolation. A value of $£329$ is likely unreasonable because almost all cars have a baseline "scrap metal" or parts value that they will not drop below, regardless of age. 
    
    **Suggested Modification:** The current model has a horizontal asymptote at $V = 0$. A more realistic model would be $V(t) = ka^t + c$, where $c$ represents the constant scrap value (the horizontal asymptote) that the car's value will level out at over time.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Sinusoidal Modelling & Reasonableness">
    The number of daylight hours, $D$, in a specific Northern Hemisphere city is modelled by the sinusoidal function:
    
    $$
    D(m) = a\cos(30m) + d
    $$
    
    where $m$ is the number of months after the summer solstice ($m = 0$ is June 21st).
    The longest day of the year has **16 hours** of daylight. The shortest day of the year, exactly 6 months later ($m = 6$), has **8 hours** of daylight.
    
    a) Determine the values of $a$ and $d$.  
    b) Calculate the predicted number of daylight hours 2 months after the summer solstice ($m = 2$).  
    c) A student claims that because this model uses extrapolation for $m = 24$ (two years later), the prediction will be unreliable. Explain why the student is incorrect in the context of sinusoidal models.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** We are given the Maximum (16) and Minimum (8).
    
    The principal axis $d$ is the average:
    $$d = \frac{16 + 8}{2} = \frac{24}{2} = 12$$
    
    The amplitude $a$ is the distance from the average to the peak:
    $$a = \frac{16 - 8}{2} = \frac{8}{2} = 4$$
    
    So, $a = 4$ and $d = 12$. The model is $D(m) = 4\cos(30m) + 12$.

    **b)** Substitute $m = 2$:
    *(Ensure your GDC is in Degree mode!)*
    $$D(2) = 4\cos(30 \times 2) + 12$$
    $$D(2) = 4\cos(60^\circ) + 12$$
    $$D(2) = 4(0.5) + 12 = 2 + 12 = 14$$
    There are **14 hours** of daylight.

    **c)** The student is incorrect because astronomical phenomena (like Earth's orbit around the sun) are strictly **periodic**. Unlike a population growth model or stock market trend which might change behaviour unexpectedly over time, a sinusoidal model is specifically designed to repeat infinitely. Therefore, predicting $m = 24$ is not a dangerous extrapolation; it will accurately reflect the same summer solstice conditions as $m=0$ and $m=12$.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Identifying Models from Context">
    A software company launches a new app. They consider three different theoretical models to predict the total number of downloads, $D$, over $t$ weeks:
    
    * **Model A:** $D(t) = mt + c$
    * **Model B:** $D(t) = a\sin(bt) + d$
    * **Model C:** $D(t) = \frac{k}{t}$
    * **Model D:** $D(t) = c - ka^{-t}$ (Exponential growth to a ceiling)
    
    The marketing director states: *"The app will initially see a massive, rapid spike in downloads due to our ad campaign. However, because there is a limited number of smartphone users in our target demographic, the total download count will eventually level off and stop growing."*
    
    a) Match the marketing director's description to the most appropriate theoretical model from the list above. Justify why the other three models are inappropriate.  
    b) If the absolute maximum number of target users is $2,000,000$, state the value of the parameter $c$ in Model D.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a) The correct model is Model D ($D(t) = c - ka^{-t}$).**
    
    *Justifications for rejecting the others:*
    * **Model A (Linear):** Incorrect because a linear model assumes a constant, steady rate of downloads forever. It does not account for an initial rapid spike, nor does it ever level off.
    * **Model B (Sinusoidal):** Incorrect because a sinusoidal model dictates that the total downloads would go up and then *go back down* periodically. Total downloads cannot decrease over time.
    * **Model C (Inverse):** Incorrect because an inverse model dictates that as time increases, the total number of downloads approaches zero. The total sum of downloads should be growing, not shrinking.
    
    Model D is correct because it grows rapidly at first (exponentially) but the negative exponent creates a horizontal asymptote (a "ceiling"), matching the physical limit of the target demographic.

    **b)** In the model $D(t) = c - ka^{-t}$, the parameter $c$ represents the horizontal asymptote. Since the downloads level off at the maximum demographic limit, **$c = 2,000,000$**.
  </TabItem>
