A mathematical function is essentially a story about how variables interact. While algebraic equations tell this story using symbols and numbers, graphs tell it visually. Graphing is an essential tool across many disciplines: interpreting climate data in geography, tracking price elasticities in economics, or finding lines of best fit in the experimental sciences. 

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Rigour vs. Intuition:** Does studying the graph of a function contain the same level of mathematical rigour as studying the function algebraically? Algebraic methods often yield exact, universally provable solutions, whereas graphical methods rely on visual estimation and scaling. What are the advantages and disadvantages of having different forms and symbolic language in mathematics?
</Aside>

## 1. The Command Terms: "Draw" vs. "Sketch"

In IB Mathematics, exam questions will explicitly use the words **draw** or **sketch**. Misunderstanding these command terms is a common way students lose marks.

<Aside type="tip" title="Definitions: Draw and Sketch">
* **Draw:** Represent by means of a labelled, accurate diagram or graph, using a pencil. A ruler should be used for straight lines. Graphs should be drawn to scale on graph paper, and plotted points must be exact.
* **Sketch:** Represent by means of a diagram or graph (labelled as appropriate). The sketch should give a general idea of the required shape or relationship, and should include important features. It does not need to be perfectly to scale, but relative positions must be correct.
</Aside>

If an exam asks you to *sketch* a curve, you do not need to plot every single coordinate. However, your curve must pass through the correct quadrants, show the correct general shape, and clearly identify key mathematical features.

## 2. Key Features of a Graph

When transferring a graph from your TI-Nspire screen to your exam paper, you must explicitly label the "anatomy" of the curve.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Graphing Features">
Whenever you sketch a graph, ensure the following features are calculated and labelled if they exist:
1. **Axes:** Label the horizontal and vertical axes (e.g., $x$ and $y$, or $t$ and $h$).
2. **Intercepts:** The $y$-intercept (where $x=0$) and all $x$-intercepts (also known as roots or zeros, where $y=0$).
3. **Turning Points:** The local maximum and minimum coordinates (vertices).
4. **Asymptotes:** Lines that the graph approaches but never touches (draw these as dashed lines).
5. **Endpoints:** If a specific domain is given (e.g., $0 \le x \le 10$), explicitly mark the coordinates at the start and end of the curve.
</Aside>

## 3. Transferring from Screen to Paper using TI-Nspire

Your TI-Nspire is an incredibly powerful tool for visualising functions, especially those not explicitly mentioned in the core syllabus (like complex rational functions or combinations of exponential and trigonometric curves).

<SteveTip title="The TI-Nspire Window Strategy">
If you type a function into `f1(x)` and see a blank screen, **do not panic**. Your calculator is likely looking at the wrong part of the graph.
1. Read the question carefully to see if a domain is given (e.g., $0 \le t \le 50$). 
2. Press **Menu > 4: Window / Zoom > 1: Window Settings** and manually set your `XMin` and `XMax` to these values.
3. Then press **Menu > 4: Window / Zoom > A: Zoom - Fit**. This tells the TI-Nspire to automatically adjust the $y$-axis to perfectly fit the curve for the $x$-values you just entered!
</SteveTip>

When copying a graph from your TI-Nspire to your exam paper:
<Steps>
1. Draw and label your axes using a ruler.
2. Use **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph** to find exact features:
   * **Zero** for $x$-intercepts.
   * **Maximum / Minimum** for turning points.
   * **Intersection** if multiple curves are graphed.
3. To find the $y$-intercept, press **Menu > 5: Trace > 1: Graph Trace** and type `0` then press `enter`.
4. Plot these specific coordinates accurately on your paper with small crosses.
5. Finally, sketch a smooth, continuous curve connecting your plotted points, ensuring the overall shape matches the screen.
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
Students often try to sketch the curve *before* plotting the key coordinates, resulting in turning points that land in the wrong place. Always calculate and plot your critical points first, then use them as "anchors" to guide your pencil.
</Aside>

## 4. Graphing Sums and Differences of Functions

In mathematical modelling, we often combine two distinct functions to create a new one. This can be done algebraically, but it can also be understood visually on a graph by adding or subtracting the $y$-coordinates of the functions at each $x$-value.

* **Sum:** $h(x) = f(x) + g(x)$
* **Difference:** $h(x) = f(x) - g(x)$

A classic economics example is Profit. If Revenue is modelled by $R(x)$ and Costs are modelled by $C(x)$, then the Profit function is the difference between them: $P(x) = R(x) - C(x)$.
