In the modern world, mathematics is often experienced through a screen. While the algebraic manipulation of equations is vital, the ability to interpret and manipulate a visual representation of data is just as important. 

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Analytical vs. Visual Approaches:** In the 20th century, a secretive group of French mathematicians writing under the pseudonym *Nicolas Bourbaki* championed a strictly analytical, rigorous approach to mathematics, stripping away visual diagrams entirely. Conversely, the Polish-born mathematician *Benoît Mandelbrot* pioneered fractal geometry, relying heavily on computer-generated visuals to understand mathematical behaviour. Today, IB Mathematics balances both: requiring the analytical rigour of algebra alongside the intuitive power of graphing technology.
</Aside>

Whether you are analysing a production possibilities curve in economics or predicting the maximum altitude of a weather balloon in the sciences, mastering the key features of graphs using technology is an essential skill.

## 1. Key Features of Graphs

Every mathematical function possesses a unique "fingerprint" made up of its key graphical features. Your Graphics Display Calculator (GDC) is designed to find these exact points with absolute precision.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Graph Features">
When evaluating a model on your GDC, always look for these critical components:
1. **Zeros / Roots ($x$-intercepts):** The points where the curve crosses the horizontal axis (where $f(x) = 0$).
2. **$y$-intercept:** The single point where the curve crosses the vertical axis (where $x = 0$).
3. **Vertex (Turning Points):** The local maximum (highest peak) or local minimum (lowest valley) of the curve.
4. **Line of Symmetry:** A vertical line (often passing through the vertex) that perfectly mirrors the left and right sides of the graph.
5. **Asymptotes:** Horizontal or vertical lines that the graph approaches infinitely closely but never reaches.
</Aside>

## 2. Step-by-Step TI-Nspire Instructions

To master the IB AI SL course, you must be able to quickly extract these features using your Graphic Display Calculator. 

<Aside type="caution" title="American Spellings on the GDC">
Note that the TI-Nspire operating system uses American English spellings, so you must select the menu option **Analyze Graph** rather than "Analyse".
</Aside>

<Steps>
1. **Finding Roots ($x$-intercepts):**
   * Press **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero**.
   * The GDC will prompt you for a **Lower Bound**. Use the touchpad to move the vertical dashed line slightly to the *left* of the root you are looking for, and click.
   * It will then prompt for an **Upper Bound**. Move the line to the *right* of the root and click again. The exact coordinate will appear.
2. **Finding the $y$-intercept:**
   * Press **Menu > 5: Trace > 1: Graph Trace**.
   * Simply press the number `0` on your keypad and hit `enter`. The cursor will jump exactly to the $y$-intercept.
3. **Finding Turning Points (Vertices):**
   * Determine visually if the peak is a mountain (maximum) or a valley (minimum).
   * Press **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 2: Minimum** (or **3: Maximum**).
   * Just like finding roots, set the Lower Bound to the left of the turning point and the Upper Bound to the right. 
4. **Finding Vertical Asymptotes:**
   * Unlike turning points, there is no direct button for asymptotes. Instead, press **Ctrl + T** to open the Table of Values alongside your graph.
   * Scroll through the $x$-values to find where the graph breaks. Look for the $y$-value that displays `#UNDEF` (undefined). This $x$-value is your vertical asymptote.
   * Press **Ctrl + T** again to close the table.
5. **Finding Horizontal Asymptotes:**
   * Horizontal asymptotes describe the end behaviour of a graph for massive values of $x$. 
   * The easiest way to find this numerically is to open a Calculator page (or Scratchpad). If your function is graphed in `f1(x)`, simply type `f1(10000)` and press `enter`.
   * The calculator will output a value very close to the horizontal asymptote. If it outputs $12.0005$, your horizontal asymptote is $y = 12$.
</Steps>

### Dynamic Graphing: The Power of Parameters

Mathematical models are often written with **parameters**—letters that represent constants which can be changed to alter the shape or position of the graph (e.g., $a$, $b$, and $c$ in $y = ax^2 + bx + c$).

<SteveTip title="Using Sliders for Parameters">
To quickly understand how a model behaves, type an equation with a parameter directly into the `f1(x)` bar, like `f1(x) = a * x^2 + 5` (ensure you put a multiplication sign between the letter and $x$). The TI-Nspire will ask if you want to create a slider for `a`. Select **Yes**! You can then click and drag the slider to visually determine the effects of altering that parameter in real-time.
</SteveTip>

## 3. Dealing with Fractional and Decimal Asymptotes

When using the Table of Values (`Ctrl + T`) on your TI-Nspire to find a vertical asymptote, you might scroll through the $x$-values and fail to see `#UNDEF`. This happens because the GDC table defaults to stepping by whole numbers (integers like 1, 2, 3). If your asymptote is at a decimal value, such as $x = 1.5$, the table will skip right over it!

When this happens, you are not out of options. You can either adjust your technology or use a quick algebraic trick.

<Aside type="tip" title="The Algebraic Method (Highly Recommended)">
The most reliable way to find a vertical asymptote for any rational function is to look at the denominator. A vertical asymptote occurs exactly where the function requires you to divide by zero (which is mathematically impossible).

Simply set the denominator of the fraction equal to $0$ and solve for $x$. This works perfectly for any fraction or decimal!
</Aside>

### Method 1: The Algebraic Approach

Consider the function:

$$
f(x) = \frac{10}{2x - 5} + 3
$$

To find the vertical asymptote, ignore the rest of the function and just set the denominator equal to zero:

$$
2x - 5 = 0
$$

$$
2x = 5
$$

$$
x = 2.5
$$

The vertical asymptote is the vertical line $x = 2.5$. 

### Method 2: Adjusting the GDC Table

If you prefer to verify this on your calculator, you simply need to tell the TI-Nspire to count in smaller steps.

<Steps>
1. Graph your function in `f1(x)` and press **Ctrl + T** to open the table.
2. With the table selected, press **Menu > 2: Table > 5: Edit Table Settings**.
3. Look for the box labelled **Table Step**. Change this from `1` to a smaller decimal, such as `0.5` or `0.1`.
4. Press **OK**. Now, as you scroll, you will see the $x$-values increasing by your new step.
5. Scroll to $x = 2.5$, and you will clearly see `#UNDEF` in the $y$-column, confirming your vertical asymptote!
</Steps>

<SteveTip title="Graphing the Denominator">
If your denominator is a complex quadratic equation and you don't want to solve it algebraically or mess with table steps, there is a "hack". Graph the denominator entirely on its own as `f2(x)`. Use the **Menu > 6 > 1: Zero** tool to find where this new graph crosses the $x$-axis. Those $x$-intercepts are exactly the same values as the vertical asymptotes of your original function!
</SteveTip>

## 4. Finding the Intersection of Curves

Often, we need to know when two different mathematical models are exactly equal. Graphically, this is the specific coordinate where their curves intersect.

A prime example is **Market Equilibrium** in economics:
* The **Demand Curve** models consumer behaviour (usually downward sloping).
* The **Supply Curve** models producer behaviour (usually upward sloping).
* The **Market Equilibrium** is the exact intersection point where the quantity supplied perfectly matches the quantity demanded.

**TI-Nspire Steps for Intersections:**
<Steps>
1. Graph your first model in `f1(x)` and your second model in `f2(x)`.
2. Press **Menu > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**.
3. Use the touchpad to set a Lower Bound to the left of the crossing point, and an Upper Bound to the right. The exact coordinates of the intersection will be revealed.
</Steps>

    The concentration of a chemical in a reaction vessel after $t$ minutes is modelled by the function $C(t) = \frac{45}{3t - 2}$ for $t > 0.67$.
    
    Find the exact equation of the vertical asymptote and explain why the domain is restricted to $t > 0.67$.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    To find the vertical asymptote, set the denominator equal to zero:
    
    $$
    3t - 2 = 0
    $$
    
    $$
    3t = 2
    $$
    
    $$
    t = \frac{2}{3}
    $$
    
    The vertical asymptote is $t = \frac{2}{3}$ (or $t \approx 0.667$).

    **Explanation:** The domain is restricted to $t > 0.67$ because the vertical asymptote occurs at $t = \frac{2}{3}$. At this exact time, the concentration function is undefined (division by zero). In the context of a chemical reaction, time $t$ moves forward, so the model only makes sense for times strictly *after* this undefined initial mixing moment.
  </TabItem>
