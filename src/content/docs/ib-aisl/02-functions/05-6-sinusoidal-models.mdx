While linear, quadratic, and exponential models describe values that continuously grow or decay, many real-world phenomena repeat themselves in a predictable, cyclic pattern. We call these **periodic phenomena**.

To model these repeating patterns, we use **sinusoidal models** based on the trigonometric functions sine ($\sin$) and cosine ($\cos$). You will see these models used anywhere a cycle rises and falls consistently over time.

**Real-World Connections:**
* **Nature:** The rise and fall of ocean tides, daylight hours changing throughout the seasons, and annual temperature cycles.
* **Physics & Engineering:** The motion of Ferris wheels, bicycle wheels, simple harmonic motion (like a weight on a spring), and alternating current (AC) electricity.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Sinusoidal Model

<FormulaBox title="Sinusoidal Functions">

$$
f(x) = a\sin(bx) + d
$$

$$
f(x) = a\cos(bx) + d
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="note" title="The Parameters of a Sinusoidal Model">
In IB Maths AI SL, you are only required to predict or find three specific features of these models:

* **The Principal Axis ($d$):** The horizontal line that runs exactly through the middle of the wave. Its equation is $y = d$. It represents the average value of the model.
  * *Formula:* $d = \frac{\text{Maximum} + \text{Minimum}}{2}$

* **The Amplitude ($a$):** The distance from the principal axis to the maximum peak (or down to the minimum trough). It represents the maximum fluctuation from the average.
  * *Formula:* $a = \frac{\text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum}}{2}$

* **The Period:** The length of time (or horizontal distance) it takes for the model to complete one full cycle before repeating. The parameter $b$ controls this.
  * *Formula:* $\text{Period} = \frac{360^\circ}{b} \quad \text{or} \quad b = \frac{360^\circ}{\text{Period}}$
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip" title="Sine vs. Cosine">
You are **not** expected to translate between $\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x)$ in this course. Both functions create the exact same wave shape! The only difference is where they start:
* A standard **sine** model starts *on* the principal axis.
* A standard **cosine** model starts *at* a maximum peak (if $a > 0$).
</Aside>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Ferris Wheel (Interpreting the Formula)">
    The height, $h$, in metres above the ground of a passenger carriage on a Ferris wheel is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    h(t) = 15\sin(12t) + 18
    $$
    
    where $t$ is the time in minutes since the ride started.
    
    a) Write down the equation of the principal axis.  
    b) Find the amplitude of the model.  
    c) Calculate the maximum height reached by the carriage.  
    d) Find the period of the model and explain what it means in context.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** The principal axis is the constant term added at the end ($d$). 
    The equation is **$h = 18$**. (This represents the height of the centre of the Ferris wheel!).

    **b)** The amplitude is the multiplier in front of the sine function ($a$).
    The amplitude is **$15 \text{ m}$**. (This represents the radius of the Ferris wheel).

    **c)** The maximum height occurs when the sine function reaches its absolute peak (which is $1$).
    
    $$
    \text{Maximum} = d + a = 18 + 15 = 33 \text{ m}
    $$
    
    The maximum height is **$33 \text{ m}$**.

    **d)** We use the formula $\text{Period} = \frac{360^\circ}{b}$. Here, $b = 12$.
    
    $$
    \text{Period} = \frac{360}{12} = 30
    $$
    
    The period is **$30 \text{ minutes}$**. In context, this means it takes exactly 30 minutes for the Ferris wheel to complete one full rotation.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Constructing a Model (Spring Mass)">
    A weight attached to a spring is pulled down and released. The height of the weight, $h$, in centimetres relative to a desk, oscillates periodically. 
    
    The maximum height reached by the weight is **$28 \text{ cm}$**, and the minimum height is **$12 \text{ cm}$**. It takes exactly **$4 \text{ seconds}$** for the weight to complete one full bounce (from max, down to min, and back to max).
    
    The height can be modelled by $h(t) = a\cos(bt) + d$, where $a > 0$ and $b > 0$.
    
    a) Find the value of $d$.  
    b) Find the value of $a$.  
    c) Find the value of $b$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** $d$ is the principal axis (the average height).
    
    $$
    d = \frac{\text{Maximum} + \text{Minimum}}{2}
    $$
    
    $$
    d = \frac{28 + 12}{2} = \frac{40}{2} = 20
    $$

    **b)** $a$ is the amplitude.
    
    $$
    a = \frac{\text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum}}{2}
    $$
    
    $$
    a = \frac{28 - 12}{2} = \frac{16}{2} = 8
    $$

    **c)** The period is the time for one full bounce, which is $4 \text{ seconds}$.
    We know that $b = \frac{360}{\text{Period}}$.
    
    $$
    b = \frac{360}{4} = 90
    $$
    
    The final model is $h(t) = 8\cos(90t) + 20$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Negative Cosine Model">
    The height of a pedal on a bicycle above the ground, $h$, in cm, as the bike is ridden is modelled by:
    
    $$
    h(t) = -14\cos(720t) + 30
    $$
    
    where $t$ is time in seconds.
    
    a) Find the initial height of the pedal at $t = 0$.  
    b) Find the period of the pedal's rotation.  
    c) A rider requires the pedal to be at a height of exactly **$23 \text{ cm}$** to generate optimal torque. Use **nSolve** on your GDC to find the first time this happens.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** Substitute $t = 0$:
    
    $$
    h(0) = -14\cos(720 \times 0) + 30 = -14\cos(0^\circ) + 30
    $$
    
    Since $\cos(0^\circ) = 1$:
    
    $$
    h(0) = -14(1) + 30 = 16 \text{ cm}
    $$
    
    *(Notice that a negative cosine graph starts at its lowest minimum point!)*

    **b)** The period is calculated using $b = 720$.
    
    $$
    \text{Period} = \frac{360}{720} = 0.5 \text{ seconds}
    $$
    
    The pedal completes a full rotation every half a second.

    **c)** We need to solve $-14\cos(720t) + 30 = 23$.
    
    Press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** and enter:
    `nSolve(-14*cos(720t) + 30 = 23, t)`
    *(Again, ensure your GDC is in Degree mode!)*
    
    $$
    t = 0.08333...
    $$
    
    The first time the pedal reaches this height is at **$0.0833 \text{ seconds}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
