title: 2.5.1 Linear and Piecewise Models
description: Understand how to model constant rates of change using linear functions, and explore piecewise models for scenarios that shift behaviours across different domains.
---

The simplest and most common mathematical models in everyday life are linear. A linear model describes a scenario where a quantity changes at a strictly **constant rate**. If you graph a linear model, it will always form a perfectly straight line.

While the real world is rarely perfectly straight, linear models provide excellent approximations for many situations, from converting currencies to calculating the total cost of renting equipment.

---

## 1. The Linear Model

In IB Applications and Interpretation, linear models are typically written in gradient-intercept form.

<FormulaBox title="Linear Function">
$$
f(x) = mx + c
$$
</FormulaBox>

When interpreting a linear model in a real-world context, the mathematical parameters $m$ and $c$ take on specific, tangible meanings:

* **$m$ (The Gradient):** This represents the **rate of change**. It tells you how much the dependent variable ($y$) changes for every single unit increase in the independent variable ($x$). For example, a wage of $15\text{ EUR}$ per hour, or a temperature drop of $2^\circ\text{C}$ per thousand metres of altitude.
* **$c$ (The $y$-intercept):** This represents the **initial value**. It is the starting point of your model when $x = 0$. For example, a flat booking fee for a taxi, or the initial amount of water in a tank before a pump is turned on.

<Aside type="note" title="Connection to Sequences">
Linear models are the continuous equivalent of **Arithmetic Sequences** (Section 1.2). While an arithmetic sequence jumps in discrete steps (like $n = 1, 2, 3$), a linear model $f(x) = mx + c$ flows continuously for all real decimal values of $x$. The gradient $m$ is exactly the same concept as the common difference $d$.
</Aside>

## 2. Piecewise Linear Models

Often, a single linear equation is not enough. Many real-world systems change their "rules" depending on the situation. 

A **piecewise model** is a function made up of multiple different mathematical rules (pieces), where each rule only applies to a specific interval (domain) of $x$-values.

**Common Real-World Examples:**
* **Income Tax:** You pay $0\%$ tax on your first $12000\text{ GBP}$, $20\%$ on earnings between $12000$ and $50000\text{ GBP}$, and $40\%$ on anything above.
* **Mobile Phone Data:** You pay a flat rate of $20\text{ USD}$ for the first $5\text{ GB}$ of data, but then you are charged $5\text{ USD}$ for every additional gigabyte.
* **Swimming Pool Depth:** A shallow end with a constant depth of $1\text{ m}$ for the first $5\text{ m}$ of length, a linear slope downwards for the next $10\text{ m}$, and a constant deep end of $2.5\text{ m}$ for the remainder.

### Mathematical Notation

Piecewise functions use a large curly brace to group the different rules alongside their specific domains:

$$
f(x) = 
\begin{cases} 
2x + 10 & \text{for } 0 \le x \le 5 \\
-3x + 35 & \text{for } x > 5 
\end{cases}
$$

<SteveTip title="Graphing Piecewise Domains on the TI-Nspire">
To graph a function with a restricted domain on your GDC, use the "given that" vertical bar symbol `|` (found by pressing `Ctrl` + `=`). 

For example, to graph $f(x) = 2x + 10$ but only for values between $0$ and $5$, type the following exactly into the `f1(x)` entry line:
`f1(x) = 2x + 10 | 0 <= x <= 5`
The calculator will instantly cut off the line outside of that region!
</SteveTip>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Simple Linear Model">
    A car rental company charges a fixed deposit plus a daily rate. The total cost, $C$, in $\text{EUR}$, to rent a car for $d$ days is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    C(d) = 45d + 150
    $$
    
    a) Write down the value of the $y$-intercept and explain its meaning in this context.  
    b) Write down the gradient of the line and explain its meaning in this context.  
    c) Calculate the total cost of renting the car for a two-week holiday.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** The $y$-intercept is the constant term: $150$. 
    In context, this represents the fixed initial deposit ($150\text{ EUR}$) required to rent the car, regardless of how many days it is rented for (when $d = 0$).

    **b)** The gradient is the coefficient of $d$: $45$.
    In context, this represents the daily rental rate. The cost increases by exactly $45\text{ EUR}$ for every additional day the car is rented.

    **c)** A two-week holiday is $14$ days. Substitute $d = 14$ into the model:
    
    $$
    C(14) = 45(14) + 150
    $$
    
    $$
    C(14) = 630 + 150 = 780
    $$
    
    The total cost for the two weeks is $780\text{ EUR}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Evaluating a Piecewise Model">
    A city parking garage charges for parking based on a piecewise linear model. The total charge, $P$, in $\text{AUD}$, for parking a car for $h$ hours is given by:
    
    $$
    P(h) = 
    \begin{cases} 
    5 & \text{for } 0 < h \le 2 \\
    5 + 3(h - 2) & \text{for } 2 < h \le 10 \\
    29 & \text{for } h > 10
    \end{cases}
    $$
    
    a) Find the cost of parking for $1.5$ hours.  
    b) Find the cost of parking for $6$ hours.  
    c) Describe the parking garage's pricing strategy in words based on this mathematical model.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** For $h = 1.5$, we look at the domains. $1.5$ falls into the first interval ($0 < h \le 2$). 
    The rule for this interval is just a constant $5$. 
    Therefore, the cost is $5\text{ AUD}$.

    **b)** For $h = 6$, the value falls into the second interval ($2 < h \le 10$). We use the middle rule:
    
    $$
    P(6) = 5 + 3(6 - 2)
    $$
    
    $$
    P(6) = 5 + 3(4)
    $$
    
    $$
    P(6) = 5 + 12 = 17
    $$
    
    The cost is $17\text{ AUD}$.

    **c)** * For the first $2$ hours, there is a flat fee of $5\text{ AUD}$. 
    * After $2$ hours, they charge the initial $5\text{ AUD}$ plus an additional hourly rate of $3\text{ AUD}$ for every hour beyond the first two. 
    * Once a car is parked for more than $10$ hours, the cost is capped at a maximum daily flat rate of $29\text{ AUD}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Temperature and Altitude">
    A meteorologist uses a linear model to estimate the outside air temperature, $T$, in degrees Celsius ($^\circ\text{C}$), at a given altitude $h$, in metres above sea level. The model is given by the equation:
    
    $$
    T(h) = 22 - 0.0065h
    $$
    
    a) Calculate the estimated air temperature at an altitude of $3000\text{ m}$.  
    b) Write down the temperature at sea level according to this model.  
    c) Interpret the meaning of the gradient ($-0.0065$) in the context of this scenario.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Substitute $h = 3000$ into the model:
    
    $$
    T(3000) = 22 - 0.0065(3000)
    $$
    
    $$
    T(3000) = 22 - 19.5 = 2.5
    $$
    
    The estimated temperature is $2.5^\circ\text{C}$.

    **b)** Sea level corresponds to an altitude of $0$, so $h = 0$. This is the $y$-intercept of the model.
    The temperature at sea level is $22^\circ\text{C}$.

    **c)** The gradient is the rate of change. In this context, the temperature decreases by $0.0065^\circ\text{C}$ for every $1\text{ m}$ increase in altitude (or, more practically, drops by $6.5^\circ\text{C}$ for every $1000\text{ m}$ of elevation gained).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Constructing a Depreciation Model">
    A small business purchases a new commercial coffee machine for $12000\text{ GBP}$. For accounting purposes, the value of the machine, $V$, depreciates (loses value) at a constant linear rate. After $4$ years, its value is estimated to be $4800\text{ GBP}$.
    
    a) Calculate the annual rate of depreciation.  
    b) Write down a linear model for $V(t)$, the value of the machine after $t$ years.  
    c) Calculate the number of years it will take for the machine's value to reach exactly $0\text{ GBP}$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** The machine goes from $12000$ to $4800$ over $4$ years. First, find the total loss:
    
    $$
    12000 - 4800 = 7200\text{ GBP}
    $$
    
    Now divide by the number of years to find the annual rate:
    
    $$
    \frac{7200}{4} = 1800
    $$
    
    The annual rate of depreciation is $1800\text{ GBP}$ per year.

    **b)** The initial value (the $y$-intercept, $c$) is $12000$. The gradient ($m$) is $-1800$ because the value is *decreasing*. 
    
    $$
    V(t) = -1800t + 12000
    $$

    **c)** Set $V(t)$ to $0$ and solve for $t$:
    
    $$
    0 = -1800t + 12000
    $$
    
    $$
    1800t = 12000
    $$
    
    $$
    t = \frac{12000}{1800} = \frac{120}{18} = \frac{20}{3} \approx 6.67
    $$
    
    It will take approximately $6.67$ years (or $6$ years and $8$ months).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Comparing Two Linear Models">
    Sarah is deciding between two mobile phone plans that charge based on the number of international minutes, $m$, used each month.
    * **Plan A** charges a fixed monthly fee of $15\text{ USD}$ plus $0.05\text{ USD}$ per minute.
    * **Plan B** has no fixed monthly fee but charges $0.12\text{ USD}$ per minute.
    
    a) Write down a linear model for the monthly cost of Plan A, $C_A(m)$, and Plan B, $C_B(m)$.  
    b) Find the exact number of international minutes for which both plans cost exactly the same.  
    c) If Sarah expects to use $300$ international minutes next month, which plan should she choose? Justify your answer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** Plan A: $C_A(m) = 0.05m + 15$  
    Plan B: $C_B(m) = 0.12m$

    **b)** Set the two models equal to each other (or use the Intersection tool on your GDC):
    
    $$
    0.05m + 15 = 0.12m
    $$
    
    $$
    15 = 0.07m
    $$
    
    $$
    m = \frac{15}{0.07} \approx 214.28
    $$
    
    The plans cost the same at approximately $214.3$ minutes.

    **c)** Substitute $m = 300$ into both models:
    
    $$
    C_A(300) = 0.05(300) + 15 = 15 + 15 = 30\text{ USD}
    $$
    
    $$
    C_B(300) = 0.12(300) = 36\text{ USD}
    $$
    
    Sarah should choose **Plan A** because it will be $6\text{ USD}$ cheaper ($30\text{ USD}$ compared to $36\text{ USD}$).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Working Backward with Piecewise Functions">
    An online courier service charges a shipping fee, $F$, in $\text{EUR}$, based on the weight of the parcel, $w$, in kilograms, according to the following piecewise model:
    
    $$
    F(w) = 
    \begin{cases} 
    8.50 & \text{for } 0 < w \le 3 \\
    8.50 + 2.20(w - 3) & \text{for } w > 3 
    \end{cases}
    $$
    
    a) Calculate the shipping fee for a parcel weighing $7.5\text{ kg}$.  
    b) A customer paid a shipping fee of $23.90\text{ EUR}$. Determine the exact weight of their parcel.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)** Since $7.5 > 3$, we must use the second part of the piecewise function:
    
    $$
    F(7.5) = 8.50 + 2.20(7.5 - 3)
    $$
    
    $$
    F(7.5) = 8.50 + 2.20(4.5)
    $$
    
    $$
    F(7.5) = 8.50 + 9.90 = 18.40
    $$
    
    The shipping fee is $18.40\text{ EUR}$.

    **b)** Because the fee is $23.90\text{ EUR}$ (which is greater than the flat rate of $8.50$), the parcel must weigh more than $3\text{ kg}$. We set the second equation equal to $23.90$ and solve for $w$:
    
    $$
    23.90 = 8.50 + 2.20(w - 3)
    $$
    
    $$
    15.40 = 2.20(w - 3)
    $$
    
    $$
    \frac{15.40}{2.20} = w - 3
    $$
    
    $$
    7 = w - 3
    $$
    
    $$
    w = 10
    $$
    
    The parcel weighed exactly $10\text{ kg}$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Domain Restrictions and Intercepts">
    A $2000\text{ L}$ water tank is being drained for maintenance. The volume of water remaining in the tank, $V$, in litres, after $t$ minutes is modelled by the linear function:
    
    $$
    V(t) = 2000 - 40t
    $$
    
    a) Find the $t$-intercept (where $V(t) = 0$).  
    b) Hence, state the practical domain for this mathematical model.  
    c) Explain why it does not make sense to evaluate $V(60)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)** Set $V(t)$ to zero and solve:
    
    $$
    0 = 2000 - 40t
    $$
    
    $$
    40t = 2000
    $$
    
    $$
    t = 50
    $$
    
    The $t$-intercept is $50$.

    **b)** The practical domain refers to the real-world limits of the $x$-values (in this case, $t$). Time cannot be negative, so $t \ge 0$. Furthermore, the tank is completely empty at $t = 50$ minutes, meaning the model stops making sense after that. 
    Therefore, the practical domain is:
    
    $$
    0 \le t \le 50
    $$

    **c)** If we evaluate $V(60)$, we get:
    
    $$
    V(60) = 2000 - 40(60) = 2000 - 2400 = -400\text{ L}
    $$
    
    It is physically impossible for a water tank to hold a negative volume of water. The model is invalid at $t = 60$ because the tank is already empty.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
