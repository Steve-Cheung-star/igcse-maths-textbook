title: 2.5.3 Exponential Growth and Decay Models
description: Understand how to model rapid change using exponential functions, exploring real-world applications like population growth, radioactive decay, and compound interest.
---

While linear models change by a fixed *amount* each time, and quadratic models have a single turning point, **exponential models** change by a fixed *percentage* or *ratio*. In an exponential model, the rate of change is proportional to the current value, leading to incredibly rapid growth or decay over time.

These functions are arguably the most powerful mathematical tools for modelling the modern world, popping up across numerous disciplines:
* **Biology:** Population growth, the spread of a virus.
* **Physics:** Radioactive decay and half-life, X-ray attenuation, and the cooling of a liquid (Newton's Law of Cooling).
* **Business & Economics:** Compound interest, depreciation of assets, and amortisation.
* **Chemistry:** The equilibrium law and rates of reaction.

<Aside type="note" title="Aim 8: Is 'Exponential Growth' Misleading?">
In popular media, you will often hear phrases like "the company experienced exponential growth!" simply to mean "it grew very fast." As a mathematician, you know this is a misuse of the term. A linear function $y = 1000000x$ grows extremely fast, but it is *not* exponential. Exponential growth specifically requires a constant *multiplier*, where the growth gets faster and faster as the value increases!
</Aside>

## 1. The Anatomy of an Exponential Model

<FormulaBox title="Exponential Models">

$$
f(x) = ka^x + c \quad (a > 0)
$$

$$
f(x) = ka^{-x} + c \quad (a > 0)
$$

$$
f(x) = ke^{rx} + c
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="note" title="Deconstructing the Parameters">
Whether you use the standard base $a$ or the continuous base $e$ (Euler's number, $e \approx 2.718$), the structure dictates the behaviour:
* **$c$**: The vertical shift, which establishes the **horizontal asymptote** (the boundary line $y = c$).
* **The Exponent ($x$, $-x$, or $rx$)**: 
  * If the exponent is positive (e.g., $a^x$ where $a>1$, or $e^{rx}$ where $r>0$), the model represents **exponential growth**.
  * If the exponent is negative (e.g., $a^{-x}$ or $e^{-rx}$), the model represents **exponential decay**.
* **$k$**: A multiplier related to the initial value. 
  * If $k$ is positive, the curve sits *above* the asymptote.
  * If $k$ is negative (e.g., $f(x) = c - ka^{-x}$), the curve sits *below* the asymptote and grows upwards towards it. This creates a **"ceiling effect"** often used to model things like terminal velocity or an object heating up to room temperature.
</Aside>

    a) **Show that** the initial velocity of the skydiver is **0 m/s**.  
    b) Find the skydiver's velocity after **4 seconds**.  
    c) Write down the equation of the horizontal asymptote and explain what it represents in the context of this scenario.  
    d) Discuss the feasibility of this model if the skydiver deploys a parachute at $t = 30$ seconds.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** To "show that", we must substitute $t = 0$ and state every step clearly without skipping straight to the answer:
    
    $$
    v(0) = 55 - 55e^{-0.18(0)}
    $$
    
    $$
    v(0) = 55 - 55e^0
    $$
    
    $$
    v(0) = 55 - 55(1) = 0 \text{ m/s}
    $$

    **b)** Substitute $t = 4$:
    
    $$
    v(4) = 55 - 55e^{-0.18(4)} = 55 - 55e^{-0.72} = 55 - 26.77... = 28.2
    $$
    
    The velocity is **28.2 m/s** (to 3 s.f.).

    **c)** As $t$ gets very large, $e^{-0.18t}$ approaches $0$. The model becomes $55 - 0$. Therefore, the horizontal asymptote is **$v = 55$**. 
    In context, this represents the skydiver's **terminal velocity**—the absolute maximum falling speed they will reach due to air resistance.

    **d)** The model is feasible for free-fall, but **not feasible after $t = 30$**. When a parachute is deployed, air resistance massively increases, causing a sudden and dramatic drop in velocity. This model does not account for that change and would incorrectly predict the velocity staying near 55 m/s forever.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Constructing a Model from a Graph">
    A cup of hot tea is placed in a cool room. The temperature of the tea, $T$ in $^\circ\text{C}$, after $m$ minutes is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    T(m) = ka^{-m} + c \quad (a > 0)
    $$
    
    The graph of the model has a horizontal asymptote at $T = 20$. The tea initially has a temperature of **$85^\circ\text{C}$**. After **10 minutes**, the temperature drops to **$50^\circ\text{C}$**.
    
    a) Write down the value of $c$.  
    b) **Show that** the value of $k$ is **65**.  
    c) Use the GDC to find the value of $a$.  
    d) Find the time it takes for the tea to reach **$30^\circ\text{C}$**.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** The horizontal asymptote corresponds to the constant term at the end of the exponential function. Therefore, $c = 20$.

    **b)** The initial temperature is $85^\circ\text{C}$, so when $m = 0$, $T(0) = 85$. Substitute $c = 20$ and $m = 0$ into the model:
    
    $$
    85 = ka^{-0} + 20
    $$
    
    Since $a^0 = 1$:
    
    $$
    85 = k(1) + 20
    $$
    
    $$
    k = 85 - 20 = 65
    $$
    
    *(This is exactly the step-by-step working required for a "show that" question!)*

    **c)** We now know the model is $T(m) = 65a^{-m} + 20$. We are given the coordinate $(10, 50)$. Substitute these values in:
    
    $$
    50 = 65a^{-10} + 20
    $$
    
    $$
    30 = 65a^{-10}
    $$
    
    Using your GDC, press **[Menu] > 3: Algebra > 1: Numerical Solve (nSolve)** and enter `nSolve(30 = 65a^(-10), a)`. 
    Because we are told $a > 0$, we find $a = 1.0803...$
    So, $a = \textbf{1.08}$ (to 3 s.f.).

    **d)** The complete model is $T(m) = 65(1.0803)^{-m} + 20$. We need to solve $T(m) = 30$.
    Graph $f_1(x) = 65(1.0803)^{-x} + 20$ and $f_2(x) = 30$. 
    Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 4: Intersection**. The intersection occurs at $x = 24.22...$
    It takes **24.2 minutes**.

    A new invasive species of frog is introduced to a large lake. The population, $P$, is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    P(t) = 150(1.35)^t

    a) Find the initial population of frogs.

    c) A local ecologist warns that if the population reaches **10,000**, the lake's ecosystem will collapse. According to the model, in what exact year will this happen?  
    d) State one biological reason why this exponential model may **not** be reliable for predicting the population after 50 years.

    $$
    P(0) = 150(1.35)^0 = 150(1) = 150 \text{ frogs}

    $$
    P(4) = 150(1.35)^4 = 150(3.3215...) = 498.22...

    **d)** Exponential models assume infinite resources. Over 50 years, the model is unreliable because the lake has a limited **carrying capacity**.     $$
    
    The half-life of Iodine-131 is approximately **8.00 days**.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Finding 'k' Using nSolve (Heating to a Ceiling)">
    A cold piece of metal is placed inside an industrial oven set to **$400^\circ\text{C}$**. The temperature of the metal, $T$, after $m$ minutes is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    T(m) = 400 - 380e^{-km}
    $$
    
    After **15 minutes**, the temperature of the metal is recorded as **$250^\circ\text{C}$**.
    
    a) Find the initial temperature of the metal.

  </TabItem>

    **b)** We are given the coordinate $(15, 250)$. Substitute these values into the model:

    390 = 400 - 380e^{-0.06198m}
