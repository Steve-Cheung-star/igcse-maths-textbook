The circle is one of the most fundamental shapes in geometry, appearing everywhere from ancient architectural designs to modern mechanical gears and satellite orbits. While you may already be familiar with calculating the perimeter (circumference) and area of a full circle, real-world problems often require us to analyse portions of a circle. 

In this section, you will learn how to calculate the length of a curved arc and the area of a "slice" (sector) of a circle.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Circle

Before calculating arcs and sectors, we must define the specific terminology used when dissecting a circle.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Circle Terminology">
* **Radius ($r$):** The distance from the centre to any point on the circumference.
* **Diameter ($d$):** The distance across the circle passing through the centre ($d = 2r$).
* **Circumference ($C$):** The total distance around the outside of the full circle.
* **Arc:** A portion or section of the circumference.
* **Sector:** A region bounded by two radii and an arc (like a slice of pizza).
* **Central Angle ($\theta$):** The angle at the centre of the circle that creates the sector.
</Aside>

## 2. Length of an Arc

An arc is simply a fraction of the circle's full circumference. Because a full circle contains $360^\circ$, we can find the length of an arc by calculating the fraction $\frac{\theta}{360}$ and multiplying it by the formula for the full circumference ($C = 2\pi r$).

<FormulaBox title="Length of an Arc">
$$
l = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r
$$
Where $\theta$ is the central angle in degrees, and $r$ is the radius.
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="tip" title="Fact: Degrees vs. Radians">
In the IB Mathematics AI SL syllabus, you are only required to work with angles measured in **degrees**. You do not need to memorise or use the alternative formulas that use radians.
</Aside>

---

## 3. Area of a Sector

Similarly, a sector is just a fraction of the circle's total area. By taking the same fraction $\frac{\theta}{360}$ and multiplying it by the full area formula ($A = \pi r^2$), we isolate the area of the specific "slice".

<FormulaBox title="Area of a Sector">
$$
A = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2
$$
Where $\theta$ is the central angle in degrees, and $r$ is the radius.
</FormulaBox>

<SteveTip title="Exact Values and Pi">
Exam questions will often ask you to "leave your answer in terms of $\pi$". This means you should treat $\pi$ like an algebraic variable ($x$) and not convert the final answer to a decimal using your calculator. For example, write $12\pi \text{ cm}^2$ rather than $37.7 \text{ cm}^2$. If the question does not specify, you should always round to **3 significant figures**.
</SteveTip>

    A circle has a radius of $8 \text{ cm}$. Calculate the length of an arc that subtends a central angle of $60^\circ$. Give your answer exact in terms of $\pi$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the knowns**
       Radius $r = 8$, Angle $\theta = 60^\circ$.
    2. **Apply the arc length formula**
       $$
       l = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r
       $$
       $$
       l = \frac{60}{360} \times 2 \times \pi \times 8
       $$
    3. **Simplify the fraction**
       $\frac{60}{360}$ simplifies to $\frac{1}{6}$.
       $$
       l = \frac{1}{6} \times 16\pi
       $$
       $$
       l = \frac{16\pi}{6} = \frac{8\pi}{3} \text{ cm}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A sector of a circle with a radius of $15 \text{ cm}$ has an area of $45\pi \text{ cm}^2$. 
    Calculate the size of the central angle $\theta$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
    1. **Set up the equation**
       We know the Area $A = 45\pi$ and the Radius $r = 15$.
       $$
       A = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2
       $$
       $$
       45\pi = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi (15)^2
       $$
    2. **Simplify the equation**
       Cancel $\pi$ from both sides and square the $15$:
       $$
       45 = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 225
       $$
    3. **Isolate $\theta$**
       Divide by $225$ and multiply by $360$:
       $$
       \frac{45}{225} = \frac{\theta}{360}
       $$
       $$
       0.2 = \frac{\theta}{360}
       $$
       $$
       \theta = 0.2 \times 360 = 72^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Find the radius of the circle.

    <Steps>
    1. **Set up the equation**
       We know the arc length $l = 10\pi$ and the Angle $\theta = 72^\circ$.

       l = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r
       $$
       $$
       10\pi = \frac{72}{360} \times 2\pi r

    2. **Simplify the fraction**

       $$
       10 = \frac{1}{5} \times 2r
       $$
    3. **Isolate $r$**
       Multiply both sides by 5:
       $$
       50 = 2r
       $$
       Divide by 2:
       $$
       r = 25 \text{ cm}
       $$

    A sheep is tethered to a corner post of a fenced square field. The tether allows the sheep to graze in a quarter-circle shape (a sector with an angle of $90^\circ$). The radius of the grazing area is $14 \text{ m}$.
    
    a) Calculate the length of the curved boundary (the arc) of the grazing area.
    
    b) Calculate the total perimeter of the grazing area.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the knowns**
       Radius $r = 14$, Angle $\theta = 90^\circ$.
    2. **Apply the arc length formula**
       $$
       l = \frac{90}{360} \times 2\pi(14)
       $$
       $$
       l = \frac{1}{4} \times 28\pi
       $$
       $$
       l = 7\pi \approx 22.0 \text{ m}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the components of the perimeter**
       The total perimeter of a sector consists of the curved arc **plus** the two straight radii that connect back to the centre.
    2. **Calculate the sum**
       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = \text{Arc Length} + 2r
       $$
       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = 22.0 + 14 + 14
       $$
       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = 50.0 \text{ m} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
