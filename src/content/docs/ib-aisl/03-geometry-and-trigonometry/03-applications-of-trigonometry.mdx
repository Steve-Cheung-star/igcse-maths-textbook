Mathematics is a tool for modelling the physical world. While abstract triangles are useful for learning the rules of trigonometry, the true power of these concepts lies in their application. From surveying land and triangulating radio transmissions to navigating ships across open oceans using bearings, trigonometry provides the geometric foundation for spatial reasoning.

In this chapter, you will learn how to translate written problems into accurate mathematical diagrams and use the trigonometric tools (SOH CAH TOA, the Sine Rule, and the Cosine Rule) to find unknown distances and angles.

## 1. Angles of Elevation and Depression

When looking at an object, your line of sight forms an angle with the **horizontal**. 

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of Sighting Angles">
* **Angle of Elevation:** The angle formed by the horizontal and the line of sight when looking *up* at an object.
* **Angle of Depression:** The angle formed by the horizontal and the line of sight when looking *down* at an object.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
Students frequently draw the angle of depression between the vertical (like a cliff face) and the line of sight. **This is incorrect.** Both the angle of elevation and the angle of depression must be drawn starting from a **horizontal** line. Notice from the diagram above that the angle of elevation and the angle of depression are alternate interior angles, meaning they are always mathematically equal.
</Aside>

## 2. Bearings and Navigation

Bearings are used in navigation and map-making to describe the exact direction of one point from another. To ensure consistency, bearings adhere to three strict rules:

<Aside type="tip" title="The Three Rules of Bearings">
1. They are always measured from **North**.
2. They are always measured in a **clockwise** direction.
3. They are always written using **three digits** (e.g., $045^\circ$, $008^\circ$, $270^\circ$).
</Aside>

## 3. Pythagoras' Theorem

For problems involving right-angled triangles where angles are not required, Pythagoras' theorem is your primary tool.

**Pythagoras' Theorem:**
For any right-angled triangle where $c$ is the hypotenuse:

$$
a^2 + b^2 = c^2
$$

You will often use Pythagoras' theorem in contexts involving distance, such as a ladder leaning against a wall or calculating the direct straight-line distance between two coordinates.

## 4. Constructing Diagrams from Written Statements

Many IB examination questions will not provide a diagram. You must translate the written scenario into a geometric model.

<Steps>
1. **Identify the start point:** Draw a clear dot and label it. If bearings are involved, draw a North line immediately.
2. **Read sequentially:** Follow the journey step-by-step. Add new points, connecting lines, and North lines at each new location.
3. **Label all given values:** Add side lengths and angles directly onto the sketch.
4. **Identify the geometric shape:** Look for the triangle. Is it right-angled? If not, you will need the Sine or Cosine rule.
5. **Deduce internal angles:** Use complementary angles ($90^\circ - \theta$), angles on a straight line ($180^\circ - \theta$), or co-interior angles ($180^\circ$ between two North lines) to find the angles *inside* the triangle.
</Steps>

## 5. Theory of Knowledge (TOK) & International-Mindedness

<Aside type="tip" title="International-Mindedness">
The applications of trigonometry extend far beyond ancient surveying. In the 18th century, a fierce scientific dispute arose between English and French scientists regarding Isaac Newton's theory of gravity, which predicted the Earth was an oblate spheroid (bulging at the equator). French scientists used extensive triangulation and surveying methods across different latitudes to measure the curvature of the Earth, ultimately proving Newton correct!
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**If the angles of a triangle can add up to less than 180°, exactly 180°, or more than 180°, what does this tell us about the nature of mathematical knowledge?** In Euclidean geometry (on a flat piece of paper), the angles of a triangle always sum to exactly $180^\circ$. However, if you draw a triangle on the surface of a sphere (like the Earth, used in navigation), the angles sum to *more* than $180^\circ$. If you draw it on a saddle shape (hyperbolic geometry), they sum to *less* than $180^\circ$. Does this mean Euclidean geometry is "wrong"? It suggests that mathematical knowledge is often relative to the axiomatic system or the specific "space" we agree to work within.
</Aside>

    A surveyor stands $45 \text{ m}$ away from the base of a vertical communications tower. She measures the angle of elevation from the ground to the top of the tower as $38^\circ$. 
    
    a) Calculate the height of the tower, $h$. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.
    
    b) The surveyor walks $15 \text{ m}$ directly towards the tower. Calculate the new angle of elevation to the top of the tower.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the sides relative to the angle**
       The height $h$ is the **Opposite**.
       The distance $45 \text{ m}$ is the **Adjacent**.
    2. **Use the appropriate trigonometric ratio**
       We use Tangent (TOA):
       $$
       \tan(38^\circ) = \frac{h}{45}
       $$
    3. **Solve for $h$**
       $$
       h = 45 \times \tan(38^\circ)
       $$
       $$
       h \approx 35.157... \approx 35.2 \text{ m}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Calculate the new adjacent distance**
       $$
       \text{New distance} = 45 - 15 = 30 \text{ m}
       $$
    2. **Set up the new trigonometric ratio**
       Let the new angle be $\theta$. We know the Opposite ($35.157 \text{ m}$) and the new Adjacent ($30 \text{ m}$).
       $$
       \tan(\theta) = \frac{35.157}{30}
       $$
    3. **Solve for $\theta$ using the inverse tangent**
       $$
       \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{35.157}{30}\right)
       $$
       $$
       \theta \approx 49.5^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    An airplane is flying at a horizontal altitude of $1200 \text{ m}$. The pilot spots the start of a runway at an angle of depression of $18^\circ$. 
    
    a) Calculate the direct line-of-sight distance, $d$, from the airplane to the runway.
    
    b) Calculate the horizontal ground distance from the airplane to the start of the runway.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Set up the geometry**
       Because the horizontal line from the plane is parallel to the ground, the angle of elevation from the runway to the plane is also $18^\circ$ (alternate interior angles).
       Using the alternate angle method (angle at the ground is $18^\circ$):
       * **Opposite** = $1200 \text{ m}$
       * **Hypotenuse** = $d$
    2. **Choose the trigonometric ratio**
       We use Sine (SOH):
       $$
       \sin(18^\circ) = \frac{1200}{d}
       $$
    3. **Solve for $d$**
       $$
       d = \frac{1200}{\sin(18^\circ)}
       $$
       $$
       d \approx 3880 \text{ m} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the missing side**
       The horizontal ground distance represents the **Adjacent** side of the right-angled triangle.
    2. **Use the appropriate ratio**
       We can use Tangent (TOA) with the Opposite side ($1200 \text{ m}$):
       $$
       \tan(18^\circ) = \frac{1200}{\text{Adjacent}}
       $$
    3. **Solve for the Adjacent side**
       $$
       \text{Adjacent} = \frac{1200}{\tan(18^\circ)}
       $$
       $$
       \text{Adjacent} \approx 3690 \text{ m} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    Ship A leaves Port $P$ on a bearing of $045^\circ$ and travels for $15 \text{ km}$. Ship B leaves Port $P$ at the same time on a bearing of $155^\circ$ and travels for $22 \text{ km}$. 
    
    a) Calculate the size of the angle $APB$ (labelled as $\theta$).
    
    b) Calculate the direct distance between Ship A and Ship B. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.
    
    c) Calculate the area of the triangle bounded by Port P, Ship A, and Ship B.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Find the internal angle at $P$**
       Both bearings are measured clockwise from North. 
       The angle between the two paths is simply the difference between their bearings:
       $$
       \text{Angle } APB = 155^\circ - 045^\circ = 110^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the correct rule**
       We have two sides ($15 \text{ km}$ and $22 \text{ km}$) and the included angle ($110^\circ$). We must use the Cosine Rule.
       $$
       c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C
       $$
    2. **Calculate the distance**
       $$
       AB^2 = 15^2 + 22^2 - 2(15)(22)\cos(110^\circ)
       $$
       $$
       AB^2 = 225 + 484 - 660\cos(110^\circ)
       $$
       $$
       AB^2 = 709 - (-225.73...) \approx 934.73
       $$
       $$
       AB = \sqrt{934.73...} \approx 30.6 \text{ km}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part c)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Apply the Area Formula**
       We use the formula $\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C$ with the two known sides and the included angle.
       $$
       \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}(15)(22)\sin(110^\circ)
       $$
    2. **Calculate the final area**
       $$
       \text{Area} = 165 \times \sin(110^\circ) \approx 155 \text{ km}^2
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A ladder of length $6 \text{ m}$ leans against a vertical brick wall. The top of the ladder reaches a height of $5.2 \text{ m}$ up the wall. 
    
    a) Calculate the distance, $x$, from the base of the wall to the foot of the ladder. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.
    
    b) The top of the ladder slips down the wall so it now rests at a height of $4.5 \text{ m}$. Calculate the new angle the ladder makes with the ground.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Apply Pythagoras' Theorem**
       The wall and the ground form a right angle ($90^\circ$). The ladder forms the hypotenuse.
       $$
       a^2 + b^2 = c^2
       $$
    2. **Substitute and rearrange**
       $$
       x^2 + 5.2^2 = 6^2
       $$
       $$
       x^2 + 27.04 = 36
       $$
       $$
       x^2 = 36 - 27.04 = 8.96
       $$
    3. **Solve for $x$**
       $$
       x = \sqrt{8.96} \approx 2.99 \text{ m}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the new dimensions**
       The ladder length (Hypotenuse) remains $6 \text{ m}$.
       The new height (Opposite) is $4.5 \text{ m}$.
       We need to find the angle at the ground, $\theta$.
    2. **Use the appropriate trigonometric ratio**
       We use Sine (SOH):
       $$
       \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}
       $$
       $$
       \sin(\theta) = \frac{4.5}{6} = 0.75
       $$
    3. **Find the angle**
       $$
       \theta = \sin^{-1}(0.75) \approx 48.6^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A hiker starts at base camp $A$ and walks $8 \text{ km}$ on a bearing of $065^\circ$ to reach a landmark $B$. From landmark $B$, the hiker then walks $12 \text{ km}$ on a bearing of $160^\circ$ to reach a waterfall $C$.
    
    a) Show that the interior angle $ABC$ is $85^\circ$.
    
    b) Calculate the direct distance from base camp $A$ to the waterfall $C$.
    
    c) Calculate the bearing of the waterfall $C$ from base camp $A$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Use parallel North lines**
       Because the North lines at $A$ and $B$ are parallel, we can use co-interior angles. The angle inside the North line at $B$ (looking back towards $A$) is $180^\circ - 065^\circ = 115^\circ$.
    2. **Calculate the angle on a point**
       The angles around point $B$ must sum to $360^\circ$.
       The interior angle $\text{Angle } ABC$ is $360^\circ - 115^\circ (\text{co-interior}) - 160^\circ (\text{bearing to } C)$.
       $$
       \text{Angle } ABC = 360^\circ - 275^\circ = 85^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part b)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Apply the Cosine Rule**
       We have two sides ($8$ and $12$) and the included angle ($85^\circ$).
       $$
       AC^2 = 8^2 + 12^2 - 2(8)(12)\cos(85^\circ)
       $$
    2. **Solve for $AC$**
       $$
       AC^2 = 64 + 144 - 192\cos(85^\circ)
       $$
       $$
       AC^2 = 208 - 16.734 \approx 191.27
       $$
       $$
       AC = \sqrt{191.27} \approx 13.8 \text{ km (to 3 s.f.)}
       $$
    </Steps>

    **Part c)**
    <Steps>
    1. **Calculate the internal angle $BAC$**
       We know the side opposite to it is $12 \text{ km}$. We have the matching pair ($85^\circ$ and $13.83 \text{ km}$). Use the Sine Rule:
       $$
       \frac{\sin(BAC)}{12} = \frac{\sin(85^\circ)}{13.83}
       $$
       $$
       \sin(BAC) = \frac{12 \times \sin(85^\circ)}{13.83} \approx 0.864
       $$
       $$
       \text{Angle } BAC = \sin^{-1}(0.864) \approx 59.8^\circ
       $$
    2. **Add to the original bearing**
       The bearing from $A$ to $B$ is $065^\circ$. The waterfall $C$ is a further $59.8^\circ$ clockwise.
       $$
       \text{Bearing of } C = 065^\circ + 59.8^\circ = 124.8^\circ \approx 125^\circ \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
