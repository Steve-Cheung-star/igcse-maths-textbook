## 1. Coordinates in 3D Space

In two-dimensional geometry, we locate a point using an $x$ and a $y$ coordinate. To specify a point in three-dimensional space, we introduce a third axis, the $z$-axis, which is perpendicular to both the $x$ and $y$ axes. A point is therefore represented by the coordinates $(x, y, z)$.

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of 3D Coordinates">
* **$x$-coordinate:** Horizontal position (left/right).
* **$y$-coordinate:** Depth position (forwards/backwards).
* **$z$-coordinate:** Vertical position (up/down).
</Aside>

### Distance and Midpoint in 3D

The formulae for finding the distance between two points and their midpoint in 3D space are direct extensions of the 2D formulae you already know. 

<FormulaBox title="Distance between Two Points in 3D">
$$
d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2}
$$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Midpoint of a 3D Line Segment">
$$
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}, \frac{z_1 + z_2}{2} \right)
$$
</FormulaBox>

## 2. Volume and Surface Area of 3D Solids

In your examinations, you will be expected to calculate the volume and surface area of various three-dimensional solids, including right-pyramids, right cones, spheres, hemispheres, and composite shapes formed by combining these solids.

<Aside type="tip" title="Fact: Provided Formulae">
You do not need to memorise most of these formulae; they are provided in the IB Mathematics AI SL formula booklet. However, you must be comfortable identifying the correct dimensions (such as the perpendicular height versus the slant height) to substitute into them.
</Aside>

<FormulaBox title="Volume of a Right-Pyramid">
$$
V = \frac{1}{3}Ah
$$
Where $A$ is the area of the base and $h$ is the perpendicular height.
</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
When calculating the surface area of composite solids (e.g., a cone placed on top of a cylinder), do **not** simply add their total surface areas together. You must subtract the areas of the faces that are hidden or touching where the shapes join. For a solid hemisphere, remember that the total surface area includes both the curved surface ($2\pi r^2$) and the flat circular base ($\pi r^2$).
</Aside>

## 3. Right-Angled Trigonometry in 3D Space

In the SL syllabus, trigonometry questions in three-dimensional shapes will only involve **right-angled triangles**. Your primary goal is to isolate a flat 2D right-angled triangle hidden inside the 3D solid.

### Identifying Triangles and Angles
You will often be asked to find the size of an angle between:
1. **Two intersecting lines:** This is the angle formed where two straight edges meet.
2. **A line and a plane:** To find this, you must drop a perpendicular line from the end of the slanted line down to the plane. The angle required is between the slanted line and its "shadow" (projection) on the plane.

<SteveTip title="Drawing 2D Cross-Sections">
Never try to perform calculations directly on the 3D diagram! The most effective exam strategy is to draw a quick 2D sketch of the specific right-angled triangle you have identified. Label the known sides and the angle you are trying to find. This immediately turns a complex 3D problem into a simple SOH CAH TOA calculation.
</SteveTip>

2. Find the shadow (projection) of the line directly onto the plane.
3. Draw a right-angled triangle using the original line, its projection, and the vertical height connecting them.
4. Use Pythagoras' theorem or SOH CAH TOA to solve for the missing length or angle.
</Steps>

## 4. Theory of Knowledge (TOK)

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK): Axiomatic Systems">
**What is an axiomatic system? Are axioms self-evident to everybody?** Much of the geometry we study is built upon Euclidean geometry, which is based on a set of fundamental assumptions called axioms (e.g., "a straight line segment can be drawn joining any two points"). Axioms are accepted as true without proof. However, if we change the surface we are working on—from a flat plane to a curved sphere, for instance—some of these axioms break down, leading to non-Euclidean geometry. If mathematical truth relies on axioms, is mathematics invented or discovered?
</Aside>

    A wooden toy is made by attaching a solid hemisphere to the flat base of a solid right cone. The radius of both the hemisphere and the cone's base is $4 \text{ cm}$. The total height of the toy is $12 \text{ cm}$.
    Calculate the total volume of the wooden toy.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Step 1: Identify dimensions**
    The radius of both shapes is $r = 4 \text{ cm}$.
    The height of the hemisphere is equal to its radius ($4 \text{ cm}$).
    Therefore, the height of the cone is:
    
    $$
    h = \text{Total height} - \text{Hemisphere height} = 12 - 4 = 8 \text{ cm}
    $$

    **Step 2: Volume of the cone**
    Using the formula $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$:
    
    $$
    V_{\text{cone}} = \frac{1}{3} \pi (4)^2 (8) = \frac{128\pi}{3} \text{ cm}^3
    $$

    **Step 3: Volume of the hemisphere**
    Using the formula for a full sphere $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ and halving it:
    
    $$
    V_{\text{hemi}} = \frac{2}{3} \pi (4)^3 = \frac{128\pi}{3} \text{ cm}^3
    $$

    **Step 4: Total Volume**
    
    $$
    V_{\text{total}} = \frac{128\pi}{3} + \frac{128\pi}{3} = \frac{256\pi}{3} \approx 268 \text{ cm}^3 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A right-rectangular pyramid has a rectangular base $ABCD$ with dimensions $AB = 8 \text{ cm}$ and $BC = 6 \text{ cm}$. The apex of the pyramid, $V$, is located directly above the centre of the base. The vertical height of the pyramid is $10 \text{ cm}$.
    Calculate the angle between the sloping edge $VA$ and the base plane $ABCD$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Step 1: Find the length of the base diagonal $AC$**
    The base is a rectangle, so triangle $ABC$ is right-angled at $B$.
    
    $$
    AC^2 = 8^2 + 6^2 = 64 + 36 = 100 \implies AC = 10 \text{ cm}
    $$

    **Step 2: Identify the 2D right-angled triangle**
    Let $M$ be the centre of the base. The vertical height is $VM = 10 \text{ cm}$.
    The projection of edge $VA$ onto the base is $AM$, which is half of $AC$:
    
    $$
    AM = \frac{10}{2} = 5 \text{ cm}
    $$

    **Step 3: Calculate the angle**
    We need angle $VAM$ (let's call it $\theta$).
    Opposite = $VM = 10$, Adjacent = $AM = 5$.
    
    $$
    \tan(\theta) = \frac{10}{5} = 2
    $$
    
    $$
    \theta = \tan^{-1}(2) \approx 63.4^\circ
    $$
  </TabItem>

    \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = 288\pi

    $$
    \frac{4}{3}r^3 = 288
    $$

    $$

    $$

    Using the formula $A = 4\pi r^2$:
    
    $$
