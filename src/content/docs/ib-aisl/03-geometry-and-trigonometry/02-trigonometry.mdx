Trigonometry is the study of triangles, specifically the relationships between their side lengths and angles. Its applications stretch far beyond the mathematics classroom: from map-making and triangulation in geography to calculating vectors in physics. In this chapter, you will build upon basic right-angled trigonometry to solve problems involving any type of triangle.

## 1. Right-Angled Trigonometry

When dealing with a right-angled triangle, the fundamental trigonometric ratios describe the relationship between the angles and the ratios of the side lengths. 

<Aside type="caution" title="Important Notice">
These foundational ratios are **not** included in the IB Mathematics AI SL formula booklet. You are expected to have them memorised. The common mnemonic to remember them is **SOH CAH TOA**.
</Aside>

### The Trigonometric Ratios (SOH CAH TOA)

$$
\sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}
$$

$$
\cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}
$$

$$
\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}
$$

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of a Right-Angled Triangle">
To use SOH CAH TOA correctly, you must be able to accurately label the three sides of a right-angled triangle relative to the reference angle $\theta$:
* **Hypotenuse:** The longest side, always directly opposite the right angle.
* **Opposite:** The side directly across from your reference angle $\theta$.
* **Adjacent:** The side next to the reference angle $\theta$ (that is not the hypotenuse).
</Aside>

</Aside>

## 2. Non-Right-Angled Trigonometry

When a triangle does not contain a right angle, SOH CAH TOA and Pythagoras' theorem no longer apply. Instead, we use the Sine Rule and the Cosine Rule. 

<Aside type="note" title="Labelling Convention">
In trigonometry, we use a standard naming convention for triangles:
* **Angles** are labelled with capital letters (e.g., $A, B, C$).
* **Sides** are labelled with lowercase letters, such that side $a$ is directly opposite angle $A$, side $b$ is opposite angle $B$, and side $c$ is opposite angle $C$.
</Aside>

<FormulaBox title="The Sine Rule">
$$
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}
$$
</FormulaBox>

**When to use the Sine Rule:**
Use the sine rule when you are dealing with two sides and two angles, specifically when you know a "matching pair" (a side and its opposite angle) and one other piece of information. *(Note: The ambiguous case of the sine rule is not examined in the SL syllabus).*

<FormulaBox title="The Cosine Rule">
To find a missing side:
$$
c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C
$$

To find a missing angle (rearranged):
$$
\cos C = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}
$$
</FormulaBox>

## 3. Area of a Triangle

<FormulaBox title="Area of a Triangle">
$$
\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin C
$$
</FormulaBox>

You no longer need to rely on $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{perpendicular height}$. As long as you have two sides ($a$ and $b$) and the included angle ($C$) strictly between them (the SAS scenario), you can find the area of any triangle.

## 4. International-Mindedness & Theory of Knowledge (TOK)

<Aside type="tip" title="International-Mindedness">
While the theorem linking the sides of a right-angled triangle bears Pythagoras' name, diagrams demonstrating this mathematical truth occur in much earlier Chinese and Indian manuscripts. Furthermore, the earliest references to formal trigonometry originate in classical Indian mathematics. In the 18th century, the techniques of trigonometry and triangulation were used to measure the curvature of the Earth, settling a fierce scientific dispute between England and France over Isaac Newton's theory of gravity!
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Is it ethical that Pythagoras gave his name to a theorem that may not have been his own creation?** History is written by the victors, and mathematical history often attributes discoveries to the civilisation or figure who formalised or popularised them in the Western canon. What criteria might we use to judge who "owns" a mathematical concept? Is mathematics discovered, or is it invented?
</Aside>

    A surveyor is mapping a triangular plot of land $ABC$. She measures the distance from point $A$ to point $B$ as $450 \text{ m}$, and the distance from $A$ to $C$ as $600 \text{ m}$. She calculates the area of the triangular plot to be $100,000 \text{ m}^2$.
    
    Calculate the acute angle at $A$. Give your answer to 1 decimal place.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    <Steps>
    1. **Set up the area formula**
       We know the area, two sides ($b = 600$, $c = 450$), and we need to find the included angle $A$.
       $$
       \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}bc\sin A
       $$
       $$
       100000 = \frac{1}{2}(600)(450)\sin A
       $$
    2. **Simplify and isolate $\sin A$**
       $$
       100000 = 135000\sin A
       $$
       $$
       \sin A = \frac{100000}{135000} = \frac{20}{27}
       $$
    3. **Find the angle**
       $$
       A = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{20}{27}\right) \approx 47.8^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
