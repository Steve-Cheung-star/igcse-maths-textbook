Have you ever wondered how your smartphone knows which cell tower to connect to, or how epidemiologists trace the source of a disease outbreak? The answer often lies in **Voronoi diagrams**. A Voronoi diagram partitions a plane into distinct regions based on distance to a specific set of points.

Building directly on your knowledge of **perpendicular bisectors** from Section 3.5, this topic bridges coordinate geometry with real-world spatial analysis in geography, ecology, meteorology, and urban planning.

## 1. The Anatomy of a Voronoi Diagram

To navigate Voronoi diagrams, you need to be familiar with four key terms:

* **Sites:** The fixed target points of interest (e.g., hospitals, cell towers, or pizza restaurants).
* **Cells (Regions):** The area surrounding a site. Any coordinate within a specific cell is closer to that cell's site than to any other site on the map.
* **Edges (Boundaries):** The line segments separating two cells. An edge is always a portion of the **perpendicular bisector** of the line segment connecting the two adjacent sites. Any point on an edge is exactly equidistant from those two sites.
* **Vertices:** The points where three (or more) edges intersect. A vertex is exactly equidistant from the three sites whose cells meet at that point.

<Aside type="note" title="Exam Scope">
In your examinations, you will **not** be required to construct complex Voronoi diagrams from scratch using a compass and straightedge. Instead, you will be given coordinates of sites and asked to calculate perpendicular bisectors, find the equations of boundaries, identify the closest site to a given point, or calculate the area of a specific polygonal cell.
</Aside>

## 2. Nearest Neighbour Interpolation

One of the most powerful applications of Voronoi diagrams is **nearest neighbour interpolation**. This is a method for estimating the value of an unknown point based on the value of the nearest known point.

**How it works:** We assume that all points within a specific Voronoi cell share the exact same characteristic as the site itself. 
* **Example (Meteorology):** If a weather station (the site) records $15\text{ mm}$ of rainfall, we estimate that *every* location within that station's Voronoi cell also received $15\text{ mm}$ of rainfall.
* **Example (Resource Management):** In agriculture, soil nutrient levels tested at specific sites are assumed to be uniform across their respective Voronoi cells.

## 3. The "Toxic Waste Dump" Problem

A classic application of Voronoi diagrams is the "toxic waste dump" problem. The objective is to find a location that is as *far away as possible* from all existing sites (such as towns or schools).

Because vertices are the points furthest away from the sites that surround them, the optimal location to place a toxic waste dump (or a competing business wanting to maximise distance from rivals) is at a **Voronoi vertex**.

<SteveTip title="The Vertex Rule">
In your examinations, the solution to the "toxic waste dump" problem will **always** be located at a vertex where exactly **three edges intersect**. You can find this coordinate by solving a system of simultaneous equations using the equations of any two of those intersecting edges.
</SteveTip>

## 4. Using your TI-Nspire CX II

When analysing Voronoi diagrams, your graphical display calculator (GDC) is your best friend, particularly for finding vertices.

<Steps>
1. **Finding Intersections (Vertices):** If you have the equations of two edges (e.g., $y = 2x - 3$ and $y = -0.5x + 7$), you can use the TI-Nspire's linear solver. Go to **Menu** $\rightarrow$ **Algebra** $\rightarrow$ **Solve System of Linear Equations** (`linSolve`). 
2. **Graphical Method:** Alternatively, graph both edge equations in a **Graphs** page, press **Menu** $\rightarrow$ **Analyse Graph** $\rightarrow$ **Intersection**, and swipe across the vertex to reveal its coordinates.
3. **Area Calculations:** If a question asks for the area of a cell, use the vertex coordinates you found. You can use the **Geometry** tools on your TI-Nspire to plot a polygon and measure its area, or apply standard formulas like the area of a triangle or trapezium.
</Steps>

## 5. Adding a New Site

When a new site is added to an existing Voronoi diagram, the diagram dynamically shifts. The new site will "steal" territory from the adjacent cells because some areas that previously belonged to other sites are now closer to the new site. The new boundaries formed will be the perpendicular bisectors between the new site and its immediate neighbours.

## 6. Theory of Knowledge (TOK) & Enrichment

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Is the division of knowledge into disciplines artificial?** Voronoi diagrams heavily connect geography, biology, urban planning, computer science, and economics. If a single mathematical concept can seamlessly describe the spread of a virus, the territories of animals, and the optimal placement of a supermarket, does the strict categorisation of school subjects limit our understanding of a highly interconnected reality?
</Aside>

**Enrichment Connections:**
* **Delaunay Triangulations:** If you draw a line connecting every pair of sites that share a Voronoi edge, you create a network of triangles called a Delaunay triangulation. This is the "dual graph" of a Voronoi diagram and is heavily used in 3D computer graphics and self-driving car navigation algorithms.

    <Steps>

    2. A vertical line passing through the midpoint $(4, 4)$ has the equation **$x = 4$**.

    A Voronoi diagram is used to model the signal coverage of two mobile phone masts. Mast $P$ is located at $(1, 2)$. The map has been damaged, and the coordinates for Mast $Q$ are missing. However, the boundary line separating their coverage zones is clearly visible and has the equation $y = -x + 6$.
    
    **a)** Find the equation of the line that passes through both Mast $P$ and Mast $Q$.
    
    **b)** Calculate the exact coordinates of Mast $Q$.
    
    **c)** A user's house is located at coordinate $(4, 4)$. Using nearest neighbour interpolation, state mathematically which mast the house connects to.
    
    **d)** State one real-world geographical or physical factor that might cause the phone to connect to the other mast instead, despite the mathematical model.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a) Equation of Line PQ**
    <Steps>
    1. The boundary has a gradient of $m = -1$.
    2. The line connecting the sites (line $PQ$) must be perpendicular to the boundary, so its gradient is $m_{\perp} = 1$.
    3. Line $PQ$ passes through Mast $P(1, 2)$. Using the point-gradient formula:
       $$y - 2 = 1(x - 1)$$
       **$y = x + 1$**
    </Steps>

    **Part b) Coordinates of Mast Q**
    <Steps>
    1. First, find the intersection of the boundary and line $PQ$ to find their midpoint ($M$):
       $$-x + 6 = x + 1$$
       $$2x = 5 \Rightarrow x = 2.5$$
       $$y = 2.5 + 1 = 3.5$$
       The midpoint is $M(2.5, 3.5)$.
    2. Next, use the midpoint formula with $P(1, 2)$ to find $Q(x_2, y_2)$:
       $$\frac{1 + x_2}{2} = 2.5 \Rightarrow 1 + x_2 = 5 \Rightarrow x_2 = 4$$
       $$\frac{2 + y_2}{2} = 3.5 \Rightarrow 2 + y_2 = 7 \Rightarrow y_2 = 5$$
       Mast $Q$ is located at **$(4, 5)$**.
    </Steps>

    **Part c) Nearest Neighbour Interpolation**
    <Steps>
    1. Calculate the distance from the house $(4, 4)$ to Mast $P(1, 2)$:
       $$d_P = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (4 - 2)^2} = \sqrt{3^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{13} \approx 3.61$$
    2. Calculate the distance from the house $(4, 4)$ to Mast $Q(4, 5)$:
       $$d_Q = \sqrt{(4 - 4)^2 + (4 - 5)^2} = \sqrt{0^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{1} = 1$$
    3. Because 1 is less than 3.61, the house is mathematically closer to Mast $Q$. It connects to **Mast $Q$**.
    </Steps>

    **Part d) Real-World Limitations**
    <Steps>
    1. While mathematically closer to $Q$, the signal might be blocked by physical barriers (e.g., a large hill, dense forest, or a tall concrete building) between the house and Mast $Q$. Alternatively, Mast $Q$ might be at maximum network capacity, forcing the phone to connect to Mast $P$. *(Any logical physical or technical barrier is an acceptable answer).*
    </Steps>
