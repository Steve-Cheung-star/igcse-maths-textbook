In calculus, the derivative $f'(x)$ is more than just a formula for gradients; it is a visual tool that describes the "behaviour" of a function. By analysing the sign of the derivative (whether it is positive, negative, or zero), we can determine exactly where a function graph is climbing upwards or sliding downwards.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of Function Behaviour

A function's behaviour is defined by how its output ($y$) changes as its input ($x$) increases (moving from left to right across the graph).

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Function Direction">
* **Increasing Functions:** As $x$ increases, $y$ also increases. The graph moves **upwards** from left to right.
* **Decreasing Functions:** As $x$ increases, $y$ decreases. The graph moves **downwards** from left to right.
* **Stationary Points:** Points where the function is momentarily neither increasing nor decreasing (the graph has a horizontal peak or valley).
</Aside>

---

## 2. Graphical Interpretation of $f'(x)$

The derivative $f'(x)$ represents the gradient of the tangent to the curve at any point. The IB syllabus heavily emphasises interpreting this visually:

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem: Derivative Sign Test">
For a function $f(x)$ over an interval:
* If **$f'(x) > 0$**, the function is **increasing** (the tangent slopes upwards).
* If **$f'(x) < 0$**, the function is **decreasing** (the tangent slopes downwards).
* If **$f'(x) = 0$**, the function has a **stationary point** (the tangent is perfectly horizontal).
</Aside>

## 3. Identifying Intervals from Graphs

In the exam, you may not be given an equation. You will often be presented with a graph of either $f(x)$ or its derivative $f'(x)$ and asked to determine the intervals of increase or decrease.

<Steps>
1. **If given the graph of $f(x)$:** Look at the physical direction of the curve. Where is it pointing up ($f'(x) > 0$)? Where is it pointing down ($f'(x) < 0$)? The turning points dictate the boundaries.
2. **If given the graph of $f'(x)$:** Look at the $y$-values of the derivative graph. Where is the line *above* the x-axis ($f'(x) > 0$)? Where is it *below* the x-axis ($f'(x) < 0$)? The x-intercepts dictate the boundaries.
3. **Write the interval** using inequalities (e.g., $1 < x < 5$ or $x > 3$).
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="The Most Common Mistake">
When given a graph of the **derivative** $y = f'(x)$, students often mistakenly look for where the graph is pointing upwards. **Do not do this!** For a derivative graph, you only care if the line is vertically **above or below** the $x$-axis. The actual "slope" of the derivative graph itself represents the second derivative, not the first.
</Aside>
