Calculus is fundamentally composed of two main operations that are the reverse of one another: differentiation and **integration**. While differentiation breaks a function down to find its rate of change (the gradient), integration builds it back up. This reverse process is formally known as **anti-differentiation**.

Integration has profound implications in the real world. For instance, in physics and sports science, if you have a function that models the velocity of a runner over time, integrating that function allows you to calculate the total distance they have travelled. 

---

## 1. Indefinite Integrals and the Power Rule

Since integration is the reverse of differentiation, we need to reverse the algebraic steps of the power rule. When differentiating, we multiplied by the power and then subtracted 1 from the power. To integrate, we do the exact opposite in reverse order:

<Steps>
1. **Increase the power:** Add 1 to the current exponent of the variable.
2. **Divide by the new power:** Divide the entire term by this newly created exponent.
3. **Add the constant of integration:** Always add $+ C$ at the end of indefinite integrals.
</Steps>

<FormulaBox title="Integral of x^n">
$$
\int x^n \mathrm{d}x = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C, \quad n \neq -1
$$
</FormulaBox>

The elongated "S" symbol $\int$ stands for integral, and the $\mathrm{d}x$ at the end tells us that we are integrating with respect to the variable $x$. 

### The Constant of Integration ($C$)
When we differentiate any constant number (like 5, -12, or 100), it becomes 0 and disappears. Therefore, when we go backward and integrate, we have no idea what original constant might have been there! To account for this missing information, we always add a capital $C$ (the constant of integration) to our answer. An integral with a $+ C$ is called an **indefinite integral**.

*Note: As noted in the formula, this rule works for all integers except $n = -1$. Integrating $x^{-1}$ involves logarithms, which is beyond the scope of the AI SL syllabus.*

To find $C$, you simply integrate the function to get your general equation, and then substitute the $x$ and $y$ values from your boundary condition to solve for $C$.

<Aside type="tip" title="Worked Example: Finding C">
**Question:** If $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + x$ and $y = 10$ when $x = 1$, find the specific equation for $y$.

**Step 1: Integrate the derivative to find the general equation for $y$.**

$$
y = \int (3x^2 + x) \mathrm{d}x
$$

$$
y = \frac{3x^3}{3} + \frac{x^2}{2} + C
$$

$$
y = x^3 + 0.5x^2 + C
$$

**Step 2: Substitute the boundary condition ($x = 1$, $y = 10$).**

$$
10 = (1)^3 + 0.5(1)^2 + C
$$

$$
10 = 1 + 0.5 + C
$$

$$
10 = 1.5 + C
$$

$$
C = 8.5
$$

**Step 3: Write the final specific equation.**

$$
y = x^3 + 0.5x^2 + 8.5
$$
</Aside>

## 3. Definite Integrals and Area Under a Curve

While indefinite integrals give us a function, **definite integrals** give us a specific numerical value. This value represents the exact area enclosed between the curve $y = f(x)$, the $x$-axis, and two vertical boundary lines $x = a$ and $x = b$ (where $f(x) > 0$).

<FormulaBox title="Area of a region enclosed by a curve and the x-axis">
$$
A = \int_a^b y \mathrm{d}x
$$
</FormulaBox>

Definite integrals are written with small numbers at the top and bottom of the integral sign, which indicate the boundaries (or limits) of the area you are calculating:

$$
\int_{\text{lower limit}}^{\text{upper limit}} f(x) \mathrm{d}x
$$

<Aside type="caution" title="Important Exam Technique">
The IB syllabus explicitly states that you are expected to write a correct mathematical expression *before* calculating the area. Even if you plan to use your calculator to find the final answer, you must write out the integral to secure your method marks.

For example, if asked to find the area under $y = 3x^2 + 4$ between $x = 2$ and $x = 6$, you must write:

$$
\text{Area} = \int_2^6 (3x^2 + 4) \mathrm{d}x
$$

</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
Calculus allows us to take dynamic, changing phenomena—like a runner's fluctuating speed or the curving physical boundaries of a landscape—and distil them into precise numerical areas and mathematical models. **Is it possible for an area of knowledge to describe the world without transforming it?** When we model a physical area with a definite integral, are we objectively capturing reality, or are we fundamentally altering it by forcing it into a structured mathematical framework?
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Basic Indefinite Integration">
    Find the following indefinite integrals:
    
    a) $\int 5x^4 \mathrm{d}x$
    
    b) $\int (3x^2 - 2x + 1) \mathrm{d}x$
    
    c) $\int \left(\frac{1}{2}x^3 + 4x\right) \mathrm{d}x$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    Apply the reverse power rule to each term: increase the power by 1 and divide by the new power. Always add $+ C$.
    
    **Part a)**
    
    $$
    \int 5x^4 \mathrm{d}x = \frac{5x^5}{5} + C = x^5 + C
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    
    $$
    \int (3x^2 - 2x + 1) \mathrm{d}x = \frac{3x^3}{3} - \frac{2x^2}{2} + 1x + C
    $$
    
    $$
    = x^3 - x^2 + x + C
    $$
    
    **Part c)**
    
    $$
    \int \left(\frac{1}{2}x^3 + 4x\right) \mathrm{d}x = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{x^4}{4}\right) + \frac{4x^2}{2} + C
    $$
    
    $$
    = \frac{1}{8}x^4 + 2x^2 + C
    $$
    
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Using Boundary Conditions">
    a) Find the indefinite integral $\int (6x^2 - 4) \mathrm{d}x$.
    
    b) Given that $y = \int (6x^2 - 4) \mathrm{d}x$ and the curve passes through the point $(2, 10)$, find the specific equation for $y$.

    $$

    $$
    
    $$
    10 = 2(8) - 8 + C
    $$
    
    $$
    10 = 16 - 8 + C
    $$
    
    $$
    10 = 8 + C \implies C = 2
    $$
    
    The specific equation is:
    
    $$
    y = 2x^3 - 4x + 2
    $$
    
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Curves from Gradients">
    A curve has a gradient function given by $\frac{dy}{dx} = -2x + 6$. 
    
    a) Given that the curve passes through the point $(1, 8)$, find the equation of the curve.
    
    b) Find the $y$-intercept of this curve.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Part a)**
    Integrate the gradient function to find the curve $y$:
    
    $$
    y = \int (-2x + 6) \mathrm{d}x
    $$
    
    $$
    y = -x^2 + 6x + C
    $$

    $$
    8 = -(1)^2 + 6(1) + C
    $$
    
    $$
    8 = -1 + 6 + C
    $$
    
    $$
    8 = 5 + C \implies C = 3
    $$
    
    The equation is $y = -x^2 + 6x + 3$.

    **Part b)**
    The $y$-intercept occurs where $x = 0$. 
    
    $$
    y = -(0)^2 + 6(0) + 3 = 3
    $$
    
    The $y$-intercept is $(0, 3)$. Notice that for polynomials, the $y$-intercept is always exactly the value of $C$.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 9: Kinematics - Finding Velocity">
    The acceleration of a drone, $a(t)$ in $\text{m s}^{-2}$, is given by the function $a(t) = 4t - 2$. 
    
    a) Given that the drone has a velocity of $3 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ when $t = 1$ second, find the specific velocity function $v(t)$.
    
    b) Find the velocity of the drone at $t = 4$ seconds.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **Part a)**
    Integrating acceleration gives velocity.
    
    $$
    v(t) = \int (4t - 2) \mathrm{d}t
    $$
    
    $$
    v(t) = \frac{4t^2}{2} - 2t + C = 2t^2 - 2t + C
    $$
    
    Substitute the boundary condition ($t = 1$, $v = 3$):
    
    $$
    3 = 2(1)^2 - 2(1) + C
    $$
    
    $$
    3 = 2 - 2 + C \implies C = 3
    $$
    
    The specific velocity function is:
    
    $$
    v(t) = 2t^2 - 2t + 3
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Substitute $t = 4$ into your new velocity function:
    
    $$
    v(4) = 2(4)^2 - 2(4) + 3
    $$
    
    $$
    v(4) = 2(16) - 8 + 3 = 32 - 8 + 3 = 27 \text{ m s}^{-1}
    $$
    
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 10: Economics Application">
    The marginal cost of producing $x$ bespoke chairs is given by the derivative $C'(x) = 0.2x + 15$. 
    
    a) Integrate the marginal cost to find the general total cost function, $C(x)$.
    
    b) If the fixed costs (the cost before any chairs are produced, $x=0$) are \$500, find the specific cost function and calculate the total cost of producing 50 chairs.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 10">
    **Part a)**
    
    $$
    C(x) = \int (0.2x + 15) \mathrm{d}x
    $$
    
    $$
    C(x) = \frac{0.2x^2}{2} + 15x + K
    $$
    
    *(Using $K$ for the constant of integration to avoid confusion with Cost $C$)*
    
    $$
    C(x) = 0.1x^2 + 15x + K
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Fixed costs mean when $x = 0$, $C(0) = 500$.
    
    $$
    500 = 0.1(0)^2 + 15(0) + K \implies K = 500
    $$
    
    The specific cost function is:
    
    $$
    C(x) = 0.1x^2 + 15x + 500
    $$
    
    To find the cost of 50 chairs, substitute $x = 50$:
    
    $$
    C(50) = 0.1(50)^2 + 15(50) + 500
    $$
    
    $$
    C(50) = 0.1(2500) + 750 + 500
    $$
    
    $$
    C(50) = 250 + 750 + 500 = 1500
    $$
    
    The total cost is **\$1500**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
