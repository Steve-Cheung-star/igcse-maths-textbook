Calculus is not just an abstract mathematical concept; it is the engine behind efficiency. Every time a manufacturer decides on the dimensions of a soda can, or a business sets the price of a new product, they are trying to find the "best" possible outcome. In mathematics, this process of finding the maximum or minimum value in a practical situation is called **optimisation**.

In this section, we take the skills developed in finding turning points (where the derivative equals zero) and apply them to real-world contexts such as business economics, agricultural yield, and material packaging. 

*Note: While optimisation is heavily used in physics for kinematics (velocity and acceleration), the IB Maths AI SL syllabus specifically excludes kinematics from examinations in this topic.*

## 1. The Optimisation Process

Tackling a contextual word problem can feel overwhelming. However, every optimisation problem can be solved using the same systematic approach.

<Steps>
1. **Identify the Goal:** Read the question carefully to determine what needs to be maximised (e.g., profit, volume, area) or minimised (e.g., cost, surface area, materials). 
2. **Formulate the Equation:** Write an equation for the quantity you are trying to optimise. Often, this requires using a secondary constraint given in the question to ensure your equation only has *one* independent variable (usually $x$).
3. **Differentiate:** Find the derivative of your formulated function.
4. **Set to Zero and Solve:** Set the derivative equal to zero to locate the turning point, and solve for the variable.
5. **Answer the Specific Question:** Read the final sentence of the question again. Does it ask for the $x$-value, or does it ask for the *maximum profit* itself? Substitute your $x$-value back into the original equations as necessary.
</Steps>

## 2. Business and Economics: Profit and Cost

A large portion of contextual optimisation in AI SL focuses on business applications. You must be comfortable with the following relationships:

* **Revenue ($R$):** The total money brought in by a business. (Revenue = Price per item $\times$ Quantity sold).
* **Cost ($C$):** The total expense to manufacture the items.
* **Profit ($P$):** The money a business actually keeps. 

The relationship between these is defined as: Profit = Revenue - Cost

<FormulaBox title="Profit Function">
$$
P(x) = R(x) - C(x)
$$
</FormulaBox>

To maximise profit, a business wants to find the exact production level $x$ where the derivative $P'(x) = 0$. Similarly, to minimise manufacturing expenses, they look for where $C'(x) = 0$.

## 3. Geometric Packaging: Area and Volume

Another classic optimisation scenario involves packaging. Companies want to **maximise the volume** (capacity) of a container while **minimising the surface area** (the material used to build it) to keep costs down.

These problems almost always require a two-step algebraic setup before you can differentiate:
1. You will be given a fixed constraint (e.g., "The surface area is exactly $600$ cm$^2$").
2. You must rearrange this constraint equation to isolate one variable (like height, $h$), and substitute it into the formula you want to optimise (like Volume).

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK): Economics and the Environment">
Governments worldwide are increasingly implementing taxes on single-use plastics and packaging (like plastic bag charges or bottle taxes). **How can this rise in tax be justified using mathematical optimisation?** When a company optimises packaging without a tax, they look strictly at manufacturing costs. By imposing a tax based on the surface area or weight of plastic used, governments mathematically force the company's "Cost Function" to change. The new optimum point (where $C'(x) = 0$) mathematically pushes the manufacturer toward using less material, achieving allocative efficiency and reducing environmental impact. Does mathematics merely describe economic reality, or can it be used as a legislative tool to engineer societal behaviour?
</Aside>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Maximising Profit">
    A technology company produces wireless earbuds. The daily profit, $P$ in EUR, made from selling $x$ pairs of earbuds is modelled by the function:
    
    $$
    P(x) = -0.2x^2 + 160x - 4500
    $$
    
    a) Find the marginal profit function, $P'(x)$.  
    b) Show that the number of pairs of earbuds the company must sell to maximise their daily profit is $400$.  
    c) Calculate the maximum possible daily profit.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a)**
    Differentiate the profit function using the power rule:
    
    $$
    P'(x) = -0.4x + 160
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Maximum profit occurs at the turning point, where the derivative equals zero.
    
    $$
    -0.4x + 160 = 0
    $$
    
    $$
    0.4x = 160
    $$
    
    $$
    x = \frac{160}{0.4} = 400
    $$
    
    The company must sell $400$ pairs of earbuds (Shown).

    **Part c)**
    Substitute $x = 400$ back into the *original* profit function.
    
    $$
    P(400) = -0.2(400)^2 + 160(400) - 4500
    $$
    
    $$
    P(400) = -0.2(160000) + 64000 - 4500
    $$
    
    $$
    P(400) = -32000 + 64000 - 4500 = 27500
    $$
    
    The maximum daily profit is $27500$ EUR.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Minimising Average Cost">
    A furniture manufacturer finds that the average cost, $C$ in USD, of producing $x$ bespoke chairs per week is given by:
    
    $$
    C(x) = 0.5x + 15 + \frac{800}{x}, \quad x > 0
    $$
    
    a) Find $C'(x)$.  
    b) Determine the number of chairs that should be produced each week to minimise the average cost.  
    c) Hence, find the minimum average cost per chair.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Part a)**
    First, rewrite the function using negative indices:
    
    $$
    C(x) = 0.5x + 15 + 800x^{-1}
    $$
    
    Now differentiate:
    
    $$
    C'(x) = 0.5 - 800x^{-2} = 0.5 - \frac{800}{x^2}
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Set the derivative to zero to find the minimum point.
    
    $$
    0.5 - \frac{800}{x^2} = 0 \implies 0.5 = \frac{800}{x^2}
    $$
    
    Multiply both sides by $x^2$:
    
    $$
    0.5x^2 = 800 \implies x^2 = 1600
    $$
    
    $$
    x = 40 \quad \text{(We reject negative } 40 \text{ as } x > 0 \text{)}
    $$
    
    They should produce $40$ chairs.

    **Part c)**
    Substitute $x = 40$ into the original average cost function.
    
    $$
    C(40) = 0.5(40) + 15 + \frac{800}{40} = 20 + 15 + 20 = 55
    $$
    
    The minimum average cost is $55$ USD per chair.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Geometric Packaging (Constrained Volume)">
    A closed rectangular cardboard box is designed to have a square base with side length $x$ cm and a height of $h$ cm.
    The total surface area of the cardboard used to make the box is exactly $600$ cm$^2$.
    
    a) Show that the height $h$ can be expressed as $h = \frac{150}{x} - 0.5x$.  
    b) Show that the volume $V$ of the box is given by $V(x) = 150x - 0.5x^3$.  
    c) Use calculus to find the value of $x$ that maximises the volume of the box.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Part a)**
    The box is closed, so it has 2 square bases (top and bottom) and 4 rectangular sides.
    Surface Area = Area of bases + Area of sides
    
    $$
    SA = 2x^2 + 4xh
    $$
    
    We are given $SA = 600$:
    
    $$
    2x^2 + 4xh = 600
    $$
    
    Rearrange to isolate $h$ by subtracting $2x^2$ from both sides:
    
    $$
    4xh = 600 - 2x^2
    $$
    
    Divide the entire equation by $4x$:
    
    $$
    h = \frac{600 - 2x^2}{4x}
    $$
    
    Split the fraction to simplify:
    
    $$
    h = \frac{600}{4x} - \frac{2x^2}{4x}
    $$
    
    $$
    h = \frac{150}{x} - 0.5x \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    The volume of a cuboid is $V = \text{length} \times \text{width} \times \text{height}$.
    
    $$
    V = x \times x \times h = x^2h
    $$
    
    Substitute the expression for $h$ from part a:
    
    $$
    V = x^2\left(\frac{150}{x} - 0.5x\right)
    $$
    
    Expand the brackets:
    
    $$
    V(x) = 150x - 0.5x^3 \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$
    
    **Part c)**
    To maximise volume, find $\frac{dV}{dx}$ and set it to zero.
    
    $$
    \frac{dV}{dx} = 150 - 1.5x^2 = 0
    $$
    
    $$
    1.5x^2 = 150 \implies x^2 = 100 \implies x = 10 \quad \text{(Reject negative)}
    $$
    
    The side length that maximises the volume is $x = 10$ cm.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Part a)**
    The fencing consists of 3 perpendicular sections of length $x$ (the two outer widths and the middle divider) and 1 horizontal section of total length $y$ (the wall covers the other horizontal side).
    
    $$
    3x + y = 120
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    We need to express Area ($A = xy$) entirely in terms of $x$. First, rearrange the constraint equation to isolate $y$:
    
    $$
    y = 120 - 3x
    $$
    
    Now substitute this expression for $y$ into the area formula:
    
    $$
    A = x(120 - 3x)
    $$
    
    Expand the brackets:
    
    $$
    A(x) = 120x - 3x^2 \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$

    To maximise Area, find $A'(x)$ and set it to zero.
    
    $$
    A'(x) = 120 - 6x = 0 \implies 6x = 120 \implies x = 20
    $$
    
    Now, find the corresponding total length $y$:
    
    $$
    y = 120 - 3(20) = 120 - 60 = 60
    $$
    
    The optimal dimensions are $x = 20$ m and $y = 60$ m.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Ticket Pricing and Revenue">
    An airline runs a daily flight. Currently, when they charge $50$ USD per ticket, they sell $200$ tickets. Market research shows that for every $5$ USD increase in the ticket price, $10$ fewer people will buy a ticket. 
    Let $x$ be the number of $5$ USD price increases.
    
    a) Write down expressions for the Ticket Price, and the Quantity Sold, in terms of $x$.  
    b) Show that the Revenue, $R$, can be modelled by $R(x) = 10000 + 500x - 50x^2$.  
    c) Find the ticket price the airline should charge to maximise their revenue.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a)**
    Ticket Price starts at $50$ and increases by $5$ for every $x$: 
    $$
    \text{Price} = 50 + 5x
    $$
    Quantity Sold starts at $200$ and decreases by $10$ for every $x$: 
    $$
    \text{Quantity} = 200 - 10x
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Revenue is Price multiplied by Quantity.
    
    $$
    R(x) = (50 + 5x)(200 - 10x)
    $$
    
    Expand the brackets using FOIL:
    
    $$
    R(x) = 10000 - 500x + 1000x - 50x^2
    $$
    
    Collect like terms:
    
    $$
    R(x) = 10000 + 500x - 50x^2 \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$
    
    **Part c)**
    To find the maximum revenue, set $R'(x) = 0$.
    
    $$
    R'(x) = 500 - 100x = 0 \implies 100x = 500 \implies x = 5
    $$
    
    The optimal number of price increases is $5$. Substitute $x = 5$ back into the *Ticket Price* expression (not the revenue expression!):
    
    $$
    \text{Optimal Ticket Price} = 50 + 5(5) = 50 + 25 = 75
    $$
    
    The airline should charge $75$ USD.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Environmental Tax and Surface Area">
    A manufacturer creates open-topped plastic storage boxes with a square base of side length $x$ cm and height $h$ cm. The required volume of each box is $4000$ cm$^3$.
    To reduce plastic waste, the local government imposes a tax of $0.02$ USD per cm$^2$ of plastic used.
    
    a) Write down an expression for the height $h$ in terms of $x$.  
    b) Show that the total Surface Area of the plastic, $S$, is $S(x) = x^2 + 16000x^{-1}$.  
    c) Find the dimensions that will minimise the plastic tax, and calculate this minimum tax in USD.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **Part a)**
    Volume = base area $\times$ height.
    
    $$
    x^2h = 4000 \implies h = \frac{4000}{x^2} = 4000x^{-2}
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    The box is open-topped, so it only has 1 base and 4 sides.
    
    $$
    S = x^2 + 4xh
    $$
    
    Substitute $h$ using the expression from part a:
    
    $$
    S(x) = x^2 + 4x(4000x^{-2})
    $$
    
    $$
    S(x) = x^2 + 16000x^{-1} \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$
    
    **Part c)**
    Minimising the surface area will directly minimise the tax. Find $S'(x)$ and set to zero.
    
    $$
    S'(x) = 2x - 16000x^{-2} = 0
    $$
    
    $$
    2x = \frac{16000}{x^2} \implies 2x^3 = 16000 \implies x^3 = 8000
    $$
    
    $$
    x = 20
    $$
    
    The optimal base is $x = 20$ cm. The height is $h = 4000 / (20)^2 = 4000 / 400 = 10$ cm.
    Dimensions: $20$ cm by $20$ cm by $10$ cm.

    Calculate the minimum Surface Area:
    $$
    S(20) = (20)^2 + \frac{16000}{20} = 400 + 800 = 1200
    $$

    The minimum tax per box is $24$ USD.

    a) Show that the surface area can be written as $S(r) = 2\pi r^2 + 660r^{-1}$.  
    b) Find $\frac{dS}{dr}$.  
    c) Calculate the radius, $r$, that minimises the amount of aluminium required.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **Part a)**
    First, use the volume constraint to isolate $h$:
    
    $$
    \pi r^2 h = 330 \implies h = \frac{330}{\pi r^2}
    $$
    
    Substitute this into the Surface Area formula:
    
    $$
    S = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r\left(\frac{330}{\pi r^2}\right)
    $$
    
    Simplify the second term (the $\pi$ cancels, and $r / r^2$ becomes $r^{-1}$):
    
    $$
    S = 2\pi r^2 + \frac{660}{r}
    $$
    
    $$
    S(r) = 2\pi r^2 + 660r^{-1} \quad \text{(Shown)}
    $$
    
    **Part b)**
    Remember that $\pi$ is just a constant multiplier.
    
    $$
    \frac{dS}{dr} = 4\pi r - 660r^{-2}
    $$
    
    **Part c)**
    Set the derivative to zero to find the minimum.
    
    $$
    4\pi r - \frac{660}{r^2} = 0 \implies 4\pi r = \frac{660}{r^2}
    $$
    
    Multiply by $r^2$:
    
    $$
    4\pi r^3 = 660 \implies r^3 = \frac{660}{4\pi} = \frac{165}{\pi}
    $$
    
    $$
    r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{165}{\pi}} \approx \sqrt[3]{52.52} \approx 3.74
    $$
    
    The radius that minimises the aluminium used is approximately $3.74$ cm.
