In calculus, we can find the exact area under a curve if we have a known mathematical function and can integrate it algebraically. However, in the real world, we frequently encounter situations where finding an exact integral is impossible. We might only have a table of discrete data points (like the measured width of a river at various intervals) or a function that is too complex to integrate by hand. 

To solve this, we use numerical methods to estimate the area. The **trapezoidal rule** is a powerful technique that approximates the region under a curve by dividing it into a series of trapezoids (or trapezia) and calculating the sum of their areas.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of the Trapezoidal Rule

A trapezoid is a four-sided polygon with one pair of parallel sides. When we divide the area under a curve into vertical strips of equal width, each strip forms a shape that closely resembles a trapezoid standing on its side.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of the Formula Variables">
To use the trapezoidal rule properly, you must correctly identify its key components:
* **$a$ and $b$**: The lower and upper limits of the area you are calculating (the start and end $x$-values).
* **$n$**: The number of intervals (strips), which is always one less than the number of data points ($y$-values).
* **$h$**: The constant width of each interval along the horizontal axis.
* **$y$**: The parallel vertical heights (ordinates) of the trapezoids. The first height is $y_0$ and the final height is $y_n$.
</Aside>

<FormulaBox title="The Trapezoidal Rule">
$$
\int_a^b y \, \mathrm{d}x \approx \frac{1}{2}h((y_0 + y_n) + 2(y_1 + y_2 + \dots + y_{n-1}))
$$

where $h = \frac{b-a}{n}$
</FormulaBox>

Notice that the first and last $y$-values ($y_0$ and $y_n$) are counted only once, while all the intermediate $y$-values are multiplied by 2. This is because every interior vertical line forms a shared boundary between two adjacent trapezoids, so its length is counted twice in the total sum.

## 2. Applying the Rule to a Table of Data

In many IB questions, you will not be given a function equation. Instead, you will be given a table of measurements. This is frequently used for modelling irregular real-world shapes, such as the surface area of a lake or a plot of land.

<Steps>
1.  **Identify $n$ and $h$:** Look at your table. Count the number of intervals ($n$) between your $x$-values. Confirm that the distance between each consecutive $x$-value is equal; this is your width $h$.
2.  **Label your ordinates:** Assign $y_0$ to the first $y$-value, $y_1$ to the second, and so on, until the final value $y_n$.
3.  **Group the values:** Add the first and last $y$-values together: $(y_0 + y_n)$.
4.  **Sum the middle values:** Add all the remaining $y$-values together and multiply the sum by 2: $2(y_1 + y_2 + \dots + y_{n-1})$.
5.  **Calculate the final approximation:** Substitute these sums back into the formula and multiply by $\frac{1}{2}h$.
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="Counting Intervals vs Points">
A very common student error is mixing up the number of data points with the number of intervals ($n$). If you are given a table with 6 data points, you only have $n = 5$ intervals. Always calculate $n$ as (Total Points - 1).
</Aside>

## 3. Applying the Rule to a Function

Sometimes you are given the mathematical equation of a curve (e.g., $y = x^2 + \sin(x)$) and asked to approximate the integral between $x = a$ and $x = b$ using a specific number of strips $n$.

<SteveTip title="Using Your GDC Efficiently">
If you are given a function and need to find the $y$-values for the trapezoidal rule, **do not calculate each point manually**. 
1. Enter your function into the graphing section of your TI-Nspire.
2. Press `Ctrl` + `T` to bring up the Table of Values.
3. Go to Table Settings (Menu > Table > Edit Table Settings), set your `Table Start` to your $a$ value, and set your `Table Step` to your $h$ value. 
This will instantly generate the exact $y_0, y_1, y_2 \dots y_n$ values you need to write down on your exam paper to earn method marks.
</SteveTip>

## 4. Real-World Contexts: Kinematics

The trapezoidal rule is heavily linked to Physics and kinematics. If you have a graph or table showing the **velocity** of an object over **time**, the area under that curve represents the total **distance** travelled. 

If a car's velocity is recorded every 2 seconds, you can use the trapezoidal rule to estimate how far the car travelled during that period, even without knowing the algebraic equation for its acceleration.

    A geographer is estimating the surface area of a small lake. She measures the width of the lake, $y$ metres, at equal 10-metre intervals along its length, $x$ metres. The measurements are recorded in the table below:

    | $x$ (m) | 0  | 10 | 20 | 30 | 40 | 50 |
    |-------|----|----|----|----|----|----|
    | $y$ (m) | 0  | 14 | 22 | 25 | 18 | 0  |

    Use the trapezoidal rule to find an approximate value for the surface area of the lake.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    First, identify the components for the formula:
    * The interval width $h = 10$.
    * There are 6 data points, which means $n = 5$ intervals.
    * $y_0 = 0$, $y_1 = 14$, $y_2 = 22$, $y_3 = 25$, $y_4 = 18$, $y_5 = 0$.

    Set up the trapezoidal rule formula:
    
    $$
    \text{Area} \approx \frac{1}{2}h((y_0 + y_5) + 2(y_1 + y_2 + y_3 + y_4))
    $$
    
    Substitute the values:
    
    $$
    \text{Area} \approx \frac{1}{2}(10) ((0 + 0) + 2(14 + 22 + 25 + 18))
    $$
    
    Simplify the brackets:
    
    $$
    \text{Area} \approx 5 (0 + 2(79))
    $$
    
    $$
    \text{Area} \approx 5 (158) = 790
    $$

    The approximate surface area of the lake is $790 \text{ m}^2$.
  </TabItem>

    Consider the curve given by the equation $y = \frac{10}{x}$. 
    
    Use the trapezoidal rule with 4 intervals ($n=4$) to estimate the value of the integral:

    **Step 1:** Calculate the interval width, $h$.
    
    $$
    h = \frac{b-a}{n} = \frac{5 - 1}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1
    $$
    
    **Step 2:** Generate the table of values for $x$ from 1 to 5, substituting into $y = \frac{10}{x}$.
    * $x_0 = 1 \implies y_0 = \frac{10}{1} = 10$
    * $x_1 = 2 \implies y_1 = \frac{10}{2} = 5$
    * $x_2 = 3 \implies y_2 = \frac{10}{3} \approx 3.333$
    * $x_3 = 4 \implies y_3 = \frac{10}{4} = 2.5$
    * $x_4 = 5 \implies y_4 = \frac{10}{5} = 2$

    **Step 3:** Apply the trapezoidal rule.
    
    $$
    \int_1^5 \frac{10}{x} \, \mathrm{d}x \approx \frac{1}{2}(1) \left((10 + 2) + 2\left(5 + \frac{10}{3} + 2.5\right)\right)
    $$
    
    $$
    \approx 0.5 \left( 12 + 2\left(10.8333\dots\right) \right)
    $$
    
    $$
    \approx 0.5 \left( 12 + 21.666\dots \right)
    $$
    
    $$
    \approx 0.5 \left( 33.666\dots \right) = 16.8333\dots
    $$
    
    Rounding to 3 significant figures, the estimate is $16.8$.
  </TabItem>

    A surveyor measures the cross-sectional area of a tunnel. The width of the tunnel is divided into 4 equal intervals of 2 metres each. The heights of the tunnel, $y$ metres, are measured at each interval. One of the measurements, $k$, was smudged on the surveyor's notes.

    |-------------------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
    | Height, $y$ (m)   | 0   | $k$ | 5.2 | 4.8 | 0   |

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part (a): Set up the expression**
       Identify the knowns: $h = 2$, $n = 4$.

       \text{Area} \approx \frac{1}{2}(2) \left( (0 + 0) + 2(k + 5.2 + 4.8) \right)

       14 = 2k \implies k = 7
       $$
       
       The missing height is $7 \text{ m}$.
       
    3. **Part (c): Calculate volume**
       The volume of a prism is the cross-sectional area multiplied by its length.
       
       $$
       \text{Volume} = \text{Area} \times \text{Length}
       $$
       
       $$
       \text{Volume} = 34 \times 50 = 1700 \text{ m}^3
       $$
    </Steps>

       $$

       $$
       
       The percentage error is $2.27\%$ (to 3 s.f.).
    </Steps>

       The width $h = \frac{5 - 0}{5} = 1$. Using the exact values (or 2 d.p. values) from the table:
       
       $$
       \text{Integral} \approx \frac{1}{2}(1) \left( (5 + 26.89) + 2(7 + 9.8 + 13.72 + 19.21) \right)
       $$
       
       $$
       \approx 0.5 \left( 31.89 + 2(49.73) \right)
       $$
       
       $$
       \approx 0.5 \left( 31.89 + 99.46 \right)
       $$
       
       $$
       \approx 0.5 \left( 131.35 \right) = 65.675
       $$
       
       *(Note: If using unrounded values, the result is exactly 65.6736)*
       
    3. **Part (c): Contextual interpretation**
       Integrating a *rate* of growth over a period of *time* gives the total accumulated amount. 
       Since the units are in thousands of cells, the integral represents the **total number of bacteria cells grown (approximately 65,675 cells) during the first 5 hours of observation.**
    </Steps>

    <Steps>
    1. **Part (a): Identify interval width**
       The measurements are taken at equal 3-metre intervals across the width. Therefore, $h = 3$.
       
    2. **Part (b): Estimate cross-sectional area**
       The depths ($y$-values) are: $y_0 = 0$, $y_1 = 2.4$, $y_2 = 3.6$, $y_3 = 1.8$, $y_4 = 0$.
       
       $$
       \text{Area} \approx \frac{1}{2}(3) ((0 + 0) + 2(2.4 + 3.6 + 1.8))
       $$
       
       $$
       \text{Area} \approx 1.5 (0 + 2(7.8))
       $$
       
       $$
       \text{Area} \approx 1.5 (15.6) = 23.4 \text{ m}^2
       $$
       
    3. **Part (c): Calculate total volume**
       Model the canal as a uniform 3D prism. 
       
       $$
       \text{Volume} = \text{Cross-Sectional Area} \times \text{Length}
       $$
       
       $$
       \text{Volume} = 23.4 \times 150 = 3510 \text{ m}^3
       $$
       
    4. **Part (d): Calculate the required area adjustment**
       First, find the total cross-sectional area needed to hold the target volume of $4000 \text{ m}^3$.
       
       $$
       \text{Target Area} = \frac{\text{Target Volume}}{\text{Length}} = \frac{4000}{150} = \frac{80}{3} \approx 26.667 \text{ m}^2
       $$
       
       Subtract the current estimated area from the target area to find the difference:
       
       $$
       \text{Additional Area Needed} = 26.667 - 23.4 = 3.2667 \dots \text{ m}^2
       $$
       
       The canal must be deepened to increase the cross-section by approximately $3.27 \text{ m}^2$ (to 3 s.f.).
