Calculus is the mathematics of change. While finding the gradient of a straight line is straightforward using $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$, curves present a challenge because their gradient is constantly changing. Differentiation is the algebraic process we use to find the exact gradient of a curve at any given point. 

The function that gives us the gradient of a curve is called the **derivative function**, denoted as $f'(x)$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$.

---

## 1. The Power Rule

The foundation of algebraic differentiation is a shortcut known as the power rule. Instead of calculating limits from first principles, we can use this rule to instantly find the derivative of any term.

<FormulaBox title="Derivative of a Power Function">
$$
f(x) = ax^n \implies f'(x) = anx^{n-1}
$$
</FormulaBox>

When dealing with a coefficient (a constant number in front of the variable), the coefficient remains and multiplies with the power that is brought down.

To apply the power rule, follow these simple steps:

<Steps>
1. **Bring the power down:** Multiply the coefficient by the current exponent.
2. **Keep the variable:** Write down the variable exactly as it is.
3. **Reduce the power:** Subtract exactly $1$ from the original exponent to find your new power.
</Steps>

## 2. Differentiating Polynomials and Notation

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables and coefficients. When differentiating a function of the form $f(x) = ax^n + bx^{n-1} + \dots$, you simply apply the power rule to **each term individually**.

You must also be comfortable with different mathematical notations for derivatives:
* If the function is $y$, the derivative is $\frac{dy}{dx}$ (the rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$).
* If the function is $A$ (Area) with respect to $r$ (radius), the derivative is $\frac{dA}{dr}$.
* If the function is $s$ (displacement) with respect to $t$ (time), the derivative is $s'(t)$ or $\frac{ds}{dt}$.

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception: Constants and Linear Terms">
Students often make mistakes with terms that do not visibly have an exponent.
* **Linear terms:** The derivative of $kx$ is simply $k$. For example, if $y = 7x$, then $\frac{dy}{dx} = 7$.
* **Constant terms:** The derivative of any constant number is always **zero**. A constant value does not change, so its rate of change is $0$. If $f(x) = 12$, then $f'(x) = 0$.
</Aside>

---

## 3. Dealing with Negative Exponents

The syllabus explicitly states that the power rule works for all integers, $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. This means you can use it for negative exponents as well. 

Often, functions will be given with $x$ in the denominator of a fraction. **You cannot differentiate a term while $x$ is trapped in a denominator.** You must rewrite the term using negative indices first.

<Aside type="tip" title="Index Law Reminder">
Recall from your prior algebra studies that:

$$
\frac{1}{x^n} = x^{-n}
$$

and 

$$
\frac{a}{bx^n} = \frac{a}{b}x^{-n}
$$

</Aside>

## 4. Technology: Finding Derivatives on the GDC

<Aside type="tip" title="The Numerical Derivative Function">
While you must know how to find the derivative algebraically, your TI-Nspire can find the numerical value of a derivative at a specific point. This is excellent for checking your answers or finding gradients directly from complex functions!

<Steps>
1. Open a Calculator page.
2. Press `menu` $\rightarrow$ `4: Calculus` $\rightarrow$ `1: Numerical Derivative at a Point`.
3. Enter your variable (`x`) and the value at which you want the gradient.
4. Type your function into the brackets and press `enter`.
</Steps>
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="Visual Verification: Graphing your Derivatives">
A fantastic way to check if your algebraic differentiation is entirely correct is to use the graphing features of your GDC to look for an overlap!

<Steps>
1. Open a Graphs page.
2. In $f_1(x)$, use the derivative template `d/dx` and type in your **original** function.
3. In $f_2(x)$, type in your **derived** algebraic derivative.
4. Hit enter. If your differentiation is correct, the graph of $f_2(x)$ will perfectly overlap $f_1(x)$! You can press `ctrl` + `T` to bring up the table and verify that the output values are identical across all values of $x$.
</Steps>
</SteveTip>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Negative Indices">
    Find the derivative of the function:
    
    $$
    y = 5x^2 - \frac{3}{x^4} + 2
    $$
    
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Step 1: Rewrite the function using negative indices.**
    
    $$
    y = 5x^2 - 3x^{-4} + 2
    $$

    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 10x - (-4 \times 3)x^{-4 - 1} + 0
    $$
    
    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 10x + 12x^{-5}
    $$
    
    **Step 3: Convert back to fraction form (optional but recommended).**
    
    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 10x + \frac{12}{x^5}
    $$
    
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Gradient at a Specific Point">
    Consider the curve $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^4 - 3x^2 + 7$.
    
    Find the gradient of the curve at the point where $x = -2$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Step 1: Find the derivative function, $f'(x)$.**
    
    $$
    f'(x) = 4\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)x^3 - 2(3)x^1 + 0
    $$
    
    $$
    f'(x) = 2x^3 - 6x
    $$
    
    **Step 2: Substitute $x = -2$ into the derivative function.**
    
    $$
    f'(-2) = 2(-2)^3 - 6(-2)
    $$
    
    $$
    f'(-2) = 2(-8) + 12
    $$
    
    $$
    f'(-2) = -16 + 12 = -4
    $$
    
    The gradient of the curve at $x = -2$ is **$-4$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Different Variables">
    The volume of a sphere is given by the formula $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$. 
    
    Find $\frac{dV}{dr}$, the rate of change of the volume with respect to its radius.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    Here, $\pi$ is just a constant number, not a variable. We differentiate with respect to $r$.
    
    $$
    \frac{dV}{dr} = 3 \times \left(\frac{4}{3}\pi\right) r^{3-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    \frac{dV}{dr} = 4\pi r^2
    $$
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Splitting Fractions">
    Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ for the function:
    
    $$
    y = \frac{6x^4 - 2x^2}{x^3}
    $$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    You cannot differentiate the numerator and denominator separately. First, split the fraction and simplify using index laws.
    
    $$
    y = \frac{6x^4}{x^3} - \frac{2x^2}{x^3}
    $$
    
    $$
    y = 6x - 2x^{-1}
    $$
    
    Now differentiate using the power rule:
    
    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 6 - (-1 \times 2)x^{-1-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 6 + 2x^{-2}
    $$
    
    Or, equivalently: 
    
    $$
    \frac{dy}{dx} = 6 + \frac{2}{x^2}
    $$
    
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Points of Zero Gradient">
    Find the coordinates of the point on the curve $f(x) = x^2 - 8x + 15$ where the gradient is $0$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **Step 1: Find the derivative to represent the gradient.**
    
    $$
    f'(x) = 2x - 8
    $$
    
    **Step 2: Set the derivative to $0$ and solve for $x$.**
    
    $$
    0 = 2x - 8
    $$
    
    $$
    8 = 2x
    $$
    
    $$
    x = 4
    $$
    
    **Step 3: Find the corresponding $y$-coordinate.**
    Substitute $x = 4$ back into the *original* function $f(x)$.
    
    $$
    f(4) = (4)^2 - 8(4) + 15
    $$
    
    $$
    f(4) = 16 - 32 + 15 = -1
    $$
    
    The coordinates of the point with zero gradient are **$(4, -1)$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Increasing and Decreasing Functions (Linking to 5.2)">
    Consider the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{3}x^3 - 2x^2 - 12x + 5$.
    
    Find the range of values for $x$ for which the function is decreasing.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    A function is decreasing when its gradient is strictly negative (i.e., $f'(x) < 0$).
    
    **Step 1: Find the derivative function.**
    
    $$
    f'(x) = x^2 - 4x - 12
    $$
    
    **Step 2: Determine where the derivative is less than zero.**
    We need to solve the inequality:
    
    $$
    x^2 - 4x - 12 < 0
    $$
    
    **Step 3: Find the roots and test the intervals.**
    Factorise the quadratic to find where the gradient equals zero:
    
    $$
    (x - 6)(x + 2) = 0
    $$
    
    The roots are $x = 6$ and $x = -2$. Since the derivative $y = x^2 - 4x - 12$ is a "positive/upwards" parabola, it dips below zero *between* its roots.
    
    The function is therefore decreasing for: **$-2 < x < 6$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Instantaneous Rate of Change (Linking to 5.1)">
    The displacement of a particle, $s$ metres, after $t$ seconds is modelled by the function $s(t) = 3t^2 - 5t + 2$. 
    
    Find the exact instantaneous rate of change (velocity) of the particle at $t = 4$ seconds.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    The instantaneous rate of change is the derivative evaluated at that specific point in time.
    
    **Step 1: Find the derivative $s'(t)$.**
    
    $$
    s'(t) = 6t - 5
    $$
    
    **Step 2: Substitute $t = 4$.**
    
    $$
    s'(4) = 6(4) - 5
    $$
    
    $$
    s'(4) = 24 - 5 = 19
    $$
    
    The rate of change is **$19 \text{ m s}^{-1}$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
