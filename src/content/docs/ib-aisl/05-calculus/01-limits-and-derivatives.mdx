Calculus is the mathematics of continuous change. While algebra and geometry give us the tools to find the average gradient of a straight line, calculus provides the machinery to find the exact gradient of a curving line at any precise mathematical instant. 

To bridge the gap between "average" change and "instantaneous" change, we must first understand the foundational concept of a **limit**. 

<Steps>
1. Understand the concept of a limit graphically and numerically.
2. Estimate the value of a limit using tables and graphs on your GDC.
3. Link the limit to the gradient of a curve to define the derivative.
4. Interpret derivative notation in real-world contexts like economics and kinematics.
</Steps>

## 1. The Intuitive Concept of a Limit

In simple terms, a **limit** is the value that a function or sequence "approaches" as the input gets closer and closer to a certain value. We are not necessarily asking what happens *at* that exact point, but rather what is happening *infinitely close* to it.

The notation for a limit is:
$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L
$$
This is read as: *"The limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ is equal to $L$."*

## 2. Estimating Limits using Technology (TI-Nspire)

Since we are estimating limits numerically, the Table function on your Graphic Display Calculator is your best tool. 

If we want to estimate the limit of $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$ as $x \to 3$:

<Steps>
1. **Define the Function:** Open a Calculator or Graph page and define your function. 
2. **Open the Table:** Press **ctrl** + **T** to bring up the function table.
3. **Edit Table Settings:** Go to **Menu** > **Table** > **Edit Table Settings...** Change the "Independent" setting from *Auto* to *Ask*.
4. **Input Approaching Values:** Type in values that get infinitely closer to 3 from the left (e.g., 2.9, 2.99, 2.999) and from the right (e.g., 3.1, 3.01, 3.001).
5. **Observe the Trend:** You will see the $f(x)$ values converging on **6**. 
</Steps>

*Note: If you type exactly `3` into the calculator, it will read `undef` (undefined) because you cannot divide by zero. The limit exists at 6, even if the function itself breaks down at exactly $x=3$.*

<Aside type="note" title="History & International-Mindedness">
**The Division by Zero Problem:** For centuries, dividing by zero baffled mathematicians. Indian mathematicians (between 500–1000 CE), such as Brahmagupta and Bhaskara II, made early attempts to formalise rules for dividing by zero. While their algebraic rules weren't perfect by modern standards, these early struggles paved the way for the development of limits and calculus centuries later!
</Aside>

## 3. The Derivative: Gradient as a Limit

In coordinate geometry, the gradient $m$ of a straight line between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is $m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$. 

But curves don't have a constant gradient. To find the gradient of a curve at exactly one point (the tangent), we imagine a secant line cutting through two points on the curve. As we move the second point closer and closer to the first point, the horizontal distance between them ($\Delta x$) approaches zero. 

The **derivative** is simply the limit of this average gradient as the distance between the points shrinks to zero.

### Understanding the Notation

When we find the derivative of a function, we are finding its **gradient function**—a formula that will tell us the gradient of the curve at any value of $x$. You must be comfortable with several forms of notation:

* **$f'(x)$ (Prime Notation):** If the original function is $f(x)$, the derivative is $f'(x)$. This was popularised by Lagrange.
* **$\frac{dy}{dx}$ (Leibniz Notation):** If the equation is given as $y = \dots$, the derivative is $\frac{dy}{dx}$. This physically represents an infinitely small change in $y$ divided by an infinitely small change in $x$.

<SteveTip title="Exam Tip: Match the Question's Notation">
In IB examinations, you must mirror the notation provided in the prompt. If the question gives the volume of a sphere as $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ and asks for the derivative, you **must** write $\frac{dV}{dr}$. Writing $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when there are no $y$ or $x$ variables in the equation can result in a loss of communication marks!
</SteveTip>

<Aside type="note" title="Aim 8: The Great Calculus Controversy">
The discovery of calculus is one of the most famous disputes in mathematical history. Both Sir Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz developed the foundational concepts of calculus independently around the same time. While Newton focused heavily on kinematics and rates of change (using "fluxions"), Leibniz developed the highly effective $\frac{dy}{dx}$ notation we still use today. 

Furthermore, the ancient Greeks’ distrust of the concept of "zero" and "nothingness" prevented brilliant minds like Archimedes from fully developing calculus centuries earlier, despite him working on very similar ideas regarding the areas under curves!
</Aside>

## 4. Derivative as a Rate of Change

The gradient doesn't just tell us how "steep" a line is; in the real world, the gradient represents the **instantaneous rate of change**. The derivative tells us how fast one variable is changing with respect to another at a specific instant.

This is why the syllabus introduces context-specific notation:
* **$\frac{dV}{dr}$:** The rate of change of Volume ($V$) with respect to the radius ($r$). E.g., How fast a balloon's volume is increasing as its radius expands.
* **$\frac{ds}{dt}$:** The rate of change of displacement ($s$) with respect to time ($t$). In physics (kinematics), this is the exact definition of **velocity**.

<Aside type="note" title="Cross-Curricular Connection: Economics">
If you study IB Economics, you already use calculus! In economics, the word **"Marginal"** means derivative. 
* **Marginal Cost ($MC$):** The derivative of the Cost function, $C'(x)$. It is the cost of producing *one more* unit.
* **Marginal Revenue ($MR$):** The derivative of the Revenue function, $R'(x)$. 
Profit is maximised when $MC = MR$. Calculus proves this perfectly!
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
Calculus relies heavily on the concept of "infinitesimals"—numbers that are infinitely small but not quite zero. But is infinitesimal behaviour applicable to real life? Can time or space be divided infinitely, or is there a smallest possible pixel of reality (like the Planck length in physics)? If the foundation of calculus relies on a concept that might not exist in the physical universe, what value does the knowledge of limits have?
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Translating Graphs to Derivatives">
    A tangent line is drawn on the curve $y = f(x)$ at the point where $x = -2$. The tangent line passes through the coordinates $(-2, 5)$ and $(0, 11)$.
    
    Calculate the exact value of $f'(-2)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    <Steps>
    1. **Understand the connection:** The derivative at a specific point, $f'(-2)$, is exactly equal to the gradient of the tangent line at that point.
    2. **Calculate the tangent's gradient:** Use the two coordinate points provided for the tangent line: $(-2, 5)$ and $(0, 11)$.
       $$
       m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}
       $$
       $$
       m = \frac{11 - 5}{0 - (-2)}
       $$
       $$
       m = \frac{6}{2} = 3
       $$
    3. **State the final answer:** Since the gradient of the tangent is 3, the value of the derivative function at that point is also 3.
       **Answer:** $f'(-2) = 3$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A perfectly spherical balloon is being inflated. The volume $V$, in $\text{cm}^3$, of the balloon is a function of its radius $r$, in cm. 
    
    The derivative of the volume function is given as $4\pi r^2$.
    
    **a)** State the correct Leibniz notation for this derivative.
    
    **b)** Calculate the exact rate of change of the volume when the radius is $5 \text{ cm}$.
    
    **c)** A student estimates this instantaneous rate of change to be $300 \text{ cm}^3\text{/cm}$. Calculate the percentage error in the student's estimate. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a:** The rate of change of Volume ($V$) with respect to radius ($r$).
       **Answer:** $\frac{dV}{dr}$
    2. **Part b:** Substitute $r = 5$ into the given derivative formula.
       $$
       \frac{dV}{dr} = 4\pi (5)^2 = 4\pi(25)
       $$
       **Answer:** $100\pi \text{ cm}^3\text{/cm}$
    3. **Part c:** Recall the percentage error formula from Topic 1: $\epsilon = \left|\frac{v_A - v_E}{v_E}\right| \times 100\%$, where $v_A$ is the approximate value and $v_E$ is the exact value.
       $$
       \epsilon = \left| \frac{300 - 100\pi}{100\pi} \right| \times 100
       $$
       $$
       \epsilon = \left| \frac{300 - 314.159...}{314.159...} \right| \times 100 \approx 4.507...
       $$
       **Answer:** The percentage error is $4.51\%$ (to 3 s.f.).
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A tech startup models its daily profit $P$, in thousands of euros, based on its daily advertising expenditure $x$, also in thousands of euros. This is modelled by the function $P(x)$.
    
    It is known that $P(10) = 50$ and $P'(10) = 2$.
    
    **a)** Explain the meaning of $P(10) = 50$ in context.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a:** Interpret the original function coordinates. The input is advertising, the output is profit.
       **Answer:** When the startup spends 10,000 euros on advertising, its daily profit is 50,000 euros.
    2. **Part b:** The function is $P$ with respect to the variable $x$.
       **Answer:** $\frac{dP}{dx}$
    3. **Part c:** This is the marginal profit (rate of change) at exactly $x=10$.
       **Answer:** When exactly 10,000 euros is spent on advertising, the profit is increasing at an instantaneous rate of 2,000 euros for every additional 1,000 euros spent.

       $$
       m = \frac{58 - 50}{15 - 10} = \frac{8}{5} = 1.6
       $$
       Because this is calculated over an interval between two distinct points on the curve, it represents a secant line.
       **Answer:** The average rate of change is 1.6 (or 1,600 euros per 1,000 euros spent). This is the gradient of a secant.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
