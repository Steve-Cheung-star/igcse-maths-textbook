In mathematics and science, we frequently encounter numbers that are either astronomically large or microscopically small. **Standard Form** (also known as scientific notation) provides an efficient, standardised way to write these numbers and perform calculations without getting lost in a sea of zeros.

From global financial figures to the microscopic measurements of biology, standard form is the universal language of scale. You will see it across your other IB subjects:
* **Chemistry:** Avogadro's number ($6.02 \times 10^{23}$) for counting particles.
* **Physics:** Expressing orders of magnitude for sub-atomic particles and distances between galaxies.
* **Sciences:** Handling the uncertainty and precision of measurements.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of Standard Form

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Standard Form">
Every number written in standard form is composed of three essential parts:
1. **The Mantissa ($a$):** A number greater than or equal to 1, but strictly less than 10.
2. **The Base:** This is always the number 10.
3. **The Exponent ($k$):** An integer (a positive or negative whole number) that indicates the power of 10.
</Aside>

This is formally written as:

$$
a \times 10^k
$$

Where $1 \le a < 10$ and $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem of Exponent Scale">
* When $k$ is **positive**, the original number is greater than or equal to 10.
* When $k$ is **negative**, the original number is a decimal between 0 and 1.
* When $k = 0$, the original number is strictly between 1 and 10, because $10^0 = 1$.
</Aside>

## 2. Converting Numbers

To convert an ordinary number into standard form, you must adjust the decimal point to create a valid mantissa ($a$) and then multiply by the appropriate power of 10 to maintain the original value.

### Converting Large Numbers
<Steps>
1. Place the decimal point after the very first non-zero digit to establish your mantissa ($a$).
2. Count the number of places the decimal point has shifted from its original position (the end of the number).
3. This count becomes your positive integer exponent ($k$).
4. Write the final answer in the form $a \times 10^k$.
</Steps>

### Converting Small Numbers
<Steps>
1. Identify the first non-zero digit and place the decimal point immediately after it to form $a$.
2. Count how many places the decimal point has shifted to the right.
3. This count becomes your negative integer exponent ($-k$).
4. Write the final answer in the form $a \times 10^{-k}$.
</Steps>

<Aside type="note" title="International-Mindedness: The Evolution of Number">
The way we write and understand numbers has evolved significantly over millennia. The earliest recorded numbering systems date back to the ancient Sumerians, who used a base-60 (sexagesimal) system. Our modern, highly efficient standard form relies heavily on the base-10 Hindu-Arabic numeral system, which introduced the revolutionary concept of zero as a placeholder.
</Aside>

## 3. Operations with Standard Form

You are required to fluently multiply, divide, add, and subtract numbers given in standard form.

### Multiplication and Division
When multiplying or dividing, handle the mantissa ($a$) and the powers of 10 separately using the laws of indices.

**Multiplication:**

$$
(A \times 10^m) \times (B \times 10^n) = (A \times B) \times 10^{m+n}
$$

**Division:**

$$
(A \times 10^m) \div (B \times 10^n) = (A \div B) \times 10^{m-n}
$$

<Aside type="caution" title="Adjusting the Mantissa">
After completing a multiplication or division, your new value for $a$ might no longer sit between 1 and 10. Always adjust it back into proper standard form! For example, $12 \times 10^4$ must be rewritten as $1.2 \times 10^5$.
</Aside>

### Addition and Subtraction
To add or subtract numbers in standard form without a calculator, you must first manipulate the terms so that they share the exact **same power of 10**. 

<Steps>
1. Identify the term with the smaller power of 10.
2. Rewrite it so its exponent matches the larger power of 10 (this will temporarily make its mantissa a decimal less than 1).
3. Add or subtract the mantissas.
4. Keep the shared power of 10 unchanged.
5. Ensure the final answer is in correct standard form.
</Steps>

## 4. Proper Exam Notation

<SteveTip title="The Calculator Notation Trap">
When performing calculations, your GDC (Graphics Display Calculator) might output an answer like `5.2E30` or `5.2 x 10^30` depending on its settings. **Calculator or computer notation is completely unacceptable in the IB exam.** If you write `5.2E30` on your exam paper, you will not receive the mark. You must translate this into proper mathematical notation:

$$
5.2 \times 10^{30}
$$

</SteveTip>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Do the names that we give things impact how we understand them?** Consider the fact that some exceptionally large numbers have been given specific, evocative names—such as the *googol* ($10^{100}$) and the *googolplex* ($10^{\text{googol}}$). Does assigning a noun to a number make it feel more tangible or easier to conceptualise than simply representing it in standard form as a pure abstraction?
</Aside>
