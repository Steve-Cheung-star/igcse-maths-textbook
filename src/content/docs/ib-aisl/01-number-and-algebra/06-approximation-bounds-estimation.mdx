In pure mathematics, an exact answer like $\frac{\pi}{3}$ or $\sqrt{2}$ is often preferred. However, Mathematics: Applications and Interpretation is deeply rooted in the real world. In reality, measurements are never perfectly precise, and it is rarely practical to use infinitely long decimals. 

Whether you are exchanging currency, measuring the dimensions of a building, or estimating the population of a city, you must know how to approximate numbers and, crucially, understand the margin of error introduced by those approximations.

---

## 1. Decimal Places and Significant Figures

When a calculator gives you a long string of digits, you must choose an appropriate degree of accuracy based on the context.

* **Decimal Places (d.p.):** Counting starts from the decimal point. We look at the digit immediately after our desired cut-off to decide whether to round up or keep the digit the same.
    * *Example:* $3.14159$ rounded to 2 d.p. is $3.14$.
* **Significant Figures (s.f.):** Counting starts from the first non-zero digit, moving left to right. This method is exceptionally useful because it scales with the size of the number (order of magnitude), which is essential in scientific contexts.
    * *Example:* $0.00456$ rounded to 2 s.f. is $0.0046$.
    * *Example:* $12345$ rounded to 3 s.f. is $12300$.

<Aside type="note" title="Real-World Contexts">
**Currency:** Different currencies have different standard approximations. For example, US Dollars, Euros, and British Pounds are typically rounded to 2 decimal places (the nearest cent or penny). Conversely, the Japanese Yen or Chilean Peso are often rounded to the nearest whole number because their base units have a much smaller relative value.
</Aside>

---

## 2. Upper and Lower Bounds

Every measurement tool has a limit to its precision. If you use a ruler to measure a line as $4.1 \text{ cm}$ (to one decimal place), the true length could be slightly longer or slightly shorter. 

The true value, $x$, lies within a range defined by an **upper bound** and a **lower bound**. The boundary is halfway between the given degree of accuracy.

### Calculating Bounds
If a value is rounded to the nearest unit (e.g., 1 d.p., nearest 10, nearest whole number), the bounds are found by adding and subtracting **half of that unit**.

$$
\text{Lower Bound} \leq \text{True Value} < \text{Upper Bound}
$$

**Example:** If $x = 4.1$ (rounded to 1 decimal place, meaning the unit is $0.1$):
* Half the unit is $0.1 \div 2 = 0.05$
* Lower bound: $4.1 - 0.05 = 4.05$
* Upper bound: $4.1 + 0.05 = 4.15$
* Inequality: $4.05 \leq x < 4.15$

---

## 3. Percentage Error and Estimation

When working with scientific measurements or approximations, it is vital to know how inaccurate your data might be. The **percentage error** quantifies how far an approximate (or measured) value is from the exact (or theoretical) value, as a percentage of the exact value.

<FormulaBox title="Percentage Error Formula">

$$
\varepsilon = \left| \frac{v_A - v_E}{v_E} \right| \times 100\%
$$

</FormulaBox>

Where $v_E$ is the exact value and $v_A$ is the approximate value of $v$.
*(Note: The vertical bars $| ... |$ represent the absolute value, meaning your percentage error will always be positive.)*

### Estimation and Reasonableness
Before you blindly accept an answer from your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC), you should always ask: *"Does this make sense?"*
* **Physical limitations:** Lengths, areas, and times cannot be negative.
* **Discrete limits:** You cannot have $4.3$ people or $-2$ cars.
* **Orders of magnitude:** If you are calculating the distance from London to Paris and your answer is $4000000 \text{ km}$, your calculations are wrong—that is closer to the distance to the moon!

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK) & Aim 8">
**Is mathematical reasoning different from scientific reasoning?** In pure math, $1 + 1 = 2$ exactly. In science, $1.0\text{ kg} + 1.0\text{ kg} = 2.0\text{ kg} \pm \text{uncertainty}$. Mathematics creates perfect idealizations, but science must grapple with the physical limitations of the real world. 

**Ethical implications of approximation (Aim 8):** Does rounding matter? If an engineer rounds down the load-bearing capacity of a bridge support, it adds a safety margin. If a pharmaceutical company rounds down the side-effect rates of a new drug, it could cost lives. Who decides what degree of accuracy is "acceptable" when public safety is on the line?
</Aside>

    A runner's time for a 100m sprint is recorded as $11.4 \text{ seconds}$, rounded to 1 decimal place.

    * **Lower Bound:** $11.4 - 0.05 = 11.35$  
    * **Upper Bound:** $11.4 + 0.05 = 11.45$  
    
    The inequality is:  
    $$
    11.35 \leq t < 11.45
    $$

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Maximum Percentage Error">
    Zola measures the radius of a circle to be $2.5 \text{ cm}$, rounded to 1 decimal place. She uses this measurement to calculate the area of the circle.  
    a) Calculate the expected area based on her measurement.  
    b) Find the upper bound of the radius.  
    c) Calculate the maximum percentage error in the calculated area due to this measurement limitation.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Part a:** The expected area uses the measured radius $v_A = 2.5$.  
    $$
    \text{Area} = \pi \times 2.5^2 = 6.25\pi \approx 19.635 \text{ cm}^2
    $$  

    **Part b:** The radius is rounded to 1 d.p. (nearest $0.1$). Half of this is $0.05$.  
    The upper bound of the radius is $2.5 + 0.05 = 2.55 \text{ cm}$.  
    *(Note: The lower bound is $2.45 \text{ cm}$).* **Part c:** To find the maximum percentage error, we must see which bound creates the largest difference from the expected area.  
    
    Max possible area using upper bound:   
    $$\text{Area}_{\text{max}} = \pi \times 2.55^2 = 6.5025\pi \approx 20.428 \text{ cm}^2$$  
    
    Min possible area using lower bound:  
    $$\text{Area}_{\text{min}} = \pi \times 2.45^2 = 6.0025\pi \approx 18.857 \text{ cm}^2$$  

    Difference using max area: $|20.428 - 19.635| = 0.793$  
    Difference using min area: $|18.857 - 19.635| = 0.778$  
    
    The maximum absolute error occurs at the upper bound. Now apply the percentage error formula, treating the "expected" area as $v_E$ and the "max" area as $v_A$:  
    
    $$
    \varepsilon = \left| \frac{20.428 - 19.635}{19.635} \right| \times 100\%
    $$  
    
    $$
    \varepsilon \approx 4.04\%
    $$  
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Narantuya is laying new flooring in her rectangular living room. She measures the length as $5.2 \text{ m}$ and the width as $3.8 \text{ m}$, with both measurements rounded to 1 decimal place.

    * Length bounds: $5.15 \leq L < 5.25$  
    * Width bounds: $3.75 \leq W < 3.85$

    Min Area $= 5.15 \times 3.75 = 19.3125 \text{ m}^2$  

    Check which yields the largest absolute difference from the expected $19.76 \text{ m}^2$:  
    Upper difference $= |20.2125 - 19.76| = 0.4525$  
    Lower difference $= |19.3125 - 19.76| = 0.4475$  

    The maximum error occurs with the upper bound area. Apply the percentage error formula:  
    $$
    \varepsilon = \left| \frac{20.2125 - 19.76}{19.76} \right| \times 100\%
    $$  
    
    $$
    \varepsilon \approx 2.29\%
    $$  
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Integrating Estimation, Models, and Error">
    Tenzin models the population growth of an endangered insect colony using the equation $P = 1200 \times 1.4^t$, where $t$ is the time in weeks. He wishes to estimate the population at $t = 3.5 \text{ weeks}$.  
    a) Calculate the estimated population at $t = 3.5 \text{ weeks}$, rounding your answer to the nearest whole number because population is discrete.  
    b) A team of biologists conducts an exact field count at $3.5 \text{ weeks}$ and finds the true population is $4050$. Calculate the percentage error of Tenzin's estimation.  
    c) Tenzin solves his equation for when the population was $500$ and gets $t \approx -2.6 \text{ weeks}$. Is this a mathematically reasonable result? Briefly explain why.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **Part a:** Substitute $t = 3.5$ into the model.  
    $$P = 1200 \times 1.4^{3.5} \approx 3896.76$$  
    Rounded to the nearest whole number: $3897$ insects.  

    **Part b:** Here, $v_A = 3897$ and the exact value $v_E = 4050$.  
    $$
    \varepsilon = \left| \frac{3897 - 4050}{4050} \right| \times 100\%
    $$  
    
    $$
    \varepsilon = \left| \frac{-153}{4050} \right| \times 100\% \approx 3.78\%
    $$  

    **Part c:** Yes, $t = -2.6$ is reasonable. A negative time value simply represents a point in time $2.6 \text{ weeks}$ *before* the start of the current model ($t=0$). Unlike a problem asking for "time taken" (which must be positive), a timeline model can extend backwards into the past.  
  </TabItem>
