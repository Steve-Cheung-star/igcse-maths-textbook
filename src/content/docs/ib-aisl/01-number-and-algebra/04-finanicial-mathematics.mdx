In previous sections, we saw how simple interest acts as an arithmetic sequence, adding a constant amount of money to an investment each year. However, in the real world, most financial systems operate on **compound interest**, where you earn interest on your initial investment *and* on the interest you have already accumulated. This creates a snowball effect, modelling a **geometric sequence**.

Understanding these mathematical models is not just for passing examinations; it is the foundation of personal financial literacy, from saving for retirement to taking out a mortgage.

<Aside type="note" title="International-Mindedness: Perspectives on Interest">
Do all societies view investment and interest in the same way? While Western financial systems are heavily built around earning and paying interest, Islamic finance principles strictly prohibit the charging or receiving of interest (*riba*), viewing it as exploitative. Instead, Islamic banking operates on profit-and-loss sharing models. Mathematics gives us the tools to model different systems, but cultural and ethical values determine how those systems are applied.
</Aside>

## 1. Compound Interest

When interest is compounded, the value of the investment is multiplied by a constant percentage increase. If interest is paid once a year, it is a straightforward geometric sequence. However, banks often compound interest multiple times a year (half-yearly, quarterly, or monthly). 

<FormulaBox title="The Compound Interest Formula">

$$
FV = PV \times \left(1 + \frac{r}{100k}\right)^{kn}
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of the Finance Formula">
You must know what each variable represents in your formula booklet:
* **$FV$**: Future Value (the final amount of money).
* **$PV$**: Present Value (the initial investment or principal).
* **$r$**: The nominal annual interest rate (as a percentage, e.g., use 5 for $5\%$, not $0.05$, because the formula divides by 100).
* **$n$**: The number of years the money is invested.
* **$k$**: The number of compounding periods per year (Yearly $k=1$, Half-yearly $k=2$, Quarterly $k=4$, Monthly $k=12$).
</Aside>

## 2. Annual Depreciation

Assets like cars, computers, and factory machinery lose value over time. This loss of value is called **depreciation**. It behaves exactly like compound interest, but instead of growing, the value decays by a constant percentage each year.

<FormulaBox title="Annual Depreciation">

$$
FV = PV \times \left(1 - \frac{r}{100}\right)^n
$$

</FormulaBox>

Notice that the plus sign has simply become a minus sign. The multiplier will be a decimal between $0$ and $1$, representing the percentage of value that the asset *retains* each year.

## 3. Real Value and Inflation

When you invest money, the numerical amount in your bank account grows. However, the cost of living (goods, services, groceries) also usually increases over time. This is known as **inflation**. 

If your bank account grows by $3\%$ a year, but inflation is $5\%$ a year, your money is actually losing **purchasing power**. The **real value** of an investment takes both the interest rate and the inflation rate into account.

<Steps>
1. Calculate the Future Value ($FV$) of the investment using the standard compound interest formula or your TVM solver.
2. Treat the inflation rate as a form of depreciation on the purchasing power of that money.
3. Multiply the $FV$ by $(1 - \frac{\text{inflation rate}}{100})^n$ to find the "real value" of the money in today's terms.
</Steps>

## 4. Technology: The Financial Solver

While you must understand the algebraic formulas, IB examinations heavily encourage the use of the Financial Packages (TVM Solver - Time Value of Money) built into your Graphics Display Calculator (GDC). This is absolutely essential when dealing with annuities (regular payments) or loan amortisation, where doing the calculations by hand is excessively complex.

Crucially, **you can also use the TVM solver for simple lump-sum investments or depreciation (non-payment scenarios)** by simply setting the payment (`PMT`) parameter to zero.

<SteveTip title="GDC Financial App Variables & Sign Conventions">
When using the Finance Solver on your TI-Nspire or Casio, you must explicitly write down the values you are inputting onto your exam paper to receive method marks. 

**CRITICAL: The Sign Convention (+/-)**
Your GDC treats money as cash flows. 
* **Outflow (Negative):** Money leaving your pocket (e.g., depositing a lump sum into a bank, making a loan payment).
* **Inflow (Positive):** Money entering your pocket (e.g., receiving a loan from the bank, withdrawing a final balance).

**The Parameters:**
* **N**: Total number of compounding periods (Years $\times$ $k$).
* **I%**: Annual interest rate (enter as a regular number, e.g., 5. For depreciation, enter as a negative rate, e.g., -15).
* **PV**: Present Value (usually negative for investments, positive for loans).
* **PMT**: Payment. **Set this to $0$ for single lump-sum non-payment scenarios**. Use negative values for regular loan repayments or regular savings.
* **FV**: Future Value.
* **P/Y** & **C/Y**: Payments per year and Compounding periods per year (usually set to $k$).
</SteveTip>

### Annuities (Regular Savings)
An annuity is a series of equal payments made at regular intervals. Instead of investing a single lump sum, you might deposit a set amount every month. You use the **PMT** parameter in your TVM solver to model this.

### Loans and Amortisation
When you take out a loan (a positive **PV** because the bank gives you money), you must make regular repayments (a negative **PMT** because you are giving money back). Over time, the outstanding balance decreases. This process of paying off a debt over time in regular instalments is called **amortisation**.
