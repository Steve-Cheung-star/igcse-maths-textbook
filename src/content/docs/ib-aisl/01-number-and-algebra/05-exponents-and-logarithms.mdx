In the previous sections, we used exponents to model compound interest and geometric sequences. However, what happens when the *exponent itself* is the unknown variable? If you know an investment grows by $5\%$ every year, how do you find exactly how long it will take to double? 

To solve these types of problems, we must first master the algebraic laws of exponents, and then introduce their mathematical inverse: **logarithms**.

---

## 1. The Laws of Exponents

Exponents (also called indices or powers) represent repeated multiplication. The IB AI SL syllabus requires you to fluently manipulate algebraic expressions using integer exponents. 

| Law | Algebraic Rule | Description |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Multiplication** | $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$ | When multiplying terms with the same base, add the exponents. |
| **Division** | $\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$ | When dividing terms with the same base, subtract the exponents. |
| **Power of a Power** | $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$ | When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents. |
| **Power of a Product** | $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ | The exponent applies to every factor inside the bracket. |
| **Negative Exponents** | $a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$ | A negative exponent indicates a reciprocal. |

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
Students frequently make errors when applying powers to coefficients. 
* **Correct:** $(2x)^4 = 2^4 \times x^4 = 16x^4$
* **Incorrect:** $(2x)^4 = 2x^4$ (The $2$ must also be raised to the power of $4$!)

Similarly, negative exponents only apply to the base they are directly attached to. 
* **Correct:** $2x^{-3} = 2 \times \frac{1}{x^3} = \frac{2}{x^3}$
* **Incorrect:** $2x^{-3} = \frac{1}{2x^3}$
</Aside>

## 2. Introduction to Logarithms

If $2^3 = 8$, how do we mathematically express the question: *"2 to what power equals 8?"*
The answer is a **logarithm**. A logarithm simply asks for the exponent required to produce a specific number.

<FormulaBox title="Logarithmic Equivalence">

$$
a^x = b \iff x = \log_a b \text{, where } a > 0, b > 0, a \neq 1
$$

</FormulaBox>

### Special Bases: Base 10 and Base $e$
While a logarithm can have any positive base, two bases are so common that they have special notations on your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC):

1. **Common Logarithm (Base 10):** Often used in scientific scales. If a base is not written, it is assumed to be 10.  
   $$ \log x \quad \text{means} \quad \log_{10} x $$
2. **Natural Logarithm (Base $e$):** Euler's number ($e \approx 2.718...$) appears constantly in natural growth and continuous compound interest.  
   $$ \ln x \quad \text{means} \quad \log_{e} x $$

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Is mathematics invented or discovered?** Consider the number $e$ (Euler's number) or the concept of logarithms. John Napier published his work on logarithms in 1614 to simplify astronomical calculations, long before $e$ was formally defined. Did Napier *invent* logarithms as a computational tool, or did he *discover* an underlying structural truth of the universe that was already there waiting to be found? 
</Aside>

## 3. Real-World Contexts

Because logarithms find the *exponent*, they are incredibly useful for compressing astronomically large ranges of numbers into manageable, linear scales. 

* **The Richter Scale:** Measures earthquake magnitude. An earthquake of magnitude 6.0 is $10$ times more powerful than a 5.0, and $100$ times more powerful than a 4.0.
* **The Decibel Scale (dB):** Measures sound intensity. 
* **pH Scale (Chemistry):** Measures the acidity or alkalinity of a solution based on the concentration of hydrogen ions, $H^+$. The formula is $\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]$.

    Simplify the following expressions using the laws of exponents. Give your answers without negative indices.
    
    a) $5^3 \times 5^{-6}$  
    b) $\frac{6^4}{6^3}$  
    c) $(2x)^4 \times 3x^{-2}$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a:** Add the exponents:
    
    $$
    5^{3 + (-6)} = 5^{-3}
    $$
    
    To remove the negative index, take the reciprocal:
    
    $$
    = \frac{1}{5^3} = \frac{1}{125}
    $$

    **Part b:** Subtract the exponents:
    
    $$
    6^{4 - 3} = 6^1 = 6
    $$

    **Part c:** Apply the power of $4$ to both the $2$ and the $x$, then multiply the coefficients and add the exponents of $x$:
    
    $$
    (16x^4) \times (3x^{-2})
    $$
    
    $$
    = (16 \times 3) \times x^{4 + (-2)}
    $$
    
    $$
    = 48x^2
    $$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Scientific Application (pH Scale)">
    Maya, a chemistry student, is analysing a buffer solution. The pH of a solution is calculated using the formula:
    
    $$
    \text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]
    $$
    
    Where $[H^+]$ is the concentration of hydrogen ions in moles per litre.
    
    a) Calculate the pH of a solution where the hydrogen ion concentration is $4.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ mol L}^{-1}$.  
    b) If a different solution has a pH of $6.2$, calculate its hydrogen ion concentration, giving your answer in standard form.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Part a:** Substitute the concentration into the formula and use your GDC.
    
    $$
    \text{pH} = -\log_{10}(4.5 \times 10^{-4})
    $$
    
    $$
    \text{pH} = -(-3.346...) \approx 3.35
    $$

    **Part b:** Substitute the pH into the formula and solve for $[H^+]$ by converting to exponential form.
    
    $$
    6.2 = -\log_{10}[H^+]
    $$
    
    Multiply by $-1$:
    
    $$
    -6.2 = \log_{10}[H^+]
    $$
    
    Convert to exponential form (base is $10$, exponent is $-6.2$):
    
    $$
    [H^+] = 10^{-6.2}
    $$
    
    Using a GDC to find the value in standard form:
    
    $$
    [H^+] \approx 6.31 \times 10^{-7} \text{ mol L}^{-1}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Logarithmic Modelling (Decibels)">
    Leo is studying sound intensity for a physics project. The sound level $L$, in decibels (dB), is modelled by the formula:
    
    $$
    L = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{I}{I_0} \right)
    $$
    
    Where $I$ is the sound intensity in $\text{W m}^{-2}$, and $I_0 = 10^{-12} \text{ W m}^{-2}$ is the standard threshold of human hearing.
    
    a) Calculate the sound level $L$ of a normal conversation, which has a sound intensity of $3.2 \times 10^{-6} \text{ W m}^{-2}$.  
    b) A live rock concert has a recorded sound level of $115 \text{ dB}$. Calculate the exact sound intensity $I$ of the concert, giving your answer in standard form.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Part a:** Substitute $I$ and $I_0$ into the formula:
    
    $$
    L = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-6}}{10^{-12}} \right)
    $$
    
    $$
    L = 10 \log_{10} (3.2 \times 10^6)
    $$
    
    $$
    L = 10 \times (6.505...) \approx 65.1 \text{ dB}
    $$

    **Part b:** Substitute $L = 115$ and solve for $I$:
    
    $$
    115 = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{I}{10^{-12}} \right)
    $$
    
    Divide by 10:
    
    $$
    11.5 = \log_{10} \left( \frac{I}{10^{-12}} \right)
    $$
    
    Convert to exponential form (the base is 10):
    
    $$
    10^{11.5} = \frac{I}{10^{-12}}
    $$
    
    Multiply both sides by $10^{-12}$:
    
    $$
    I = 10^{11.5} \times 10^{-12}
    $$
    
    $$
    I = 10^{-0.5} \approx 3.16 \times 10^{-1} \text{ W m}^{-2}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Continuous Growth (Base e)">
    A biologist, Elias, models the continuous growth of a bacteria population using the formula:
    
    $$
    P(t) = 450 \times e^{0.12t}
    $$
    
    Where $P$ is the population size and $t$ is the time in hours.
    Find the time it takes for the bacteria population to reach $2000$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    Substitute $P(t) = 2000$ into the equation:
    
    $$
    2000 = 450 \times e^{0.12t}
    $$
    
    Divide both sides by $450$:
    
    $$
    \frac{2000}{450} = e^{0.12t}
    $$
    
    $$
    \frac{40}{9} = e^{0.12t}
    $$
    
    Convert the exponential equation into logarithmic form. Because the base is $e$, we use the natural logarithm ($\ln$):
    
    $$
    \ln\left(\frac{40}{9}\right) = 0.12t
    $$
    
    Divide by $0.12$ to isolate $t$:
    
    $$
    t = \frac{\ln(40/9)}{0.12}
    $$
    
    Using a GDC:
    
    $$
    t \approx \frac{1.49165...}{0.12} \approx 12.43
    $$
    
    It will take approximately $12.4$ hours for the population to reach $2000$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
