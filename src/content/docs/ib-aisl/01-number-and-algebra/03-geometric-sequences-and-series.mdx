In the previous section, we explored patterns that change by adding or subtracting a constant amount. However, many phenomena in the natural and financial world—such as the rapid spread of a virus, the exponential growth of a population, or the steady depreciation of a car—change by a constant **percentage** or **ratio**. These are known as **geometric sequences**.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Geometric Sequence

A geometric sequence is an ordered list of numbers where each term is found by multiplying the previous term by a fixed, non-zero number called the **common ratio**.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Sequence Notation">
To successfully model geometric patterns, you need to be familiar with the following notation:
* **$u_n$**: The value of the $n$th term in the sequence.
* **$n$**: The position of the term in the sequence (where $n$ is a positive integer: $1, 2, 3, \dots$).
* **$u_1$**: The first term of the sequence.
* **$r$**: The common ratio between consecutive terms ($r = u_{n+1} \div u_n$).
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
When checking if a sequence is geometric, you must divide a term by the *previous* term, not the other way around! For example, in the sequence $10, 5, 2.5 \dots$, the common ratio is $r = 5 \div 10 = 0.5$, not $10 \div 5 = 2$. Furthermore, the common ratio can be a fraction (e.g., $r = \frac{1}{3}$) or a negative number.
</Aside>

## 2. The General Term

To find the value of any specific term in a geometric sequence without continually multiplying, we use the $n$th term formula. Since you begin at $u_1$ and multiply by $r$ for every subsequent step, reaching the $n$th term requires multiplying by $r$ exactly $(n-1)$ times.

<FormulaBox title="The nth Term of a Geometric Sequence">

$$
u_n = u_1 \times r^{n-1}
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="tip" title="Order of Operations">
When calculating $u_n$, remember your laws of indices! You must evaluate the exponent $r^{n-1}$ **before** multiplying by $u_1$. A classic mistake is calculating $(u_1 \times r)^{n-1}$, which will give an incorrect and usually massively inflated result.
</Aside>

---

## 3. Geometric Series and Sigma Notation

If you add the terms of a geometric sequence together, you create a **geometric series**. We use specific formulae to calculate the sum of the first $n$ terms, denoted as $S_n$.

<FormulaBox title="The Sum of a Geometric Series">

$$
S_n = \frac{u_1(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} = \frac{u_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}
$$

</FormulaBox>

Both versions of this formula are provided in your IB formula booklet and both will give the exact same answer. However, as a general rule of thumb to avoid negative numbers in your working:
* Use the first version when $r > 1$ (growth).
* Use the second version when $r < 1$ (decay or fractional ratio).

### Sigma Notation ($\Sigma$)
Just as with arithmetic series, geometric series are often represented using sigma notation to instruct you to sum the terms generated by a geometric rule.

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n} u_1 \times r^{k-1}
$$

## 4. Real-World Applications

Geometric sequences are one of the most powerful tools in applied mathematics because they model exponential growth and decay perfectly.

<Steps>
1. **Population Growth:** Modelling the increase in bacteria, animals, or human populations over time.
2. **Spread of Disease:** Tracking the number of new infections when an outbreak begins.
3. **Financial Salaries:** Calculating a salary that increases by a fixed percentage (e.g., $4.5\%$ raise) every year.
4. **Depreciation:** Calculating the decreasing value of assets like cars or technology over time.
5. **Physics Connections:** Modelling radioactive decay (half-lives) and the charging or discharging of capacitors.
</Steps>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Intuition vs. Mathematical Truth**
How do mathematicians reconcile the fact that some conclusions seem to conflict with our intuitions? Consider for instance that a finite area can be bounded by an infinite perimeter. A famous example of this is the *Koch snowflake*, a shape created by continually adding smaller triangles to the sides of a larger triangle in a geometric progression. Its area converges to a finite number, but its perimeter grows infinitely large. When mathematics defies human common sense, should we trust the numbers or our perception?
</Aside>

    A geometric sequence begins $162, 108, 72, 48 \dots$
    
    a) Identify the first term $u_1$ and calculate the exact common ratio $r$ as a fraction.  
    b) Find the 8th term of the sequence, giving your answer as an exact fraction.  
    c) Calculate the sum of the first 10 terms, rounding your answer to 2 decimal places.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a:**
    The first term is $u_1 = 162$.
    To find the common ratio, divide the second term by the first term:
    
    $$
    r = \frac{108}{162} = \frac{2}{3}
    $$

    **Part b:**
    Use the $n$th term formula with $n = 8$:
    
    $$
    u_n = u_1 \times r^{n-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    u_8 = 162 \times \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{8-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    u_8 = 162 \times \frac{128}{2187} = \frac{20736}{2187} = \frac{256}{27}
    $$

    **Part c:**
    Use the sum formula for $r < 1$ with $n = 10$:
    
    $$
    S_{10} = \frac{162\left(1 - \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{10}\right)}{1 - \frac{2}{3}}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{10} = \frac{162(1 - 0.0173415...)}{\frac{1}{3}}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{10} \approx 477.57
    $$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Two Arbitrary Terms (Show That)">
    The second term of a geometric sequence is $250$, and the fifth term is $16$. 
    
    a) Show that the common ratio $r = 0.4$.  
    b) Find the first term, $u_1$.  
    c) Calculate the sum of the first 8 terms.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **Part a:**
    Set up equations for the given terms:
    
    $$
    u_2 = u_1 r = 250
    $$
    
    $$
    u_5 = u_1 r^4 = 16
    $$

    Divide the equation for $u_5$ by the equation for $u_2$:
    
    $$
    \frac{u_1 r^4}{u_1 r} = \frac{16}{250}
    $$
    
    $$
    r^3 = \frac{16}{250} = \frac{8}{125}
    $$

    Take the cube root of both sides to *show* the result:
    
    $$
    r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{8}{125}} = \frac{2}{5} = 0.4
    $$

    **Part b:**
    Substitute $r = 0.4$ back into the first equation to find $u_1$:
    
    $$
    u_1(0.4) = 250
    $$
    
    $$
    u_1 = \frac{250}{0.4} = 625
    $$

    **Part c:**
    Now use the sum formula for the first 8 terms:
    
    $$
    S_8 = \frac{625(1 - 0.4^8)}{1 - 0.4}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_8 = \frac{625(1 - 0.00065536)}{0.6} \approx 1041.24
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Algebraic Consecutive Terms">
    The first three terms of a geometric sequence are $x, x+3$, and $x+12$.
    
    a) Show that $x = 1.5$.  
    b) Hence, write down the common ratio of the sequence.  
    c) Find the 6th term of the sequence.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Part a:**
    Because it is a geometric sequence, the ratio of consecutive terms must be equal:
    
    $$
    \frac{x+3}{x} = \frac{x+12}{x+3}
    $$
    
    Cross-multiply to solve for $x$:
    
    $$
    (x+3)(x+3) = x(x+12)
    $$
    
    $$
    x^2 + 6x + 9 = x^2 + 12x
    $$
    
    Subtract $x^2$ from both sides:
    
    $$
    6x + 9 = 12x
    $$
    
    $$
    9 = 6x
    $$
    
    $$
    x = \frac{9}{6} = 1.5
    $$
    
    **Part b:**
    Using $x = 1.5$, the first three terms are:
    * $u_1 = 1.5$
    * $u_2 = 1.5 + 3 = 4.5$
    * $u_3 = 1.5 + 12 = 13.5$
    
    The common ratio is:
    
    $$
    r = \frac{4.5}{1.5} = 3
    $$

    **Part c:**
    Find the 6th term using $u_1 = 1.5$ and $r = 3$:
    
    $$
    u_6 = 1.5 \times 3^{6-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    u_6 = 1.5 \times 3^5 = 1.5 \times 243 = 364.5
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A new tech start-up offers an employee a starting salary of £35,000. Each year, the employee receives a $4.5\%$ pay increase. 
    
    a) Write down the common ratio $r$ for this geometric sequence.  
    b) Calculate the employee's salary in their 5th year.  
    c) Calculate the total amount of money earned by the employee over their first 10 years at the company.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a:**
    A $4.5\%$ increase means retaining $100\%$ and adding $4.5\%$, which is $104.5\%$. The multiplier (common ratio) is therefore:
    
    $$
    r = 1.045
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We need the 5th term ($u_5$) where $u_1 = 35000$:
    
    $$
    u_5 = 35000 \times 1.045^{5-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    u_5 = 35000 \times 1.045^4 \approx 41738.16
    $$
    
    The salary in the 5th year is £41,738.16.

    **Part c:**
    Since we want the *total* amount earned over 10 years, we use the geometric sum formula ($S_{10}$):
    
    $$
    S_{10} = \frac{35000(1.045^{10} - 1)}{1.045 - 1}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{10} = \frac{35000(1.55296... - 1)}{0.045} = 430087.12
    $$
    
    The total amount earned is £430,087.12.

    0.85^n < 0.125

    A local business launches a viral marketing campaign. On the first day, a promotional video is shared by 50 people. Every subsequent day, the number of new people sharing the video is 3 times the number of people who shared it the previous day.
    
    a) Calculate the number of *new* people who share the video on the 5th day.  
    b) Calculate the *total* number of people who have shared the video by the end of the 7th day.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **Part a:**
    This is a geometric sequence where $u_1 = 50$ and $r = 3$. We need to find $u_5$:
    
    $$
    u_5 = 50 \times 3^{5-1}
    $$
    
    $$
    u_5 = 50 \times 3^4 = 50 \times 81 = 4050
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We need the total sum of all people over 7 days, which means calculating $S_7$:
    
    $$
    S_7 = \frac{50(3^7 - 1)}{3 - 1}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_7 = \frac{50(2187 - 1)}{2}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_7 = 25 \times 2186 = 54650
    $$
    
    By the end of the 7th day, a total of 54,650 people have shared the video.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 9: Exceeding a Boundary (Optimization)">
    A geometric series has a first term of $u_1 = 5$ and a common ratio of $r = 1.2$.
    
    a) Write down an expression for the sum of the first $n$ terms, $S_n$.  
    b) Find the minimum number of terms required for the sum of the series to exceed $10,000$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **Part a:**
    Using the sum formula $S_n = \frac{u_1(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}$:
    
    $$
    S_n = \frac{5(1.2^n - 1)}{1.2 - 1}
    $$
    
    $$
    S_n = \frac{5(1.2^n - 1)}{0.2} = 25(1.2^n - 1)
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We need to find the smallest integer $n$ such that $S_n > 10000$. Set up the inequality:
    
    $$
    25(1.2^n - 1) > 10000
    $$
    
    Divide by 25:
    
    $$
    1.2^n - 1 > 400
    $$
    
    $$
    1.2^n > 401
    $$
    
    Using the Table or Graph function on your GDC for $y = 1.2^x$:
    * When $n = 32$, $1.2^{32} \approx 341.8$ (Sum is too small)
    * When $n = 33$, $1.2^{33} \approx 410.2$ (Sum exceeds 10,000)
    
    The minimum number of terms required is 33.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
