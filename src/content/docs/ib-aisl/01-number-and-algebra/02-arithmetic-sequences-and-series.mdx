Mathematics is often described as the science of patterns. When a pattern of numbers increases or decreases by a constant amount each time, we call it an **arithmetic sequence**. Understanding these sequences allows us to model everything from simple interest in a bank account to the steady depreciation of a car's value.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of an Arithmetic Sequence

An arithmetic sequence is an ordered list of numbers where the difference between any two consecutive terms is always exactly the same.

<Aside type="note" title="The Hierarchy of Sequence Notation">
To master arithmetic sequences, you must be fluent in the specific notation used in IB Mathematics:
* **$u_n$**: The value of the $n$th term in the sequence.
* **$n$**: The position of the term in the sequence (where $n$ is a positive integer: $1, 2, 3, \dots$).
* **$u_1$**: The first term of the sequence.
* **$d$**: The common difference between consecutive terms ($d = u_{n+1} - u_n$).
</Aside>

## 2. The $n$th Term Formula

To find any term in an arithmetic sequence without listing out all the previous numbers, we use the $n$th term formula. Since you start at $u_1$ and add $d$ for every step you take, reaching the $n$th term requires $(n-1)$ steps. The formula for the $n$th term is given in your formula booklet.

<FormulaBox title="The nth Term of an Arithmetic Sequence">

$$
u_n = u_1 + (n-1)d
$$

</FormulaBox>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
Students often incorrectly substitute the term value into $n$. Remember, $n$ is always the **position** (e.g., 5th, 10th), while $u_n$ is the actual **value** of the number at that position. $n$ must always be a positive integer!
</Aside>

---

## 3. Arithmetic Series and Sigma Notation

While a **sequence** is a list of numbers separated by commas, a **series** is the sum of those numbers. If you add up the terms of an arithmetic sequence, you get an arithmetic series.

There are two formulae for the sum of the first $n$ terms, denoted as $S_n$. Both are provided in your formula booklet.

<FormulaBox title="The Sum of an Arithmetic Series (Using Common Difference)">

$$
S_n = \frac{n}{2}(2u_1 + (n-1)d)
$$

</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="The Sum of an Arithmetic Series (Using Last Term)">

$$
S_n = \frac{n}{2}(u_1 + u_n)
$$

</FormulaBox>

### Sigma Notation ($\Sigma$)
Mathematicians use the uppercase Greek letter Sigma ($\Sigma$) as shorthand to represent a sum. 

<Aside type="note" title="International-Mindedness: Mathematical Alphabets">
Mathematics is a truly global language, heavily borrowing from the Greek alphabet (like $\Sigma$ for sum, or $\pi$, $\theta$). Historically, the study of sequences and algebra spans cultures. Aryabhatta (born 476 CE in India) is sometimes considered the "father of algebra", although this title is also frequently attributed to the Persian mathematician al-Khawarizmi, whose text *Al-Jabr* gave algebra its modern name.
</Aside>

When you see sigma notation, it reads as "the sum of":

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n} (ak + b)
$$

<Steps>
1. **The bottom limit ($k=1$)** tells you the first value to substitute into the expression.
2. **The top limit ($n$)** tells you the final value to substitute.
3. You evaluate the expression for every integer from the bottom limit to the top limit, and then add all the results together.
</Steps>

## 4. Working Backwards with Technology

Often in IB exams, you will be given the sum or a specific term and asked to work backwards to find $n$, $u_1$, or $d$. While algebraic manipulation is important, your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC) is a highly efficient tool for these questions.

<SteveTip title="The Danger of nSolve with Quadratics">
The `nSolve` function on your GDC is fantastic for linear equations, but it **only returns a single solution**. 

When you use the arithmetic sum formula ($S_n$), you are often forming a quadratic equation for $n$. Quadratics can have two valid solutions! If you rely purely on `nSolve`, you might miss a second correct answer. 

For quadratics, it is much safer to rearrange your equation to equal zero and graph it to find all the $x$-intercepts (zeros), or use the built-in Polynomial Root Finder.
</SteveTip>

## 5. Real-World Applications and Approximations

In examinations, arithmetic sequences are frequently applied to real-life contexts. 

### Simple Interest
Simple interest is a perfect real-world example of an arithmetic sequence. Because the interest is calculated only on the original principal amount, the total amount of money grows by a constant value each year (the common difference, $d$).

### Approximating Real Data
In reality, data rarely perfectly follows a mathematical model. When given a table of values that roughly increase by a constant amount, you may be asked to model it as an arithmetic sequence.

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge (TOK)">
**Is all knowledge concerned with identification and use of patterns?** Arithmetic sequences are highly predictable linear patterns. But look at Fibonacci numbers ($1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8...$), found everywhere from the arrangement of leaves to the spirals of galaxies. As humans, we are wired to seek patterns to make sense of the universe. If a pattern (like the golden ratio) appears repeatedly in nature, is the mathematics inventing the pattern, or merely discovering what is already there?
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Finding the nth Term">
    An arithmetic sequence has a first term $u_1 = 7$ and a common difference $d = 4$.
    
    a) Find the 15th term of the sequence.  
    b) Determine which term in the sequence has a value of $115$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a:**
    Use the $n$th term formula:
    
    $$
    u_n = u_1 + (n-1)d
    $$
    
    Substitute $n=15$, $u_1=7$, and $d=4$:
    
    $$
    u_{15} = 7 + (15-1)4
    $$
    
    $$
    u_{15} = 7 + (14)(4) = 7 + 56 = 63
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We know $u_n = 115$, and we need to find the position $n$.
    
    $$
    115 = 7 + (n-1)4
    $$
    
    $$
    108 = 4(n-1)
    $$
    
    $$
    27 = n - 1
    $$
    
    $$
    n = 28
    $$
    
    So, $115$ is the 28th term.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Consider the following series:
    
    $$
    \sum_{n=1}^{20} (3n + 2)

    a) Find the first term, $u_1$, and the common difference, $d$.  
    b) Calculate the sum of the series.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Part a:**
    First, find the first few terms by substituting $n$:
    * When $n=1$: $u_1 = 3(1) + 2 = 5$
    * When $n=2$: $u_2 = 3(2) + 2 = 8$
    * When $n=3$: $u_3 = 3(3) + 2 = 11$
    
    This shows an arithmetic sequence with $u_1 = 5$ and a common difference $d = 3$.

    **Part b:**
    We need the sum of the first 20 terms ($n=20$). Let's use the sum formula:
    
    $$
    S_{20} = \frac{20}{2} (2(5) + (20-1)3)
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{20} = 10 (10 + (19)3)
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{20} = 10 (10 + 57) = 10(67) = 670
    $$
  </TabItem>

    * Week 2: 12.5 km
    * Week 3: 15.2 km
    * Week 4: 17.4 km

    **Part c:** Model as an arithmetic sequence with $u_1 = 10$ and $d = 2.5$. Find $u_{10}$:
    
    $$
    u_{10} = 10 + (10-1)2.5
    $$
    
    $$
    u_{10} = 10 + 9(2.5) = 10 + 22.5 = 32.5 \text{ km}

    An arithmetic series has a sum of $770$ for the first $20$ terms. The common difference is $3$.
    
    a) Formulate an equation for the sum of the first 20 terms in terms of the first term, $u_1$.

    $$
    77 = 2u_1 + 57

    $$
    20 = 2u_1
    $$
    
    $$
    u_1 = 10
    $$

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Using Technology to find 'n'">
    An arithmetic sequence begins $14, 18, 22, \dots$ 
    The sum of the first $n$ terms of this sequence is $1520$. 
    
    a) Write down the common difference, $d$.  
    b) Formulate an equation for the sum of the first $n$ terms equal to $1520$.  
    c) Use technology to find the value of $n$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a:**
    $d = 18 - 14 = 4$. We also have $u_1 = 14$ and $S_n = 1520$.

    **Part b:**
    Set up the sum equation substituting the known parameters:
    
    $$
    1520 = \frac{n}{2}(2(14) + (n-1)4)
    $$
    
    This equation is a quadratic with respect to $n$.

    **Part c:**
    **Safe GDC Method:** Set the equation to 0: $0 = \frac{n}{2}(28 + 4n - 4) - 1520$.
    Graph $f(x) = \frac{x}{2}(4x + 24) - 1520$ and find the positive zero (x-intercept), or use the Polynomial Root Finder.
    
    $$
    n = 26
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Given Two Arbitrary Terms">
    The 4th term of an arithmetic sequence is $17$, and the 10th term is $35$.
    
    a) Write down two simultaneous equations relating $u_1$ and $d$.  
    b) Solve these equations to find the value of $d$ and the value of $u_1$.  
    c) Calculate the sum of the first 15 terms.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **Part a:**
    We set up a system of simultaneous equations using the $n$th term formula ($u_n = u_1 + (n-1)d$):
    
    $$
    u_4 = u_1 + 3d = 17
    $$
    
    $$
    u_{10} = u_1 + 9d = 35
    $$

    **Part b:**
    Subtract the first equation from the second equation to eliminate $u_1$:
    
    $$
    (u_1 + 9d) - (u_1 + 3d) = 35 - 17
    $$
    
    $$
    6d = 18
    $$
    
    $$
    d = 3
    $$
    
    Substitute $d = 3$ back into the first equation:
    
    $$
    u_1 + 3(3) = 17
    $$
    
    $$
    u_1 + 9 = 17
    $$
    
    $$
    u_1 = 8
    $$

    **Part c:**
    Now find $S_{15}$ with $u_1 = 8$ and $d = 3$:
    
    $$
    S_{15} = \frac{15}{2}(2(8) + (15-1)3)
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{15} = 7.5(16 + 14 \times 3)
    $$
    
    $$
    S_{15} = 7.5(16 + 42) = 7.5(58) = 435
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
