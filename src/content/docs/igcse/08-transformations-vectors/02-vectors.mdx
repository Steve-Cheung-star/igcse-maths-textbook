In mathematics, quantities are split into two categories. A **scalar** has only a magnitude (size), like temperature, mass, or speed. A **vector** has both a magnitude *and* a direction, like velocity or a translation from one point to another.

<Aside type="note" title="Vector Hierarchy">

  1. **Column Vectors:** Represented as $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, showing the exact horizontal and vertical steps to get from start to finish.
  2. **Position Vectors:** Vectors that specifically start from the origin $(0,0)$.
  3. **Displacement Vectors:** Vectors that describe the journey between two specific points, denoted using the ray command, e.g., $\overrightarrow{AB}$.

</Aside>

---

## 1. Describing Vectors and Translations

A vector describes a journey. On a grid, we write this journey as a **column vector**:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}
$$

* **Top number ($x$):** The horizontal movement. Positive means right; negative means left.
* **Bottom number ($y$):** The vertical movement. Positive means up; negative means down.

### Notation
Vectors can be written in three ways:
* As a directed line segment between two points: **$\overrightarrow{AB}$** (starts at $A$, ends at $B$).
* As a bold lowercase letter: **$\mathbf{a}$** (in your handwriting, you should underline it like $\underline{a}$).
* As a column vector: $\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

<SteveTip title="Points vs Vectors">

  Do not confuse coordinates with vectors! A coordinate $(4, 3)$ tells you a specific *location* on the grid. A vector $\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ tells you a *movement* (go right 4, up 3), which can start from absolutely anywhere on the page.

</SteveTip>

## 2. Multiplying by a Scalar

A scalar is just a regular number. When you multiply a vector by a scalar, you are scaling its length.

<Aside type="tip" title="Rules of Scalar Multiplication">

  * **Multiply by a number > 1:** The vector gets longer.
  * **Multiply by a fraction (between 0 and 1):** The vector gets shorter.
  * **Multiply by a negative number:** The vector reverses its direction exactly $180^\circ$.

</Aside>

Algebraically, you simply multiply both the top and bottom numbers of the column vector by the scalar:

$$
\begin{aligned}
3 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \times 2 \\ 3 \times -1 \end{pmatrix} \\
&= \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Vector Addition & Subtraction

Vectors can be added and subtracted both algebraically (using column vectors) and geometrically (drawing them).

### Algebraic Method
To add or subtract column vectors, simply apply the operation to the $x$ values, and do the same for the $y$ values.

* **Addition:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 + 2 \\ 5 + (-1) \end{pmatrix} \\
&= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

* **Subtraction:**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 4 - (-1) \\ 2 - 3 \end{pmatrix} \\
&= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Geometric Method (Head-to-Tail)
To add vectors geometrically, you connect them **head-to-tail**. 
1. Draw the first vector.
2. Start the tail of the second vector exactly at the head (arrow tip) of the first vector.
3. The **resultant vector** (the answer) is the straight line drawn from the very start (the first tail) to the very end (the final head).

To subtract a vector, you add its negative. So, $\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{q}$ is identical to $\mathbf{p} + (-\mathbf{q})$. You draw $\mathbf{p}$, then draw $\mathbf{q}$ but pointing it strictly $180^\circ$ backwards!

    Given $\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}$. 
    
    Calculate as a single column vector:
    
    a) $4\mathbf{u}$

    b) $\mathbf{u} + 2\mathbf{v}$

    c) $3\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}$

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. **Part a:** Multiply both components of $\mathbf{u}$ by $4$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         4\mathbf{u} &= 4 \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 20 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. **Part b:** Add $\mathbf{u}$ to twice of $\mathbf{v}$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \mathbf{u} + 2\mathbf{v} &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} + 2\begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} -6 \\ 14 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      3. **Part c:** Subtract $\mathbf{v}$ from thrice of $\mathbf{u}$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         3\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v} &= 3\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ -6 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 15 - (-3) \\ -6 - 7 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 18 \\ -13 \end{pmatrix}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>

    You are given the vector equation $3\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{s}$, where $\mathbf{x}$ is an unknown column vector.
    
    a) Calculate the column vector for $3\mathbf{r}$.

    b) Rearrange the equation to make $\mathbf{x}$ the subject.

    c) Write the final column vector for $\mathbf{x}$.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    <Steps>
      1. **Part a:** Multiply $\mathbf{r}$ by $3$. $3\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 15 \end{pmatrix}$.
      2. **Part b:** Subtract $3\mathbf{r}$ from both sides to isolate $\mathbf{x}$. The rearranged equation is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{s} - 3\mathbf{r}$.
      3. **Part c:** Substitute the vectors and subtract.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \mathbf{x} &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 15 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 8 - 6 \\ -1 - 15 \end{pmatrix} \\
         &= \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -16 \end{pmatrix}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Applied Displacements and Journey Costs">
    A warehouse robot navigates a grid system. It moves from its charging station at the origin $O$ to point $A$ using the displacement vector $\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ 20 \end{pmatrix}$. From point $A$, it travels to point $B$ using the vector $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -5 \\ 10 \end{pmatrix}$.
    
    a) Find the direct resultant displacement vector $\overrightarrow{OB}$.

    b) The robot consumes power based on every grid unit it traverses. Moving 1 unit horizontally or vertically costs exactly 2 GBP. Calculate the total cost to travel directly from $O$ to $B$ via the displacement vector (using the sum of the absolute $x$ and $y$ components).

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    <Steps>
      1. **Part a:** The direct route from $O$ to $B$ is the sum of the two vectors: $\overrightarrow{OB} = \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$.
         $$
         \overrightarrow{OB} = \begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ 20 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} -5 \\ 10 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 30 \end{pmatrix}
         $$
      2. **Part b:** The vector $\overrightarrow{OB} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 30 \end{pmatrix}$ requires 10 horizontal units and 30 vertical units. Total units = $10 + 30 = 40$ units. 
      3. Multiply the total units by the cost per unit: $40 \times 2 = 80$. The total cost for the direct journey is 80 GBP.
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>
