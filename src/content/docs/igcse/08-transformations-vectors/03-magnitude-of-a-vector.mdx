Even though you may have seen a preview of this in the previous section, it is crucial to formally define how we measure the "size" of a vector. A vector has both direction and magnitude. The **magnitude** is simply its physical length.

---

## 1. Notation and the Modulus Sign

Because magnitude represents a length, it is a **scalar** quantity and must always be a positive number (or zero). We denote the magnitude of a vector by placing it inside **modulus signs** (vertical bars):

* The magnitude of vector $\mathbf{a}$ is written as **$|\mathbf{a}|$**.
* The magnitude of the displacement vector $\overrightarrow{AB}$ is written as **$|\overrightarrow{AB}|$**.

<SteveTip title="Absolute Value">
You might recognise the modulus lines from "absolute value" in algebra. They do the exact same job here: they strip away the "direction" (the positive/negative signs of the components) and leave you strictly with the positive size!
</SteveTip>

---

## 2. Calculating Magnitude

Every column vector $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ forms a right-angled triangle with the horizontal and vertical grid lines. 

* The horizontal movement ($x$) is the base of the triangle.
* The vertical movement ($y$) is the height of the triangle.
* The vector itself is the **hypotenuse**.

Therefore, to calculate the exact length of the vector, we apply **Pythagoras' Theorem** ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$).

<Aside type="tip" title="Magnitude Formula">
For any vector $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, its magnitude is calculated as:

$$
|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
$$
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Squaring Negatives">
A very common exam mistake is entering negative numbers incorrectly into a calculator. Remember that squaring a negative component always results in a positive number! 
$(-3)^2$ is $+9$, not $-9$. A vector's length can never be negative. If your calculator says $\sqrt{-16}$, you have made a mistake.
</Aside>

## Practice Questions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Basic Magnitude">
    Given the vector $\mathbf{p} = \begin{pmatrix} -5 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix}$.

    a) Write down the horizontal and vertical components of the vector $\mathbf{p}$.

    b) State the square of each component.

    c) Calculate the exact magnitude of the vector $\mathbf{p}$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The horizontal component is the top number: $x = -5$.
      2. The vertical component is the bottom number: $y = 12$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. $(-5)^2 = (-5) \times (-5) = 25$.
      2. $12^2 = 12 \times 12 = 144$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute into the formula: $|\mathbf{p}| = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + 12^2}$.
      2. Add the squares: $|\mathbf{p}| = \sqrt{25 + 144}$.
      3. Simplify: $|\mathbf{p}| = \sqrt{169} = 13$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Magnitude from Coordinates">
    Point $A$ has coordinates $(-2, 4)$ and point $B$ has coordinates $(4, -4)$.

    a) Calculate the horizontal change ($x$-movement) and vertical change ($y$-movement) from $A$ to $B$.

    b) Write down the displacement vector $\overrightarrow{AB}$.

    c) Calculate the magnitude $|\overrightarrow{AB}|$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Change in $x = x_2 - x_1 = 4 - (-2) = 6$.
      2. Change in $y = y_2 - y_1 = -4 - 4 = -8$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Place the changes into a column vector: $\overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix}$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Apply the magnitude formula: $|\overrightarrow{AB}| = \sqrt{6^2 + (-8)^2}$.
      2. Simplify the squares: $|\overrightarrow{AB}| = \sqrt{36 + 64}$.
      3. Add to find the square root: $|\overrightarrow{AB}| = \sqrt{100} = 10$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Scalar Multiples & Magnitude">
    Given $\mathbf{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}$.

    a) Find the vector $2\mathbf{q}$.

    b) Calculate the magnitude of $2\mathbf{q}$.

    c) Calculate the magnitude of $\mathbf{q}$.

    d) Describe the relationship between $|\mathbf{q}|$ and $|2\mathbf{q}|$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Multiply both components by 2: $2\mathbf{q} = 2 \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find the magnitude of this new vector: $|2\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{6^2 + (-8)^2}$.
      2. Simplify the squares: $|2\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{36 + 64}$.
      3. $|2\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{100} = 10$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find the magnitude of the original vector: $|\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2}$.
      2. Simplify the squares: $|\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{9 + 16}$.
      3. $|\mathbf{q}| = \sqrt{25} = 5$.
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. The magnitude $|2\mathbf{q}|$ is exactly twice the magnitude of $|\mathbf{q}|$ (since $10 = 2 \times 5$). Multiplying a vector by a scalar multiplies its length by the same factor.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Missing Component (Structured)">
    A vector is given as $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} k \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}$, where $k$ is an unknown number. The magnitude of the vector is $10$.

    a) Write down an expression for the magnitude of $\mathbf{v}$ in terms of $k$.

    b) Set your expression equal to $10$ and square both sides of the equation.

    c) Solve the equation to find the two possible values of $k$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use Pythagoras' theorem: $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{k^2 + 8^2}$.
      2. Simplify: $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{k^2 + 64}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Set the magnitude to 10: $\sqrt{k^2 + 64} = 10$.
      2. Square both sides to remove the square root: $k^2 + 64 = 100$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Subtract 64 from both sides: $k^2 = 100 - 64$.
      2. Simplify: $k^2 = 36$.
      3. Take the square root of both sides (remembering positive and negative roots): $k = 6$ or $k = -6$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Vector Addition and Magnitude">
    Let $\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

    a) Calculate the resultant vector $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$.

    b) Find the exact magnitude of $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$.

    c) Calculate $|\mathbf{a}|$ and $|\mathbf{b}|$ to 2 decimal places, and confirm whether $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}|$ is equal to $|\mathbf{a}| + |\mathbf{b}|$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Add the horizontal components: $1 + 5 = 6$.
      2. Add the vertical components: $7 + 1 = 8$.
      3. Resultant vector: $\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the magnitude formula: $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{6^2 + 8^2}$.
      2. $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Calculate $|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 7^2} = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$.
      2. Calculate $|\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{5^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{26} \approx 5.10$.
      3. Sum of magnitudes = $7.07 + 5.10 = 12.17$.
      4. Since $10 \neq 12.17$, $|\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}|$ does **not** equal $|\mathbf{a}| + |\mathbf{b}|$. (This is the triangle inequality principle).
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    a) Find the displacement vector $\overrightarrow{PQ}$ in terms of $y$.

    b) Form an equation representing the magnitude of $\overrightarrow{PQ}$.

    c) Solve the equation to find the two possible values of $y$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Calculate the $x$-movement: $8 - 2 = 6$.
      2. Calculate the $y$-movement: $y - 5$.
      3. Displacement vector: $\overrightarrow{PQ} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ y - 5 \end{pmatrix}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The distance is the magnitude: $\sqrt{6^2 + (y - 5)^2} = 10$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Square both sides: $36 + (y - 5)^2 = 100$.
      2. Subtract 36 from both sides: $(y - 5)^2 = 64$.
      3. Take the square root of both sides: $y - 5 = 8$ or $y - 5 = -8$.
      4. Solve for $y$: $y = 13$ or $y = -3$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Applying Pythagoras (Word Problem)">
    A ship sails $15$ km East and then $x$ km South. Its final distance from the starting point is $25$ km.

    a) Write down its displacement vector in terms of $x$, assuming East is the positive $x$-direction and North is the positive $y$-direction.

    b) Use the magnitude formula to set up an equation.

    c) Solve the equation to find the distance the ship sailed South.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. East is the positive $x$ direction, so the horizontal component is $15$.
      2. South is the negative $y$ direction, so the vertical component is $-x$.
      3. Displacement vector: $\begin{pmatrix} 15 \\ -x \end{pmatrix}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The straight-line distance is the magnitude: $\sqrt{15^2 + (-x)^2} = 25$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Square both sides: $15^2 + x^2 = 25^2$.
      2. Expand the squares: $225 + x^2 = 625$.
      3. Subtract 225: $x^2 = 400$.
      4. Take the square root: $x = 20$. (Distance must be positive).
      5. The ship sailed $20$ km South.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
