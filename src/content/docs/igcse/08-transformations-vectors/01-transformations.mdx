A transformation changes the position, size, or orientation of a shape. The original shape is called the **Object**, and the new shape is called the **Image** (often denoted with a prime symbol, e.g., $A'$). 

<Aside type="note" title="Transformation Anatomy">
Transformations can be broadly categorised into two anatomical families:
1. **Isometric Transformations:** These preserve the exact size and shape of the object. The object and image are congruent.
   * Reflection (Flip)
   * Rotation (Turn)
   * Translation (Slide)
2. **Non-Isometric Transformations:** These change the size of the object. The object and image are mathematically similar, but not congruent.
   * Enlargement (Grow/Shrink)
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="The Golden Exam Rule">
Always use a straight edge or ruler to draw the edges of your shapes, projection lines, and mirror lines in the exam. Furthermore, if a question asks for a "single transformation", you will score zero if you list two (e.g., "a translation and a reflection").
</SteveTip>

## 1. Reflection

A reflection is a "flip" of a shape across a straight line, acting as a mirror. Questions can feature horizontal lines ($y = c$), vertical lines ($x = c$), or diagonal lines ($y = x$ or $y = -x$).

<div class="not-content">
  <TransformationLab category="reflection" client:only="react" />
</div>

<Aside type="tip" title="Properties & Inverse of Reflection">
* **To describe fully:** You must state the word "Reflection" and the equation of the mirror line (e.g., $x = 0$, $y = 2$, $y = -x$).
* **Key Fact:** Every point on the image is the exact same perpendicular distance from the mirror line as the corresponding point on the object.
* **The Inverse:** The inverse of a reflection is **exactly the same reflection**. Flipping an image back over the same mirror line returns it to the object's position.
</Aside>

## 2. Rotation

A rotation turns a shape around a fixed pivot point. This point does not have to be the origin $(0,0)$; it can be anywhere on the grid!

<div class="not-content">
  <TransformationLab category="rotation" client:only="react" />
</div>

<Aside type="tip" title="Properties & Inverse of Rotation">
* **To describe fully, you must state 3 things:**
  1.  **Transformation type:** "Rotation"
  2.  **Centre of rotation:** Given as a coordinate $(x, y)$.
  3.  **Angle & Direction:** $90^\circ$ clockwise, $90^\circ$ anticlockwise, or $180^\circ$.
* **The Inverse:** To reverse a rotation, keep the same centre and angle, but **reverse the direction**. For example, the inverse of $90^\circ$ clockwise is $90^\circ$ anticlockwise. (The inverse of $180^\circ$ is just another $180^\circ$ turn).
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="Direction for 180°">
A common misconception is thinking you must provide a direction for a $180^\circ$ rotation. Direction is not needed for $180^\circ$ rotations, because turning halfway clockwise or halfway anticlockwise lands you in the exact same position!
</Aside>

## 3. Enlargement

An enlargement changes the size of the shape, altering the side lengths. The centre of enlargement acts as a "projector" lens from which the shape grows or shrinks.

<div class="not-content">
  <TransformationLab category="enlargement" client:only="react" />
</div>

<Aside type="tip" title="Properties & Inverse of Enlargement">
* **To describe fully, you must state 3 things:**
  1.  **Transformation type:** "Enlargement"
  2.  **Centre of enlargement:** The coordinate $(x, y)$.
  3.  **Scale Factor (SF):** How many times bigger the shape gets.
* **The Inverse:** Keep the same centre, but use the **reciprocal of the scale factor**. If a shape was enlarged by a scale factor of $3$, the inverse transformation is an enlargement by a scale factor of $\frac{1}{3}$. 
</Aside>

**Scale Factor Rules:**
* **Positive Integer ($SF > 1$):** Shape gets bigger and moves further from the centre.
* **Fractional ($0 < SF < 1$):** Shape gets smaller and moves closer to the centre.
* **Negative ($SF < 0$):** Shape passes *through* the centre of enlargement, gets flipped upside down, and ends up on the opposite side.

---

## 4. Translation

A translation is a straight slide. The shape is not rotated, flipped, or resized.

<Aside type="tip" title="Properties & Inverse of Translation">
* **To describe fully:** State the word "Translation" and provide the column vector:
$$
\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}
$$
  * **Top number ($x$):** Movement Left/Right (positive = right, negative = left).
  * **Bottom number ($y$):** Movement Up/Down (positive = up, negative = down).
* **The Inverse:** Simply **invert the signs** of the vector. The inverse of $\begin{pmatrix} -4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$. 
</Aside>

    Shape $P$ is mapped onto Shape $Q$ by a translation of $\begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$, followed by an enlargement of scale factor $2$ with centre $(1, 1)$. 
    
    Describe fully the transformations needed to map Shape $Q$ **back** to Shape $P$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
      1. To reverse a sequence of transformations, you must work backwards through the sequence (like putting on socks then shoes -> take off shoes then socks).
      2. **Step 1:** Reverse the enlargement first.
         * The original was SF $2$ from centre $(1,1)$.
         * The inverse is an **Enlargement, scale factor $\frac{1}{2}$, centre $(1,1)$**.
      3. **Step 2:** Reverse the translation.
         * The original was vector $\begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$.
         * The inverse is a **Translation by vector $\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}$**. (Invert the signs).
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
