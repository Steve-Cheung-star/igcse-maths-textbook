Just as subtraction is the opposite of addition, and division is the opposite of multiplication, **logarithms are the mathematical opposite of exponentials**. 

When you have an equation like $10^x = 1000$, it is easy to see that $x = 3$. But what if the equation is $10^x = 50$? We need a mathematical tool to "undo" the exponent and solve for $x$. That tool is the logarithm.

### A Brief History of the Logarithm
Before calculators existed, scientists and astronomers spent months doing tedious long multiplication by hand. In the late 16th century, a Scottish mathematician named **John Napier** spent over 20 years manually calculating millions of geometric ratios. In 1614, he published his invention: logarithms. Napier's brilliant system allowed mathematicians to turn horrific multiplication problems into simple addition problems, changing the course of science forever!

---

## 1. The Inverse Relationship

A logarithm simply asks the question: *"What power do I need to raise the base to, in order to get this number?"*

The fundamental rule you must memorize is the conversion between exponential form and logarithmic form:
$$y = a^x \iff x = \log_a y$$

Here, $a$ is the **base**. Notice that the base of the exponent becomes the base of the logarithm. 

### Equivalent Statements
To get comfortable with logs, you must be able to translate fluently between exponential and logarithmic forms.

| Exponential Form | Logarithmic Form | How to read it |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $10^2 = 100$ | $\log_{10} 100 = 2$ | "The power of 10 that gives 100 is 2" |
| $10^3 = 1000$ | $\log_{10} 1000 = 3$ | "The power of 10 that gives 1000 is 3" |
| $10^0 = 1$ | $\log_{10} 1 = 0$ | "The power of 10 that gives 1 is 0" |
| $10^{-1} = 0.1$ | $\log_{10} 0.1 = -1$ | "The power of 10 that gives 0.1 is -1" |
| $2^5 = 32$ | $\log_2 32 = 5$ | "The power of 2 that gives 32 is 5" |

<SteveTip title="The Default Base 10 & The TI-Nspire">
In the IGCSE 0607 syllabus, **all logarithms are assumed to be base 10 unless stated otherwise**. If an exam paper writes $\log 50$, it specifically means $\log_{10} 50$.

**Calculator Warning:** When you press **Ctrl + $10^x$** on your TI-Nspire, it brings up the template $\log_{\Box}(\Box)$. Unlike standard written math, the calculator **does not** assume base 10! You *must* manually type `10` into the small base box, otherwise, it will return an error or evaluate algebraically. 
</SteveTip>

## 2. The Cancellation Properties

Because logarithms and exponentials are inverse functions, they effectively "cancel" each other out when nested together. These two cancellation properties are incredibly useful for solving algebraic equations:

1. **Logarithm of an Exponential:** $$\log_a(a^x) = x$$
   *(Example: $\log_{10}(10^7) = 7$)*
   
2. **Exponential of a Logarithm:** $$a^{\log_a(x)} = x$$
   *(Example: $10^{\log_{10}(45)} = 45$)*

**Key properties to notice:**
* The exponential graph has a horizontal asymptote at $y = 0$ (the x-axis) and a y-intercept at **$(0, 1)$**.
* The logarithmic graph has a vertical asymptote at $x = 0$ (the y-axis) and an x-intercept at **$(1, 0)$**. Notice how it dives steeply downwards as $x$ approaches zero.
* You **cannot** take the logarithm of zero or a negative number. The domain is strictly $x > 0$.

## 4. Solving Exponential Equations

When the unknown variable is trapped in the power (e.g., $a^x = b$), you can solve it by taking the logarithm (base 10) of both sides. 

Using the rules of logarithms, the power $x$ drops down to the front, giving us the standard solution formula:
$$a^x = b \implies x = \frac{\log b}{\log a}$$

<Aside type="note" title="Example Walkthrough">
**Solve for $x$: $3^x = 45$**

1. Apply the formula: $x = \frac{\log 45}{\log 3}$
2. Type this into your GDC (remembering to input base 10!).
3. $x \approx 3.46$ (to 3 significant figures).
</Aside>

## 5. Real-World Contexts: Growth and Decay

Logarithms are incredibly useful in finance and science when you need to figure out **how long** something takes to grow or decay.

### Compound Interest (Growth)
If you want to know how many years it will take for an investment to reach a certain value, you are solving an exponential equation.

**Example:**
500 dollars is invested at a compound interest rate of 4% per year. How many full years will it take for the investment to exceed 800 dollars?

1. Set up the compound interest formula: $500 \times \left(1 + \frac{4}{100}\right)^n = 800$
2. Divide by 500: $1.04^n = 1.6$
3. Use the logarithm rule: $n = \frac{\log 1.6}{\log 1.04}$
4. Calculate: $n \approx 12.0003$
5. Since it asks for *full* years to *exceed* 800, the answer is **13 years**.

### Depreciation (Decay)
The exact same logic applies when things lose value over time.

**Example:**
A new car is bought for 25,000 dollars. It depreciates (loses value) at a rate of 15% per year. How many years will it take for its value to drop to exactly 10,000 dollars?

1. Set up the decay formula (using subtraction instead of addition): $25000 \times \left(1 - \frac{15}{100}\right)^n = 10000$
2. Simplify the bracket and divide by 25,000: $0.85^n = 0.4$
3. Use the logarithm rule: $n = \frac{\log 0.4}{\log 0.85}$
4. Calculate: $n \approx 5.64$
5. **Answer:** It will take **5.64 years**.
