In previous chapters, you learned how to extract key features from a given quadratic equation. In this section, we work in reverse: you will act as a mathematical detective, using visual clues from a graph (like its turning point or where it crosses the axes) to figure out its original equation.

---

## 1. Using the Vertex Form

Every quadratic function can be written in **vertex form**:
$$y = a(x - h)^2 + k$$

This form is incredibly powerful because the coordinates of the vertex (the turning point) are exactly **$(h, k)$**.

### Finding the Equation Given the Vertex and One Point
If you are given the vertex and exactly one other coordinate on the curve, you can find the full equation.

**Example:** A parabola has a vertex at $(2, 1)$ and passes through the point $(0, 5)$. Find its equation.
1. **Substitute the vertex $(h, k)$:** Here, $h = 2$ and $k = 1$.
   $$y = a(x - 2)^2 + 1$$
2. **Substitute the other point $(x, y)$ to find $a$:**
   The point is $(0, 5)$, so plug in $x = 0$ and $y = 5$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   5 &= a(0 - 2)^2 + 1 \\
   5 &= a(-2)^2 + 1 \\
   5 &= 4a + 1 \\
   4 &= 4a \\
   a &= 1
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Write the final equation:**
   $$y = 1(x - 2)^2 + 1 \implies y = (x - 2)^2 + 1$$

<Aside type="caution" title="The Negative Sign Trap">
Notice the minus sign in $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$. If your vertex has a negative x-coordinate, say $(-3, 4)$, the equation becomes $y = a(x - (-3))^2 + 4$, which simplifies to $y = a(x + 3)^2 + 4$. The sign inside the bracket always looks like the *opposite* of the vertex's x-coordinate!
</Aside>

## 2. Using the x-intercepts Form

If a graph crosses the x-axis, those crossing points are called **x-intercepts** or **roots**. If you know both roots, let's call them $p$ and $q$, you can build the quadratic using the **factored form**:
$$y = a(x - p)(x - q)$$

### Finding the Equation Given the x-intercepts and One Point
If you are given the two roots and one extra coordinate on the curve, use the factored form.

**Example:** A quadratic graph crosses the x-axis at $x = -1$ and $x = 3$. It passes through the point $(1, -4)$. Find its equation.
1. **Substitute the roots $p$ and $q$:**
   Here, $p = -1$ and $q = 3$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   y &= a(x - (-1))(x - 3) \\
   y &= a(x + 1)(x - 3)
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Substitute the other point $(x, y)$ to find $a$:**
   The point is $(1, -4)$, so plug in $x = 1$ and $y = -4$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   -4 &= a(1 + 1)(1 - 3) \\
   -4 &= a(2)(-2) \\
   -4 &= -4a \\
   a &= 1
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Write the final equation:**
   $$y = 1(x + 1)(x - 3) \implies y = (x + 1)(x - 3)$$
   *(You can expand this to $y = x^2 - 2x - 3$ if the question asks for the form $ax^2 + bx + c$.)*

## 3. The Special Case: When $a = 1$

Often, an exam question will explicitly tell you that the function is of the form $y = x^2 + bx + c$. Because there is no number written in front of the $x^2$, this immediately tells you that **$a = 1$**.

This saves you half the work! You do not need a secondary point to substitute. You simply plug your vertex $(h, k)$ into $y = (x - h)^2 + k$, or your roots into $y = (x - p)(x - q)$, and expand the brackets to find your final answer.

<SteveTip title="Expanding Vertex Form Quickly">
If a question tells you $y = x^2 + bx + c$ has a vertex at $(4, -3)$, write $y = (x - 4)^2 - 3$. 
Expand carefully using $(A - B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
y &= (x^2 - 8x + 16) - 3 \\
y &= x^2 - 8x + 13
\end{aligned}
$$
You have successfully found the coefficients without doing any messy simultaneous equations!
</SteveTip>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Given Vertex and a Point">
    The turning point of a quadratic graph is $(3, 4)$. The graph crosses the y-axis at $(0, -14)$.
    
    a) Write down the incomplete equation of the curve in vertex form.

    b) Use the given coordinate to find the value of the constant $a$.

    c) Write the final equation in the form $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$.

    d) Expand your answer to write the equation in the form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    The vertex is $(3, 4)$, so substitute $h = 3$ and $k = 4$ into the vertex form:
    $$y = a(x - 3)^2 + 4$$

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The y-intercept $(0, -14)$ gives us an $x$ and $y$ value to substitute:
         $$-14 = a(0 - 3)^2 + 4$$
      2. Simplify the bracket and subtract $4$ from both sides:
         $$-14 = 9a + 4$$
         $$-18 = 9a$$
      3. Divide by $9$:
         $$a = -2$$
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    Substitute $a = -2$ back into the equation from part (a):
    **$$y = -2(x - 3)^2 + 4$$**

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. Expand the squared bracket first:
         $$y = -2(x^2 - 6x + 9) + 4$$
      2. Multiply through by $-2$:
         $$y = -2x^2 + 12x - 18 + 4$$
      3. Simplify the constants:
         **$$y = -2x^2 + 12x - 14$$**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Given Roots and a Point">
    A quadratic curve has x-intercepts at $x = -2$ and $x = 5$. It passes through the point $(4, 18)$.
    
    a) Write down the incomplete equation of the curve in factored form.

    b) Find the value of the leading coefficient $a$.

    c) Write the expanded equation of the curve in the form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$.

    d) Find the coordinates of the y-intercept.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    Use the factored form $y = a(x - p)(x - q)$ with roots $-2$ and $5$:
    $$y = a(x + 2)(x - 5)$$

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute the point $x = 4, y = 18$:
         $$18 = a(4 + 2)(4 - 5)$$
      2. Simplify the brackets:
         $$18 = a(6)(-1)$$
         $$18 = -6a$$
      3. Divide by $-6$:
         $$a = -3$$
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute $a = -3$ into the factored form:
         $$y = -3(x + 2)(x - 5)$$
      2. Expand the brackets:
         $$y = -3(x^2 - 5x + 2x - 10)$$
         $$y = -3(x^2 - 3x - 10)$$
      3. Distribute the $-3$:
         **$$y = -3x^2 + 9x + 30$$**
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    The y-intercept occurs where $x = 0$. Using the expanded form from part (c), the constant term $c$ is the y-intercept.
    The coordinates are **$(0, 30)$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: The a = 1 Shortcut">
    The graph of $y = x^2 + px + q$ has a minimum turning point at $(-5, -2)$. 
    
    a) Write the equation of the curve in vertex form.

    b) Expand your equation to find the values of $p$ and $q$.

    c) State the coordinates of the y-intercept.

    d) Does this graph cross the x-axis? Justify your answer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    The form $y = x^2 + px + q$ indicates that $a = 1$. Substitute the vertex $(-5, -2)$ directly into vertex form:
    **$$y = (x + 5)^2 - 2$$**

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Expand the squared binomial:
         $$y = (x^2 + 10x + 25) - 2$$
      2. Simplify:
         $$y = x^2 + 10x + 23$$
      3. Compare this to $y = x^2 + px + q$:
         **$p = 10$ and $q = 23$**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    The y-intercept is the constant term $q$.
    Coordinates: **$(0, 23)$**.

    **d)**
    **Yes**. The vertex $(-5, -2)$ has a negative y-coordinate, meaning the lowest point of the graph is below the x-axis. Because $a = 1$ (which is positive), the parabola opens upwards. Therefore, it must cross the x-axis to go back up.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Symmetry and Roots">
    A quadratic curve has a vertical line of symmetry at $x = 4$. One of its x-intercepts is located at $x = 1$. The curve passes through the point $(5, -8)$.
    
    a) Find the coordinate of the other x-intercept.

    b) Write the incomplete factored form of the equation.

    c) Find the full equation of the curve in factored form.

    d) Find the exact coordinates of the vertex.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The line of symmetry $x = 4$ is exactly halfway between the two roots.
      2. The distance from the known root ($x = 1$) to the symmetry line is $3$ units.
      3. Add $3$ units to the other side of the symmetry line: $4 + 3 = 7$.
      4. The other x-intercept is **$x = 7$**.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    Using roots $1$ and $7$:
    **$$y = a(x - 1)(x - 7)$$**

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute the point $(5, -8)$ to find $a$:
         $$-8 = a(5 - 1)(5 - 7)$$
      2. Simplify:
         $$-8 = a(4)(-2)$$
         $$-8 = -8a$$
      3. Solve for $a$:
         $$a = 1$$
      4. The equation is **$$y = (x - 1)(x - 7)$$**.
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. The x-coordinate of the vertex lies on the line of symmetry, so $x = 4$.
      2. Substitute $x = 4$ into the equation to find the y-coordinate:
         $$y = (4 - 1)(4 - 7)$$
         $$y = (3)(-3) = -9$$
      3. The vertex coordinates are **$(4, -9)$**.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Vertex on an Axis">
    A parabola touches the x-axis at $x = 3$ and passes through the point $(1, 8)$.
    
    a) State the coordinates of the vertex.

    b) Find the equation of the parabola in vertex form.

    c) Expand the equation into the form $y = ax^2 + bx + c$.

    d) Find the value of $y$ when $x = 6$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
    When a parabola "touches" the x-axis without crossing it, that point is the turning point. 
    The vertex is **$(3, 0)$**.

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the vertex form with $h = 3$ and $k = 0$:
         $$y = a(x - 3)^2 + 0 \implies y = a(x - 3)^2$$
      2. Substitute the point $(1, 8)$:
         $$8 = a(1 - 3)^2$$
      3. Solve for $a$:
         $$8 = a(-2)^2$$
         $$8 = 4a \implies a = 2$$
      4. The equation is **$$y = 2(x - 3)^2$$**.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Expand the square:
         $$y = 2(x^2 - 6x + 9)$$
      2. Distribute the $2$:
         **$$y = 2x^2 - 12x + 18$$**
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    Substitute $x = 6$ into either equation (vertex form is easier):
    $$y = 2(6 - 3)^2$$
    $$y = 2(3)^2 = 2(9) = 18$$
    **$y = 18$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Translating a Described Graph">
    A bridge is supported by a parabolic arch. The arch meets the ground (the x-axis) at the origin $(0,0)$ and at a point $6\text{ m}$ away $(6,0)$. The maximum height of the arch is $18\text{ m}$.
    
    a) State the two x-intercepts.

    b) Find the coordinates of the vertex.

    c) Find the equation of the quadratic function representing the arch.

    d) State the domain for which the height $y$ is non-negative ($y \ge 0$).
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    The intercepts are given by the ground points: **$x = 0$ and $x = 6$**.

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The x-coordinate of the vertex is halfway between $0$ and $6$, which is $x = 3$.
      2. The maximum height is $18$, which is the y-coordinate.
      3. The vertex is **$(3, 18)$**.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Using the factored form with roots $0$ and $6$:
         $$y = a(x - 0)(x - 6) \implies y = ax(x - 6)$$
      2. Substitute the vertex $(3, 18)$ to find $a$:
         $$18 = a(3)(3 - 6)$$
         $$18 = a(3)(-3)$$
         $$18 = -9a \implies a = -2$$
      3. The equation is **$$y = -2x(x - 6)$$** (or $y = -2x^2 + 12x$).
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    The height $y$ is non-negative above the ground. This occurs between the two x-intercepts.
    **$0 \le x \le 6$**
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Using Simultaneous Equations">
    The curve $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ passes through three coordinates: $(0, 5)$, $(1, 10)$, and $(-1, 4)$.
    
    a) Use the first coordinate to find the value of $c$.

    b) Substitute the other two coordinates to set up a pair of simultaneous equations for $a$ and $b$.

    c) Solve the simultaneous equations to find $a$ and $b$.

    d) Write the final equation and state whether it has a maximum or minimum point.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    Substitute $x = 0, y = 5$:
    $$5 = a(0)^2 + b(0) + c \implies \mathbf{c = 5}$$

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute $(1, 10)$ and $c=5$:
         $$10 = a(1)^2 + b(1) + 5 \implies a + b + 5 = 10 \implies \mathbf{a + b = 5}$$
      2. Substitute $(-1, 4)$ and $c=5$:
         $$4 = a(-1)^2 + b(-1) + 5 \implies a - b + 5 = 4 \implies \mathbf{a - b = -1}$$
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Add the two equations together to eliminate $b$:
         $$(a + b) + (a - b) = 5 + (-1)$$
         $$2a = 4 \implies \mathbf{a = 2}$$
      2. Substitute $a=2$ back into $a + b = 5$:
         $$2 + b = 5 \implies \mathbf{b = 3}$$
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    The full equation is **$$y = 2x^2 + 3x + 5$$**.
    Because $a = 2$ (a positive number), the parabola opens upwards and therefore has a **minimum point**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Extracting from Standard Form">
    A quadratic function is defined as $y = 2x^2 + bx + c$. Its turning point is located at $(1, -5)$.
    
    a) Using the axis of symmetry formula $x = -\frac{b}{2a}$, find the value of $b$.

    b) Substitute the vertex into the equation to find the value of $c$.

    c) State the coordinates of the y-intercept.

    d) Find the exact roots of the equation in the form $x = \frac{p \pm \sqrt{q}}{r}$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The x-coordinate of the vertex is $1$, and we know $a = 2$.
      2. Substitute these into the symmetry formula:
         $$1 = -\frac{b}{2(2)}$$
         $$1 = -\frac{b}{4}$$
      3. Multiply by $-4$:
         **$$b = -4$$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The incomplete equation is now $y = 2x^2 - 4x + c$.
      2. Substitute the vertex $(1, -5)$:
         $$-5 = 2(1)^2 - 4(1) + c$$
         $$-5 = 2 - 4 + c$$
         $$-5 = -2 + c$$
      3. Add $2$ to both sides:
         **$$c = -3$$**
         *(The full equation is $y = 2x^2 - 4x - 3$)*
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    The y-intercept is the $c$ value.
    The coordinates are **$(0, -3)$**.

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. Set the equation to zero: $2x^2 - 4x - 3 = 0$.
      2. Apply the quadratic formula with $a=2, b=-4, c=-3$:
         $$x = \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(2)(-3)}}{2(2)}$$
      3. Simplify the discriminant:
         $$x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - (-24)}}{4}$$
         $$x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{40}}{4}$$
      4. You can leave it in this exact form, or simplify the surd:
         $$x = \frac{4 \pm 2\sqrt{10}}{4} \implies \mathbf{x = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{10}}{2}}$$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
