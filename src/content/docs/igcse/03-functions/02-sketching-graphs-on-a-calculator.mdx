The Graphic Display Calculator (GDC) is the defining tool of the IGCSE 0607 Extended syllabus. Exams will frequently present you with unfamiliar, complex equations and expect you to find their exact features. You are not expected to use algebra for these; you are expected to use your TI-Nspire efficiently.

---

## 1. Setting Up Your TI-Nspire for Exams

Before you sketch a single graph, you must ensure your calculator is correctly set up. A misconfigured GDC will give you wildly incorrect answers, especially in trigonometry.

<Aside type="caution" title="The Degree vs. Radian Trap">
By default, or after a reset, the TI-Nspire often switches to **Radian** mode. The IGCSE 0607 course uses **Degrees**. 
Look at the top right of your screen. If it says **RAD**, you must change it immediately!
Go to `Doc` > `7: Settings & Status` > `2: Document Settings` and change the Angle to **Degree**. Make sure to select "Make Default".
</Aside>

### Resetting and Clearing
Before a real exam, it is best practice to start with a completely clean slate so previous variables don't mess up your current calculations.
* **To clear all documents:** Press `Home` (On button) > `2: My Documents` > Press `Menu` > `C: Clear My Documents`. 
* **To close a current document quickly:** Press `Ctrl` + `W` and select "No" when asked to save. This gives you a fresh start.

### Deleting Graphs
If your graph screen is getting cluttered, you have two ways to clean it up:
1. **Delete a single graph:** Press `Tab` to open the entry line, press the up arrow (`↑`) to highlight the function you want to remove (e.g., $f1(x)$), and press the `Del` key.
2. **Delete everything on the graph:** Press `Menu` > `1: Actions` > `6: Delete All`.

## 2. Sketching, Tables, and Windows

### Sketching a Graph
When you enter a function into a new Graphs document, the curve might not appear on the screen. This means the graph exists outside your current **Viewing Window**. 
Always check the domain given in the question (e.g., $-5 \leq x \leq 5$) and set your window to match:
* Press `Menu` > `4: Window / Zoom` > `1: Window Settings` and adjust your $XMin$, $XMax$, $YMin$, and $YMax$.

### Producing a Table of Values
If an exam asks you to plot points on a grid, do not calculate them manually! 
1. Enter the function and draw the graph.
2. Press `Ctrl` + `T` to bring up the split-screen table.
3. To change what the table counts by, press `Menu` > `2: Table` > `5: Edit Table Settings`. Change the "Table Step".
4. Press `Ctrl` + `T` again to hide the table when you are done.

## 3. Finding Zeros, Maxima, and Minima

You must be able to instruct your TI-Nspire to locate specific coordinates on a curve using the **Analyze Graph** menu.

* **Zeros (Roots or x-intercepts):** The points where the graph crosses the horizontal x-axis ($y = 0$).
* **Local Maximum:** A "peak" or hilltop on the graph.
* **Local Minimum:** A "trough" or valley on the graph.

When you select `Menu` > `6: Analyze Graph` > `1: Zero` (or Minimum/Maximum), the Nspire will ask for a **Lower Bound**. Click your trackpad *to the left* of the point you want. It then asks for an **Upper Bound**. Swipe and click *to the right* of the point. A shaded region will appear, and the calculator will find the exact point inside that region.
</SteveTip>

## 6. Solving Inequalities & Finding Number of Solutions

### The "Greater Than" Case: $f(x) > g(x)$
When an exam asks you to solve an inequality like $f(x) > g(x)$, you are looking for the domain (x-values) where the curve of $f(x)$ is visually **above** the curve of $g(x)$. 
1. Graph both functions.
2. Find their points of intersection.
3. Read the x-values where the condition is met and write your answer as an inequality. Because the symbol is strictly $>$, we use standard `<` or `>` in our answer (the boundaries are *not* included).

Let's look at a visual representation of $f(x) > g(x)$ where $f(x) = -x^2 + 4$ (the red parabola) and $g(x) = x + 2$ (the purple line):

If the inequality has an "or equal to" sign ($\leq$ or $\geq$), it means the intersection points are **included** in the answer. You use $\leq$ or $\geq$ in your final domain.

Using the exact same functions, let's find $f(x) \leq g(x)$. The red parabola is *below* the purple line on the outer "tails" of the graph. Because it is $\leq$, we include the intersection boundaries with solid lines and closed dots.

### Number of Solutions ($f(x) = k$)
