Once data has been collected, it needs to be presented in a way that is easy to understand at a glance. Examiners will test your ability to both **draw** these charts accurately and **interpret** them to find averages or compare different sets of data.

<Aside type="note" title="Types of Categorical & Discrete Data Charts">
* **Bar Charts:** Compare quantities using bar height. Includes dual and composite variations.
* **Pie Charts:** Show proportions of a whole using angles.
* **Pictograms:** Represent data using symbols or icons.
* **Stem-and-Leaf Diagrams:** Order and display exact raw data while showing its shape.
</Aside>

---

## 1. Simple Frequency Distributions

Before drawing any chart, data is usually organised into a **frequency distribution**. For simple discrete data, this is often done using a tally chart to count occurrences, which are then summed into a frequency column.

| Number of Siblings | Tally | Frequency |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0 | |||| | 4 |
| 1 | |||| ||| | 8 |
| 2 | ||| | 3 |

This frequency table is the blueprint for everything that follows.

## 2. Bar Charts

A standard bar chart uses rectangular bars to represent frequencies. For discrete or categorical data, there **must be gaps** between the bars.

<Aside type="tip" title="Bar Chart Rules">
1. All bars must be the **same width**.
2. The **gaps** between the bars must be equal.
3. The vertical axis (Frequency) must have a uniform scale and start from zero.
4. Always label both axes!
</Aside>

### Dual (Side-by-Side) and Composite (Stacked) Bar Charts
When comparing two different groups (e.g., Boys and Girls), we use more advanced bar charts.
* **Dual Bar Chart:** Places the bars for each category side-by-side. Excellent for directly comparing groups.
* **Composite (Stacked) Bar Chart:** Stacks the bars on top of each other. Excellent for showing the *total* frequency while still showing the breakdown.

## 3. Pie Charts

A pie chart uses sectors of a circle to show the relative sizes of data. The whole circle represents the total frequency ($360^\circ$).

### Calculating Angles
To draw a pie chart, you must convert frequencies into angles.

<Aside type="tip" title="The Angle Formula">

$$
\text{Angle} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Total Frequency}} \times 360^\circ
$$

</Aside>

**Example:**
A shop sells 60 ice creams: 30 Vanilla, 20 Chocolate, and 10 Strawberry.
* Total Frequency = 60
* Vanilla Angle = $\frac{30}{60} \times 360^\circ = 180^\circ$
* Chocolate Angle = $\frac{20}{60} \times 360^\circ = 120^\circ$
* Strawberry Angle = $\frac{10}{60} \times 360^\circ = 60^\circ$

*(Always check that your angles add up to exactly $360^\circ$!)*

A pie chart does **not** show the actual frequencies, only the proportions. If you are given a pie chart and asked "How many people chose Chocolate?", you **cannot** answer it unless you are given the Total Frequency in the question!
</Aside>

## 4. Pictograms

A pictogram uses pictures or symbols to represent frequencies. In modern data presentation, it adds a fun visual flair while still retaining mathematical precision.

For example, a student tracked how many times their teacher, Mr. Cheung, was called alternative names by the class over a term. The student used a heart symbol to represent the counts.

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rule of Pictograms">
You **MUST** include a key! Without a key telling the examiner what one symbol represents (e.g., "1 heart = 4 times"), the entire diagram is meaningless.
</SteveTip>

If a full symbol represents 4 units, then half a symbol represents 2 units, and a quarter of a symbol represents 1 unit. You can show fractions of a symbol to represent precise data.

## 5. Stem-and-Leaf Diagrams

A stem-and-leaf diagram is a brilliant way to group data while keeping every single exact original value visible. 

**Data:** $12, 14, 18, 21, 23, 23, 27, 30, 32, 45$

The "stem" is usually the tens digit, and the "leaf" is the units digit. 
* Remember: The leaves **must** be ordered from smallest to largest!
* You **must** include a key.

| Stem | Leaf |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | $2 \ \ \ 4 \ \ \ 8$ |
| **2** | $1 \ \ \ 3 \ \ \ 3 \ \ \ 7$ |
| **3** | $0 \ \ \ 2$ |
| **4** | $5$ |

**Key:** $1 | 2$ represents $12$

### Finding Averages from a Stem-and-Leaf
Because the data is perfectly ordered, it is very easy to find the median and quartiles directly from the diagram.

<Steps>
  1. **Count the leaves** to find total frequency ($n = 10$).
  2. **Find the median:** The middle of 10 values is between the 5th and 6th value.
     * Counting through the leaves: 12, 14, 18, 21, **23, 23**...
     * The 5th value is 23, the 6th is 23. The median is $23$.
  3. **Mode:** The most common leaf in a single row. The row for '2' has two '3's. Mode = $23$.
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
When asked for the mode, students often just write down the leaf. If the most common leaf is '3' in the '2' row, the mode is **23**, not just 3!
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Pie Chart Angles">
    A survey of 120 people asked for their favourite season.
    * Spring: 30
    * Summer: 45
    * Autumn: 15
    * Winter: 30

    Calculate the sector angle for Summer.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. Verify the total frequency: $30 + 45 + 15 + 30 = 120$.
      2. Use the angle formula: 
         
         $$
         \text{Angle} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Total}} \times 360^\circ
         $$
         
      3. Calculate for Summer:
         
         $$
         \text{Angle} = \frac{45}{120} \times 360^\circ
         $$
         
      4. Simplify: $\frac{45}{120} = \frac{3}{8}$. 
         $\frac{3}{8} \times 360 = 135^\circ$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Working Backwards with Pie Charts">
    In a pie chart showing the favourite sports of a school, the sector for Football has an angle of $144^\circ$. 
    If 80 students chose Football, calculate the total number of students in the school.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. Set up the formula algebraically:
         
         $$
         \frac{80}{\text{Total}} \times 360 = 144
         $$
         
      2. Rearrange to make Total the subject. Divide both sides by 360:
         
         $$
         \frac{80}{\text{Total}} = \frac{144}{360}
         $$
         
      3. Simplify the fraction: $\frac{144}{360} = 0.4$.
         
         $$
         \frac{80}{\text{Total}} = 0.4
         $$
         
      4. Multiply by Total and divide by 0.4:
         
         $$
         \text{Total} = \frac{80}{0.4} = 200 \ \text{students}
         $$
         
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Interpreting a Stem-and-Leaf">
    A stem-and-leaf diagram shows the marks of 15 students in a test.
    
    | Stem | Leaf |
    | :--- | :--- |
    | **2** | $0 \ \ 5 \ \ 8$ |
    | **3** | $1 \ \ 2 \ \ 2 \ \ 4 \ \ 9$ |
    | **4** | $0 \ \ 3 \ \ 5 \ \ 5 \ \ 5$ |
    | **5** | $1 \ \ 4$ |
    
    Key: $2 | 0$ represents 20 marks.

    Find:
    a) The range of the marks
    b) The mode
    c) The median
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
      1. **Range:** Maximum mark - Minimum mark. 
         Max = $54$, Min = $20$.
         Range = $54 - 20 = 34$.
      2. **Mode:** Look for the most repeated leaf in a single row. In the '4' row, the leaf '5' appears three times.
         Mode = $45$.
      3. **Median:** With 15 students, the middle position is $\frac{15 + 1}{2} = 8$th value.
         Count 8 leaves from the top:
         Row 2: 20 (1), 25 (2), 28 (3)
         Row 3: 31 (4), 32 (5), 32 (6), 34 (7), **39 (8)**.
         The 8th value is $39$. Median = $39$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Interpreting Composite Bar Charts">
    A composite bar chart shows the ticket sales for a cinema over the weekend. 
    The Friday bar reaches a total height of 200. The bottom section of the Friday bar (Adults) reaches up to 120. The top section is for Children.
    
    Calculate the number of Child tickets sold on Friday.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
      1. Understand the stacked nature of the chart. The top of the bar shows the **Total** tickets.
      2. Total tickets = 200.
      3. The bottom section represents Adult tickets directly. Adults = 120.
      4. The remaining height represents the Child tickets.
         
         $$
         200 - 120 = 80
         $$
         
      5. 80 Child tickets were sold.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
