When analysing data, we usually want to find two things: a single value that represents the "centre" of the data (an **average**), and a value that tells us how stretched out or consistent the data is (a **measure of spread**).

<Aside type="note" title="The Three Averages">
* **Mode:** The most frequent value.
* **Median:** The middle value when the data is in order.
* **Mean:** The sum of all values divided by the total number of values.
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="How to Remember Which is Which">
It is incredibly common to mix these up in an exam! Use these tricks to remember:
* m**O**de is the m**O**st frequent (both have the letter 'o').
* med**I**an is the m**I**ddle value (both have the letter 'i').
* The **Mean** is just plain mean 😈 because I make you do the most arithmetic!
</SteveTip>

## 1. Averages for Individual Data

Let's look at a raw list of data: $4, 7, 2, 7, 9, 10, 3$.

Before doing anything with median or quartiles, **always sort the data into ascending order:**
$2, 3, 4, 7, 7, 9, 10$

### The Mode
The **mode** is the number that appears most often. 
In our list, the number $7$ appears twice, while all others appear once. 
* **Mode = 7**

### The Median
The **median** is the exact middle value. Cross off numbers from both ends until you reach the centre.
$2, 3, 4, \mathbf{7}, 7, 9, 10$
* **Median = 7**

*(If there are two middle numbers, add them together and divide by 2).*

### The Mean
To find the **mean** (often represented by $\bar{x}$), add up all the numbers and divide by how many numbers there are.

$$
\text{Mean} = \frac{2 + 3 + 4 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 10}{7} = \frac{42}{7} = 6
$$

## 2. Measures of Spread: Range and Quartiles

Averages tell you about the centre, but they don't tell you how spread out the numbers are. For that, we use the **range** and the **interquartile range (IQR)**.

<Aside type="tip" title="Range vs. IQR">
* **Range:** The difference between the largest and smallest values. It is heavily affected by extreme outliers.
* **Interquartile Range (IQR):** The spread of the middle 50% of the data. It ignores extreme outliers, making it a more reliable measure of consistency.
</Aside>

### Calculating Quartiles
Quartiles split the data into four equal parts.

<Steps>
  1. **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** The median of the lower half of the data.
  2. **Median ($Q_2$):** The middle of the whole data set.
  3. **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** The median of the upper half of the data.
</Steps>

Using our previous sorted list: $\mathbf{2}, 3, \mathbf{4}, 7, \mathbf{7}, 9, \mathbf{10}$
* **Minimum** = $2$
* **$Q_1$ (Lower Quartile)** = $3$ (middle of $2, 3, 4$)
* **Median** = $7$
* **$Q_3$ (Upper Quartile)** = $9$ (middle of $7, 9, 10$)
* **Maximum** = $10$

$$
\text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum} = 10 - 2 = 8
$$

$$
\text{Interquartile} \ \text{Range} \ \text{(IQR)} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 9 - 3 = 6
$$

## 4. Grouped Discrete Data

When discrete data is collected in large amounts, it is often grouped into a frequency table. The data values ($x$) are exact numbers, and the frequency ($f$) tells us how many times that exact number occurs.

### The Mode
To find the mode from a frequency table, simply look for the row with the highest frequency. 

### Calculating the Mean
To find the mean, you must multiply each data value by its frequency ($f \times x$) to find the total for that row. Then, sum all the $fx$ values and divide by the total frequency ($\sum f$).

$$
\text{Mean} = \frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}
$$

**Example:** The number of pets per household in a street.

| Number of Pets ($x$) | Frequency ($f$) | $f \times x$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0$ | $5$ | $0 \times 5 = 0$ |
| $1$ | $8$ | $1 \times 8 = 8$ |
| $2$ | $4$ | $2 \times 4 = 8$ |
| $3$ | $3$ | $3 \times 3 = 9$ |
| **Totals:** | **$\sum f = 20$** | **$\sum fx = 25$** |

* **Mode:** $1$ pet (it has the highest frequency of $8$).
* **Mean:**

$$
\text{Mean} = \frac{25}{20} = 1.25 \ \text{pets}
$$

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
When dividing to find the mean of a frequency table, always divide by the **Total Frequency** ($\sum f$). A very common mistake is dividing by the number of rows in the table!
</Aside>

## 5. Grouped Continuous Data

Continuous data is grouped into classes or intervals (e.g., $10 < h \le 20$). Because we do not know the exact values inside each interval, we can only calculate an **estimate** of the mean.

### The Modal Class
We cannot find a single mode. Instead, we find the **modal class** (or modal group)—the interval with the highest frequency.

### Estimating the Mean
Because the exact values are unknown, we use the **midpoint** ($x$) of each class as a representative value for that entire group.

**Example:** The heights of 40 plants are recorded.

| Height, $h$ (cm) | Frequency ($f$) | Midpoint ($x$) | $f \times x$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0 < h \le 10$ | $8$ | $5$ | $8 \times 5 = 40$ |
| $10 < h \le 20$ | $20$ | $15$ | $20 \times 15 = 300$ |
| $20 < h \le 30$ | $12$ | $25$ | $12 \times 25 = 300$ |
| **Totals:** | **$\sum f = 40$** | | **$\sum fx = 640$** |

* **Modal Class:** $10 < h \le 20$ (it has the highest frequency of $20$).
* **Estimated Mean:**

$$
\text{Estimated} \ \text{Mean} = \frac{640}{40} = 16 \ \text{cm}
$$

## 6. Working Backwards (Adding & Removing Data)

These questions test a deeper understanding of averages. They usually involve adding or removing data, or finding an unknown frequency.

### Adding Data to the Mean
The golden rule is to **always calculate the TOTAL sum of the data first**.

<Aside type="tip" title="The Mean Formula Rearranged">

$$
\text{Total} \ \text{Sum} = \text{Mean} \times \text{Number} \ \text{of} \ \text{Values}
$$

</Aside>

**Example: Adding a Value**
A student's mean score across $4$ tests is $75$. What must they score on their $5$th test to raise their mean to $80$?

<Steps>
  1. Find the total sum of the first 4 tests: $4 \times 75 = 300$
  2. Find the total sum needed for 5 tests to have a mean of 80: $5 \times 80 = 400$
  3. The 5th test must make up the difference: $400 - 300 = 100$
</Steps>

### Removing Data from the Mean
The same logic applies when data is removed. Find the original total, subtract the removed value, and divide by the *new* number of values.

**Example: Removing a Value**
A group of $6$ friends has a mean height of $160 \text{ cm}$. One friend, who is $180 \text{ cm}$ tall, leaves the group. Calculate the new mean height of the remaining $5$ friends.

<Steps>
  1. Original total height of 6 friends: $6 \times 160 = 960 \text{ cm}$
  2. Subtract the friend who left: $960 - 180 = 780 \text{ cm}$
  3. Divide by the new number of friends (5): $\text{New Mean} = \frac{780}{5} = 156 \text{ cm}$
</Steps>

### Finding a Missing Frequency
If you are *given* the mean but a frequency is missing from a grouped table, set up a simple linear equation to solve for the missing frequency.

**Example:**
The table shows the time taken to solve a puzzle. The estimated mean time is $14$ minutes. Find the missing frequency, $y$.

| Time, $t$ (mins) | Frequency ($f$) | Midpoint ($x$) | $f \times x$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0 < t \le 10$ | $5$ | $5$ | $25$ |
| $10 < t \le 20$ | $y$ | $15$ | $15y$ |
| $20 < t \le 30$ | $3$ | $25$ | $75$ |

<Steps>
  1. **Express total frequency ($\sum f$) algebraically:** $\sum f = 5 + y + 3 = 8 + y$
  2. **Express total $fx$ ($\sum fx$) algebraically:** $\sum fx = 25 + 15y + 75 = 100 + 15y$
  3. **Set up the mean equation and solve for $y$:** $\frac{100 + 15y}{8 + y} = 14$
  4. Multiply both sides by $(8 + y)$: $100 + 15y = 112 + 14y$
  5. Subtract $14y$ and subtract $100$ from both sides: $y = 12$
</Steps>
