Collecting and organising data is only the first step. The real power of statistics lies in **interpreting** that data—figuring out what the numbers actually mean in the real world, comparing different groups, and knowing when *not* to trust a statistic.

---

## 1. Reading and Inferring from Diagrams

When given a table, bar chart, pie chart, or scatter graph, you need to go beyond just reading the numbers. You need to draw **inferences** (logical conclusions based on the evidence).

* **Read the axes carefully:** Look at the scales. Does the y-axis start at $0$, or has it been truncated to exaggerate a difference?
* **Look for the mode:** What is the most common outcome? 
* **Spot the outliers:** Are there any data points that don't fit the general trend? Why might that be?



---

## 2. Comparing Sets of Data

In the exam, you will frequently be asked to compare two sets of data (e.g., the test scores of Class A vs. Class B, or the heights of male vs. female plants). 

<Aside type="note" title="The Golden Rule of Comparing Data">
To get full marks when comparing two data sets, you **must** make two distinct statements:
1. **Compare an Average (Central Tendency):** Use the Mean or Median to compare the overall "typical" value.
2. **Compare the Spread (Dispersion):** Use the Range or Interquartile Range (IQR) to compare how consistent the data is.

*Always include the actual numbers in your answer to prove your point!*
</Aside>

### Example: Comparing Test Scores
Here are the summary statistics for a maths test taken by two different classes (out of 100 marks):

| | Class X | Class Y |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mean Score** | $65$ | $72$ |
| **Median Score** | $66$ | $74$ |
| **Range** | $40$ | $15$ |

**How to write the comparison:**
1. **Average:** "On average, Class Y performed better on the test because their median score was $74$, which is higher than Class X's median of $66$."
2. **Spread:** "Class Y's scores were also much more consistent than Class X's, because Class Y had a smaller range of $15$ compared to Class X's range of $40$."



<SteveTip title="Range = Consistency">
Remember that a **smaller range** or **smaller IQR** means the data is clustered closer together. In real-world terms, this usually translates to the data being more **consistent** or **reliable**.
</SteveTip>

## 3. Restrictions on Drawing Conclusions

Just because data shows a certain result doesn't mean the conclusion is absolute fact. You must be able to critically evaluate data and identify its limitations.

Here are the most common restrictions and flaws to look out for:

* **Sample Size is Too Small:** Surveying 5 people is not enough to draw a conclusion about an entire school of 1000 students.
* **Biased Sample:** If you want to find out the most popular hobby in a town, you shouldn't only survey people walking out of a gym. The sample does not represent the general population.
* **Correlation Does Not Imply Causation:** Just because two things increase at the same time does not mean one caused the other. (e.g., Ice cream sales and shark attacks both increase in the summer. Ice cream does not cause shark attacks; the warmer weather is a hidden third variable).
* **Misleading Graphs:** Graphs where the vertical axis doesn't start at zero, or 3D pie charts that distort the sizes of the slices, can trick the viewer into making false inferences.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Comparing Data">
    Two athletes, Sarah and David, run the $100\text{m}$ sprint multiple times during training. Their times (in seconds) are recorded.
    
    * **Sarah:** Mean $= 12.1\text{s}$, Range $= 0.4\text{s}$
    * **David:** Mean $= 11.5\text{s}$, Range $= 1.8\text{s}$
    
    Write two sentences comparing their running performances.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. First, compare the averages (mean). Remember that for racing, a *lower* time is better.
      2. **Statement 1 (Average):** On average, David is the faster runner because his mean time is $11.5\text{s}$, which is lower than Sarah's mean of $12.1\text{s}$.
      3. Next, compare the spread (range). A smaller range means the runner gets roughly the same time every race.
      4. **Statement 2 (Spread):** However, Sarah is a much more consistent runner than David because her range is only $0.4\text{s}$, compared to David's highly varied range of $1.8\text{s}$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    A local newspaper wants to know if the town residents support the building of a new shopping mall. A reporter stands outside the town's existing shopping mall on a Tuesday morning and asks $20$ shoppers. 
    
    The headline the next day reads: *"95% of Town Demands New Shopping Mall!"*
    
    Give two distinct reasons why the data collected may not support this conclusion.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
      1. Analyse the sample size. Is it large enough?
      2. **Reason 1:** The sample size is far too small. Surveying only $20$ people is not enough to accurately represent the views of an entire town.
      3. Analyse the location and time (bias). Who is being asked? Who is being left out?
      4. **Reason 2:** The sample is heavily biased. The reporter is asking people who are already at a shopping mall, meaning they likely enjoy shopping more than the average resident. Also, by asking on a Tuesday morning, they are missing anyone who works standard weekday hours or is at school.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
