When dealing with grouped continuous data, a **cumulative frequency diagram** (sometimes called an ogive) is an incredibly powerful visual tool. It plots the running total of frequencies against the data values, allowing us to estimate averages and the spread of the data.

---

## 1. Cumulative Frequency Tables and Diagrams

To draw a cumulative frequency diagram, you first need to convert a standard frequency table into a cumulative frequency table by keeping a running total.

### Example: Creating the Table
The table below shows the time taken ($t$ minutes) by 80 students to complete a puzzle.

| Time ($t$ mins) | Frequency | Cumulative Frequency (Running Total) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0 < t \le 10$ | 4 | **4** |
| $10 < t \le 20$ | 12 | 4 + 12 = **16** |
| $20 < t \le 30$ | 28 | 16 + 28 = **44** |
| $30 < t \le 40$ | 22 | 44 + 22 = **66** |
| $40 < t \le 50$ | 10 | 66 + 10 = **76** |
| $50 < t \le 60$ | 4 | 76 + 4 = **80** |

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rule of Plotting">
When plotting your points, you **must** plot the cumulative frequency against the **upper class boundary** of the interval. 
For example, the first point is plotted at $(10, 4)$, the second at $(20, 16)$, and so on. **Never** plot against the midpoint!
</SteveTip>

### Drawing the Diagram

Just like with scatter diagrams, the exam board has strict rules for drawing these:
1. Plot the points using small, clear **crosses ($\times$)**.
2. Join the points with a **smooth curve**. Do *not* use a ruler to connect the points with straight lines!

## 2. Estimating the Median, Quartiles, and IQR

Once your curve is drawn, you can use it to estimate key statistical values. To do this, you read **across** from the cumulative frequency (y-axis) to your curve, and then **down** to the data value (x-axis).

* **Total Frequency ($n$):** The maximum y-value of your curve (in our example, $n = 80$).
* **Median ($Q_2$):** The value at $50\%$ of the total frequency. Read across from $n \div 2$.
* **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** The value at $25\%$ of the total frequency. Read across from $n \div 4$.
* **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** The value at $75\%$ of the total frequency. Read across from $3n \div 4$.
* **Interquartile Range (IQR):** The difference between the Upper and Lower Quartiles.
    $$
    \text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1
    $$

<Aside type="note" title="What about Percentiles?">
A percentile simply tells you the value below which a given percentage of data falls. For example, to find the **90th percentile** for our 80 students, calculate $90\%$ of $80$ ($0.9 \times 80 = 72$). Read across from $72$ on the y-axis, and down to the x-axis.
</Aside>

### Example: Reading the Curve

Let's use our graph of the 80 students to find the Median and IQR.

<Steps>
  1. **Median:** Half of 80 is 40. Read across from 40 on the y-axis, hit the curve, and go down. The median is roughly **28.5 minutes**.
  2. **Lower Quartile ($Q_1$):** A quarter of 80 is 20. Read across from 20 on the y-axis and down. $Q_1$ is roughly **21.5 minutes**.
  3. **Upper Quartile ($Q_3$):** Three-quarters of 80 is 60. Read across from 60 on the y-axis and down. $Q_3$ is roughly **37 minutes**.
  4. **IQR:** Subtract $Q_1$ from $Q_3$.
      $$
      \text{IQR} = 37 - 21.5 = 15.5 \text{ minutes}
      $$
</Steps>

## 3. Estimating Probability

Because the y-axis gives us a running total of the data, we can also use cumulative frequency curves to estimate basic probabilities. 

If you are asked to find the probability of a value being **less than** a certain amount, you simply read the cumulative frequency at that point and divide by the total number of items. 

If you need the probability of a value being **more than** a certain amount, remember that the graph reads from the bottom up! You need to subtract the cumulative frequency reading from the total frequency first.

### Example: Calculating Probability
Using our puzzle times graph, **what is the probability that a randomly selected student took more than 45 minutes to complete the puzzle?**

<Steps>
  1. Find 45 minutes on the x-axis.
  2. Read a straight line up to the curve, and across to the y-axis. At 45 minutes, the cumulative frequency is roughly **72**.
  3. This means 72 students took *less* than 45 minutes.
  4. To find the number who took *more* than 45 minutes, subtract from the total (80): 
     $80 - 72 = 8 \text{ students}$
  5. Write it as a probability: 
     $P(>45 \text{ mins}) = \frac{8}{80} = \frac{1}{10}$ (or 10%)
</Steps>

    A teacher records the distances ($d$ km) students travel to school. Some entries in her frequency and cumulative frequency tables are missing.
    
    | Distance ($d$ km) | Frequency | Cumulative Frequency |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $0 < d \le 10$ | $5$ | $5$ |

    | $30 < d \le 40$ | $c$ | $50$ |
    | $40 < d \le 50$ | $10$ | $d$ |
    
    a) Work backwards to find the values of $a$ and $b$.

    b) Find the values of $c$ and $d$.

    c) State the total number of students in the survey.

    d) Estimate the probability that a randomly chosen student travels more than $30$ km.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. **a) Find $a$ and $b$:** The CF jumps from $5$ to $17$. The difference is the frequency for that class. $a = 17 - 5 = \mathbf{12}$. For $b$, the previous CF is $17$. Add the next frequency ($15$). $b = 17 + 15 = \mathbf{32}$.
      2. **b) Find $c$ and $d$:** The CF jumps from $32$ to $50$. $c = 50 - 32 = \mathbf{18}$. For $d$, the previous CF is $50$. Add the final frequency ($10$). $d = 50 + 10 = \mathbf{60}$.
      3. **c) Total students:** The final cumulative frequency gives the total. There are **$60$ students**.
      4. **d) Probability > 30 km:** Students traveling $30$ km or less is given by the CF at $30$, which is $b = 32$. Students traveling more than $30$ km $= 60 - 32 = 28$. Probability $= \frac{28}{60} = \mathbf{\frac{7}{15}}$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
