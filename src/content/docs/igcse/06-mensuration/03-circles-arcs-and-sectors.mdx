Circles are a massive part of the IGCSE geometry syllabus. In this section, you will extend your knowledge from basic whole circles to calculating fractions of a circle, known as arcs and sectors. 

<Aside type="note" title="Circle Hierarchy & Anatomy">
* **Circumference:** The perimeter (total distance around the outside) of the full circle.
* **Arc:** A partial curve along the circumference. 
* **Sector:** A 2D slice of the circle's area, bounded by two radii and an arc (like a slice of pizza).
</Aside>



---

## 1. The Full Circle: Area and Circumference

Even though the formulas for the area and circumference of a circle are often given in the exam's formula sheet, you should know them by heart to save time.

* **Circumference:**
  $$
  C = \pi d \quad \text{or} \quad C = 2\pi r
  $$
* **Area:**
  $$
  A = \pi r^2
  $$

## 2. Minor vs. Major Arcs and Sectors

When you slice a circle using two radii, you don't just create one sector—you create two! 

* **Minor Sector/Arc:** The smaller slice (subtended by an angle less than $180^\circ$).
* **Major Sector/Arc:** The larger remaining slice (subtended by a reflex angle greater than $180^\circ$).

## 3. Calculating Arc Length and Sector Area

An arc is just a *fraction* of the full circumference. A sector is just a *fraction* of the full area. 
Because a full circle is $360^\circ$, the fraction of the circle you are dealing with is always exactly $\frac{\theta}{360}$, where $\theta$ is the angle at the centre.

<Aside type="tip" title="Arc Length and Sector Area Formulas">
* **Arc Length:**
  $$
  \text{Arc Length} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r
  $$
* **Sector Area:**
  $$
  \text{Sector Area} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2
  $$
</Aside>

### The Perimeter Trap
<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
**Arc Length is NOT the same as Sector Perimeter!**
Arc length is only the curved edge. If an exam asks for the **perimeter of a sector**, you must calculate the arc length and then **add the two straight radii** that form the straight edges!
$$
\text{Perimeter of Sector} = \text{Arc Length} + 2r
$$
</Aside>

### Example: Area and Perimeter of a Sector

Let's see this in action. Consider a minor sector with a radius of $8 \text{ cm}$ and an angle of $45^\circ$.

    <text x="130" y="95" font-size="14" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif" transform="rotate(-45 130,95)">8 cm</text>
    
    {/* Arc length label */}
    <text x="215" y="105" font-size="14" font-weight="bold" fill="#3b82f6" text-anchor="middle" font-family="sans-serif">Arc length</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Calculate the Area:**
   $$
   \text{Area} = \frac{45}{360} \times \pi \times 8^2
   $$
   $$
   \text{Area} = \frac{1}{8} \times 64\pi = 8\pi \approx 25.1 \text{ cm}^2
   $$
2. **Calculate the Arc Length:**
   $$
   \text{Arc Length} = \frac{45}{360} \times 2 \times \pi \times 8
   $$
   $$
   \text{Arc Length} = \frac{1}{8} \times 16\pi = 2\pi \approx 6.28 \text{ cm}
   $$
3. **Calculate the Total Perimeter:**
   Add the arc length to the two straight radii.
   $$
   \text{Perimeter} = 6.28 + 8 + 8
   $$
   $$
   \text{Perimeter} = 22.28 \text{ cm}
   $$
</Steps>

       $$
       \text{Sector Area} = \frac{45}{360} \times \pi \times 12^2
       $$
       $$
       \text{Sector Area} = 0.125 \times 144\pi = 18\pi = 56.5\text{ cm}^2
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = \text{Arc Length} + 15 + 15
       $$
       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = 70.686 + 30 = 100.686
       $$

       $$
       \text{Perimeter} = 100.7\text{ cm}
       $$

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Working Backwards (Extended)">
    A sector of a circle has a radius of $10\text{ cm}$ and an exact area of $20\pi\text{ cm}^2$. 
    Calculate the angle at the centre of the sector, $\theta$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
    1. Write out the sector area formula and substitute the known values:
       $$
       \text{Area} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi r^2
       $$
       $$
       20\pi = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi \times 10^2
       $$
    2. Simplify the equation by cancelling $\pi$ from both sides and squaring the 10:
       $$
       20 = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 100
       $$
    3. Rearrange to solve for $\theta$:
       $$
       \frac{20}{100} = \frac{\theta}{360}
       $$
       $$
       0.2 = \frac{\theta}{360}
       $$
       $$
       \theta = 0.2 \times 360 = 72^\circ
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
