Moving from 2D to 3D introduces two distinct measurements: **Volume** (the amount of 3D space inside an object) and **Surface Area** (the total 2D area of all the exposed outer surfaces). 

<SteveTip title="The Formula Sheet is Your Friend">

In the IGCSE Extended exam, you are **given** the formulas for the curved surface area of cylinders and cones, the surface area of spheres, and the volumes of prisms, pyramids, cylinders, cones, and spheres. You do not need to memorise them all, but you **must** know how to apply them and how to piece them together for compound shapes!

</SteveTip>

## 1. Prisms and Cylinders (The "Extruded" Shapes)

A **prism** is any 3D solid that has a perfectly uniform (constant) cross-section running all the way through its length. If you slice it like a loaf of bread, every slice looks exactly the same. 

A **cylinder** is simply a prism with a circular cross-section. 

<Aside type="tip" title="General Prism Formulas">

* **Volume:**
  $$
  \text{Volume} = \text{Area of cross-section} \times \text{length}
  $$
* **Surface Area:**
  Calculate the area of the two identical ends, and add the areas of all the rectangular sides connecting them.
  $$
  \text{Total SA} = 2 \times (\text{Area of cross-section}) + (\text{Perimeter of cross-section} \times \text{length})
  $$

</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Cylinder-Specific Formulas">

While a cylinder follows the general rules of a prism, its circular nature gives it specific algebraic formulas that you should be comfortable with:
* **Base Area (Cross-section):** $\pi r^2$
* **Curved Surface Area (CSA):** $2\pi r h$
* **Total Surface Area (TSA):** $2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$
* **Volume:** $\pi r^2 h$

</Aside>

### Example: Volume of a Cylinder
Calculate the exact volume of a cylinder with a radius of $6\text{ cm}$ and a height of $10\text{ cm}$. Give your answer in terms of $\pi$.

<Steps>

1. Identify the area of the base (a circle):
   $$
   \text{Base Area} = \pi r^2 = \pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi\text{ cm}^2
   $$
2. Multiply by the height to find the volume:
   $$
   \text{Volume} = 36\pi \times 10 = 360\pi\text{ cm}^3
   $$

</Steps>

## 2. Pyramids and Cones (The "Pointy" Shapes)

Any 3D shape that tapers from a flat base to a single point (an apex) has exactly **one-third** the volume of a prism with the same base and height. A cone is simply a pyramid with a circular base.

<Aside type="tip" title="General Pyramid & Cone Volume">

* **Volume:**
  $$
  \text{Volume} = \frac{1}{3} \times \text{Base Area} \times \text{Perpendicular Height}
  $$

</Aside>

### The Square Pyramid
A square pyramid has a square base. Its volume is $\frac{1}{3} \times (x \times x) \times h$, where $x$ is the side length of the square base.

### Cones and the Pythagoras Trap
The surface area of a cone is made of two parts: the flat circular base and the curved surface wrapped around the side.

<Aside type="caution" title="Height vs. Slant Height">

For the **volume**, you must use the perpendicular height ($h$). 
For the **curved surface area**, you must use the slanted height ($l$). 
Examiners will often give you one, and force you to calculate the other using Pythagoras' Theorem:
$$
l^2 = r^2 + h^2
$$

</Aside>

* **Total Surface Area (TSA):** $\pi r^2 + \pi r l$
* **Volume:** $\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$

</Aside>

## 3. Spheres and Hemispheres

Spheres are perfectly symmetrical 3D objects. 

* **Volume of a Sphere:**
  $$
  V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3
  $$
* **Surface Area of a Sphere:**
  $$
  SA = 4 \pi r^2
  $$

### The Hemisphere Surface Area Trap
A hemisphere is exactly half of a sphere. 
* The **volume** is simply half the volume of a sphere: $\frac{2}{3} \pi r^3$.
* The **surface area**, however, is a classic exam trap! When you cut a sphere in half, you expose a brand new flat circular base.

<Aside type="caution" title="Hemisphere Total Surface Area">

Total SA of a solid hemisphere = Curved SA + Flat Base Area
$$
\text{Total SA} = (2\pi r^2) + (\pi r^2) = 3\pi r^2
$$
Always read the question carefully to check if the hemisphere is "solid" (needs the base area added) or "open/hollow" like a bowl (curved area only).

</Aside>

## 4. Advanced Concepts: Working Backwards & Melting Shapes

Examiners love to test your algebraic skills alongside your geometric knowledge. They will frequently give you the volume or surface area and ask you to **work backwards** to find a missing dimension (like the radius).

Another common scenario is **conservation of volume**, where one shape is melted down to form another. The key here is setting up an equation where $\text{Volume}_{\text{old}} = \text{Volume}_{\text{new}}$.

### Example: Working Backwards
The surface area of a sphere is $144\pi\text{ cm}^2$. Calculate its radius.

<Steps>

1. Write down the surface area formula:
   $$
   SA = 4\pi r^2
   $$
2. Substitute the known value:
   $$
   144\pi = 4\pi r^2
   $$
3. Cancel $\pi$ from both sides:
   $$
   144 = 4r^2
   $$
4. Divide by 4 and square root:
   $$
   36 = r^2 \implies r = 6\text{ cm}
   $$

</Steps>

### Example: Melting Shapes
Three identical solid metal spheres, each with a radius of $2\text{ cm}$, are melted down to form a single solid cone with a base radius of $4\text{ cm}$. Calculate the perpendicular height of the cone.

<Steps>

1. Calculate the total volume of the 3 spheres:
   $$
   \text{Vol}_{\text{spheres}} = 3 \times \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \right) = 3 \times \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi \times 2^3 \right) = 4 \pi \times 8 = 32\pi\text{ cm}^3
   $$
2. Set this equal to the volume formula of the cone:
   $$
   \text{Vol}_{\text{cone}} = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h
   $$
   $$
   32\pi = \frac{1}{3} \pi (4)^2 h
   $$
3. Solve for $h$: Cancel $\pi$ and simplify:
   $$
   32 = \frac{1}{3} \times 16 \times h
   $$
   $$
   32 = \frac{16h}{3}
   $$
   $$
   96 = 16h \implies h = 6\text{ cm}
   $$

</Steps>

## 5. Similar Solids and Linear Equations

Exam questions often link **Similarity** with 3D shapes. If two shapes are mathematically similar, their lengths, surface areas, and volumes are connected by a scale factor $k$:
* **Linear Scale Factor:** Ratio of lengths = $k$
* **Area Scale Factor:** Ratio of surface areas = $k^2$
* **Volume Scale Factor:** Ratio of volumes = $k^3$

Sometimes, you won't be given straightforward numbers. Instead, you'll be given algebraic expressions for the dimensions, meaning you will have to set up and solve a linear equation.

## 6. Formula Summary Reference

Here is a quick cheat sheet compiling all the essential formulas you need to know for this topic. 

| Shape | Base / Cross-Section Area | Curved Surface Area (CSA) | Total Surface Area (TSA) | Volume |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Prism** | Area = $A$ | $\text{Perimeter} \times \text{length}$ | $2A + (\text{Perimeter} \times \text{length})$ | $A \times \text{length}$ |
| **Cylinder** | $\pi r^2$ | $2\pi r h$ | $2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h$ | $\pi r^2 h$ |
| **Pyramid** | Base Area | Sum of slanted faces | Base Area + Sum of slanted faces | $\frac{1}{3} \times \text{Base Area} \times h$ |
| **Cone** | $\pi r^2$ | $\pi r l$ | $\pi r^2 + \pi r l$ | $\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$ |
| **Sphere** | N/A | $4\pi r^2$ | $4\pi r^2$ | $\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ |
| **Solid Hemisphere**| $\pi r^2$ | $2\pi r^2$ | $3\pi r^2$ | $\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3$ |

*(Note: $h$ always denotes perpendicular height, and $l$ denotes slant height for cones.)*
