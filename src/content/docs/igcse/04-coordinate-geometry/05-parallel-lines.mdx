In coordinate geometry, this translates to one very simple, golden rule:
**Parallel lines have the exact same gradient ($m$).**

If you know the gradient of one line, you instantly know the gradient of any line parallel to it.

---

## 1. Identifying Parallel Gradients

To find the gradient of a parallel line, you must first identify the gradient of the original line. 

* **If the equation is in $y = mx + c$ form:** Simply look at the number in front of the $x$. 
    * *Example:* Any line parallel to $y = \mathbf{5}x + 2$ will have a gradient of $5$.
    * *Example:* Any line parallel to $y = \mathbf{-0.5}x - 8$ will have a gradient of $-0.5$.

* **If the equation is in general form ($ax + by = c$):** You cannot read the gradient directly. You **must** rearrange the equation to make $y$ the subject first.
    * *Example:* Find the gradient of a line parallel to $2x + 3y = 12$.
        $$
        \begin{aligned}
        3y &= -2x + 12 \\
        y &= \mathbf{-\frac{2}{3}}x + 4
        \end{aligned}
        $$
        The gradient of the original line is $-\frac{2}{3}$, so the parallel line's gradient is also $\boldsymbol{-\frac{2}{3}}$.

---

## 2. Finding the Equation of a Parallel Line

Usually, exam questions will give you an original line and a specific coordinate point, asking you to find the equation of a *new* parallel line that passes through that point.

<Aside type="tip" title="The Parallel Line Method">
<Steps>
1. **Find the original gradient:** Identify $m$ from the given equation (rearrange if necessary).
2. **Steal the gradient!** Your new line will use this exact same $m$.
3. **Substitute:** Plug your "stolen" gradient and the coordinates of the given point into $y = mx + c$.
4. **Solve for $c$:** Calculate the $y$-intercept of your *new* line.
5. **Write the final equation:** Combine your gradient and new $c$ value.
</Steps>
</Aside>

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the equation of the line parallel to $y = 4x - 1$ that passes through $(1, -3)$.

1.  **Find the original gradient:** The equation is $y = 4x - 1$, so the gradient is $4$.
2.  **Steal the gradient:** Our new line will also have $m = 4$. So, we know our new line looks like $y = 4x + c$.
3.  **Substitute the point:** The new line passes through $x = 1$ and $y = -3$. 
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    -3 &= 4(1) + c
    \end{aligned}
    $$
4.  **Solve for $c$:**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    -3 &= 4 + c \\
    c &= -3 - 4 \\
    c &= \mathbf{-7}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
5.  **Write the final equation:**
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = 4x - 7}$

<SteveTip title="Don't recycle the old intercept!">
A classic exam mistake is accidentally copying the $y$-intercept from the original equation. **The ONLY thing you "steal" from the first line is the gradient.** Once you have $m$, completely ignore the old equation—it has done its job! Your new line will almost always have a different $c$ value.
</SteveTip>

### 2.1 The General Form Shortcut ($ax + by = c$)

If the original equation is already in the general form $ax + by = c$, you don't actually need to rearrange it to find $m$ if you use this brilliant shortcut!

Because parallel lines share the same gradient, their $x$ and $y$ coefficients ($a$ and $b$) can remain absolutely identical. The only thing that changes is the constant on the end. 

**Shortcut Rule:** A line parallel to $ax + by = c$ will have the equation **$ax + by = k$**.

**Example:** Find the equation of the line parallel to $3x - 2y = 8$ that passes through $(4, 5)$.
1.  **Keep the $x$ and $y$ terms identical:** The new line is $3x - 2y = k$.
2.  **Substitute your point $(4, 5)$ to find $k$:**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3(4) - 2(5) &= k \\
    12 - 10 &= k \\
    2 &= k
    \end{aligned}
    $$
3.  **Write the final equation:** **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{3x - 2y = 2}$ 

### 2.2 The Point-Gradient Shortcut

If you remember the **point-gradient formula** ($y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$), finding parallel lines becomes a one-step process once you've stolen the gradient!

**Example:** Find the equation of the line parallel to $y = 7x + 2$ that passes through $(3, 10)$.
1.  **Steal the gradient:** $m = 7$.
2.  **Drop $m$ and the point $(3, 10)$ straight into the formula:**
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y - 10 = 7(x - 3)}$
    *(If the question asks for $y = mx + c$ form, simply expand the bracket: $y = 7x - 21 + 10 \rightarrow y = 7x - 11$.)*

    Find the equation of the line that is parallel to $y = 3x - 2$ and passes through the point $(2, 5)$.
    
    a) State the gradient of the original line.
    
    b) Write down the gradient of the new parallel line.
    
    c) Substitute the new gradient and the point $(2, 5)$ into $y = mx + c$ to find the $y$-intercept.
    
    d) Write the final equation of the parallel line.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The equation is in the form $y = mx + c$.
      2. Looking at $y = 3x - 2$, the gradient is the coefficient of $x$.
      3. **Original gradient = 3**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Parallel lines have identical gradients.
      2. **New gradient ($m$) = 3**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use $y = mx + c$ and substitute $m = 3$, $x = 2$, and $y = 5$.
      2. $5 = (3)(2) + c$
      3. $5 = 6 + c$
      4. **$c = -1$**
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. Substitute $m = 3$ and $c = -1$ into the general equation.
      2. **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = 3x - 1}$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
