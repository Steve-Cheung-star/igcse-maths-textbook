The **Cartesian coordinate system** is a brilliant way of identifying the exact location of any point on a flat surface (a plane) using two numbers. These numbers represent distances from a fixed starting point called the **origin**.

<Aside type="note" title="A Neat Piece of Maths History">
**The Fly on the Ceiling** In the 17th century, a French mathematician and philosopher named René Descartes was lying in bed, watching a fly crawl across his ceiling. He realised that he could describe the fly's exact position at any moment by measuring its distance from two adjacent walls. 

By turning those "walls" into intersecting number lines, he invented the Cartesian coordinate system, forever bridging the gap between algebra (equations) and geometry (shapes)!
</Aside>

## 1. The Anatomy of the Coordinate Plane

A 2D coordinate system is built using two perpendicular number lines that intersect at zero.

* **The x-axis:** The horizontal number line. Positive numbers are to the right; negative numbers are to the left.
* **The y-axis:** The vertical number line. Positive numbers are up; negative numbers are down.
* **The Origin:** The point (0, 0) where the axes cross.

### The Four Quadrants
The two intersecting axes divide the grid into four distinct regions called **quadrants**. They are numbered anti-clockwise using Roman numerals, starting from the top right.

## 2. Reading and Writing Coordinates

Coordinates are always written as an **ordered pair** in brackets: $(x, y)$.

1.  **The first number ($x$):** Tells you how far to move horizontally (left or right) from the origin.
2.  **The second number ($y$):** Tells you how far to move vertically (up or down) from the origin.

<SteveTip title="The Alphabet Rule">
If you forget which number comes first, just remember the alphabet: **X** comes before **Y**. 
You must walk *across* the floor ($x$) before you can climb *up* the ladder ($y$)!
</SteveTip>

### Special Case: Points on the Axes
If a point lies directly on one of the lines, one of its coordinates must be **zero**.
* **On the x-axis:** The point hasn't moved up or down. The $y$-coordinate is always 0. Example: $(5, 0)$.
* **On the y-axis:** The point hasn't moved left or right. The $x$-coordinate is always 0. Example: $(0, -4)$.

## 3. Using Coordinates with Shapes

A very common exam question involves giving you three corners (vertices) of a shape, like a rectangle or square, and asking you to deduce the coordinates of the missing corner without physically drawing it.

**How to solve it logically:**
1. Rectangles have straight vertical and horizontal sides.
2. The side from $A$ to $D$ must be perfectly vertical. This means $D$ must have the exact same $x$-coordinate as $A$. Therefore, the $x$-coordinate is **1**.
3. The side from $C$ to $D$ must be perfectly horizontal. This means $D$ must have the exact same $y$-coordinate as $C$. Therefore, the $y$-coordinate is **4**.
4. **Answer:** The missing vertex is $D(1, 4)$.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Quadrants & Signs">
    a) In which quadrant does the point $P(-6, 9)$ lie?
    
    b) Point $Q$ lies in Quadrant III. What must be true about the signs of its $x$ and $y$ coordinates?
    
    c) A point $R$ has a $y$-coordinate of 0 and an $x$-coordinate of $-4$. State the coordinates of $R$ and describe its location on the grid.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The $x$-coordinate is negative (move left).
      2. The $y$-coordinate is positive (move up).
      3. This places the point in the top-left section.
      4. **Quadrant II**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Quadrant III is the bottom-left section of the coordinate grid.
      2. To reach the bottom-left, you must move left (negative $x$) and down (negative $y$).
      3. **Both the $x$ and $y$ coordinates must be negative.**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The $x$-value is $-4$ and the $y$-value is 0.
      2. The coordinates are **$(-4, 0)$**.
      3. Because the $y$-value is 0, the point has not moved vertically.
      4. **It lies directly on the negative x-axis.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Translating Words to Coordinates">
    a) Point $A$ is 5 units to the right of the y-axis and 8 units below the x-axis. Write down its coordinates.
    
    b) Point $B$ is initially located at the origin. It is then moved 3 units left and 7 units up. What are its new coordinates?

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. 5 units right of the y-axis means the $x$-coordinate is 5.
      2. 8 units below the x-axis means the $y$-coordinate is $-8$.
      3. **$(5, -8)$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The origin is $(0, 0)$.
      2. Moving 3 units left means subtracting 3 from the $x$-coordinate: $0 - 3 = -3$.
      3. Moving 7 units up means adding 7 to the $y$-coordinate: $0 + 7 = 7$.
      4. **$(-3, 7)$**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Point $B$ is at $(-3, 7)$.
      2. The distance to the x-axis is purely based on its vertical position ($y$-coordinate).
      3. It is 7 units vertically above the x-axis.
      4. **7 units**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Missing Vertex of a Rectangle">
    Three vertices of a rectangle are $A(-3, -2)$, $B(4, -2)$, and $C(4, 3)$.
    
    a) Find the coordinates of the fourth vertex, $D$.
    
    b) Calculate the horizontal length of the rectangle.
    
    c) Calculate the vertical height of the rectangle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Points $A$ and $B$ form the bottom horizontal edge. $B$ and $C$ form the right vertical edge.
      2. Point $D$ must complete the top-left corner.
      3. It must align vertically with $A$, taking its $x$-coordinate: $-3$.
      4. It must align horizontally with $C$, taking its $y$-coordinate: 3.
      5. **$D(-3, 3)$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The horizontal length is the distance between $A(-3, -2)$ and $B(4, -2)$.
      2. The distance from $x = -3$ to $x = 4$ is $4 - (-3) = 7$.
      3. **7 units**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The vertical height is the distance between $B(4, -2)$ and $C(4, 3)$.
      2. The distance from $y = -2$ to $y = 3$ is $3 - (-2) = 5$.
      3. **5 units**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Properties of a Square">
    A square has two adjacent bottom vertices at $P(2, 1)$ and $Q(8, 1)$. The entire square lies in Quadrant I.
    
    a) What is the side length of the square?
    
    b) Find the coordinates of the top two vertices, $R$ and $S$.
    
    c) If the square was drawn downwards into Quadrant IV instead, what would the coordinates of the two bottom vertices be?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The bottom edge goes from $x = 2$ to $x = 8$.
      2. The length is $8 - 2 = 6$.
      3. **6 units**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Because it is a square, the vertical height must also be 6 units.
      2. Since it lies in Quadrant I, the square extends upwards.
      3. Add 6 to the $y$-coordinates of $P$ and $Q$.
      4. $1 + 6 = 7$.
      5. $S$ sits above $P$: **$S(2, 7)$**.
      6. $R$ sits above $Q$: **$R(8, 7)$**.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. If drawn into Quadrant IV, the square extends downwards by 6 units.
      2. Subtract 6 from the $y$-coordinates of $P$ and $Q$.
      3. $1 - 6 = -5$.
      4. The new vertices would be **$(2, -5)$ and $(8, -5)$**.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

      2. Half of 6 units right is 3 units right.
      3. Half of 10 units up is 5 units up.
      4. Starting from $(0, 0)$ and applying these movements gives $(3, 5)$.
      5. **$(3, 5)$**

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Coordinate Reflections">
    Point $T$ has coordinates $(4, -3)$.
    
    a) If $T$ is reflected perfectly across the x-axis to become point $T'$, find the coordinates of $T'$.
    
    b) If the original point $T$ is reflected across the y-axis to form point $T''$, what are its coordinates?
    
    c) Which quadrant does $T''$ lie in?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Reflecting across the horizontal x-axis flips the point vertically.
      2. The $x$-coordinate stays the same. The $y$-coordinate changes its sign.
      3. $-3$ becomes 3.
      4. **$T'(4, 3)$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Reflecting across the vertical y-axis flips the point horizontally.
      2. The $y$-coordinate stays the same. The $x$-coordinate changes its sign.
      3. 4 becomes $-4$.
      4. **$T''(-4, -3)$**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Point $T''$ has a negative $x$ and a negative $y$.
      2. This places it in the bottom-left section of the grid.
      3. **Quadrant III**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Algebraic Coordinates">
    The coordinates of a point $K$ are given algebraically as $(2a - 8, b + 5)$.
    
    a) If point $K$ lies on the y-axis, what is the value of $a$?
    
    b) If point $K$ lies on the x-axis, what is the value of $b$?
    
    c) If point $K$ is located precisely at the origin, find the values of $a$ and $b$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Any point on the y-axis has an $x$-coordinate of 0.
      2. Set the $x$-expression to zero: $2a - 8 = 0$.
      3. Solve for $a$: $2a = 8 \implies a = 4$.
      4. **$a = 4$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Any point on the x-axis has a $y$-coordinate of 0.
      2. Set the $y$-expression to zero: $b + 5 = 0$.
      3. Solve for $b$: $b = -5$.
      4. **$b = -5$**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The origin is $(0, 0)$. Both coordinates must equal 0.
      2. As calculated in parts (a) and (b), this requires the specific solutions for both variables.
      3. **$a = 4, b = -5$**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
