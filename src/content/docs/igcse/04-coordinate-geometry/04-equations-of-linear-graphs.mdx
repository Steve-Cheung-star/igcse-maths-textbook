Every straight line on a coordinate grid can be described by an algebraic equation. This equation is essentially a "rule" that connects the $x$-coordinate of every point on the line to its corresponding $y$-coordinate.

---

## 1. The Standard Form: $y = mx + c$

The most useful and common way to write the equation of a straight line is the **gradient-intercept form**. 

$$
y = mx + c
$$

* **$\boldsymbol{m}$ represents the gradient (steepness).** It tells you how many units the line goes *up* (or down) for every 1 unit it moves to the *right*.
* **$\boldsymbol{c}$ represents the $y$-intercept.** This is the exact value where the line crosses the vertical $y$-axis (where $x = 0$).

### 1.1 Finding the equation from a graph

To find the equation of a line when looking at its graph, you need to identify $c$ and calculate $m$.

**Example Walkthrough:**
1.  **Find $\boldsymbol{c}$:** Look at the $y$-axis. The line crosses at 1, so $\boldsymbol{c = 1}$.
2.  **Find $\boldsymbol{m}$:** Pick two easy-to-read points on the line, like $(0, 1)$ and $(4, 3)$. 
    $$
    m = \frac{\text{Rise}}{\text{Run}} = \frac{\text{Change in } y}{\text{Change in } x} = \frac{2}{4} = \boldsymbol{0.5}
    $$
3.  **Write the equation:** Substitute $m$ and $c$ into $y = mx + c$.
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = 0.5x + 1}$

*(Note: Always give your final equation in a fully simplified form. Write $y = 0.5x + 1$ or $y = \frac{1}{2}x + 1$, not $y = \frac{2}{4}x + 1$.)*

## 2. Finding an Equation Given the Gradient and a Point

Often, you are given the gradient ($m$) and just one coordinate point that the line passes through. How you handle this depends on whether that point is the $y$-intercept or not.

### Case A: The point is the $y$-intercept $(0, c)$
If the given point has an $x$-coordinate of 0, it **is** the $y$-intercept. You have everything you need to write the equation immediately.

**Example:** A line has a gradient of -3 and passes through $(0, 5)$.
* $m = -3$
* Because $x=0$, we know $c = 5$.
* **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = -3x + 5}$

### Case B: The point is NOT the $y$-intercept $(x, y)$
If the point is anywhere else, you must use algebra to solve for $c$. This is the absolute most important method to master for your exams!

<Aside type="tip" title="Mastering the Gradient-Intercept Method">
<Steps>
1. Start with the standard formula: $y = mx + c$
2. Substitute your known gradient ($m$) into the equation.
3. Substitute the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of your given point into the equation.
4. Solve the resulting simple linear equation to find $c$.
5. Rewrite the final equation with both $m$ and $c$ filled in.
</Steps>
</Aside>

**Example:** A line has a gradient of 4 and passes through the point $(2, 11)$. Find its equation.
1. Start with the formula: $y = mx + c$
2. Substitute the gradient: $y = 4x + c$
3. Substitute the coordinates ($x = 2$, $y = 11$):
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   11 &= 4(2) + c \\
   11 &= 8 + c
   \end{aligned}
   $$
4. Solve for $c$: 
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   c &= 11 - 8 \\
   c &= \mathbf{3}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
5. Rewrite the final equation:
   **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = 4x + 3}$

<SteveTip title="Check Your Work!">
Once you find your final equation, plug the original $x$-coordinate back in. In the example above, if $x=2$, then $y = 4(2) + 3 = 11$. It matches the original point! This takes 5 seconds and guarantees you haven't made an arithmetic mistake.
</SteveTip>

## 3. Horizontal and Vertical Lines

Lines that are perfectly flat or perfectly upright have special, very simple equations. They do not have an $x$ and a $y$ in the same equation.

## 4. The General Form: $ax + by = c$

Sometimes, equations are written with both variables on the same side, like $3x + 2y = 12$. This is often called the **general form**. 

<Aside type="danger" title="Common Misconception">
Students frequently look at $3x + 2y = 12$ and assume the gradient is 3 because it is the number attached to $x$. **This is wrong!** You can only read the gradient when $y$ is completely by itself on one side of the equals sign.
</Aside>

### 4.1 Converting General Form to $y = mx + c$

To find the gradient and $y$-intercept, you must rearrange the equation to make $y$ the subject.

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the gradient and $y$-intercept of the graph with equation $5x + 4y = 8$.

1.  **Isolate the $y$ term:** Subtract $5x$ from both sides.
    $$
    4y = -5x + 8
    $$
2.  **Make $y$ the subject:** Divide *everything* by 4.
    $$
    y = -\frac{5}{4}x + \frac{8}{4}
    $$
3.  **Simplify your fractions!** (This is where many marks are lost).
    $$
    y = -\frac{5}{4}x + 2 \quad (\text{or } y = -1.25x + 2)
    $$
4.  **Extract your values:**
    **Answer:** The gradient ($m$) is $\boldsymbol{-\frac{5}{4}}$, and the $y$-intercept ($c$) is $\boldsymbol{2}$.

**Another Example (Watch the simplification):**
Find the gradient of the line $3x + 6y = 10$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
6y &= -3x + 10 \\
y &= -\frac{3}{6}x + \frac{10}{6} 
\end{aligned}
$$
*Wait!* You must simplify the fraction $-\frac{3}{6}$ to $-\frac{1}{2}$. 
The final gradient is **$-\frac{1}{2}$**.

### 4.2 Converting $y = mx + c$ into General Form

Higher-tier questions may ask you to give your final equation in the form $ax + by = c$ where $a, b,$ and $c$ are **integers** (whole numbers). This means you need to get rid of any fractions.

**Example Walkthrough:**
Rewrite the equation $y = \frac{2}{3}x - 4$ in the form $ax + by = c$, where $a, b, c$ are integers.

1.  **Clear the fraction:** Multiply *every* single term by the denominator (3).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    (3)y &= (3)\frac{2}{3}x - (3)4 \\
    3y &= 2x - 12
    \end{aligned}
    $$
2.  **Rearrange the terms:** Move the $x$ and $y$ terms to the same side. Subtract $2x$ from both sides:
    $$
    \boldsymbol{-2x + 3y = -12}
    $$
    *(Note: Writing $2x - 3y = 12$ is also perfectly correct and means the exact same thing!)*

**Example Walkthrough:**
Find the fully simplified equation of the line passing through $A(2, 5)$ and $B(6, 17)$.

1.  **Calculate the gradient ($m$):**
    $$
    m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{17 - 5}{6 - 2} = \frac{12}{4} = \mathbf{3}
    $$
2.  **Substitute $m$ and one point into $y = mx + c$ to find $c$:**
    Using point $A(2, 5)$ where $x = 2$ and $y = 5$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    5 &= (3)(2) + c \\
    5 &= 6 + c \\
    c &= \mathbf{-1}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
3.  **Write the final equation:**
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = 3x - 1}$

## 6. * The Point-Gradient Form (Advanced Shortcut)

While not strictly required in the basic IGCSE 0607 syllabus, the **point-gradient form** is an incredibly powerful tool. It is especially useful in higher-level maths (like finding the equation of a tangent line in calculus) because it lets you build the equation in a single step without having to solve for $c$ first.

$$
y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)
$$
*(Where $m$ is the gradient, and $(x_1, y_1)$ is the given coordinate point).*

### Connecting the "Three Forms"
Let's look at how this single formula can easily transform into the other two major forms we've covered. 

**Question:** A line has a gradient of $\frac{2}{3}$ and passes through the point $(6, 1)$. Find its equation.

**Step 1: Point-Gradient Form (The Setup)**
Drop $m = \frac{2}{3}$, $x_1 = 6$, and $y_1 = 1$ directly into the formula:
$$
y - 1 = \frac{2}{3}(x - 6)
$$
*(You are technically done building the line right here! But exams usually want it simplified into a specific format.)*

**Step 2: Gradient-Intercept Form ($y = mx + c$)**
To get here, just expand the bracket and make $y$ the subject:
$$
\begin{aligned}
y - 1 &= \frac{2}{3}x - 4 \quad \text{(expanded the bracket)} \\
y &= \frac{2}{3}x - 3 \quad \text{(added 1 to both sides)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 3: General Form ($ax + by = c$ with integers)**
Take your $y = mx + c$ answer, clear the fraction by multiplying everything by 3, and rearrange:
$$
\begin{aligned}
3y &= 2x - 9 \\
\boldsymbol{-2x + 3y} &\boldsymbol{= -9}
\end{aligned}
$$
