When we look at a straight line drawn between two points on a coordinate grid, we are looking at a **line segment**. Because it has a definite start and end, we can measure exactly how long it is, find the exact coordinate of its middle, and even split it algebraically into specific fractional ratios.

## 1. Calculating the Length of a Line Segment

If you draw a right-angled triangle between your two points, the horizontal distance is your "run", the vertical distance is your "rise", and the line segment itself is the **hypotenuse**.

### The Distance Formula

In mathematics, the Greek letter delta ($\Delta$) is used to represent the "change in" or "difference". Therefore, $\Delta x$ is the horizontal distance ($x_2 - x_1$) and $\Delta y$ is the vertical distance ($y_2 - y_1$).

By applying Pythagoras ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$) directly to the coordinates, we get the official distance formula:

$$
d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2} = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}
$$

**Standard Example Walkthrough:**
Calculate the length of the line joining $A(1, 2)$ and $B(7, 5)$.

1. Find the change in $x$: $\Delta x = 7 - 1 = 6$
2. Find the change in $y$: $\Delta y = 5 - 2 = 3$
3. Apply the distance formula:
$$
\begin{aligned}
d^2 &= 6^2 + 3^2 \\
d^2 &= 36 + 9 \\
d^2 &= 45 \\
d &= \sqrt{45} \approx 6.71 \text{ (to 3 sig figs)}
\end{aligned}
$$

### 1.1 Harder Problems: Missing Coordinates & Quadratics

In higher-tier exams, you might be given the length of the line and asked to find an unknown coordinate. This usually requires forming and solving a quadratic equation.

**Example Walkthrough:**
The distance between the points $P(2, 5)$ and $Q(x, 1)$ is exactly $5$ units. Find the possible values of $x$.

1. Substitute what you know into the distance formula:
$$
\begin{aligned}
5^2 &= (x - 2)^2 + (1 - 5)^2 \\
25 &= (x - 2)^2 + (-4)^2 \\
25 &= (x - 2)^2 + 16
\end{aligned}
$$
2. Isolate the bracket by subtracting 16 from both sides:
$$
\begin{aligned}
9 &= (x - 2)^2
\end{aligned}
$$
3. Square root both sides. *Crucial step: Remember that a square root has both a positive and negative answer!*
$$
\begin{aligned}
\pm 3 &= x - 2
\end{aligned}
$$
4. Solve for the two possible values of $x$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
x &= 2 + 3 \quad \text{or} \quad x = 2 - 3 \\
\boldsymbol{x} &\boldsymbol{= 5} \quad \text{or} \quad \boldsymbol{x = -1}
\end{aligned}
$$
*(Why two answers? Because from point P, a line of length 5 could stretch downwards to the right, landing at $x=5$, or downwards to the left, landing at $x=-1$.)*

## 2. Finding the Midpoint of a Line Segment

The **midpoint** is the exact centre of a line segment. To find it, you simply need to find the **average** of the $x$-coordinates and the **average** of the $y$-coordinates.

### The Midpoint Formula

$$
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)
$$

**Standard Example Walkthrough:**
Find the midpoint of the line connecting $(-4, 2)$ and $(6, -4)$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
x\text{-midpoint} &= \frac{-4 + 6}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \\
y\text{-midpoint} &= \frac{2 + (-4)}{2} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1
\end{aligned}
$$
**Answer:** $\boldsymbol{M(1, -1)}$.

### 2.1 Harder Problems: Solving Linear Equations

Sometimes the coordinates themselves contain unknown letters (algebraic terms), and you are given the final midpoint. You must split the problem into two separate linear equations.

**Example Walkthrough:**
The midpoint of the line segment joining $A(2a, 5)$ and $B(10, 3b)$ is the point $M(8, 1)$. Find the values of $a$ and $b$.

**Step 1: Equate the x-components**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{2a + 10}{2} &= 8 \\
2a + 10 &= 16 \\
2a &= 6 \\
\boldsymbol{a} &\boldsymbol{= 3}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 2: Equate the y-components**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{5 + 3b}{2} &= 1 \\
5 + 3b &= 2 \\
3b &= -3 \\
\boldsymbol{b} &\boldsymbol{= -1}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Method 1: The "Vector / Jump" Method (Recommended)
Because the midpoint is exactly halfway, whatever journey you take from $A$ to get to $M$, you must repeat exactly to get from $M$ to $B$.

1. **Find the jump from A to M:**
   * $\Delta x$: From $1$ to $4$ is a jump of **$+3$**.
   * $\Delta y$: From $7$ to $3$ is a jump of **$-4$**.
2. **Apply the exact same jump from M to B:**
   * $x$-coordinate: $4 + 3 = \mathbf{7}$
   * $y$-coordinate: $3 - 4 = \mathbf{-1}$
3. **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{B(7, -1)}$.

### Method 2: The Algebraic Method
If you prefer formulas, set up the midpoint formula with point $B$ as unknown $(x, y)$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1 + x}{2} &= 4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 1 + x = 8 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol{x = 7} \\
\frac{7 + y}{2} &= 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 7 + y = 6 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol{y = -1}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 4. Dividing a Line Segment in a Given Ratio ($m:n$)

Sometimes you need to find a specific point $P$ that divides a line segment $AB$ into a specific ratio, like $2:3$, rather than just splitting it exactly in half ($1:1$). 

There are two main ways to solve these problems: using logic and fractional steps, or using the formal Section Formula (often called the "Criss-Cross" method).

### Method 1: The Fractional Steps Method

This method builds on the visual vector jumps we used for finding endpoints.

**Example Walkthrough:**
Find the coordinates of the point $P$ which divides the line segment joining $A(2, 1)$ and $B(12, 16)$ in the ratio $2:3$.

1. **Find the total number of parts:**
   $2 + 3 = \mathbf{5 \text{ parts total}}$.
   This means point $P$ is exactly $\frac{2}{5}$ of the way along the line from $A$ to $B$.

2. **Find the total distance ($\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$) from A to B:**
   $\Delta x = 12 - 2 = 10$
   $\Delta y = 16 - 1 = 15$

3. **Calculate the fraction of that distance:**
   $x\text{-move} = \frac{2}{5} \times 10 = 4$
   $y\text{-move} = \frac{2}{5} \times 15 = 6$

4. **Add these moves onto the starting point $A(2, 1)$:**
   $x\text{-coordinate}: 2 + 4 = \mathbf{6}$
   $y\text{-coordinate}: 1 + 6 = \mathbf{7}$

**Answer:** The point $P$ is $\boldsymbol{(6, 7)}$.

### Method 2: The Section Formula (Criss-Cross Method)

If you prefer pure algebra, you can use the formula below. It is often called the "Criss-Cross" method because the *first* part of the ratio ($m$) multiplies the coordinates of the *second* point ($B$), and the *second* part of the ratio ($n$) multiplies the coordinates of the *first* point ($A$).

$$
P = \left( \frac{mx_2 + nx_1}{m+n}, \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m+n} \right)
$$

**Using the exact same example ($A(2, 1)$, $B(12, 16)$, ratio $2:3$):**

1. Identify variables: $(x_1, y_1)=(2, 1)$, $(x_2, y_2)=(12, 16)$, $m=2$, $n=3$.
2. Set up the $x$ and $y$ calculations:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x_P &= \frac{m x_2 + n x_1}{m+n} = \frac{2(12) + 3(2)}{2+3} = \frac{24 + 6}{5} = \frac{30}{5} = \boldsymbol{6} \\
    y_P &= \frac{m y_2 + n y_1}{m+n} = \frac{2(16) + 3(1)}{2+3} = \frac{32 + 3}{5} = \frac{35}{5} = \boldsymbol{7}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

**Answer:** The point $P$ is $\boldsymbol{(6, 7)}$.

    A circle is drawn on a coordinate grid. The line segment connecting $A(-2, 5)$ and $B(6, -1)$ forms a complete diameter of the circle.
    
    a) Find the coordinates of the center of the circle.
    
    b) Calculate the exact length of the diameter.
    
    c) State the exact radius of the circle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The center of a circle is the exact midpoint of its diameter.
      2. Use the midpoint formula: $(\frac{-2 + 6}{2}, \frac{5 + (-1)}{2})$.
      3. Simplify: $(\frac{4}{2}, \frac{4}{2})$.
      4. **Center = $(2, 2)$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The diameter is the distance between $A$ and $B$.
      2. $\Delta x = 6 - (-2) = 8$. $\Delta y = -1 - 5 = -6$.
      3. $d = \sqrt{8^2 + (-6)^2} = \sqrt{64 + 36} = \sqrt{100}$.
      4. **Diameter = 10 units**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The radius is exactly half of the diameter.
      2. $10 \div 2 = 5$.
      3. **Radius = 5 units**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    Calculate the exact coordinates of the midpoint for the line segments connecting each of the following pairs of points:
    a) $(2, 4)$ and $(8, 10)$
    b) $(-4, 3)$ and $(6, 1)$
    c) $(-5, -5)$ and $(-1, 3)$
    d) $(0.5, 4)$ and $(3.5, -1)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)** Add the $x$-coordinates and divide by 2, then do the same for the $y$-coordinates: $(\frac{2+8}{2}, \frac{4+10}{2})$. The midpoint is **$(5, 7)$**.
    
    **b)** Average the $x$-coordinates: $\frac{-4+6}{2} = 1$. Average the $y$-coordinates: $\frac{3+1}{2} = 2$. The midpoint is **$(1, 2)$**.
    
    **c)** Average the $x$-coordinates: $\frac{-5+(-1)}{2} = -3$. Average the $y$-coordinates: $\frac{-5+3}{2} = -1$. The midpoint is **$(-3, -1)$**.
    
    **d)** Average the $x$-coordinates: $\frac{0.5+3.5}{2} = 2$. Average the $y$-coordinates: $\frac{4+(-1)}{2} = 1.5$. The midpoint is **$(2, 1.5)$**.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 9: Calculating Distances">
    Calculate the exact distance between the following pairs of points. Where necessary, leave your answer in exact surd form (e.g., $\sqrt{20}$).
    a) $(1, 1)$ and $(4, 5)$
    b) $(-2, 3)$ and $(4, -5)$
    c) $(0, 0)$ and $(5, 5)$
    d) $(-3, 4)$ and $(7, 4)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **a)** $\Delta x = 4 - 1 = 3$ and $\Delta y = 5 - 1 = 4$. Using Pythagoras: $d = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25}$. The distance is **$5$**.
    
    **b)** $\Delta x = 4 - (-2) = 6$ and $\Delta y = -5 - 3 = -8$. Using Pythagoras: $d = \sqrt{6^2 + (-8)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100}$. The distance is **$10$**.
    
    **c)** $\Delta x = 5 - 0 = 5$ and $\Delta y = 5 - 0 = 5$. Using Pythagoras: $d = \sqrt{5^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{25 + 25} = \sqrt{50}$. The exact distance is **$\sqrt{50}$** (or $5\sqrt{2}$).
    
    **d)** Because the $y$-coordinates are identical, this is a perfectly horizontal line. You can just find the difference between the $x$-coordinates: $7 - (-3) = 10$. The distance is **$10$**.
  </TabItem>
