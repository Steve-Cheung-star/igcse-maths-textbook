## 1. The Negative Reciprocal Rule (Visualising Rise/Run)

Unlike parallel lines, perpendicular lines travel in entirely different directions. Look at the diagram above to understand how their gradients are connected.

If the **original line (Line 1)** has a gradient of $m_1 = \frac{a}{b}$ (meaning it goes **up $a$** for every **$b$ right**), then the **perpendicular line (Line 2)** must travel **right $a$** for every **down $b$**.

The gradient of the perpendicular line is:

$$
m_2 = \frac{-b}{a}
$$

This is the **negative reciprocal**. You flip the fraction and change the sign. Multiplying them always gives $-1$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
m_1 \times m_2 &= -1 \\
\left(\frac{a}{b}\right) \times \left(-\frac{b}{a}\right) &= -1
\end{aligned}
$$

<SteveTip title="Flip and Flip">
To find the perpendicular gradient, remember the two 'flips':
1.  Flip the **fraction** upside down.
2.  Flip the **sign** (positive $\leftrightarrow$ negative).
</SteveTip>

## 2. Identifying Perpendicular Gradients from Equations

You must ensure the equation is in the form $y = mx + c$ before you can identify the original gradient and calculate its negative reciprocal.

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the gradient of a line perpendicular to $2y = 3x + 1$.

1.  **Find the original gradient:** The equation is not in $y = mx + c$ form yet. Rearrange it:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \frac{2y}{2} &= \frac{3x + 1}{2} \\
    y &= \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{1}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    The original gradient ($m_1$) is $\frac{3}{2}$.

2.  **Find the perpendicular gradient:** Flip $\frac{3}{2}$ and change the sign to negative.

    **Answer:** The perpendicular gradient ($m_2$) is $\boldsymbol{-\frac{2}{3}}$.

<SteveTip title="Speed Trick: The Standard Form Shortcut">
If you are given an equation in standard form **$ax + by = c$**, you don't actually need to rearrange it to $y = mx + c$! 

The perpendicular line will *always* take the form **$bx - ay = k$** (or $-bx + ay = k$). 

If you need the line to pass through a specific point $(p, q)$, simply substitute $x = p$ and $y = q$ into your new form to find $k$.

$$
b(p) - a(q) = k
$$

</SteveTip>

## 3. The Unique Perpendicular through a Point

For any given line and any given point ($P$) **not on that line**, there is **exactly one** unique perpendicular line that passes through $P$.

**Syllabus Example:** Find the equation of the line passing through $(-6, 4)$ which is perpendicular to the line $y = 3x - 2$.

<Steps>
1.  **Find the perpendicular gradient ($m_2$):** The original gradient is $3$. The perpendicular gradient is $m_2 = -\frac{1}{3}$.

2.  **Substitute into $y = mx + c$:** Use your perpendicular gradient and the given point $x = -6$, $y = 4$.

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    4 &= \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)(-6) + c \\
    4 &= 2 + c \\
    c &= \mathbf{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

3.  **Write the final equation:**

    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 2}$
</Steps>

## 4. The Perpendicular Bisector

This is a classic Extended-tier question. A **perpendicular bisector** is a line that cuts exactly through the **midpoint** of a line segment connecting two points, at a perfect $90^\circ$ angle.

  <div style={{ width: '100%', maxWidth: '600px' }}>
    <PerpendicularLab mode="bisector" client:only="react" />
  </div>
</div>

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the points $(-3, 8)$ and $(9, -2)$.

<Steps>
1.  **Find the Midpoint ($M$):** You *must* know where the middle is.

    $$
    M = \left( \frac{-3 + 9}{2}, \frac{8 + (-2)}{2} \right) = \left( \frac{6}{2}, \frac{6}{2} \right) = \mathbf{(3, 3)}
    $$

    *Our perpendicular bisector must pass through $(3, 3)$.*

2.  **Find the Original Gradient ($m_1$):**

    $$
    m_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{-2 - 8}{9 - (-3)} = \frac{-10}{12} = \mathbf{-\frac{5}{6}}
    $$

3.  **Find the Perpendicular Gradient ($m_2$):**

    $$
    m_2 = \mathbf{\frac{6}{5}}
    $$

4.  **Build the Equation:** Use $m = \frac{6}{5}$ and the midpoint $(3, 3)$. Substitute into $y = mx + c$:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3 &= \left(\frac{6}{5}\right)(3) + c \\
    3 &= \frac{18}{5} + c \\
    c &= 3 - \frac{18}{5} \\
    c &= \frac{15}{5} - \frac{18}{5} \\
    c &= \mathbf{-\frac{3}{5}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = \frac{6}{5}x - \frac{3}{5}}$
</Steps>

## 5. Shortest Distance (Perpendicular Distance)

In geometry, the shortest distance between an external point and a straight line is **always** the perpendicular distance.

<div className="not-content" style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '2rem 0' }}>
  <div style={{ width: '100%', maxWidth: '600px' }}>
    <PerpendicularLab mode="distance" client:only="react" />
  </div>
</div>


This concept is used in optimization problems (e.g., finding the closest point on a road, a boundary, or a river).

**To find the shortest distance, you must:**
1.  Find the equation of the unique **perpendicular line** passing through the external point (Concept 3).
2.  Find the **point of intersection** ($I$) between the original line and your perpendicular line (by solving simultaneous equations).
3.  Calculate the **distance** between the original external point and the intersection point $I$ (using the Distance Formula).

**The Steps:**

<Steps>
1.  **Find the equation of the base line $AB$:** Gradient of $AB = \frac{4 - 1}{7 - 1} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$.

    Using $A(1, 1)$ in $y = mx + c$:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    1 &= \frac{1}{2}(1) + c \\
    c &= \frac{1}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    Equation of $AB$: **$y = 0.5x + 0.5$**

2.  **Find the equation of the perpendicular height from $C$:**
    
    The perpendicular gradient is the negative reciprocal of $\frac{1}{2}$, which is **$-2$**.

    Using point $C(3, 8)$ in $y = mx + c$:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    8 &= -2(3) + c \\
    8 &= -6 + c \\
    c &= 14
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    Equation of perpendicular: **$y = -2x + 14$**

3.  **Find the intersection point ($I$) to get the height's end point:**
    
    Set the two line equations equal to each other:

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    0.5x + 0.5 &= -2x + 14 \\
    2.5x &= 13.5 \\
    x &= \mathbf{5.4}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    Substitute $x$ back in to find $y$: 
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    y &= -2(5.4) + 14 \\
    y &= \mathbf{3.2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    The intersection is **$I(5.4, 3.2)$**.

4.  **Calculate lengths using the Distance Formula:**
    
    **Base Length ($AB$):**
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    AB &= \sqrt{(7 - 1)^2 + (4 - 1)^2} \\
    &= \sqrt{36 + 9} \\
    &= \mathbf{\sqrt{45}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    **Perpendicular Height ($CI$):**
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    CI &= \sqrt{(5.4 - 3)^2 + (3.2 - 8)^2} \\
    &= \sqrt{(2.4)^2 + (-4.8)^2} \\
    &= \sqrt{5.76 + 23.04} \\
    &= \mathbf{\sqrt{28.8}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

5.  **Calculate Final Area:**
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Base} \times \text{Height} \\
    &= \frac{1}{2} \times \sqrt{45} \times \sqrt{28.8} \\
    &= \frac{1}{2} \times \sqrt{1296} \\
    &= \frac{1}{2} \times 36 \\
    &= \mathbf{18 \text{ units}^2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

</Steps>

    A straight road follows the equation $y = 0.5x + 3$ on a map grid. A drone base is located at the point $D(9, -2)$. 
    
    a) Write down the equation of the line representing the shortest path from the drone base to the road.
    
    b) Find the coordinates of the point on the road closest to the drone base.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The shortest path is perpendicular to the road. Original gradient $m = 0.5$ (or $\frac{1}{2}$).
      2. Perpendicular gradient is $-2$.
      3. Use drone base $D(9, -2)$ in $y = mx + c$: $-2 = (-2)(9) + c \rightarrow -2 = -18 + c \rightarrow c = 16$.
      4. **Answer:** $y = -2x + 16$
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find the intersection of $y = 0.5x + 3$ and $y = -2x + 16$.
      2. $0.5x + 3 = -2x + 16 \rightarrow 2.5x = 13 \rightarrow x = 5.2$.
      3. Substitute $x$ back: $y = -2(5.2) + 16 = -10.4 + 16 = 5.6$.
      4. **Answer:** The closest point is $(5.2, 5.6)$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 9: Geometric Proof with Gradients">
    Points $P(-2, 1)$, $Q(1, 7)$, and $R(5, 5)$ form a triangle.
    
    a) Calculate the gradient of line segment $PQ$.
    
    b) Calculate the gradient of line segment $QR$.
    
    c) Hence, prove that triangle $PQR$ is a right-angled triangle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the gradient formula $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$ for $P(-2, 1)$ and $Q(1, 7)$.
      2. $m_{PQ} = \frac{7 - 1}{1 - (-2)} = \frac{6}{3}$.
      3. **Answer:** $m_{PQ} = 2$
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the formula for $Q(1, 7)$ and $R(5, 5)$.
      2. $m_{QR} = \frac{5 - 7}{5 - 1} = \frac{-2}{4}$.
      3. **Answer:** $m_{QR} = -0.5$ (or $-\frac{1}{2}$)
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Multiply the two gradients together: $m_{PQ} \times m_{QR} = 2 \times (-\frac{1}{2}) = -1$.
      2. Since the product of the gradients is $-1$, the lines $PQ$ and $QR$ are perpendicular.
      3. **Answer:** Therefore, angle $PQR$ is exactly $90^\circ$, proving it is a right-angled triangle.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
