Powers (indices) and roots are inverse operations. If a power "builds" a number, a root "breaks it down" to its original base. For IGCSE 0607, speed is your best friend—memorising the common values will save you precious minutes in Paper 2 (Non-Calculator).

## 1. Squares and Square Roots

A **square number** is the result of multiplying an integer by itself.
$$x^2 = x \times x$$

The **square root** ($\sqrt{x}$) asks: "What number, multiplied by itself, gives me $x$?"

<Aside type="tip" title="Memorising the Big Squares">
Struggling to remember the squares for 13, 14, and 15? Use this trick:
* $13^2 = \mathbf{169}$
* $14^2 = \mathbf{196}$ *(Just swap the 6 and 9 from the 13 squared!)*
* $15^2 = \mathbf{225}$ *(Any number ending in 5 will always have a square that ends in 25).*
</Aside>

## 2. Cubes and Cube Roots

A **cube number** is the result of multiplying a number by itself three times.
$$x^3 = x \times x \times x$$

The **cube root** ($\sqrt[3]{x}$) asks: "What number, multiplied by itself three times, gives me $x$?"

### The IGCSE "Must-Know" List
The syllabus specifically requires you to recall the cubes and roots of **1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 10**:

* $1^3 = 1 \implies \sqrt[3]{1} = 1$
* $2^3 = 8 \implies \sqrt[3]{8} = 2$
* $3^3 = 27 \implies \sqrt[3]{27} = 3$
* $4^3 = 64 \implies \sqrt[3]{64} = 4$
* $5^3 = 125 \implies \sqrt[3]{125} = 5$
* $10^3 = 1000 \implies \sqrt[3]{1000} = 10$

<SteveTip title="The 64 'Double Agent' Trap">
Note that **64** is both a square number ($8^2$) and a cube number ($4^3$). Examiners love to use 64 in roots questions to see if you are paying attention to the little index number on the root symbol!
</SteveTip>

## 3. Calculating with Powers and Roots

### Other Powers
Beyond squares and cubes, you may see higher powers like $2^4$ or $2^5$.
* **Power of 4:** $2^4 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 16$
* **Power of 5:** $2^5 = 32$

<SteveTip title="The Negative Base Trap: (-3)² vs -3²">
Students lose marks here every single year! When raising negative numbers to a power, **brackets matter**:

1. **Even Powers of Negatives are Positive:** $$(-3)^2 = -3 \times -3 = 9$$
   *(The negatives pair up and cancel out).*

2. **Odd Powers of Negatives are Negative:**
   $$(-3)^3 = -3 \times -3 \times -3 = -27$$

3. **The Calculator Trap:** If you type `-3^2` into your calculator without brackets, it reads it as "the negative version of 3 squared" and gives you **-9**. If you mean to square negative 3, you **must** use brackets: `(-3)^2`.
</SteveTip>

### Order of Operations (BIDMAS/BEDMAS)
When roots are part of a larger calculation, treat the line of the root symbol like brackets. You must evaluate the entire value "under" the root sign before doing anything else.

**Example:** Work out $5 \times \sqrt{8 + 2^3}$
1. Resolve the power inside: $2^3 = 8$
2. Add inside the root: $8 + 8 = 16$
3. Find the root: $\sqrt{16} = 4$
4. Multiply: $5 \times 4 = 20$

## 4. Advanced Indices (Extended Curriculum)

In the Extended curriculum, powers are not always positive whole numbers. You must know how to handle negative powers, fractions, and decimal bases.

### Negative Indices
A negative power indicates a **reciprocal** (flip it over). It does NOT make the final number negative!
$$a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$$

<SteveTip title="The Negative Power Illusion">
A negative power and a negative base do completely different things. Do not mix them up!

* **Standard:** $3^{-2} = \frac{1}{3^2} = \frac{1}{9}$ *(The power flips it).*
* **Negative Base:** $(-3)^{-2} = \frac{1}{(-3)^2} = \frac{1}{9}$ *(The power flips it, and the square makes the -3 positive).*
* **No Brackets:** $-3^{-2} = -\left(\frac{1}{3^2}\right) = -\frac{1}{9}$ *(The power flips the 3, and the minus sign sits in front).*
</SteveTip>

$$a^{\frac{m}{n}} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m$$

* *Example:* $16^{\frac{3}{4}} = (\sqrt[4]{16})^3 = 2^3 = 8$

### Combined and Fractional Bases
Be careful when the "base" isn't a whole integer, or when the index is both negative and a fraction.

* **Fractional Base:** Raise both the top and bottom to the power.
  $$\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^3 = \frac{2^3}{3^3} = \frac{8}{27}$$
  *If the power is negative, flip the fraction to make the power positive!*
  $$\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{-2} = \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{16}{9}$$

* **Decimal Base:** Convert the decimal to a fraction first. It makes calculations much easier.
  $$0.5^3 = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{1}{8}$$
