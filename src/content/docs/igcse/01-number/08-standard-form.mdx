Scientists and mathematicians use standard form (sometimes called scientific notation) to write ridiculously large or microscopic numbers efficiently. Instead of writing $300,000,000$ metres per second for the speed of light, we can write it much more neatly as $3 \times 10^8$. 

---

## 1. The Golden Rule of Standard Form

For a number to be in *true* standard form, it must look exactly like this:
$$A \times 10^n$$

<Aside type="note" title="The Anatomy of Standard Form">
* **$A$ (The Front Number):** Must be a number between 1 and 10. It can be exactly 1, but it **cannot** be 10 or bigger ($1 \leq A < 10$).
* **$n$ (The Power/Index):** Must be an integer (a positive or negative whole number).
* **Positive powers** mean the original number was very large.
* **Negative powers** mean the original number was a very small decimal.
</Aside>

### Ordinary Number to Standard Form
* **Large Numbers:** Put the decimal point after the first digit. Count how many places it moved left. This is your positive power.
  $$45,000 \rightarrow 4.5 \times 10^4$$
* **Small Decimals:** Put the decimal point after the first non-zero digit. Count how many places it moved right. This is your negative power.
  $$0.00032 \rightarrow 3.2 \times 10^{-4}$$

### Standard Form to Ordinary Number
* **Positive Power:** Move the decimal to the right (making the number bigger). Fill the empty jumps with zeroes.
  $$6.1 \times 10^5 \rightarrow 610,000$$
* **Negative Power:** Move the decimal to the left (making the number smaller). 
  $$8.9 \times 10^{-3} \rightarrow 0.0089$$

## 3. Calculating with Standard Form

In Paper 2 (Non-Calculator), you will be expected to multiply and divide numbers given in standard form using your index laws (from Section 1.3).

### Multiplying and Dividing
**Step 1:** Group the front numbers together and group the powers of 10 together.
**Step 2:** Multiply (or divide) the front numbers.
**Step 3:** Use the index laws on the powers of 10 (add the powers for multiplication, subtract them for division).

*Example: Multiply $(3 \times 10^4)$ by $(2 \times 10^5)$*
1. Group: $(3 \times 2) \times (10^4 \times 10^5)$
2. Multiply the numbers: $6$
3. Add the powers: $10^9$
4. Final Answer: $6 \times 10^9$

<SteveTip title="The 'Not Quite' Standard Form Trap">
This is the most common place students lose marks! 

Imagine you multiply $(4 \times 10^3)$ by $(5 \times 10^4)$.
You get $20 \times 10^7$. 

**Stop!** That is mathematically correct, but it is **NOT** in standard form because the front number ($20$) is greater than 10. You must adjust it. 
To make the front number 10 times smaller ($2.0$), you must make the power 10 times bigger (add 1 to the power).
Correct Answer: **$2 \times 10^8$**
</SteveTip>

### Adding and Subtracting
The safest and most reliable method for adding and subtracting standard form in a non-calculator paper is to **convert them to ordinary numbers first**, do the sum, and convert the answer back.

*Example: $(4.5 \times 10^3) + (2 \times 10^2)$*
1. Convert to ordinary: $4500 + 200$
2. Add together: $4700$
3. Convert back to standard form: $4.7 \times 10^3$

    * **a)** $3,400,000$
    * **b)** $52,000$
    * **c)** $8,000,000,000$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    * **a)** The decimal moves 6 places left: $\mathbf{3.4 \times 10^6}$
    * **b)** The decimal moves 4 places left: $\mathbf{5.2 \times 10^4}$
    * **c)** The decimal moves 9 places left: $\mathbf{8 \times 10^9}$
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Converting Small Numbers">

    * **a)** $0.000057$
    * **b)** $0.003$
    * **c)** $0.00000091$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    * **a)** The decimal moves 5 places right: $\mathbf{5.7 \times 10^{-5}}$
    * **b)** The decimal moves 3 places right: $\mathbf{3 \times 10^{-3}}$
    * **c)** The decimal moves 7 places right: $\mathbf{9.1 \times 10^{-7}}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Multiplication with Adjustment (Extended)">
    Calculate the following. Give your final answers in standard form.
    * **a)** $(6 \times 10^5) \times (3 \times 10^2)$
    * **b)** $(5 \times 10^4) \times (4 \times 10^3)$
    * **c)** $(8 \times 10^{-3}) \times (7 \times 10^6)$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    * **a)** $6 \times 3 = 18$ and $10^5 \times 10^2 = 10^7$. We get $18 \times 10^7$. Adjust by making 18 smaller (1.8) and power bigger: $\mathbf{1.8 \times 10^8}$
    * **b)** $5 \times 4 = 20$ and $10^4 \times 10^3 = 10^7$. We get $20 \times 10^7$. Adjust: $\mathbf{2 \times 10^8}$
    * **c)** $8 \times 7 = 56$ and $10^{-3} \times 10^6 = 10^3$. We get $56 \times 10^3$. Adjust: $\mathbf{5.6 \times 10^4}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Division (Extended)">
    Calculate the following. Give your final answers in standard form.
    * **a)** $(8 \times 10^6) \div (2 \times 10^{-2})$
    * **b)** $(9 \times 10^5) \div (3 \times 10^8)$
    * **c)** $(4 \times 10^{-4}) \div (8 \times 10^{-2})$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    * **a)** $8 \div 2 = 4$. Subtract powers: $6 - (-2) = 8$. Answer: $\mathbf{4 \times 10^8}$
    * **b)** $9 \div 3 = 3$. Subtract powers: $5 - 8 = -3$. Answer: $\mathbf{3 \times 10^{-3}}$
    * **c)** $4 \div 8 = 0.5$. Subtract powers: $-4 - (-2) = -2$. We get $0.5 \times 10^{-2}$. Adjust by making 0.5 bigger (5) and power smaller: $\mathbf{5 \times 10^{-3}}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
