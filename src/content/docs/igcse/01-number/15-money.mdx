Money calculations in the IGCSE exam test your ability to handle decimals accurately, round correctly to two decimal places, and navigate exchange rates between different global currencies. As you'll see, currency conversion is just a real-world application of ratios!

---

## 1. Calculating with Money

When performing basic arithmetic with money, the most important rule is **formatting**. 

* **Two Decimal Places:** Most global currencies (Pounds, Dollars, Euros) are subdivided into 100 parts (pence, cents). Therefore, answers must *always* be given to exactly two decimal places, even if the second decimal is a zero. 
  * *Correct:* £4.50
  * *Incorrect:* £4.5
* **Do Not Round Early:** If a calculation involves multiple steps, keep the full decimal on your calculator and only round the *final* answer to two decimal places.

<SteveTip title="The .00 Habit">
While some mark schemes are lenient if you miss the final zero (writing £8.5 instead of £8.50), it is a "pro" habit to always include it. Showing the examiner you understand currency formatting ensures you never risk losing an accuracy mark on a strict paper!
</SteveTip>

## 2. Currency Conversion (The Exchange Rate)

An exchange rate tells you how much one unit of a currency is worth in another currency. 
For example: **£1 = $1.25**

The traditional way to approach conversions is to memorise the multiply/divide rule:
* **Changing FROM the base currency (the "1"):** Multiply by the exchange rate. (e.g., £40 into Dollars: £40 × 1.25 = $50)
* **Changing TO the base currency:** Divide by the exchange rate. (e.g., $100 into Pounds: $100 ÷ 1.25 = £80)

<Aside type="caution" title="Exam Tip: The 'Base' Currency">
The base currency is the one with the "1" next to it. Don't blindly assume your home currency is the base! An exam might give you $1 = €0.92, making the Dollar the base currency in that specific question.
</Aside>

---

## 3. The Ratio Method (The Foolproof Strategy)

If you struggle to remember whether to multiply or divide, setting up a **ratio table** makes it impossible to get wrong.

**Example:** The exchange rate is €1 = ¥160 (Japanese Yen). Convert ¥4000 into Euros.

1. **Set up the headings:** Write the two currencies as a ratio.
   **€ : ¥**
2. **Write the exchange rate:** **1 : 160**
3. **Write the value you know:** Place the 4000 under the Yen column.
   **€ : ¥**
   **1 : 160**
   **? : 4000**
4. **Find the scale factor:** How do you get from 160 to 4000? You divide! **4000 ÷ 160 = 25**. 
5. **Apply it to the other side:** Multiply the 1 by 25. The answer is **€25**.

<SteveTip title="Comparing Value for Money">
If a question asks which of two items is cheaper and they are in different currencies, **convert them both to the same currency** first! You cannot compare £45 and $60 directly without using the exchange rate.
</SteveTip>

    4. Calculate change: **£20.00 - £11.55 = £8.45**
    **Answer:** £8.45
