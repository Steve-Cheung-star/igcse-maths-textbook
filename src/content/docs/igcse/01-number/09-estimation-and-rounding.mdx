Estimation is the "common sense" check of mathematics. In a high-pressure exam, it is easy to type a number into your calculator incorrectly and get an answer that is 10 times too large. Estimation gives you the "ballpark" figure so you can spot those mistakes instantly.

---

## 1. Place Value: The Red vs. Blue Divide

To round correctly, you must know the name of the "target" column. Think of the decimal point as a mirror. As you move left, numbers get **10x larger** (the Red Side). As you move right, they get **10x smaller** (the Blue Side).

### The Decimal Mirror
* **Whole Numbers (Red Side):** These end in **-s** (Tens, Hundreds, Thousands, Millions).
* **Decimal Parts (Blue Side):** These end in **-ths** (Tenths, Hundredths, Thousandths).

## 2. Rounding: The "Next Door" Rule

Rounding is the process of making a number "simpler" while keeping its value close to the original. 

1. **Find your target:** Underline the digit in the column you are rounding to.
2. **Look next door:** Look at the digit immediately to the **right**.
3. **The Decision:** * If it is **5, 6, 7, 8, or 9**, round the target digit **UP**.
   * If it is **0, 1, 2, 3, or 4**, keep the target digit **THE SAME**.
4. **Fill the gaps:** For whole numbers, fill any remaining columns before the decimal with **placeholder zeros**.

### The "Chain Reaction" (Rounding 9s)
When you round a $9$ up, it becomes a $10$. You put down the $0$ and "carry" the $1$ to the next column on the left.
* **Example:** Round $0.096$ to **2 decimal places**.
* The 2nd d.p. is $9$. Next door is $6$ (round up).
* $9$ becomes $10$. The $1$ carries to the first d.p.
* **Answer:** $0.10$

<SteveTip title="The Essential Zero">
If a question asks for **hundredths** (2 d.p.) and your answer is $4.5$, you **must** write **$4.50$**. In science and maths, $4.5$ means "somewhere between 4.45 and 4.55," but $4.50$ means "exactly 4.50." The zero proves your level of accuracy.
</SteveTip>

## 3. Significant Figures (s.f.)

Significant figures (s.f.) are used to show the precision of a measurement. Unlike decimal places, they don't care where the decimal point is—they care about the **digits themselves**.



### The Three Types of Zeros
1.  **Leading Zeros (The Placeholders):** $0.0004$
    * These are **NEVER** significant. They just tell you how small the number is.
2.  **Trapped Zeros (The Sandwiches):** $4005$
    * These are **ALWAYS** significant. They are part of the actual measurement.
3.  **Trailing Zeros (The Precision-Makers):** $5.200$
    * In a decimal, these **ARE** significant because they show exactly how precisely you measured the number.

## 4. Estimation Strategies

In the IGCSE, "Estimate" is code for: **Round everything to 1 significant figure first.**

<SteveTip title="The 1 s.f. Method Mark">
If you calculate $\frac{19.2}{4.1}$ on your calculator ($4.68...$) and then round it to $5$, you get **0 marks**. 
You **must** show the rounded values first: $\frac{20}{4} = 5$. The examiner is testing your ability to simplify the problem, not your ability to use a calculator.
</SteveTip>

### Advanced Estimation Tricks
* **Roots:** Don't round $\sqrt{35}$ to $\sqrt{40}$ (1 s.f.) if you can't solve it. Round it to the nearest **square number** ($\sqrt{36} = 6$).
* **Division by Decimals:** * Dividing by $0.5$ is the same as **doubling** ($\times 2$).
  * Dividing by $0.2$ is the same as **multiplying by 5**.
  * Dividing by $0.1$ is the same as **multiplying by 10**.

### Part C: Complex Estimation (Syllabus Examples)

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Fraction Estimation">
    By writing each number correct to **1 significant figure**, estimate the value of:
    $$ \frac{41.3}{9.79 \times 0.765} $$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    1. Round $41.3 \rightarrow 40$ (1 s.f.)
    2. Round $9.79 \rightarrow 10$ (1 s.f.)
    3. Round $0.765 \rightarrow 0.8$ (1 s.f. - leading zeros don't count!)
    4. Substitute: $\frac{40}{10 \times 0.8} = \frac{40}{8}$
    **Answer:** $5$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Root Estimation">
    Estimate the value of $\sqrt{98.2} \times 4.9$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    1. Round $98.2 \rightarrow 100$ (This is 1 s.f. AND a square number).
    2. Round $4.9 \rightarrow 5$.
    3. Calculate: $\sqrt{100} \times 5 = 10 \times 5$
    **Answer:** $50$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: The Division Trap">
    Estimate the value of:
    $$ \frac{19.8 \times 3.04}{0.485} $$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    1. Round to 1 s.f.: $20$, $3$, and $0.5$.
    2. Calculation: $\frac{20 \times 3}{0.5} = \frac{60}{0.5}$
    3. Use the division trick: $60 \times 2 = 120$.
    **Answer:** $120$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
