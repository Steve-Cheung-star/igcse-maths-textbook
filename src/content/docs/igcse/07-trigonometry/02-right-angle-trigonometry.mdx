While Pythagoras' theorem connects the three *sides* of a right-angled triangle, **Trigonometry** connects the sides with the **angles**. 

Before doing any calculations, you must know how to label your triangle based on the specific angle you are looking at (usually represented by the Greek letter theta, $\theta$).

* **Hypotenuse (H):** Always the longest side, directly opposite the 90° angle.
* **Opposite (O):** The side directly across from the angle $\theta$.
* **Adjacent (A):** The side next to the angle $\theta$ (the side that connects the 90° angle and $\theta$).

## 1. The Trigonometric Ratios: SOH CAH TOA

To remember the three trigonometric formulas, we use the famous acronym **SOH CAH TOA**. 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{\textbf{SOH:}} \quad \sin(\theta) &= \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \\
\\
\text{\textbf{CAH:}} \quad \cos(\theta) &= \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \\
\\
\text{\textbf{TOA:}} \quad \tan(\theta) &= \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 2. Finding Missing Angles (Inverse Trig)

When you know two sides but need the angle, you must use the **inverse trigonometric functions** on your calculator ($\sin^{-1}$, $\cos^{-1}$, $\tan^{-1}$).

<SteveTip title="Strict Rounding Rule for Angles">
In IGCSE Mathematics, **sides** are rounded to 3 significant figures, but **angles in degrees** must be rounded to **1 decimal place (1 d.p.)** unless exact. Do not lose easy accuracy marks here!
</SteveTip>

## 3. Structured Question: Combining Pythagoras and Trig (Shared Sides)

A very common investigative Paper 6 style question chains triangles together. You must solve the first triangle to find a "shared side," then use that side (as an exact value) to solve the second triangle.

### Example Question
In the compound solid shape below:
1.  Calculate the exact perpendicular height ($h$).
2.  Calculate the hypotenuse ($y$) of the right triangle.
3.  Calculate the angle ($\alpha$).

Give final side answers to 3 s.f. and angles to 1 d.p.

    <text x="235" y="95" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#ea580c" text-anchor="middle" transform="rotate(39 235 95)">y</text>

    <path d="M 270 160 A 30 30 0 0 1 276 141" fill="none" stroke="#b91c1c" stroke-width="2" />

#### Part 1: Calculate $h$ exactly.
Relative to the 28° angle, $h$ is Opposite and 13 is Hypotenuse. This is SOH.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sin(28^\circ) &= \frac{h}{13} \\
h &= 13 \times \sin(28^\circ) \\
h &\approx 6.103... \text{ cm}
\end{aligned}
$$

#### Part 2: Calculate hypotenuse $y$.
Use Pythagoras' theorem on the right triangle. The legs are $h$ and 15. We need $y$. **Use the unrounded value of $h$!**
$$
\begin{aligned}
h^2 + 15^2 &= y^2 \\
(6.103...)^2 + 225 &= y^2 \\
37.249... + 225 &= y^2 \\
262.249... &= y^2 \\
y &= \sqrt{262.249...} \\
y &\approx 16.194... \\
y &= 16.2 \text{ cm (to 3 s.f.)}
\end{aligned}
$$

#### Part 3: Calculate angle $\alpha$.
Relative to $\alpha$, we know Adjacent = 15 cm. We found hypotenuse $y \approx 16.2$ cm, but let's use the known legs ($h$ and 15) for maximum accuracy using TOA. **Use unrounded $h$!**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\tan(\alpha) &= \frac{\text{Opposite (h)}}{\text{Adjacent (15)}} \\
\tan(\alpha) &= \frac{6.103...}{15} \\
\alpha &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{6.103...}{15}\right) \\
\alpha &\approx 22.140...^\circ \\
\alpha &= 22.1^\circ \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Because horizontal lines are parallel to each other, the angle of elevation from point A to point B is **always exactly equal** to the angle of depression from point B to point A (Alternate Angles).
</Aside>

### Example 4: Angle of Elevation
A surveyor stands 40 m away from the base of a building. The angle of elevation to the top of the building is 52°. If the surveyor's eye level is 1.6 m above the ground, calculate the total height of the building to 3 s.f.

<Steps>
1. **Calculate the triangle's height ($h$) using TOA:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \tan(52^\circ) &= \frac{h}{40} \\
   h &= 40 \times \tan(52^\circ) \\
   h &\approx 51.197... \text{ m}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Add the surveyor's eye level for total height:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \text{Total Height} &= 51.197... + 1.6 \\
   &\approx 52.8 \text{ m (to 3 s.f.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

    <path d="M 90 50 A 40 40 0 0 1 83 72" fill="none" stroke="#2563eb" stroke-width="2" />
    <text x="100" y="70" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#2563eb">θ</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Find angle $\theta$ inside the triangle using TOA:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \tan(\theta) &= \frac{14 \text{ (Opposite)}}{30 \text{ (Adjacent)}} \\
   \theta &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{14}{30}\right) \\
   \theta &\approx 25.0^\circ
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Calculate the final bearing from North (at A):**
   The path from A to P is due East (because A is West of P). Due East is a bearing of 090°.
   Point B is further clockwise. The total bearing is the 090° turn to East, plus the extra turn of angle $\theta$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \text{Bearing} &= 90^\circ + \theta \\
   &= 90^\circ + 25.0^\circ \\
   &= 115.0^\circ \text{ (to 1 d.p.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 6. The Shortest Distance from a Point to a Line

A classic IGCSE question asks for the "shortest distance" from a point to a line. 
**The shortest distance is ALWAYS the perpendicular distance (it creates a 90° angle with the line).** This instantly creates right-angled triangles for you to solve.

### Example 6: Shortest Distance
Town B is 50 km from Town A on a bearing of 070°. A straight road runs completely due East from Town A. What is the shortest distance from Town B to this road?

3. **Set up the right-angled triangle:** We need the perpendicular drop from B to the road (this is the **Opposite** side). We know the Hypotenuse (50 km) and the angle (20°). Use SOH.

       &= 65.1 \text{ km (to 3 s.f.)}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
