In non-calculator papers, you are expected to know the exact values (in surd/fraction form) for specific "special" angles: **0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90°**. 

Memorizing a table is one way to do it, but knowing *how to build* the table or *where the values come from* is a much safer strategy for an exam.

---

## 1. The "Special Triangles" Method (Understanding Why)

If you forget a value, you can instantly re-derive it by sketching one of two simple right-angled triangles.

### Triangle 1: The 45° Triangle (Isosceles Right-Angled)
Imagine a right-angled triangle where the two shorter sides are both exactly 1 unit long. Because it is isosceles, the two base angles must be equal: (180 - 90) ÷ 2 = 45°. By Pythagoras, the hypotenuse is $\sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$.

Using SOH CAH TOA on the 45° angle:
* **$\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$**
* **$\cos(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$**
* **$\tan(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{1} = 1$**

### Triangle 2: The 30°-60° Triangle (Equilateral Cut in Half)
Start with an equilateral triangle where all sides are 2 units and all angles are 60°. Drop a perpendicular line down the middle. This creates a right-angled triangle with a base of 1, a hypotenuse of 2, and a top angle of 30°. By Pythagoras, the height is $\sqrt{2^2 - 1^2} = \sqrt{3}$.

Using SOH CAH TOA on the **30°** angle:
* **$\sin(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}$**
* **$\cos(30^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$**
* **$\tan(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$**

Using SOH CAH TOA on the **60°** angle:
* **$\sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$**
* **$\cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}$**
* **$\tan(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1} = \sqrt{3}$**

## 2. Rationalised vs. Unrationalised Forms

You will often see two different versions of the same exact value, depending on the textbook or marking scheme. 

Mathematically, it is traditionally considered "messy" to leave a surd (square root) in the denominator of a fraction. To fix this, we **rationalise the denominator** by multiplying the top and bottom by that surd.

* **For 45°:** $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} = \mathbf{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}$
* **For 30°:** $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \mathbf{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}}$

<Aside type="tip" title="Which one should you use?">
  For Cambridge IGCSE, **both forms are completely acceptable** and will score full marks unless the question specifically says "Give your answer with a rational denominator." Don't let the different formats confuse you—they are exactly the same number!
</Aside>

## 3. The "0-1-2-3-4" Table Generation Trick

If you struggle with visualizing triangles, use this foolproof trick to generate the entire table from scratch the moment your exam starts. 

<Steps>
  1. **Write the angles:** 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°.
  2. **Write the numbers 0 to 4:** Write 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 under the angles.
  3. **Square root them all:** $\sqrt{0}, \sqrt{1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{4}$.
  4. **Divide them all by 2:** This gives you the entire **Sine** row! (Simplify where possible: $\sqrt{4}/2 = 2/2 = 1$).
  5. **Reverse the row for Cosine:** Just write the Sine row backwards.
  6. **Divide Sin by Cos for Tangent:** Because $\tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}$.
</Steps>

### The Master Table of Exact Values

| Angle ($\theta$) | $0^\circ$ | $30^\circ$ | $45^\circ$ | $60^\circ$ | $90^\circ$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$\sin(\theta)$** | $0$ | $\frac{1}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ *(or $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$)* | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $1$ |
| **$\cos(\theta)$** | $1$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ *(or $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$)* | $\frac{1}{2}$ | $0$ |
| **$\tan(\theta)$** | $0$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$ *(or $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$)* | $1$ | $\sqrt{3}$ | **Undefined** |

*(Note: $\tan(90^\circ)$ is undefined because dividing by zero ($\frac{1}{0}$) is mathematically impossible).*

## 4. The Viral "Finger Method"

You’ve likely seen this on social media. While it is purely a memorization gimmick and doesn't teach you *why* the math works, it is undeniably useful for some students in a high-pressure exam scenario.

1. Hold out your **left hand**, palm facing you. 
2. Assign angles to your fingers starting from the pinky: Pinky = 0°, Ring = 30°, Middle = 45°, Index = 60°, Thumb = 90°.
3. Put down the finger for the angle you want.
4. **For Sine:** Count the fingers **BELOW** the folded finger. Put that number in a square root and divide by 2. $\frac{\sqrt{\text{bottom fingers}}}{2}$
5. **For Cosine:** Count the fingers **ABOVE** the folded finger. Put that number in a square root and divide by 2. $\frac{\sqrt{\text{top fingers}}}{2}$
6. **For Tangent:** Put the square root of bottom fingers over the square root of top fingers. $\frac{\sqrt{\text{bottom fingers}}}{\sqrt{\text{top fingers}}}$

*Example for sin(60°): Put down your index finger. There are 3 fingers below it. So, $\sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.*

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Evaluating Expressions">

    a) Calculate the exact value of $\sin(30^\circ) \times \cos(60^\circ) + \tan(45^\circ)$.

    b) Evaluate the exact value of $\cos^2(45^\circ) + \sin^2(45^\circ)$.

    c) Write down the exact value of $\frac{\tan(60^\circ)}{\sin(60^\circ)}$ in its simplest integer form.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">

    <Steps>
      1. **Part (a):**
         * $\sin(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}$, $\cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}$, $\tan(45^\circ) = 1$
         * $\left(\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2}\right) + 1 = \frac{1}{4} + 1 = \frac{5}{4}$
      2. **Part (b):**
         * $\cos(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, $\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$
         * $\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$
      3. **Part (c):**
         * $\tan(60^\circ) = \sqrt{3}$, $\sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
         * $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}} = \sqrt{3} \times \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} = 2$
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>

    A right-angled triangle $ABC$ has an angle $\angle BAC = 60^\circ$. The side adjacent to the $60^\circ$ angle is $AB = 8 \text{ cm}$.

    a) State the exact trigonometric ratio needed to find the hypotenuse $x$.

    b) Calculate the exact length of the hypotenuse $x$.

    c) Calculate the exact length of the opposite side $y$, leaving your answer in the form $a\sqrt{b}$.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">

    <Steps>
      1. **Part (a):**
         We have Adjacent ($8$) and want Hypotenuse ($x$). This requires Cosine (CAH).
         $$ \cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2} $$
      2. **Part (b):**
         $$ \cos(60^\circ) = \frac{8}{x} $$
         $$ \frac{1}{2} = \frac{8}{x} \implies x = 16 \text{ cm} $$
      3. **Part (c):**
         Using Tangent (TOA) for the opposite side $y$:
         $$ \tan(60^\circ) = \frac{y}{8} $$
         $$ \sqrt{3} = \frac{y}{8} \implies y = 8\sqrt{3} \text{ cm} $$
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>

    b) Simplify the fraction and determine the exact size of angle $\theta$.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">

    Quadrilateral $ABCD$ contains two right-angled triangles sharing the diagonal side $AC$. 
    In triangle $ABC$, $\angle ABC = 90^\circ$, $\angle BAC = 30^\circ$, and $AB = 10\sqrt{3} \text{ cm}$.
    In triangle $ADC$, $\angle ADC = 90^\circ$ and $\angle CAD = 45^\circ$.

    a) Calculate the exact length of the shared side $AC$.

    b) Calculate the exact length of $CD$.

    c) Write down the exact length of $AD$.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">

    <Steps>
      1. **Part (a):**
         In $\triangle ABC$, $AB$ ($10\sqrt{3}$) is the Adjacent to the $30^\circ$ angle, and $AC$ is the Hypotenuse.
         $$ \cos(30^\circ) = \frac{\text{Adj}}{\text{Hyp}} \implies \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{10\sqrt{3}}{AC} $$
         Multiply both sides by $AC$ and divide by $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$:
         $$ AC = 10\sqrt{3} \times \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} = 20 \text{ cm} $$
      2. **Part (b):**
         In $\triangle ADC$, $AC$ ($20$) is the Hypotenuse, and $CD$ is the Opposite to the $45^\circ$ angle.
         $$ \sin(45^\circ) = \frac{CD}{20} \implies \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{CD}{20} $$
         $$ CD = 20 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = 10\sqrt{2} \text{ cm} $$
      3. **Part (c):**
         Because $\triangle ADC$ has a $45^\circ$ angle and a $90^\circ$ angle, it is isosceles. Therefore, Adjacent = Opposite.
         $$ AD = CD = 10\sqrt{2} \text{ cm} $$
    </Steps>

  </TabItem>

    b) Calculate the exact length of the adjacent side.

    c) State the exact perimeter of the triangle.

    d) Calculate the exact area of the triangle.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">

    An equilateral triangle has a side length of $12 \text{ cm}$. A perpendicular line is dropped from the top vertex to the base.

    a) State the size of the internal angles of the equilateral triangle.

    b) Using exact trigonometric values, calculate the exact perpendicular height of the triangle.

    c) Calculate the exact area of the equilateral triangle.

  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">

    <Steps>
      1. **Part (a):**
         All internal angles in an equilateral triangle are $60^\circ$.
      2. **Part (b):**
         The perpendicular line creates a right-angled triangle with a hypotenuse of $12$, an angle of $60^\circ$, and an unknown height (Opposite).
         $$ \sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\text{Height}}{12} \implies \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{\text{Height}}{12} $$
         $$ \text{Height} = 12 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 6\sqrt{3} \text{ cm} $$
      3. **Part (c):**
         The base of the full equilateral triangle is $12 \text{ cm}$.
         $$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} $$
         $$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 12 \times 6\sqrt{3} = 36\sqrt{3} \text{ cm}^2 $$
    </Steps>
