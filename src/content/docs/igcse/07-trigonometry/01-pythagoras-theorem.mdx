Pythagoras' theorem is a fundamental rule in geometry that relates the three sides of a **right-angled triangle**. It states that the square of the longest side (the **hypotenuse**) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two shorter sides.

<SteveTip title="The Hypotenuse">
The hypotenuse is *always* the longest side of a right-angled triangle, and it is *always* directly opposite the $90^\circ$ right angle. Identifying the hypotenuse correctly is the most important step!
</SteveTip>

The formula is universally written as:
$$
a^2 + b^2 = c^2
$$
Where $c$ is the hypotenuse, and $a$ and $b$ are the two shorter sides (legs).

## 1. Basic Pythagoras: Finding Missing Sides

Before moving to complex shapes or coordinate grids, you must master the two basic mechanics: finding the hypotenuse, and working backward to find a shorter side.

### Example 1a: Finding the Hypotenuse
If you know the two shorter sides, you square them, add them together, and then square root the result to find the hypotenuse.

    <text x="110" y="170" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">12 cm</text>
    <text x="30" y="105" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">5 cm</text>
    <text x="125" y="90" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#ef4444" text-anchor="middle">c = ?</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Set up the equation:** Let $a = 5$ and $b = 12$. The hypotenuse is $c$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   5^2 + 12^2 &= c^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$

2. **Solve for $c$:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   25 + 144 &= c^2 \\
   169 &= c^2 \\
   c &= \sqrt{169} \\
   &= 13 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

### Example 1b: Finding a Shorter Side (A Leg)
If you already know the hypotenuse and one shorter side, you must **subtract** the squares instead of adding them.

<div style={{ display: 'flex', justifyContent: 'center', margin: '2rem 0' }}>
  <svg viewBox="0 0 250 200" width="100%" maxWidth="250px" style={{ background: '#f8fafc', borderRadius: '8px', border: '1px solid #e2e8f0' }}>

    <rect x="185" y="135" width="15" height="15" fill="none" stroke="#d97706" stroke-width="1.5" />
    <text x="125" y="170" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">9 cm</text>
    <text x="115" y="80" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">15 cm</text>
    <text x="220" y="100" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#ef4444" text-anchor="middle">x = ?</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Set up the equation:** The hypotenuse is $c = 15$. Let $a = 9$ and the unknown side be $b = x$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   9^2 + x^2 &= 15^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$

2. **Solve for $x$:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   81 + x^2 &= 225 \\
   x^2 &= 225 - 81 \\
   x^2 &= 144 \\
   x &= \sqrt{144} \\
   &= 12 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 2. Pythagorean Triples (The "Cheat Codes")

A **Pythagorean triple** is a set of three whole numbers that perfectly fit Pythagoras' theorem. Memorizing the most common ones is a massive advantage for **Paper 2** (where calculators are not allowed) and **Paper 6** (where spotting number patterns is key to investigations).

If you recognize two sides of a right-angled triangle from a triple, you can instantly know the third side without doing any calculations!

**The Essential Triples to Memorize:**
* **3, 4, 5**
* **5, 12, 13**
* **8, 15, 17**
* **7, 24, 25**

<Aside type="note" title="Multiples of Triples">
Any multiple of a Pythagorean triple is *also* a Pythagorean triple. 
For example, if you multiply the **3-4-5** triangle by 2, you get **6-8-10**. If you multiply it by 10, you get **30-40-50**. Notice that **Example 1b** above (9, 12, 15) is just the 3-4-5 triangle multiplied by 3!
</Aside>

## 3. Accuracy: Surds, 3 s.f., and Shared Edges

In IGCSE Mathematics, you must leave your final answers to **3 significant figures (s.f.)** unless the value is exact or the question specifies otherwise. 

However, in multi-step questions with triangles that share an edge, **never round your intermediate answers**. If a side length is a long decimal, keep it in its exact **surd form** (e.g., $\sqrt{18}$) until the very final step. Rounding too early will create compounding errors that cost you accuracy marks.

### Example 2: Triangles with a Shared Edge
Calculate the exact length of $x$, and give your final answer to 3 significant figures.

    {/* Right Triangle */}

    <rect x="150" y="145" width="15" height="15" fill="none" stroke="#db2777" stroke-width="1.5" />

    {/* Labels Left */}
    <text x="100" y="180" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">3 cm</text>
    <text x="80" y="100" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">3 cm</text>
    
    {/* Labels Shared */}
    <text x="135" y="115" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#64748b" text-anchor="middle">h</text>
    
    {/* Labels Right */}
    <text x="245" y="100" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b" text-anchor="middle">10 cm</text>
    <text x="235" y="180" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#ef4444" text-anchor="middle">x = ?</text>
  </svg>

<Steps>
1. **Find the shared edge ($h$) exactly:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   3^2 + 3^2 &= h^2 \\
   9 + 9 &= h^2 \\
   18 &= h^2 \\
   h &= \sqrt{18} \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   *(Do **not** write $h = 4.24$. Keep it as $\sqrt{18}$!)*

2. **Use the exact surd to find $x$:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   x^2 + (\sqrt{18})^2 &= 10^2 \\
   x^2 + 18 &= 100 \\
   x^2 &= 100 - 18 \\
   &= 82 \\
   x &= \sqrt{82} \\
   &\approx 9.05538... \\
   &\implies 9.06 \text{ cm (to 3 s.f.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="What happens if you round too early?">
If you had rounded $h = \sqrt{18}$ to **4.2 cm** early on, your next step would be:
$x^2 + 4.2^2 = 100 \implies x^2 + 17.64 = 100 \implies x^2 = 82.36 \implies x = 9.075...$
Your final answer would be **9.08 cm**, which is mathematically incorrect and will lose you the final accuracy mark! Always keep surds.
</Aside>

## 5. Pythagoras in Circles: Chords and Distances

A **chord** is a straight line segment whose endpoints lie on the circle. Pythagoras' theorem frequently appears in circle problems due to a very specific and important circle property.

<Aside type="note" title="Crucial Circle Property">
A perpendicular line drawn from the **centre** of a circle to a **chord** will always cut that chord exactly in half (bisect it). 

Drawing the radius from the centre to the end of the chord forms a **right-angled triangle**, allowing you to use Pythagoras' theorem!
</Aside>

### Example 4: Finding the Length of a Chord
A circle has a radius of **13 cm**. A chord is located at a perpendicular distance of **5 cm** from the centre of the circle. Calculate the exact length of the chord.

    <text x="265" y="215" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#059669">B</text>

    {/* Labels */}
    <text x="110" y="180" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#ef4444">5 cm</text>
    <text x="190" y="165" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#7e22ce">13 cm</text>
    <text x="190" y="220" font-family="sans-serif" font-weight="bold" fill="#1e293b">x</text>
  </svg>
</div>

<Steps>
1. **Set up the right-angled triangle:**
   Let $x$ be half the length of the chord. 
   The hypotenuse is the radius ($c = 13$). The shorter side is the distance from the centre ($a = 5$).
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   a^2 + x^2 &= c^2 \\
   5^2 + x^2 &= 13^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$

2. **Solve for half the chord ($x$):**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   25 + x^2 &= 169 \\
   x^2 &= 169 - 25 \\
   &= 144 \\
   x &= \sqrt{144} \\
   &= 12 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

3. **Find the full chord length:**
   Because the perpendicular bisects the chord, the full length is twice the value of $x$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \text{Full Chord} &= 2 \times 12 \\
   &= 24 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 6. The Converse of Pythagoras' Theorem

The **converse** of Pythagoras' theorem works backwards to check if an unknown triangle has a right angle. 

If you know the lengths of all three sides of a triangle, you can test if it is a right-angled triangle:
* **If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$**, then the triangle **IS** a right-angled triangle.
* **If $a^2 + b^2 \neq c^2$**, then the triangle **IS NOT** a right-angled triangle.

*(Always ensure $c$ is the longest side when setting up your test!)*

### Example 5: Checking for a Right Angle
A triangle has side lengths of **7 cm**, **24 cm**, and **25 cm**. Is this a right-angled triangle?

<Steps>
1. **Identify the longest side:** The longest side is **25 cm**, so this must be $c$.
2. **Calculate the sum of the squares of the shorter sides:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   a^2 + b^2 &= 7^2 + 24^2 \\
   &= 49 + 576 \\
   &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Calculate the square of the longest side:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   c^2 &= 25^2 \\
   &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
4. **Compare the results:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   625 &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   Since $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, this **is** a right-angled triangle.
</Steps>

## 7. Classifying Triangles on a Grid

By calculating the distances between three coordinate points, we can determine the exact side lengths of a triangle. Once we have the side lengths, we can classify the triangle based on its properties:
* **Equilateral:** All three sides are equal.
* **Isosceles:** Exactly two sides are equal.
* **Scalene:** All three sides are different lengths.
* **Right-angled:** The sides satisfy $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.

### Example 6: Classifying by Coordinates
The points $A(2, 2)$, $B(5, 6)$, and $C(8, 2)$ form a triangle. Find the exact lengths of the three sides and classify the triangle.

    <circle cx="270" cy="160" r="5" fill="#1e293b" />

    <line x1="90" y1="160" x2="180" y2="160" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-dasharray="4 4" stroke-width="2" />

    <rect x="170" y="150" width="10" height="10" fill="none" stroke="#ef4444" stroke-width="1.5" />
  </svg>

<Steps>
1. **Find the length of $AB$ using Pythagoras:**
   The horizontal distance is $5 - 2 = 3$. The vertical distance is $6 - 2 = 4$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   AB^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\
   &= 9 + 16 \\
   &= 25 \\
   AB &= \sqrt{25} \\
   &= 5 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

2. **Find the length of $BC$ using Pythagoras:**
   The horizontal distance is $8 - 5 = 3$. The vertical distance is $6 - 2 = 4$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   BC^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\
   &= 9 + 16 \\
   &= 25 \\
   BC &= \sqrt{25} \\
   &= 5 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

3. **Find the length of $AC$ (Horizontal Line):**
   Because $A$ and $C$ are on the same $y$-level, we can just subtract their $x$-coordinates.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   AC &= 8 - 2 \\
   &= 6 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

4. **Classify the Triangle:**
   Because two sides are equal in length ($AB = 5$ and $BC = 5$), the triangle is **Isosceles**.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: The Converse Theorem (Core)">
    A triangle has side lengths of **9 cm**, **40 cm**, and **41 cm**. Prove mathematically whether or not this is a right-angled triangle.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify the longest side as $c$:** $c = 41$. The legs are $a = 9$ and $b = 40$.
    2. **Calculate $a^2 + b^2$:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       9^2 + 40^2 &= 81 + 1600 \\
       &= 1681
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Calculate $c^2$:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       41^2 &= 1681
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Conclusion:**
       Because $9^2 + 40^2 = 41^2$, the triangle **is** a right-angled triangle.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    The points $P(1, 1)$, $Q(4, 5)$, and $R(7, 1)$ form a triangle. Calculate the lengths of all three sides and state the specific classification of the triangle.

       PQ &= \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    2. **Length of $QR$:**

       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       QR^2 &= 3^2 + (-4)^2 \\
       QR &= \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Length of $PR$ (Horizontal line):**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       PR &= 7 - 1 = 6 \text{ units}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Classification:**
       Because $PQ = QR = 5$, two sides are equal. The triangle is an **Isosceles Triangle**.
    </Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 10: Compound Shapes (Extended)">
    A right-angled trapezium has two parallel vertical sides of length **12 cm** and **7 cm**. The horizontal bottom edge is **12 cm** long. Calculate the exact perimeter of the trapezium.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 10">
    <Steps>
    1. **Create a right-angled triangle:**
       Draw a horizontal line across from the top of the 7 cm side to the 12 cm side. This forms a rectangle at the bottom and a right-angled triangle at the top.
    2. **Find the legs of the newly formed triangle:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Horizontal leg} &= 12 \text{ cm (same as bottom edge)} \\
       \text{Vertical leg} &= 12 - 7 = 5 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    3. **Find the slant edge (hypotenuse $c$):**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       5^2 + 12^2 &= c^2 \\
       25 + 144 &= c^2 \\
       169 &= c^2 \\
       c &= \sqrt{169} = 13 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Calculate total perimeter:**
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       \text{Perimeter} &= 12 + 7 + 12 + 13 \\
       &= 44 \text{ cm}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
