Pythagoras' theorem is a fundamental rule in geometry that relates the three sides of a **right-angled triangle**. It states that the square of the longest side (the **hypotenuse**) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two shorter sides.

<SteveTip title="The Hypotenuse">

The hypotenuse is *always* the longest side of a right-angled triangle, and it is *always* directly opposite the $90^\circ$ right angle. Identifying the hypotenuse correctly is the most important step!

</SteveTip>

The formula is universally written as:
$$
a^2 + b^2 = c^2
$$
Where $c$ is the hypotenuse, and $a$ and $b$ are the two shorter sides (legs).

## 1. Basic Pythagoras: Finding Missing Sides

Before moving to complex shapes or coordinate grids, you must master the two basic mechanics: finding the hypotenuse, and working backward to find a shorter side.

## 2. Pythagorean Triples (The "Cheat Codes")

A **Pythagorean triple** is a set of three whole numbers that perfectly fit Pythagoras' theorem. Memorizing the most common ones is a massive advantage for **Paper 2** (where calculators are not allowed) and **Paper 6** (where spotting number patterns is key to investigations). 

If you recognize two sides of a right-angled triangle from a triple, you can instantly know the third side without doing any calculations!

**The Essential Triples to Memorize:**
* **3, 4, 5**
* **5, 12, 13**
* **8, 15, 17**
* **7, 24, 25**

<Aside type="note" title="Multiples of Triples">

Any multiple of a Pythagorean triple is *also* a Pythagorean triple. For example, if you multiply the **3-4-5** triangle by 2, you get **6-8-10**. If you multiply it by 10, you get **30-40-50**. Notice that **Example 1b** above (9, 12, 15) is just the 3-4-5 triangle multiplied by 3!

</Aside>

## 3. Accuracy: Surds, 3 s.f., and Shared Edges

In IGCSE Mathematics, you must leave your final answers to **3 significant figures (s.f.)** unless the value is exact or the question specifies otherwise. 

However, in multi-step questions with triangles that share an edge, **never round your intermediate answers**. If a side length is a long decimal, keep it in its exact **surd form** (e.g., $\sqrt{18}$) until the very final step. Rounding too early will create compounding errors that cost you accuracy marks.

### Example 2: Triangles with a Shared Edge
Calculate the exact length of $x$, and give your final answer to 3 significant figures.

<Steps>

1. **Find the shared edge ($h$) exactly:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   3^2 + 3^2 &= h^2 \\
   9 + 9 &= h^2 \\
   18 &= h^2 \\
   h &= \sqrt{18} \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   *(Do **not** write $h = 4.24$. Keep it as $\sqrt{18}$!)*
2. **Use the exact surd to find $x$:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   x^2 + (\sqrt{18})^2 &= 10^2 \\
   x^2 + 18 &= 100 \\
   x^2 &= 100 - 18 \\
   &= 82 \\
   x &= \sqrt{82} \\
   &\approx 9.05538... \\
   &\implies 9.06 \text{ cm (to 3 s.f.)}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="What happens if you round too early?">

If you had rounded $h = \sqrt{18}$ to **4.2 cm** early on, your next step would be:
$x^2 + 4.2^2 = 100 \implies x^2 + 17.64 = 100 \implies x^2 = 82.36 \implies x = 9.075...$
Your final answer would be **9.08 cm**, which is mathematically incorrect and will lose you the final accuracy mark! Always keep surds.

</Aside>

## 5. Pythagoras in Circles: Chords and Distances

A **chord** is a straight line segment whose endpoints lie on the circle. Pythagoras' theorem frequently appears in circle problems due to a very specific and important circle property.

<Aside type="note" title="Crucial Circle Property">

A perpendicular line drawn from the **centre** of a circle to a **chord** will always cut that chord exactly in half (bisect it). Drawing the radius from the centre to the end of the chord forms a **right-angled triangle**, allowing you to use Pythagoras' theorem!

</Aside>

### Example 4: Finding the Length of a Chord
A circle has a radius of **13 cm**. A chord is located at a perpendicular distance of **5 cm** from the centre of the circle. Calculate the exact length of the chord.

<Steps>

1. **Set up the right-angled triangle:**
   Let $x$ be half the length of the chord. The hypotenuse is the radius ($c = 13$). The shorter side is the distance from the centre ($a = 5$).
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   a^2 + x^2 &= c^2 \\
   5^2 + x^2 &= 13^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Solve for half the chord ($x$):**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   25 + x^2 &= 169 \\
   x^2 &= 169 - 25 \\
   &= 144 \\
   x &= \sqrt{144} \\
   &= 12 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Find the full chord length:**
   Because the perpendicular bisects the chord, the full length is twice the value of $x$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \text{Full Chord} &= 2 \times 12 \\
   &= 24 \text{ cm}
   \end{aligned}
   $$

</Steps>

## 6. The Converse of Pythagoras' Theorem

The **converse** of Pythagoras' theorem works backwards to check if an unknown triangle has a right angle. If you know the lengths of all three sides of a triangle, you can test if it is a right-angled triangle:

* **If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$**, then the triangle **IS** a right-angled triangle.
* **If $a^2 + b^2 \neq c^2$**, then the triangle **IS NOT** a right-angled triangle.

*(Always ensure $c$ is the longest side when setting up your test!)*

### Example 5: Checking for a Right Angle
A triangle has side lengths of **7 cm**, **24 cm**, and **25 cm**. Is this a right-angled triangle?

<Steps>

1. **Identify the longest side:** The longest side is **25 cm**, so this must be $c$.
2. **Calculate the sum of the squares of the shorter sides:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   a^2 + b^2 &= 7^2 + 24^2 \\
   &= 49 + 576 \\
   &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Calculate the square of the longest side:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   c^2 &= 25^2 \\
   &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
4. **Compare the results:**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   625 &= 625
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   Since $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, this **is** a right-angled triangle.

</Steps>

## 7. Classifying Triangles on a Grid

By calculating the distances between three coordinate points, we can determine the exact side lengths of a triangle. Once we have the side lengths, we can classify the triangle based on its properties:

* **Equilateral:** All three sides are equal.
* **Isosceles:** Exactly two sides are equal.
* **Scalene:** All three sides are different lengths.
* **Right-angled:** The sides satisfy $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.

### Example 6: Classifying by Coordinates
The points $A(2, 2)$, $B(5, 6)$, and $C(8, 2)$ form a triangle. Find the exact lengths of the three sides and classify the triangle.

<Steps>

1. **Find the length of $AB$ using Pythagoras:**
   The horizontal distance is $5 - 2 = 3$. The vertical distance is $6 - 2 = 4$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   AB^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\
   &= 9 + 16 \\
   &= 25 \\
   AB &= \sqrt{25} \\
   &= 5 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Find the length of $BC$ using Pythagoras:**
   The horizontal distance is $8 - 5 = 3$. The vertical distance is $6 - 2 = 4$.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   BC^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\
   &= 9 + 16 \\
   &= 25 \\
   BC &= \sqrt{25} \\
   &= 5 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
3. **Find the length of $AC$ (Horizontal Line):**
   Because $A$ and $C$ are on the same $y$-level, we can just subtract their $x$-coordinates.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   AC &= 8 - 2 \\
   &= 6 \text{ units}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
4. **Classify the Triangle:**
   Because two sides are equal in length ($AB = 5$ and $BC = 5$), the triangle is **Isosceles**.

</Steps>
