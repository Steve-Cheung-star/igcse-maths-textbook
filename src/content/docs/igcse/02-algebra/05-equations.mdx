An equation is a mathematical statement that two expressions are equal. Solving an equation means finding the value(s) of the unknown variable that make the statement true. This section covers everything from basic linear equations to complex algebraic fractions and using your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC).

---

## 1. Linear Equations

Linear equations are equations where the highest power of the unknown variable is $1$ (e.g., $x$, not $x^2$). The goal is to isolate the variable on one side of the equals sign using inverse operations.

### Basic Linear Equations
**Example 1:** Solve $3x + 4 = 10$
$$
\begin{aligned}
3x &= 10 - 4 \\
3x &= 6 \\
x &= 2
\end{aligned}
$$

### Equations with Brackets
Expand the brackets first, then collect like terms.

**Example 2:** Solve $5 - 2x = 3(x + 7)$
$$
\begin{aligned}
5 - 2x &= 3x + 21
\end{aligned}
$$
*Move all $x$ terms to one side, and numbers to the other. To avoid negative coefficients, add $2x$ to both sides:*
$$
\begin{aligned}
5 - 21 &= 3x + 2x \\
-16 &= 5x \\
x &= -\frac{16}{5} \quad \text{(or } -3.2\text{)}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 2. Fractional Equations

Equations with fractions require you to eliminate the denominators. You can do this by finding a common denominator or using cross-multiplication (if it is a single fraction equal to a single fraction).

### Method 1: Cross-Multiplication
Use this when you have one fraction strictly equal to another.

**Example:** Solve $\frac{x}{2} = \frac{2x + 1}{3}$

Multiply the numerator of one side by the denominator of the other:
$$
\begin{aligned}
3(x) &= 2(2x + 1) \\
3x &= 4x + 2 \\
-2 &= x
\end{aligned}
$$

<Aside type="caution" title="Variables in the Denominator">
The same cross-multiplication rule applies even if the variable is in the denominator!

Solve $\frac{x}{x + 2} = \frac{4}{x - 6}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
x(x - 6) &= 4(x + 2) \\
x^2 - 6x &= 4x + 8 \\
x^2 - 10x - 8 &= 0 \quad \text{(This forms a quadratic equation to solve later!)}
\end{aligned}
$$
</Aside>

### Method 2: Finding a Common Denominator
Use this when adding or subtracting multiple fractions in an equation.

**Example:** Solve $\frac{x + 2}{3} + \frac{2x - 1}{2} = 1$

The Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of 3 and 2 is 6. Multiply **every single term** in the equation by 6 to eliminate the fractions.

$$
\begin{aligned}
6 \times \left(\frac{x + 2}{3}\right) + 6 \times \left(\frac{2x - 1}{2}\right) &= 6 \times (1) \\
2(x + 2) + 3(2x - 1) &= 6 \\
2x + 4 + 6x - 3 &= 6 \\
8x + 1 &= 6 \\
8x &= 5 \\
x &= \frac{5}{8}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Simultaneous Equations

Simultaneous equations involve two or more equations with two or more unknowns (usually $x$ and $y$). You must find the pair of values that makes **both** equations true at the same time. The most common algebraic methods are **Elimination** and **Substitution**.

**Example (Elimination):** Solve the simultaneous equations:

1) $3x + 2y = 12$
2) $4x - y = 5$

*Step 1: Match the coefficients of one variable. Let's multiply equation (2) by 2 to match the $y$ terms.*

(2) $\times 2 \Rightarrow 8x - 2y = 10$

*Step 2: Add or subtract the equations to eliminate $y$.*
$$
\begin{aligned}
(3x + 2y) + (8x - 2y) &= 12 + 10 \\
11x &= 22 \\
x &= 2
\end{aligned}
$$

*Step 3: Substitute $x = 2$ back into either original equation to find $y$.*
$$
\begin{aligned}
3(2) + 2y &= 12 \\
6 + 2y &= 12 \\
2y &= 6 \\
y &= 3
\end{aligned}
$$

**Solution:** $x = 2$, $y = 3$.

## 4. Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation takes the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$. Depending on the equation, you can solve it via factorisation, the quadratic formula, or your GDC.

### Solving by Factorisation
If the expression factorises nicely, use the **Zero Product Property** (If $ab = 0$, then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$).

**Example:** Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$

$$
\begin{aligned}
(x - 2)(x - 3) &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore, either $x - 2 = 0$ or $x - 3 = 0$.

**Solution:** $x = 2$ or $x = 3$.

### The Quadratic Formula
If an equation does not factorise easily, use the quadratic formula (this is provided in your exam).

$$
x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
$$

**Example:** Solve $2x^2 - 5x - 4 = 0$. Give your answers exact in surd form.

**Step 1: Identify $a$, $b$, and $c$.**

Here, $a = 2$, $b = -5$, and $c = -4$. *(Always take the sign in front of the number!)*

**Step 2: Substitute carefully into the formula.**

$$
\begin{aligned}
x &= \frac{-(-5) \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(2)(-4)}}{2(2)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 3: Simplify the discriminant (the part under the root) and denominator.**

$$
\begin{aligned}
x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 - (-32)}}{4} \\
x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 + 32}}{4} \\
x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{57}}{4}
\end{aligned}
$$

<SteveTip title="Exact Surd Form">
Sometimes the exam will ask for the exact answer. This means you must leave your answer containing the square root (surd form), rather than a rounded decimal like $3.12$. 
</SteveTip>

## 5. Changing the Subject of a Formula

Changing the subject means rearranging a formula so that a different variable is isolated on its own on the left-hand side.

### Dealing with Powers and Roots
Use the inverse operation to remove powers or roots.

**Example 1:** Make $x$ the subject of $y = \sqrt{x + 2}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
y^2 &= x + 2 \quad \text{(Square both sides)} \\
y^2 - 2 &= x \quad \text{(Subtract 2)} \\
x &= y^2 - 2
\end{aligned}
$$

### The Subject Appears Twice (Extended)
If the letter you want to make the subject appears more than once, you **must factorise** to isolate it.

**Example 2:** Make $x$ the subject of $y = \frac{2x + 1}{x - 3}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
y(x - 3) &= 2x + 1 \quad \text{(Multiply by the denominator)} \\
yx - 3y &= 2x + 1 \quad \text{(Expand the bracket)}
\end{aligned}
$$

*Group all terms containing $x$ on one side, and everything else on the other:*

$$
\begin{aligned}
yx - 2x &= 3y + 1 
\end{aligned}
$$

*Factorise out the $x$:*

$$
\begin{aligned}
x(y - 2) &= 3y + 1 \\
x &= \frac{3y + 1}{y - 2} \quad \text{(Divide by the bracket)}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 6. Solving Equations with a GDC

For complex or "unfamiliar" equations, your Graphic Display Calculator is your best friend.

**Example:** Solve $2x - 1 = \frac{1}{x^3}$ using a GDC.

This is highly complex to solve algebraically, but very easy graphically.

**Method 1: Intersection**
1. Enter $y_1 = 2x - 1$
2. Enter $y_2 = \frac{1}{x^3}$
3. Graph both functions.
4. Use `[Menu] → [Analyse Graph] → [Intersection]` to find the $x$-coordinate where the two graphs cross.

**Method 2: Finding Zeros (Roots)**
1. Rearrange the equation to equal zero: $2x - 1 - \frac{1}{x^3} = 0$
2. Enter this entire expression into $y_1$.
3. Graph the function.
4. Use `[Menu] → [Analyse Graph] → [Zero]` to find the $x$-intercepts. The $x$-values are your solutions!

    A boat travels 24 km upstream in 2 hours, and 24 km downstream in 1.5 hours. Let $v$ be the boat's speed in still water and $c$ be the speed of the current. Form two equations and solve for $v$ and $c$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 22">
    Upstream speed = $(v - c)$. Downstream speed = $(v + c)$. 
    
    $\text{Speed} = \text{Distance} \div \text{Time}$.
    
    Upstream Eq: 
    
    $$v - c = \frac{24}{2} \Rightarrow v - c = 12$$
    
    Downstream Eq: 
    
    $$v + c = \frac{24}{1.5} \Rightarrow v + c = 16$$
    
    Add equations together to eliminate $c$: 
    
    $$(v - c) + (v + c) = 12 + 16$$
    
    $$2v = 28$$
    
    $$v = 14$$
    
    Substitute $v=14$ into the downstream equation: 
    
    $$14 + c = 16$$
    
    $$c = 2$$
    
    **Answer:** Boat speed = $\boldsymbol{14 \text{ km/h}}$, Current = $\boldsymbol{2 \text{ km/h}}$.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 23: Polygon Formula">
    The number of diagonals, $D$, in a polygon with $n$ sides is given by the formula $D = \frac{n(n-3)}{2}$. A certain polygon has 35 diagonals. Find the number of sides it has, taking care to discard any mathematically invalid solutions.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 23">
    Substitute $D = 35$ into the formula:
    
    $$35 = \frac{n(n-3)}{2}$$
    
    Multiply by 2: 
    
    $$70 = n^2 - 3n$$
    
    Rearrange: 
    
    $$n^2 - 3n - 70 = 0$$
    
    Factorise: 
    
    $$(n - 10)(n + 7) = 0$$
    
    This gives $n = 10$ or $n = -7$.
    
    *Extraneous solution:* A polygon cannot have a negative number of sides ($-7$).
    
    **Answer:** The polygon has $\boldsymbol{10}$ sides.
  </TabItem>
