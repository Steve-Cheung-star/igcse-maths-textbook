While an equation tells you that two expressions are exactly equal, an **inequality** tells you how two expressions compare in size. Instead of a single answer, solving an inequality usually gives you a *range* of possible values.

---

## 1. Inequalities on a Number Line

We use four main inequality symbols. When graphing these on a number line, we use different types of circles to show whether the value itself is included.

* **Strict Inequalities:** Use an **open circle** ($\circ$)
    * $<$ (Less than)
    * $>$ (Greater than)
* **Inclusive Inequalities:** Use a **closed circle** ($\bullet$)
    * $\leqslant$ (Less than or equal to)
    * $\geqslant$ (Greater than or equal to)

### Examples

## 2. Solving Linear Inequalities

You can solve linear inequalities using the exact same methods you use for linear equations (expanding brackets, collecting like terms, moving variables to one side). 

However, there is **one crucial rule** you must never forget:

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rule of Inequalities">
If you multiply or divide an inequality by a **negative number**, you MUST flip the inequality sign!

For example, when dividing by -2:
$$
\begin{aligned}
  -2x &< 10 \\[1ex]
    x &> -5 
\end{aligned}
$$
*(Notice how the sign vertically flipped from $<$ to $>$!)*
</SteveTip>

### Basic Linear Inequalities
**Example 1:** Solve $3x < 2x + 4$
$$
\begin{aligned}
3x - 2x &< 4 \\
x &< 4
\end{aligned}
$$

### Compound Inequalities
Sometimes an inequality has three parts. Whatever you do to the middle, you must do to *both* the left and the right sides.

**Example 2:** Solve $-3 \leqslant 3x - 2 < 7$

*Step 1: Add 2 to all three parts to isolate the $x$ term in the middle.*
$$
\begin{aligned}
-3 + 2 &\leqslant 3x < 7 + 2 \\
-1 &\leqslant 3x < 9
\end{aligned}
$$

*Step 2: Divide all three parts by 3.*
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\frac{1}{3} &\leqslant x < 3
\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Inequalities in Two Variables (Graphs)

Inequalities involving both $x$ and $y$ (like $y > x$ or $x + y \leqslant 4$) represent an entire **region** on a Cartesian plane, rather than just a segment on a number line.

### Drawing the Boundary Line
First, treat the inequality as an equation (e.g., $y = x$) and draw this line. The type of line you draw depends on the symbol:
* **Broken/Dashed lines ($---\!-$):** Used for strict inequalities ($<$ or $>$).
* **Solid lines (—):** Used for inclusive inequalities ($\leqslant$ or $\geqslant$).

### Shading the Region
Once the line is drawn, it splits the graph into two sides. You must shade one side.

<Aside type="caution" title="IGCSE Shading Convention">
In IGCSE Mathematics, the strict convention is to **shade the UNWANTED region**. 
This leaves the correct region "clear" or blank. Always double-check if the exam question says "shade the unwanted region" to be completely sure.
</Aside>

**How to decide which side to shade (the "unwanted" side):**
1.  **Gradient-Intercept Form ($y = mx + c$):**
    * If the required region is $y > mx + c$, the *wanted* region is **above** the line. You should shade **below** the line.
    * If the required region is $y < mx + c$, the *wanted* region is **below** the line. You should shade **above** the line.
2.  **Test a Point:**
    * Pick a coordinate not on the line, usually (0,0) is easiest.
    * Substitute $x=0, y=0$ into the inequality.
    * If the statement is TRUE, (0,0) is in the *wanted* region. Shade the other side.
    * If the statement is FALSE, (0,0) is in the *unwanted* region. Shade the side containing (0,0).

**Example:** Graph the region $y \leqslant 4 - x$ (or $x + y \leqslant 4$)
1.  Draw the line $y = 4 - x$. Because it is $\leqslant$, use a **solid line**.
2.  Test (0,0): $0 \leqslant 4 - 0 \Rightarrow 0 \leqslant 4$. This is TRUE.
3.  The origin (0,0) is in the wanted region. Therefore, **shade the side away from the origin** (above the line).

## 4. Solving Inequalities using a GDC

Your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC) can quickly find graphical regions or solve complex linear inequalities.

**Graphing Inequality Regions:**
1.  On the graph screen, delete the `=` sign in the `f1(x)=` entry line.
2.  A menu will appear allowing you to select $\leqslant, <, \geqslant,$ or $>$.
3.  Type your expression (e.g., `f1(x) > 2x - 3`).
4.  The calculator will automatically draw the correct line (dashed or solid) and shade the region. 
    *(Note: Calculators usually shade the **wanted** region by default, so remember to interpret this carefully against the IGCSE "shade unwanted" convention!)*

  </TabItem>
