## 1. Letters as Generalised Numbers (Variables)

In arithmetic, we deal with specific, known numbers (like 5, 12, or -3). In **algebra**, we use letters to represent numbers that are either unknown or can change. These letters are called **variables**.

Using variables allows us to write general rules, formulae, and expressions that work for *any* number.

### The "Invisible" Maths Rules (Algebraic Notation)
To make algebra cleaner and faster to read, mathematicians drop certain symbols. You must memorise these shorthand rules:

* **Multiplication is invisible:** Instead of writing $3 \times a$, we write $\boldsymbol{3a}$. The number always goes *before* the letter.
* **Multiplying letters:** Instead of $a \times b$, we write $\boldsymbol{ab}$. Usually, letters are written in alphabetical order.
* **Division is a fraction:** The $\div$ symbol is rarely used in algebra. Instead of $x \div 5$, we write $\boldsymbol{\frac{x}{5}}$.
* **The invisible "1":** If you have just one $x$, we write $\boldsymbol{x}$, not $1x$. Similarly, $-1y$ is written as $\boldsymbol{-y}$.
* **Repeated multiplication:** $x \times x$ is written using indices as $\boldsymbol{x^2}$.

## 2. Writing Algebraic Expressions

An **expression** is a mathematical phrase made up of numbers, variables, and operation symbols ($+$, $-$, $\times$, $\div$). It does *not* contain an equals sign ($=$).

You will often need to translate word problems into algebraic expressions. Look for keywords:
* **Addition (+):** sum, more than, increased by, total. *(e.g., "5 more than $x$" $\rightarrow x + 5$)*
* **Subtraction (-):** difference, less than, decreased by. *(e.g., "3 less than $y$" $\rightarrow y - 3$)*
* **Multiplication ($\times$):** product, times, double ($2\times$), triple ($3\times$). *(e.g., "double $m$" $\rightarrow 2m$)*
* **Division ($\div$):** quotient, half ($\div 2$), shared equally. *(e.g., "half of $p$" $\rightarrow \frac{p}{2}$)*

**Examples:**
* "$x$ multiplied by 4, then subtract 7" $\rightarrow \boldsymbol{4x - 7}$
* "The sum of $a$ and $b$, divided by 3" $\rightarrow \boldsymbol{\frac{a + b}{3}}$

## 3. Substitution

**Substitution** is the process of replacing variables (letters) in an expression or formula with specific numbers to calculate a final value. 

Think of an algebraic expression as a recipe, and substitution as plugging in the specific ingredients you have today.

### The Golden Rules of Substitution:
1. **Always use brackets** when replacing a letter with a number, *especially* if the number is negative or a fraction.
2. Follow **BIDMAS / BODMAS** (Brackets, Indices, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction) when calculating the final answer.

### Example A: Basic Substitution
Evaluate the expression $5x - 2y$ when $x = 4$ and $y = 3$.
1. Substitute the numbers using brackets: $5(4) - 2(3)$
2. Multiply: $20 - 6$
3. Subtract: $\boldsymbol{14}$

### Example B: Substituting Negative Numbers
Evaluate $a^2 - 4b$ when $a = -3$ and $b = -2$.
1. Substitute using brackets: $(-3)^2 - 4(-2)$
2. Evaluate indices: $(-3)^2 = -3 \times -3 = 9$. The expression becomes: $9 - 4(-2)$
3. Multiply: $-4 \times -2 = +8$. The expression becomes: $9 + 8$
4. Add: $\boldsymbol{17}$

<SteveTip title="Exam Trick: The Calculator Bracket Trap">
Calculators are stupid if you don't use brackets for certain numbers! 

**1. Negative Numbers:**
If you type `-3^2` into a calculator, it will calculate $-(3 \times 3) = -9$. 
But mathematically, substituting $x = -3$ into $x^2$ means $(-3) \times (-3)$, which is **$+9$**.
*Always type `(-3)^2` into your calculator to get the correct positive answer.*

**2. Fractions:**
Fractional bases must also be inside brackets! If you substitute $x = \frac{2}{3}$ into $x^2$ and type `2/3^2` into your calculator, it only squares the 3, giving you $\frac{2}{9}$. 
*Always type `(2/3)^2` to correctly square the entire fraction and get **$\frac{4}{9}$**.*

*(Note: Positive whole numbers and decimals are perfectly fine without brackets. Typing `5^2` or `2.5^2` will work exactly as expected!)*
</SteveTip>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Translating Words to Expressions">
    Write an algebraic expression for each of the following statements:
    * **a)** Multiply $p$ by 6, then add $q$.
    * **b)** The sum of $a$ and $b$, divided by 3.
    * **c)** The square of $x$, divided by the difference between $y$ and $z$.
    * **d)** 5 less than double $m$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    * **a)** "Multiply $p$ by 6" is $6p$. Then add $q$. Answer: $\boldsymbol{6p + q}$
    * **b)** The sum is $a + b$. "Divided by" means setting it up as a fraction. Answer: $\boldsymbol{\frac{a + b}{3}}$
    * **c)** The square of $x$ is $x^2$. The difference is $y - z$. Answer: $\boldsymbol{\frac{x^2}{y - z}}$
    * **d)** Double $m$ is $2m$. "5 less than" means you take 5 away from it. Answer: $\boldsymbol{2m - 5}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
