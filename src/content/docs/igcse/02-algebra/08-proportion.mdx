<Aside type="note">
In mathematics, **proportion** (or variation) describes how one variable changes in relation to another. We use the proportional symbol **$\propto$** to show this relationship. 

To solve proportion problems, we always replace the $\propto$ symbol with an equals sign ($=$) and a **constant of proportionality**, usually represented by the letter **$k$**.
</Aside>

## 1. Translating Proportionality Statements

Exams will use specific phrasing to tell you exactly what power or root to use in your equation. You must memorise these translations before you can set up your algebra:

| English Phrasing | Algebraic Translation | Proportionality Equation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "...is proportional to $x$" | $x$ | $y = kx$ |
| "...the **square** of $x$" | $x^2$ | $y = kx^2$ |
| "...the **cube** of $x$" | $x^3$ | $y = kx^3$ |
| "...the **square root** of $x$" | $\sqrt{x}$ | $y = k\sqrt{x}$ |
| "...the **cube root** of $x$" | $\sqrt[3]{x}$ | $y = k\sqrt[3]{x}$ |

<SteveTip title="The Golden Rule of Proportion">
  Every standard proportion problem follows the exact same three steps:
  <Steps>
  1. **Set it up:** Write the equation with $k$ using the correct translation from the table above.
  2. **Find $k$:** Substitute the known pair of values to calculate your constant $k$.
  3. **Solve:** Rewrite the full equation with your new $k$ value, then use it to find the final unknown quantity.
  </Steps>
</SteveTip>

## 2. Direct Proportion



If two quantities are in **direct proportion**, as one increases, the other increases at the same relative rate. Graphically, standard direct proportion ($y = kx$) is a straight line passing through the origin $(0,0)$.

### Algebraic Direct Proportion
Variables aren't always proportional to just $x$. They can be proportional to powers or roots of $x$, as shown in the translation table.

**Example:** $y$ is directly proportional to the square of $x$. When $x = 3$, $y = 36$. Find the value of $y$ when $x = 5$.

<Steps>
1. **Set it up**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   y &\propto x^2 \\
   y &= kx^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Find $k$**
   Substitute $x = 3$ and $y = 36$:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   36 &= k(3)^2 \\
   36 &= 9k \\
   k &= 4
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   So, the exact formula connecting $x$ and $y$ is **$y = 4x^2$**.
3. **Solve for the unknown**
   Substitute $x = 5$ into your new formula:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   y &= 4(5)^2 \\
   y &= 4(25) \\
   y &= 100
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

### Direct Proportion in Word Problems
Real-world scenarios often use direct proportion. Common examples include currency conversion (more USD = more EUR), or mass and volume (more volume of a liquid = more mass). 

**Example:** The distance a heavy ball falls ($d$) is directly proportional to the square of the time ($t$) it has been falling. If it falls 20 m in 2 seconds, how far does it fall in 5 seconds?

<Steps>
1. **Set up**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   d &\propto t^2 \\
   d &= kt^2
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Find $k$**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   20 &= k(2)^2 \\
   20 &= 4k \\
   k &= 5
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   The equation is $d = 5t^2$.
3. **Solve**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   d &= 5(5)^2 \\
   d &= 5(25) \\
   d &= 125
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   The ball falls **125 m**.
</Steps>

## 3. Inverse Proportion



If two quantities are in **inverse proportion**, as one increases, the other *decreases* at a proportional rate. We express this by making the variable a denominator (the reciprocal). Graphically, inverse proportion creates a curve that approaches, but never touches, the axes (an asymptote).

* **Standard Inverse:** $y \propto \frac{1}{x}$ which becomes $y = \frac{k}{x}$
* **Inverse Square:** $y \propto \frac{1}{x^2}$ which becomes $y = \frac{k}{x^2}$

### Algebraic Inverse Proportion
**Example:** $p$ is inversely proportional to the square root of $q$. When $q = 25$, $p = 4$. Find the value of $q$ when $p = 10$.

<Steps>
1. **Set it up**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   p &\propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{q}} \\
   p &= \frac{k}{\sqrt{q}}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Find $k$**
   Substitute $q = 25$ and $p = 4$:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   4 &= \frac{k}{\sqrt{25}} \\
   4 &= \frac{k}{5} \\
   k &= 20
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   The exact formula is **$p = \frac{20}{\sqrt{q}}$**.
3. **Solve for the unknown**
   Substitute $p = 10$:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   10 &= \frac{20}{\sqrt{q}} \\
   \sqrt{q} &= \frac{20}{10} \\
   \sqrt{q} &= 2
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   Square both sides to find $q$:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   q &= 2^2 \\
   q &= 4
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

### Inverse Proportion Word Problems (The "Man-Power" Rule)
The most common real-world application of inverse proportion is "Work and Time" problems involving man-power or speed. 

<Aside type="caution">
  **Common Misconception:** Students often try to apply direct proportion to speed/time or worker/time scenarios. If 2 builders take 10 days, 4 builders will NOT take 20 days. They will take 5 days. Always check if the relationship is "more is less" before calculating.
</Aside>

<SteveTip title="The More-is-Less Logic">
  Think logically about the scenario: If you have **more** workers on a job, it will take **less** time to finish it. If you drive at a **higher** speed, it will take **less** time to reach your destination. This "more is less" relationship is the definition of **inverse proportion**.
</SteveTip>

**Example:** A team of 5 decorators can finish painting a house in 12 days. Assuming they all work at the exact same rate, how many decorators are needed to finish the house in 4 days?

<Steps>
1. **Set up**
   Time ($T$) is inversely proportional to Workers ($W$). 
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   T &= \frac{k}{W}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
2. **Find $k$**
   Here, $k$ represents the total number of "man-days" required for the whole job.
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   12 &= \frac{k}{5} \\
   k &= 60
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   The equation is $T = \frac{60}{W}$.
3. **Solve**
   We need the job done in $T = 4$ days. 
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   4 &= \frac{60}{W} \\
   4W &= 60 \\
   W &= 15
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   You will need **15 decorators**.
</Steps>

## 4. Linking Multiple Proportionality Models

In harder higher-tier questions, you may be given two separate proportionality relationships that share a common variable. You will need to find the equations for both separately, and then substitute them into a combined algebraic expression. 

These questions heavily test your ability to simplify algebraic fractions, use the rules of indices, and manipulate surds.

### Example: Combining and Simplifying
**Question:** $x$ is directly proportional to the square of $w$. When $w = 2$, $x = 12$.
$y$ is inversely proportional to the fourth power of $w$. When $w = 2$, $y = 3$.
Find an expression for $\sqrt{xy}$ in its simplest form, in terms of $w$.

<Steps>
1. **Find the equation for $x$**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   x &= k_1 w^2 \\
   12 &= k_1 (2)^2 \\
   12 &= 4k_1 \\
   k_1 &= 3
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   **Equation 1:** $x = 3w^2$
2. **Find the equation for $y$**
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   y &= \frac{k_2}{w^4} \\
   3 &= \frac{k_2}{2^4} \\
   3 &= \frac{k_2}{16} \\
   k_2 &= 48
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   **Equation 2:** $y = \frac{48}{w^4}$
3. **Substitute both into the required expression and simplify**
   We need to find $xy$. First, find $xy$:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   xy &= (3w^2) \times \left(\frac{48}{w^4}\right) \\[1ex]
   xy &= \frac{144w^2}{w^4} \\[1ex]
   xy &= \frac{144}{w^2}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
   Now, take the square root of the whole expression. Remember to square root both the numerator and the denominator:
   $$
   \begin{aligned}
   \sqrt{xy} &= \sqrt{\frac{144}{w^2}} \\[1ex]
   \sqrt{xy} &= \frac{12}{w}
   \end{aligned}
   $$
</Steps>

## 5. Identifying the Best Variation Model

Sometimes, instead of being told the relationship, you are given a table of data and asked to find the model that fits. 

<Aside type="tip">
To do this, you test different formulas to see which one produces a **constant value for $k$** across all pairs of data.
* If $y = kx$, then **$k = \frac{y}{x}$** (Direct Linear)
* If $y = kx^2$, then **$k = \frac{y}{x^2}$** (Direct Square)
* If $y = \frac{k}{x}$, then **$k = yx$** (Inverse Linear)
</Aside>

### Example: Finding the Model from Data
Identify the correct variation model for the data below:

| $x$ | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $y$ | 12 | 27 | 48 |

Let's test the data against different models to see which gives a constant $k$.

**Test 1: Linear ($k = \frac{y}{x}$)**
* $12 \div 2 = 6$
* $27 \div 3 = 9$ 

This is not constant. The relationship is NOT $y = kx$.

**Test 2: Square ($k = \frac{y}{x^2}$)**
* $12 \div 2^2 = 12 \div 4 = 3$
* $27 \div 3^2 = 27 \div 9 = 3$
* $48 \div 4^2 = 48 \div 16 = 3$

Because $\frac{y}{x^2}$ always equals $3$, the constant of proportionality is $k = 3$. 
The correct variation model is **$y \propto x^2$** (or **$y = 3x^2$**).

    Determine the relationship between $x$ and $y$ from the following tables, giving your answer in the form $y = f(x)$.  

    a)  
    | $x$ | 2 | 4 | 5 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 10 | 5 | 4 |  

    b)  
    | $x$ | 1 | 3 | 5 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 4 | 36 | 100 |  

    c)  
    | $x$ | 2 | 5 | 8 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 14 | 35 | 56 |  

    d)  
    | $x$ | 2 | 3 | 4 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 18 | 8 | 4.5 |  

    e)  
    | $x$ | 1 | 8 | 27 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 5 | 10 | 15 |  

    f)  
    | $x$ | 3 | 6 | 9 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | $y$ | 12 | 24 | 36 |  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="ã Solution 5">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $xy = 2 \times 10 = 20$, $4 \times 5 = 20$, $5 \times 4 = 20$.  
      2. Since $xy$ is constant, this is inverse linear proportion. Equation: **$y = \frac{20}{x}$**
      </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $y \div x^2 = 4 \div 1^2 = 4$, $36 \div 3^2 = 4$, $100 \div 5^2 = 4$.  
      2. Since $y \div x^2$ is constant, this is direct square proportion. Equation: **$y = 4x^2$**
      </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $y \div x = 14 \div 2 = 7$, $35 \div 5 = 7$, $56 \div 8 = 7$.  
      2. Since $y \div x$ is constant, this is direct linear proportion. Equation: **$y = 7x$**
      </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $y \times x^2 = 18 \times 2^2 = 72$, $8 \times 3^2 = 72$, $4.5 \times 4^2 = 72$.  
      2. Since $yx^2$ is constant, this is inverse square proportion. Equation: **$y = \frac{72}{x^2}$** 
      </Steps>

    **e)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $y \div \sqrt[3]{x} = 5 \div 1 = 5$, $10 \div 2 = 5$, $15 \div 3 = 5$.  
      2. Since $y \div \sqrt[3]{x}$ is constant, this is direct cube root proportion. Equation: **$y = 5\sqrt[3]{x}$** 
      </Steps>

    **f)**
    <Steps>
      1. Test for constant $k$: $y \div x = 12 \div 3 = 4$, $24 \div 6 = 4$, $36 \div 9 = 4$.  
      2. Since $y \div x$ is constant, this is direct linear proportion. Equation: **$y = 4x$** 
      </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Þ Question 6 (Extended/Hard)">
    Combine proportionality models to find single expressions:  

    a) $a$ is directly proportional to the cube of $b$. When $b = 2$, $a = 24$. $c$ is inversely proportional to $b$. When $b = 3$, $c = 2$. Find an expression for $\frac{a}{c}$ in terms of $b$. Give your answer in its simplest form.  

    b) $x$ is directly proportional to $y^2$. When $y = 3$, $x = 18$. $z$ is directly proportional to $y$. When $y = 4$, $z = 20$. Find an expression for $xz$ in terms of $y$.  

    c) $p$ is inversely proportional to $q^2$. When $q = 2$, $p = 5$. $r$ is inversely proportional to $q$. When $q = 5$, $r = 2$. Find an expression for $\frac{p}{r}$ in terms of $q$.  

    d) $m$ is directly proportional to $\sqrt{n}$. When $n = 16$, $m = 12$. $w$ is inversely proportional to $\sqrt{n}$. When $n = 9$, $w = 2$. Find an expression for $mw$ in terms of $n$.  

    e) $u$ is directly proportional to $v^3$. When $v = 2$, $u = 40$. $t$ is directly proportional to $v^2$. When $v = 3$, $t = 27$. Find an expression for $\frac{u}{t}$ in terms of $v$.  

    f) $g$ is inversely proportional to $h^3$. When $h = 2$, $g = 3$. $f$ is directly proportional to $h^4$. When $h = 2$, $f = 32$. Find an expression for $gf$ in terms of $h$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="ã Solution 6">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $a$: $a = k_1 b^3 \implies 24 = k_1(8) \implies k_1 = 3 \implies a = 3b^3$  
      2. Find $c$: $c = \frac{k_2}{b} \implies 2 = \frac{k_2}{3} \implies k_2 = 6 \implies c = \frac{6}{b}$  
      3. Combine: $\frac{a}{c} = \frac{3b^3}{\frac{6}{b}} = 3b^3 \times \frac{b}{6} = \frac{3b^4}{6} = \frac{b^4}{2}$  
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $x$: $x = k_1 y^2 \implies 18 = k_1(9) \implies k_1 = 2 \implies x = 2y^2$  
      2. Find $z$: $z = k_2 y \implies 20 = k_2(4) \implies k_2 = 5 \implies z = 5y$  
      3. Combine: $xz = (2y^2)(5y) = 10y^3$  
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $p$: $p = \frac{k_1}{q^2} \implies 5 = \frac{k_1}{4} \implies k_1 = 20 \implies p = \frac{20}{q^2}$  
      2. Find $r$: $r = \frac{k_2}{q} \implies 2 = \frac{k_2}{5} \implies k_2 = 10 \implies r = \frac{10}{q}$  
      3. Combine: $\frac{p}{r} = \frac{\frac{20}{q^2}}{\frac{10}{q}} = \frac{20}{q^2} \times \frac{q}{10} = \frac{2}{q}$  
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $m$: $m = k_1 \sqrt{n} \implies 12 = k_1(4) \implies k_1 = 3 \implies m = 3\sqrt{n}$  
      2. Find $w$: $w = \frac{k_2}{\sqrt{n}} \implies 2 = \frac{k_2}{3} \implies k_2 = 6 \implies w = \frac{6}{\sqrt{n}}$  
      3. Combine: $mw = (3\sqrt{n}) \times \left(\frac{6}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 18$  
    </Steps>

    **e)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $u$: $u = k_1 v^3 \implies 40 = k_1(8) \implies k_1 = 5 \implies u = 5v^3$  
      2. Find $t$: $t = k_2 v^2 \implies 27 = k_2(9) \implies k_2 = 3 \implies t = 3v^2$  
      3. Combine: $\frac{u}{t} = \frac{5v^3}{3v^2} = \frac{5v}{3}$  
    </Steps>

    **f)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find $g$: $g = \frac{k_1}{h^3} \implies 3 = \frac{k_1}{8} \implies k_1 = 24 \implies g = \frac{24}{h^3}$  
      2. Find $f$: $f = k_2 h^4 \implies 32 = k_2(16) \implies k_2 = 2 \implies f = 2h^4$  
      3. Combine: $gf = \left(\frac{24}{h^3}\right)(2h^4) = 48h$  
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Þ Question 7 (Hard)">
    Calculate the percentage changes in these proportionality scenarios:  

    a) $y$ is directly proportional to the square of $x$. If the value of $x$ is increased by 20%, calculate the percentage increase in $y$.  

    b) $p$ is directly proportional to the cube of $q$. If $q$ is increased by 10%, calculate the percentage increase in $p$.  

    c) $m$ is inversely proportional to $n$. If $n$ is increased by 25%, calculate the percentage decrease in $m$.  

    d) $w$ is inversely proportional to the square of $v$. If $v$ is increased by 100%, calculate the percentage decrease in $w$.  

    e) $c$ is directly proportional to the square root of $d$. If $d$ is increased by 44%, calculate the percentage increase in $c$.  

    f) $a$ is inversely proportional to the square root of $b$. If $b$ is decreased by 36%, calculate the percentage increase in $a$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="ã Solution 7">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $y_1 = kx_1^2$  
      2. **New $x$:** $x_2 = 1.2x_1$  
      3. **Substitute:** $y_2 = k(1.2x_1)^2 = k(1.44x_1^2) = 1.44(kx_1^2)$  
      4. **Compare:** $y_2 = 1.44y_1$. A multiplier of 1.44 represents a **44% increase**.  
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $p_1 = kq_1^3$  
      2. **New $q$:** $q_2 = 1.1q_1$  
      3. **Substitute:** $p_2 = k(1.1q_1)^3 = k(1.331q_1^3) = 1.331(kq_1^3)$  
      4. **Compare:** $p_2 = 1.331p_1$. A multiplier of 1.331 represents a **33.1% increase**.  
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $m_1 = \frac{k}{n_1}$  
      2. **New $n$:** $n_2 = 1.25n_1$  
      3. **Substitute:** $m_2 = \frac{k}{1.25n_1} = \frac{1}{1.25} \times \left(\frac{k}{n_1}\right) = 0.8m_1$  
      4. **Compare:** A multiplier of 0.8 represents a **20% decrease**.  
    </Steps>

    **d)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $w_1 = \frac{k}{v_1^2}$  
      2. **New $v$:** $v_2 = 2v_1$ (100% increase means it doubles)  
      3. **Substitute:** $w_2 = \frac{k}{(2v_1)^2} = \frac{k}{4v_1^2} = 0.25w_1$  
      4. **Compare:** A multiplier of 0.25 represents a **75% decrease**.  
    </Steps>

    **e)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $c_1 = k\sqrt{d_1}$  
      2. **New $d$:** $d_2 = 1.44d_1$  
      3. **Substitute:** $c_2 = k\sqrt{1.44d_1} = 1.2k\sqrt{d_1} = 1.2c_1$  
      4. **Compare:** A multiplier of 1.2 represents a **20% increase**.  
    </Steps>

    **f)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Initial:** $a_1 = \frac{k}{\sqrt{b_1}}$  
      2. **New $b$:** $b_2 = 0.64b_1$ (100% - 36% = 64%)  
      3. **Substitute:** $a_2 = \frac{k}{\sqrt{0.64b_1}} = \frac{k}{0.8\sqrt{b_1}} = \frac{1}{0.8}a_1 = 1.25a_1$  
      4. **Compare:** A multiplier of 1.25 represents a **25% increase**.  
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="Þ Question 8 (Structured Context)">
    A spherical weather balloon's volume ($V$) is directly proportional to the cube of its radius ($r$). When the balloon is inflated to a radius of 3 m, its volume is $36\pi$ cubic meters.  
    
    a) Find the exact equation linking $V$ and $r$.  

    b) What is the volume of the balloon when the radius is increased to 5 m? Give your answer in terms of $\pi$.  

    c) A new safety regulation requires the balloon's volume not to exceed $288\pi$ cubic meters. What is the maximum possible radius of the balloon?  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="ã Solution 8">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Set up:** $V = kr^3$  
      2. **Find $k$:** $36\pi = k(3^3) \implies 36\pi = 27k \implies k = \frac{36\pi}{27} \implies k = \frac{4\pi}{3}$. Equation: **$V = \frac{4\pi}{3}r^3$** 
      </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Solve:** $V = \frac{4\pi}{3}(5^3) \implies V = \frac{4\pi}{3}(125) \implies V = \frac{500\pi}{3}$. The volume is **$\frac{500\pi}{3}$ cubic meters**.  
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Solve for r:** $288\pi = \frac{4\pi}{3}r^3$  
      2. **Rearrange:** $r^3 = \frac{288\pi \times 3}{4\pi} \implies r^3 = 72 \times 3 \implies r^3 = 216$  
      3. **Cube root:** $r = \sqrt[3]{216} \implies r = 6$. The maximum radius is **6 m**.  
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    <Steps>

    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Solve for T:** $40000 = \frac{16000000}{T^2}$  
      2. **Rearrange:** $T^2 = \frac{16000000}{40000} \implies T^2 = 400$  
      3. **Square root:** $T = \sqrt{400} \implies T = 20$. The maximum operating temperature is **20ô¯C**.  
    </Steps>
