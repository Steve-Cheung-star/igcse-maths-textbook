title: 2.7 Sequences
description: Recognise patterns, continue number sequences, and use the difference method to find the nth term for linear, quadratic, cubic, exponential, and rational sequences.
---

A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers (or sometimes patterns/shapes) that follow a specific mathematical rule. Each number in the sequence is called a **term**.

We often use **subscript notation** to refer to specific terms. For a sequence named $T$:
* $T_1$ is the 1st term.
* $T_2$ is the 2nd term.
* $T_n$ is the **$n$th term** (a general formula for any term in the sequence, where $n$ is the position).

---

## 1. Term-to-Term Rules

A **term-to-term rule** tells you how to get from one term to the very next term. While easy to spot, term-to-term rules aren't great if you want to find the 100th term, because you'd have to calculate all 99 terms before it!

**Example:** Find the next two terms and the term-to-term rule for the sequence: $5, 9, 13, 17, \dots$
* The pattern is adding $4$ each time.
* **Term-to-term rule:** Add $4$.
* **Next two terms:** $17 + 4 = 21$, and $21 + 4 = 25$.

---

## 2. Linear (Arithmetic) Sequences

A sequence is **linear** (or arithmetic) if it increases or decreases by a **constant amount** every time. This constant amount is called the **first difference**.

### Finding the $n$th Term
The $n$th term of a linear sequence always takes the form:
$$T_n = dn + c$$
Where **$d$** is the constant difference, and **$c$** is the "zeroth term" (the value that would come just before the 1st term).

**Example 1: Increasing Sequence**
Find the $n$th term of the sequence: $7, 10, 13, 16, 19, \dots$

3.  **Combine:** The $n$th term is $T_n = -4n + 19$.

## 3. Quadratic Sequences

A sequence is **quadratic** if the *first difference* changes, but the **second difference** is constant. 
The general formula is:
$$T_n = an^2 + bn + c$$

### The Algebraic Method
We can find $a$, $b$, and $c$ by memorising a system of three simple equations. We set these equations equal to the **first number of each row** in our differences table.

**Example:** Find the $n$th term for $4, 11, 22, 37, 56, \dots$

Match the first numbers of each row (working from the bottom upwards) to these equations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#ef4444} 2a &= \color{#ef4444} 4 \quad \text{(2nd Difference)} \\
\color{#3b82f6} 3a + b &= \color{#3b82f6} 7 \quad \text{(1st Difference)} \\
a + b + c &= 4 \quad \text{(Sequence)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, substitute your findings vertically to solve for the letters one by one:

**Step 1: Solve for $a$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#ef4444} 2a &= 4 \\
a &= 2
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 2: Substitute $a$ to solve for $b$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#3b82f6} 3(2) + b &= 7 \\
6 + b &= 7 \\
b &= 1
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 3: Substitute $a$ and $b$ to solve for $c$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
2 + 1 + c &= 4 \\
3 + c &= 4 \\
c &= 1
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 4: Write the final formula**
Substitute your values for $a$, $b$, and $c$ back into $an^2 + bn + c$:
$$T_n = 2n^2 + n + 1$$

## 4. Cubic Sequences

A sequence is **cubic** if the **third difference** is constant. 
The general formula involves $n^3$:
$$T_n = an^3 + bn^2 + cn + d$$

We use the same algebraic method as quadratics, but with four equations to match the four rows!

**Example:** Find the $n$th term for $3, 10, 29, 66, \dots$

Match the first number of each row to the cubic sequence equations (working upwards):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#a855f7} 6a &= \color{#a855f7} 6 \quad \text{(3rd Difference)} \\
\color{#ef4444} 12a + 2b &= \color{#ef4444} 12 \quad \text{(2nd Difference)} \\
\color{#3b82f6} 7a + 3b + c &= \color{#3b82f6} 7 \quad \text{(1st Difference)} \\
a + b + c + d &= 3 \quad \text{(Sequence)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Substitute vertically to find $a, b, c$, and $d$:

**Step 1: Solve for $a$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#a855f7} 6a &= 6 \\
a &= 1
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 2: Substitute $a$ to solve for $b$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#ef4444} 12(1) + 2b &= 12 \\
12 + 2b &= 12 \\
2b &= 0 \\
b &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 3: Substitute $a$ and $b$ to solve for $c$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
\color{#3b82f6} 7(1) + 3(0) + c &= 7 \\
7 + 0 + c &= 7 \\
c &= 0
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 4: Substitute $a$, $b$, and $c$ to solve for $d$**
$$
\begin{aligned}
1 + 0 + 0 + d &= 3 \\
1 + d &= 3 \\
d &= 2
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 5: Write the final formula**
$$T_n = 1n^3 + 0n^2 + 0n + 2$$
$$T_n = n^3 + 2$$

## 5. Combining Sequences (Fractions)

Sometimes you will be given a rational sequence (a sequence of fractions). 
The trick here is **not to divide them**, but to **completely separate the numerators (tops) and the denominators (bottoms)**. Treat them as two entirely independent sequences, figure out their formulas separately, and finally stack them back into a single fraction.

### Example 1: Standard Rational Sequence
Find the $n$th term of the sequence: $\frac{3}{2}, \frac{5}{6}, \frac{7}{12}, \frac{9}{20}, \dots$

**Step 1: The Numerators (Tops)**
The top numbers are: $3, 5, 7, 9, \dots$
This is a linear sequence that increases by $2$ each time ($2n$). The 0th term is $1$.
*Top Formula:* $2n + 1$

**Step 2: The Denominators (Bottoms)**
The bottom numbers are: $2, 6, 12, 20, \dots$
* 1st diff: $+4, +6, +8$
* 2nd diff: $+2$ (It's a quadratic sequence!)

Using our equations:
* $\color{#ef4444} 2a = 2 \implies a = 1$
* $\color{#3b82f6} 3a + b = 4 \implies 3(1) + b = 4 \implies b = 1$
* $a + b + c = 2 \implies 1 + 1 + c = 2 \implies c = 0$
*Bottom Formula:* $1n^2 + 1n + 0$, which simplifies to $n^2 + n$.

**Step 3: Recombine**
Stack your two formulas back into a fraction.
$$T_n = \frac{2n + 1}{n^2 + n}$$

### Example 2: Disguised (Simplified) Fractions
Sometimes, fractions are simplified by the examiner to hide the true underlying pattern. If a sequence of fractions seems to have a broken pattern—or contains random whole numbers—try **"un-simplifying"** them to reveal consistent differences.

Find the $n$th term of the sequence: $\frac{2}{3}, 1, \frac{6}{5}, \frac{4}{3}, \frac{10}{7}, \dots$

This looks chaotic. Let's convert the whole number $1$ and the fraction $\frac{4}{3}$ into equivalent fractions that fit the flow of the other numerators and denominators.

* $T_1 = \frac{2}{3}$
* $T_2 = 1$ *(Let's write this as $\frac{4}{4}$ to bridge the gap between 2 and 6)*
* $T_3 = \frac{6}{5}$
* $T_4 = \frac{4}{3}$ *(Multiply top and bottom by 2 to get $\frac{8}{6}$)*
* $T_5 = \frac{10}{7}$

Now look at our un-simplified sequence: **$\frac{2}{3}, \frac{4}{4}, \frac{6}{5}, \frac{8}{6}, \frac{10}{7}, \dots$**

The hidden patterns are now incredibly obvious!
* **Numerators:** $2, 4, 6, 8, 10 \dots$ (Linear, going up by $2$. Formula: **$2n$**)
* **Denominators:** $3, 4, 5, 6, 7 \dots$ (Linear, going up by $1$. Formula: **$n + 2$**)

**Final Recombined Formula:**
$$T_n = \frac{2n}{n + 2}$$

## 6. Exponential (Geometric) Sequences

In an exponential sequence, instead of adding or subtracting, you **multiply or divide** by a constant factor to get to the next term. This factor is called the **common ratio ($r$)**.

The formula for the $n$th term is usually written as:
$$T_n = a \times r^{n-1}$$
*(Where $a$ is the 1st term, and $r$ is the common ratio).*

<Aside type="note" title="Why to the power of (n-1)?">
To get to the 5th term, you don't multiply by the ratio five times. You start at the 1st term and multiply by it **four** times. That's why the power is $n-1$.
</Aside>

**Example 1: Standard Growth**
$3, 6, 12, 24, 48, \dots$
* 1st term ($a$) = $3$
* Multiplier ($r$) = $\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times 2}$
* $n$th term: **$T_n = 3 \times \textcolor{#ea580c}{2}^{n-1}$**

**Example 2: Fractional Ratios (Decay)**
$80, 40, 20, 10, \dots$
* 1st term ($a$) = $80$
* Multiplier ($r$) = $\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times \frac{1}{2}}$ (or $\textcolor{#ea580c}{0.5}$)
* $n$th term: **$T_n = 80 \times \left(\textcolor{#ea580c}{\frac{1}{2}}\right)^{n-1}$**

**Example 3: Negative Ratios (Alternating Signs)**
$2, -6, 18, -54, \dots$
Notice how the signs flip-flop from positive to negative. This means you are multiplying by a negative number!
* 1st term ($a$) = $2$
* Multiplier ($r$) = $\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times -3}$
* $n$th term: **$T_n = 2 \times (\textcolor{#ea580c}{-3})^{n-1}$**
*(Always put negative ratios in brackets so it is clear the power applies to the negative sign!)*

<SteveTip title="Simplifying using Index Laws">
Sometimes, geometric sequences can be simplified further if the 1st term ($a$) and the ratio ($r$) share a common base. You can use index laws to combine them!

For example, look at the sequence: $5, 25, 125, 625, \dots$
* $a = 5$ and $r = \textcolor{#ea580c}{5}$. 
* The standard formula is: **$T_n = 5 \times \textcolor{#ea580c}{5}^{n-1}$**

Since $5$ is the same as $5^1$, we are multiplying bases that are the same. We add the powers:
$$5^1 \times 5^{n-1} = 5^{1 + n - 1} = 5^n$$
So, a much cleaner answer is simply **$T_n = 5^n$**.
</SteveTip>

When tackling this, build a table to separate out the sequences:

| Figure ($n$) | Filled Tiles | Outline Tiles | Total Tiles |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | 1 | 8 | 9 |
| **2** | 4 | 12 | 16 |
| **3** | 9 | 16 | 25 |
| **$n$** | ? | ? | ? |

* **Filled Tiles:** $1, 4, 9 \dots$ These are perfectly square numbers. The formula is **$n^2$**.
* **Outline Tiles:** $8, 12, 16 \dots$ This is a linear sequence increasing by $\color{#3b82f6}{4}$ each time ($4n$). The 0th term is $4$. The formula is **$4n + 4$**.
* **Total Tiles:** $9, 16, 25 \dots$ These are square numbers, but shifted. Notice that Figure 1 is $3^2$, Figure 2 is $4^2$, and Figure 3 is $5^2$. The side length of the entire grid is always $(n+2)$. Thus, the formula is **$(n+2)^2$**.

<SteveTip title="Self-Checking in Investigations">
Notice how the math perfectly aligns! If you add the pieces together, you get the whole:
**Filled Tiles + Outline Tiles = Total Tiles**
$$n^2 + (4n + 4) = n^2 + 4n + 4$$
And if you expand $(n+2)^2$, you get $n^2 + 4n + 4$. The formulas prove each other!
</SteveTip>

    Find the $n$th term of the following linear sequences:
    
    a) $-2, 3, 8, 13, 18, \dots$
    
    b) $22, 16, 10, 4, -2, \dots$

    **b)**
    1. The sequence goes down by $6$. So, $-6n$.
    2. The 0th term is $22 + 6 = 28$.
    3. Combine: **$T_n = -6n + 28$** (or **$T_n = 28 - 6n$**)
  </TabItem>

    Find an expression for the $n$th term of the quadratic sequence: 
    
    $5, 14, 27, 44, \dots$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    **1. Check differences:**
    * Sequence: $\boldsymbol{5}, 14, 27, 44$
    * 1st diff: $\color{#3b82f6}{\boldsymbol{+9}}, +13, +17$
    * 2nd diff: $\color{#ef4444}{\boldsymbol{+4}}, +4$
    
    **2. Use the algebraic equations:**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \color{#ef4444} 2a &= \color{#ef4444} 4 \\
    a &= 2
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \color{#3b82f6} 3a + b &= \color{#3b82f6} 9 \\
    3(2) + b &= 9 \\
    b &= 3
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    a + b + c &= 5 \\
    2 + 3 + c &= 5 \\
    c &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    **3. Write the final formula:**
    $$\boldsymbol{T_n = 2n^2 + 3n}$$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Cubic Practice">
    Find an expression for the $n$th term of the cubic sequence: 
    
    $1, 14, 51, 124, \dots$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    **1. Check differences:**
    * Sequence: $\boldsymbol{1}, 14, 51, 124$
    * 1st diff: $\color{#3b82f6}{\boldsymbol{+13}}, +37, +73$
    * 2nd diff: $\color{#ef4444}{\boldsymbol{+24}}, +36$
    * 3rd diff: $\color{#a855f7}{\boldsymbol{+12}}$
    
    **2. Use the algebraic equations vertically:**
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \color{#a855f7} 6a &= \color{#a855f7} 12 \\
    a &= 2
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \color{#ef4444} 12a + 2b &= \color{#ef4444} 24 \\
    12(2) + 2b &= 24 \\
    24 + 2b &= 24 \\
    b &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \color{#3b82f6} 7a + 3b + c &= \color{#3b82f6} 13 \\
    7(2) + 3(0) + c &= 13 \\
    14 + c &= 13 \\
    c &= -1
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    a + b + c + d &= 1 \\
    2 + 0 - 1 + d &= 1 \\
    1 + d &= 1 \\
    d &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    **3. Write the final formula:**
    $$\boldsymbol{T_n = 2n^3 - n}$$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Find the $n$th term of the sequence: 
    
    $\frac{2}{5}, \frac{4}{12}, \frac{6}{23}, \frac{8}{38}, \dots$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    **1. Numerators (Tops):**
    Sequence is $2, 4, 6, 8, \dots$
    This is a simple linear sequence going up by $2$.
    *Top Formula:* $2n$
    
    **2. Denominators (Bottoms):**
    Sequence is $\boldsymbol{5}, 12, 23, 38, \dots$
    * 1st diff: $\color{#3b82f6}{\boldsymbol{+7}}, +11, +15$
    * 2nd diff: $\color{#ef4444}{\boldsymbol{+4}}, +4$
    
    Using quadratic equations:
    * $\color{#ef4444} 2a = 4 \implies a = 2$
    * $\color{#3b82f6} 3a + b = 7 \implies 6 + b = 7 \implies b = 1$
    * $a + b + c = 5 \implies 2 + 1 + c = 5 \implies c = 2$
    
    *Bottom Formula:* $2n^2 + n + 2$
    
    **3. Combine them:**
    $$\boldsymbol{T_n = \frac{2n}{2n^2 + n + 2}}$$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    2. Find the common multiplier ($r$): $-14 \div 7 = \textcolor{#ea580c}{-2}$. The signs alternate, so the multiplier is negative!
    
    Use the formula $a \times r^{n-1}$:
    $$\boldsymbol{T_n = 7 \times (\textcolor{#ea580c}{-2})^{n-1}}$$
  </TabItem>
