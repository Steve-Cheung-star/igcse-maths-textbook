## 1. Fundamental Angle Properties

Before dealing with complex shapes, you need to master the basic rules of angles. You must know these properties perfectly, as they will be the "reasons" you write down in the exam.

<Aside type="note" title="Three-Letter Notation">
  In IGCSE questions, angles are frequently named using three letters, such as angle $ABC$ or $\angle ABC$. The middle letter always represents the vertex (the point) where the angle is formed. The lines forming the angle are $AB$ and $BC$.
</Aside>

### Key Angle Rules

* **Angles on a straight line:** Angles that meet at a point on a straight line always sum to $180^\circ$.
* **Angles at a point:** Angles that complete a full circle around a single point always sum to $360^\circ$.
* **Vertically opposite angles:** When two straight lines intersect, the angles opposite each other are exactly equal.

## 2. Angles in Parallel Lines

When a straight line (called a transversal) cuts across two or more parallel lines, it creates specific pairs of angles. Parallel lines are indicated by matching arrows on the lines.

<Aside type="note" title="Parallel Line Rules">
  * **Alternate angles are equal:** They form a 'Z' shape. They are on opposite sides of the transversal and between the parallel lines.
  * **Corresponding angles are equal:** They form an 'F' shape. They are in the same relative position at each intersection.
  * **Co-interior angles sum to 180°:** They form a 'C' shape. They are on the same side of the transversal and between the parallel lines. These are also known as supplementary angles.
</Aside>

  Examiners are extremely strict about terminology. If you write "Z-angle" or "F-angle" as your reason, you will score **zero marks** for the explanation. You must write "alternate angle", "corresponding angle", or "co-interior angles sum to 180°".
</SteveTip>

## 3. Types of Triangles and their Angles

A triangle is a 3-sided polygon. No matter the type of triangle, its internal angles will always sum to $180^\circ$.

<Aside type="tip" title="Exterior Angle Theorem">
  The exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the two opposite interior angles. This can act as a fantastic shortcut in complex exam questions!
</Aside>

### Triangles Refresher
* **Equilateral:** All three sides equal. All three angles exactly **$60^\circ$**. (3 lines of symmetry).
* **Isosceles:** Two sides equal. The base angles opposite the equal sides are exactly **equal**. (1 line of symmetry).

## 4. Quadrilaterals and Concave Shapes

A quadrilateral is any 4-sided shape. The sum of the interior angles of any quadrilateral is **$360^\circ$**.

### Convex vs. Concave
Most quadrilaterals you deal with (squares, rectangles, parallelograms, trapeziums, kites) are **convex**. This means all their interior angles are less than $180^\circ$ and all vertices point outwards.

A **concave** quadrilateral (often resembling a dart or arrowhead) has one internal angle that is greater than $180^\circ$ (a reflex angle). Its interior angles still sum to $360^\circ$.

## 5. Angles in Polygons

A polygon is any closed 2D shape with straight sides. Polygons can be **regular** (all sides and angles are equal) or **irregular** (sides and angles differ).

### Interior and Exterior Angles

* **Interior Angle:** The angle inside the polygon at a vertex.
* **Exterior Angle:** The angle formed outside the polygon if you extend one of its sides in a straight line.

<Aside type="tip" title="The Golden Rule of Polygon Angles">
  For absolutely any polygon (regular or irregular), at any given vertex:
  $$\text{Interior Angle} + \text{Exterior Angle} = 180^\circ$$
</Aside>

### Calculating Angle Sums

For a polygon with $n$ sides:

1. **Sum of Interior Angles:**
   $$\text{Sum} = (n - 2) \times 180^\circ$$
2. **Sum of Exterior Angles:**
   $$\text{Sum} = 360^\circ$$

### Regular Polygons
Because regular polygons share the total angle sum equally among all their vertices, you can use these shortcuts:

* **One Exterior Angle:** $\dfrac{360^\circ}{n}$
* **One Interior Angle:** $180^\circ - \text{Exterior Angle}$

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Misconception">
  Many students try to find the interior angle of a regular polygon by using $\dfrac{(n-2) \times 180}{n}$. While correct, this is prone to algebraic errors. It is almost always faster to find the exterior angle first ($\dfrac{360}{n}$) and then subtract it from $180^\circ$!
</Aside>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Angles on a Straight Line">
    Three angles lie on a straight line. Their sizes are $2x^\circ$, $3x^\circ$, and $4x^\circ$.
    
    a) Write down an equation for the sum of these angles.

    b) Solve your equation to find the value of $x$.

    c) Calculate the size of the largest angle.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Angles on a straight line add up to $180^\circ$.
      2. **Equation: $2x + 3x + 4x = 180$**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Simplify the left side: $9x = 180$.
      2. Divide by 9: $x = \dfrac{180}{9}$.
      3. **$x = 20$**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The largest angle is $4x$.
      2. Substitute $x = 20$: $4 \times 20$.
      3. **The largest angle is $80^\circ$.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Angles around a Point">
    Four angles perfectly surround a central point. Three of the angles are $110^\circ$, $85^\circ$, and $90^\circ$.
    
    a) Calculate the size of the fourth angle.

    b) State the geometrical reason for your calculation.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Add the known angles: $110 + 85 + 90 = 285^\circ$.
      2. Subtract from 360: $360 - 285 = 75^\circ$.
      3. **The fourth angle is $75^\circ$.**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. **Angles at a point sum to $360^\circ$.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Parallel Lines Application">
    A transversal line cuts through two parallel lines. One of the interior acute angles is measured at $55^\circ$.
    
    a) What is the size of the alternate angle to this $55^\circ$ angle?

    b) What is the size of the co-interior angle to this $55^\circ$ angle?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Alternate angles are perfectly equal.
      2. **The alternate angle is $55^\circ$.**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Co-interior angles sum to $180^\circ$.
      2. Calculate $180 - 55$.
      3. **The co-interior angle is $125^\circ$.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Isosceles Triangle">
    In an isosceles triangle, the single unequal angle (the vertex angle) is $40^\circ$.
    
    a) Calculate the size of the remaining two angles.

    b) If the triangle was equilateral instead, what would the cost of outlining it be if the outline material costs 10.50 EUR per degree of its interior angles?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. The total sum of a triangle is $180^\circ$.
      2. Subtract the known angle: $180 - 40 = 140^\circ$.
      3. Divide equally between the two base angles: $140 \div 2 = 70^\circ$.
      4. **The remaining angles are $70^\circ$ each.**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. An equilateral triangle has three $60^\circ$ angles, summing to $180^\circ$.
      2. Multiply the total degrees by the cost: $180 \times 10.50$.
      3. **The cost would be 1890 EUR.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Regular Polygons">
    A regular polygon has 12 sides.
    
    a) Calculate the size of one exterior angle.

    b) Use your answer to part (a) to calculate the size of one interior angle.

    c) Calculate the total sum of all the interior angles in this polygon.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the exterior angle formula: $\dfrac{360}{n}$.
      2. Substitute $n = 12$: $\dfrac{360}{12}$.
      3. **The exterior angle is $30^\circ$.**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Interior and exterior angles sum to $180^\circ$.
      2. Calculate $180 - 30$.
      3. **The interior angle is $150^\circ$.**
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Multiply the single interior angle by the number of sides.
      2. $150 \times 12 = 1800^\circ$.
      3. *(Alternative method: $(12 - 2) \times 180 = 1800^\circ$)*.
      4. **The total sum is $1800^\circ$.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Polygon Cost Calculation">
    A designer is creating a large regular octagonal sign. Each interior angle of the sign will be fitted with a custom corner bracket.
    
    a) Calculate the size of one interior angle of the regular octagon.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Find the exterior angle first: $360 \div 8 = 45^\circ$.
      2. Subtract from 180: $180 - 45 = 135^\circ$.
      3. **One interior angle is $135^\circ$.**
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. An octagon has 8 interior angles, so 8 brackets are needed.
      2. Calculate total cost: $8 \times 15.50$.
      3. **The total cost is 124 GBP.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
