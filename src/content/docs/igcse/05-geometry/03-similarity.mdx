In geometry, it is crucial to distinguish between shapes that are **congruent** and shapes that are **similar**. Two shapes are said to be congruent if they have the exact same shape and the exact same size. Two shapes are said to be **similar** if they have the same shape but different sizes. Think of similarity as a mathematical enlargement or reduction; the shape can also be rotated or reflected, but its internal proportions and angles remain completely identical.

## 1. Proving Similarity in Triangles

Before calculating missing lengths, you are often required to prove *why* two shapes are similar. For triangles, this simplifies into checking any of the following standard conditions:

* **AA (Angle-Angle) Similarity:** If 2 pairs of angles are the same, the third pair must also be the same (since angles in a triangle sum to $180^\circ$).
* **SSS (Side-Side-Side) Similarity:** All 3 pairs of sides are in the exact same proportion.
* **SAS (Side-Angle-Side) Similarity:** 2 pairs of sides are proportional and the included angles between those sides are the same.

<SteveTip title="State the Reason">
Examiners will not award marks if you simply write "the angles are the same". You must use the correct geometric vocabulary to justify your proof: state whether angles are **alternate**, **corresponding**, **vertically opposite**, or **shared/common** angles.
</SteveTip>

## 2. Calculating Lengths: Proportions and Linear Equations

Once similarity is established, we use the **Linear Scale Factor (LSF)** to find missing lengths. A highly reliable method that minimises confusion is to always calculate the scale factor as a number greater than 1. 

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Linear Scale Factor (LSF)} = \frac{\text{Length of BIGGER shape}}{\text{Corresponding length of SMALLER shape}}
\end{aligned}
$$

* **To find a BIGGER missing length:** Take the smaller corresponding length and **multiply** by the LSF.
* **To find a SMALLER missing length:** Take the bigger corresponding length and **divide** by the LSF.

At the Extended level, side lengths are often given as algebraic expressions, requiring you to set up and solve a linear equation using proportional ratios.

**Example 1: Algebraic Similarity** In the similar triangles $ADE$ and $ABC$ from Configuration A (Nested), you are given:  
$AD = x$, $DB = 4\text{ cm}$, $AE = 6\text{ cm}$, and $EC = 8\text{ cm}$.  
Find the value of $x$.

<Steps>
  1. First, clearly identify the corresponding sides of the **small triangle** and the **large triangle**:
     * Small triangle side $AD = x$
     * Large triangle side $AB = x + 4$ 
     * Small triangle side $AE = 6$
     * Large triangle side $AC = 6 + 8 = 14$
  2. Since the triangles are similar, the ratios of their corresponding sides must be equal. We set up an equation and solve:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \frac{\text{Large Side } AB}{\text{Small Side } AD} &= \frac{\text{Large Side } AC}{\text{Small Side } AE} \\[1.5ex]
     \frac{x + 4}{x} &= \frac{14}{6} \\[1.5ex]
     \frac{x + 4}{x} &= \frac{7}{3} \\[1.5ex]
     3(x + 4) &= 7x \\[1.5ex]
     3x + 12 &= 7x \\[1.5ex]
     12 &= 4x \\[1.5ex]
     x &= 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

**Answer:** $x = 3$ (So the small side $AD$ is $3\text{ cm}$, and the large side $AB$ is $7\text{ cm}$).

## 3. Relationships Between Lengths, Areas, and Volumes

The most critical part of this topic is understanding how scaling a 1-dimensional length affects 2-dimensional areas and 3-dimensional volumes. 

<Aside type="note" title="Anatomy of Scale Factors">
When two solids are mathematically similar, their measurements scale based on their dimensions:
* **1D - Linear Scale Factor (LSF):** Used for length, width, height, perimeter, radius, and circumference.
* **2D - Area Scale Factor (ASF):** Area also applies to total surface area and cross-sectional area. 
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  \text{ASF} &= (\text{LSF})^2
  \end{aligned}
  $$
* **3D - Volume Scale Factor (VSF):** Volume also applies to capacity, mass, and weight (assuming uniform density). 
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  \text{VSF} &= (\text{LSF})^3
  \end{aligned}
  $$
</Aside>

### Working Backwards Between Scale Factors

You will frequently be given the areas or volumes of two similar shapes and asked to find a length. You cannot jump directly from Area to Volume. You must **always return to the Linear Scale Factor first**.

* To find LSF from Area: $\text{LSF} = \sqrt{\text{ASF}}$
* To find LSF from Volume: $\text{LSF} = \sqrt[3]{\text{VSF}}$
* To convert Area to Volume: $\text{VSF} = (\sqrt{\text{ASF}})^3$

**Example 2: Working Backwards with Area** Two similar rectangular badges are printed. The small badge has a width of $4\text{ cm}$ and an area of $12\text{ cm}^2$. The large badge has an area of $108\text{ cm}^2$. Find the width of the large badge.

<Steps>
  1. Find the Area Scale Factor (Big $\div$ Small):
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{ASF} &= \frac{108}{12} \\[1.5ex]
     \text{ASF} &= 9
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Square root to find the Linear Scale Factor:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{LSF} &= \sqrt{9} \\[1.5ex]
     \text{LSF} &= 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. We need the width of the *larger* badge, so we **multiply** the small width by the LSF:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Large Width} &= 4 \times 3 \\[1.5ex]
     \text{Large Width} &= 12\text{ cm}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

**Answer:** $12\text{ cm}$

**Example 3: Volume and Mass (Jellies)** The two mathematically similar jellies shown above have heights of $10\text{ cm}$ and $15\text{ cm}$.   
If the small jelly requires $200\text{ g}$ of gelatin mix to make, calculate the mass of gelatin mix required for the large jelly.

Remember: Mass and weight scale with volume!
<Steps>
  1. Find the Linear Scale Factor (LSF) (Big $\div$ Small):
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{LSF} &= \frac{15}{10} \\[1.5ex]
     \text{LSF} &= 1.5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Convert LSF to Volume Scale Factor (VSF):
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{VSF} &= (1.5)^3 \\[1.5ex]
     \text{VSF} &= 3.375
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Multiply the small mass by the VSF to find the large mass:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Mass of large jelly} &= 200 \times 3.375 \\[1.5ex]
     \text{Mass of large jelly} &= 675\text{ g}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

**Answer:** $675\text{ g}$


**Example 4: Areas leading to Quadratics** Two mathematically similar triangles have a Linear Scale Factor of $2$.   
The area of the smaller triangle is given by the expression $x$.   
The area of the larger triangle is given by the expression $x^2 - 12$.  
Calculate the exact area of the smaller triangle.

<Steps>
  1. We know the Linear Scale Factor is $2$.
  2. Therefore, the Area Scale Factor must be $2^2 = 4$.
  3. This tells us that $4 \times (\text{Small Area}) = \text{Large Area}$.
  4. Substitute the expressions to form an equation and solve:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     4(x) &= x^2 - 12 \\[1.5ex]
     0 &= x^2 - 4x - 12 \\[1.5ex]
     0 &= (x - 6)(x + 2)
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  5. Solve for $x$: 
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x = 6 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  6. Since an area cannot be negative, $x$ must be $6$. The area of the smaller triangle is $6$.
</Steps>

**Answer:** $6$

### Algebraic Similar Triangles

<Tabs>

    Triangles $ABE$ and $CDE$ form an hourglass shape intersecting at $E$, with $AB$ parallel to $CD$.  
    $AB = 5\text{ cm}$ and $CD = 15\text{ cm}$.   
    If $AE = x + 1$ and $CE = 4x - 1$, set up an equation and find the value of $x$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. Identify corresponding sides: $AB$ corresponds to $CD$, and $AE$ corresponds to $CE$.
      2. Find the LSF from small to large: 
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{LSF} &= \frac{15}{5} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{LSF} &= 3
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      3. Apply the LSF to the algebraic sides and solve: 
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         3 \times (AE) &= CE \\[1.5ex]
         3(x + 1) &= 4x - 1 \\[1.5ex]
         3x + 3 &= 4x - 1 \\[1.5ex]
         4 &= x
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>
    **Answer:** $x = 4$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Working Backwards with Area

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Areas of Polygons">
    Two regular hexagons are similar. The larger hexagon has a perimeter of $60\text{ cm}$ and an area of $250\text{ cm}^2$. The smaller hexagon has an area of $90\text{ cm}^2$. Calculate the perimeter of the smaller hexagon.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    Perimeter is a 1-dimensional measurement, so it uses the LSF.
    <Steps>
      1. Find the Area Scale Factor (Big $\div$ Small):
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{ASF} &= \frac{250}{90} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{ASF} &= \frac{25}{9}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. Square root to find the Linear Scale Factor:
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{LSF} &= \sqrt{\frac{25}{9}} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{LSF} &= \frac{5}{3}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      3. We need the smaller perimeter, so divide the large perimeter by the LSF:
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{Small Perimeter} &= 60 \div \frac{5}{3} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{Small Perimeter} &= 60 \times \frac{3}{5} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{Small Perimeter} &= 36\text{ cm}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>
    **Answer:** $36\text{ cm}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Working Backwards with Volume and Mass

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Melting Gold Ingots">
    Two mathematically similar gold ingots have widths of $12\text{ cm}$ and $21\text{ cm}$. The lighter ingot weighs $2.05\text{ kg}$. Calculate the weight of the heavier ingot.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
      1. Find the Linear Scale Factor (Big $\div$ Small):
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{LSF} &= \frac{21}{12} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{LSF} &= \frac{7}{4}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. Cube it to find the Volume Scale Factor (which applies to weight):
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{VSF} &= \left(\frac{7}{4}\right)^3 \\[1.5ex]
         \text{VSF} &= \frac{343}{64}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      3. We need the heavier weight, so multiply to scale up:
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{Heavy Weight} &= 2.05 \times \frac{343}{64} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{Heavy Weight} &= 10.9859...
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>
    **Answer:** $11.0\text{ kg}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

### Complex Area to Volume Conversions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: The Russian Dolls">
    A set of Russian dolls are mathematically similar.   
    The smallest doll has a surface area of $150\text{ cm}^2$ and a volume of $250\text{ cm}^3$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
      1. Find the Area Scale Factor (Big $\div$ Small):
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{ASF} &= \frac{540}{150} \\[1.5ex]
         \text{ASF} &= 3.6
         \end{aligned}

         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{LSF} &= \sqrt{3.6}
         \end{aligned}
         $$

         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{VSF} &= (\sqrt{3.6})^3 \\[1.5ex]
         \text{VSF} &\approx 6.8305
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      4. Multiply the small volume by the VSF:
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{Large Volume} &= 250 \times 6.8305 \\[1.5ex]
         \text{Large Volume} &\approx 1707.6\text{ cm}^3
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>
    **Answer:** $1707.6\text{ cm}^3$
  </TabItem>
