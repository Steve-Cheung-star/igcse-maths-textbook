Circle theorems are a set of geometrical rules that allow you to find missing angles in circles. In the IGCSE Extended exam, you will frequently be asked to calculate unknown angles in complex, multi-layered diagrams and—crucially—state the mathematical reasons for your answers. 

<Aside type="note" title="Anatomy of a Circle">
Before diving into the theorems, ensure you know the correct terminology:
* **Radius:** A line from the centre to the circumference.
* **Diameter:** A line passing through the centre, touching the circumference twice (two radii).
* **Chord:** A straight line connecting two points on the circumference.
* **Tangent:** A straight line that touches the outside of the circle at exactly one point.
* **Arc:** A portion of the circumference.
* **Segment:** The area bounded by a chord and an arc.
* **Subtend:** To "hold up" or "create" an angle. A chord or arc subtends an angle at the circumference or centre.
</Aside>



<SteveTip title="Give Reasons for your Answers">
When a question says "give a reason", you **must** use the exact terminology specified in the syllabus. Writing "it looks like 90°" or "arrowhead rule" will score zero marks. Memorise the bolded theorem names exactly as they are written below.
</SteveTip>

## 1. Basic Circle Theorems

These first two theorems are the most fundamental and are often the first step in solving a complex exam question.

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 1: Angle in a Semicircle">
The **angle in a semicircle = 90°**. 
Any triangle drawn inside a circle where one side is the **diameter** will always be a right-angled triangle, with the 90° angle touching the circumference.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 2: Tangent and Radius">
The **angle between tangent and radius = 90°**. 
Where a radius meets a tangent exactly at the circumference, they are completely perpendicular.
</Aside>

## 2. The Central Angle Theorem (4 Cases)

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 3: Angle at the Centre">
The **angle at the centre is twice the angle at the circumference** (when both are subtended by the same arc).
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="The Cyclic Quad Trap!">
Students constantly mix up **Case 4 (The Kite)** with a Cyclic Quadrilateral. 
In Case 4, one of the vertices is at the **centre** of the circle. This is NOT a cyclic quadrilateral! A true cyclic quad must have **all four** points touching the circumference. If you apply cyclic quad rules to Case 4, your answer will be completely wrong.
</Aside>

## 3. Angles in the Same Segment & Cyclic Quads

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 4: Angles in the Same Segment">
**Angles in the same segment are equal**.
If two angles at the circumference are subtended by the exact same chord or arc, they are identical. This creates a classic "bowtie" pattern.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 5: Cyclic Quadrilateral">
**Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°** (they are supplementary).
</Aside>

## 4. The Alternate Segment Theorem (Extended)

This is the most advanced circle theorem and appears exclusively in the Extended syllabus. It connects tangents and triangles inscribed in a circle.

<Aside type="tip" title="Theorem 6: Alternate Segment Theorem">
The angle between a tangent and a chord through the point of contact is equal to the angle in the alternate segment.
</Aside>

To identify this in an exam:
1. Look for a **tangent** touching the circle at a single point.
2. Look for a **triangle** sitting inside the circle, where one of the triangle's vertices rests exactly on the tangent.
3. The angle between the tangent line and one side of the triangle equals the angle *inside* the triangle on the opposite (alternate) side.
