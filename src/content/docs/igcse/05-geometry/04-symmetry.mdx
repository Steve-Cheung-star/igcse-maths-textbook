Symmetry is a fundamental geometrical concept that describes how shapes can be mapped onto themselves through reflection or rotation. Mastering symmetry is essential for classifying polygons and understanding the properties of three-dimensional solids.

---

## 1. Symmetry in Two Dimensions

In two-dimensional geometry, we focus on two main types of symmetry: line symmetry and rotational symmetry.

### Line Symmetry

A 2D shape has line symmetry (or reflectional symmetry) if it can be folded along a straight line so that both halves match exactly. The fold line is called the **line of symmetry**.

<Aside type="tip" title="Regular Polygons Rule">
  A regular polygon with $n$ sides always has exactly $n$ lines of symmetry. For example, a regular hexagon has 6 lines of symmetry.
</Aside>

### Rotational Symmetry

A shape has rotational symmetry if it still looks exactly the same after a rotation of less than a full turn (360 degrees).

* **Order of rotational symmetry:** The number of times a shape fits exactly onto its original outline during one complete 360-degree turn.
* **Centre of rotation:** The fixed point around which the shape is rotated.

<Steps>
  1. Place tracing paper over the shape and draw its outline.
  2. Place your pen on the centre of the shape.
  3. Rotate the tracing paper one full turn (360 degrees).
  4. Count how many times the traced outline matches the original shape underneath.
</Steps>

<SteveTip title="Order 1 is No Symmetry">
  Every shape fits onto itself after a full 360-degree turn. If a shape ONLY fits onto itself once during a full rotation, we say it has an **order of rotational symmetry of 1**. This effectively means it has NO rotational symmetry.
</SteveTip>

### Symmetry Properties of Quadrilaterals

It is crucial to memorise the symmetries of special quadrilaterals, as these define many of their geometric properties.

<Aside type="note" title="Quadrilateral Symmetries">
  * **Square:** 4 lines of symmetry; rotational symmetry order 4.
  * **Rectangle:** 2 lines of symmetry; rotational symmetry order 2.
  * **Rhombus:** 2 lines of symmetry (the diagonals); rotational symmetry order 2.
  * **Kite:** 1 line of symmetry; rotational symmetry order 1.
  * **Isosceles Trapezium:** 1 line of symmetry; rotational symmetry order 1.
</Aside>

<Aside type="caution" title="The Parallelogram Trap">
  A common error is to draw lines of symmetry horizontally or vertically through a parallelogram. A standard parallelogram has **0 lines of symmetry**, but it does have **rotational symmetry of order 2**.
</Aside>

## 2. Symmetry in Three Dimensions

When we move from 2D to 3D, our concepts of symmetry upgrade from lines and points to planes and axes.

### Planes of Symmetry

A **plane of symmetry** is a flat, two-dimensional surface that cuts a solid into two identical halves that are mirror images of each other. Think of it like a perfectly straight blade slicing a cake into two symmetrical pieces.

<Aside type="tip" title="Planes in a Cuboid vs Cube">
  A standard cuboid (where all faces are distinct rectangles) has exactly 3 planes of symmetry. A perfect cube, however, has **9 planes of symmetry** (3 parallel to the faces, and 6 cutting across the diagonals).
</Aside>

### Axes of Symmetry

An **axis of symmetry** is a straight line passing through the centre of a 3D solid around which the solid can be rotated to map exactly onto its original position more than once during a full 360-degree turn.

### Symmetries of Common Solids

* **Cylinder:** Has an infinite number of vertical planes of symmetry passing through its central vertical axis, plus 1 horizontal plane of symmetry cutting it exactly in half. Its central axis has an infinite order of rotational symmetry.
* **Cone:** Similar to a cylinder, a right circular cone has an infinite number of planes of symmetry passing through its apex and central axis. However, it does NOT have a horizontal plane of symmetry.
* **Prism:** The symmetries of a prism depend entirely on its cross-section. An equilateral triangular prism has 4 planes of symmetry (3 cutting vertically through the rectangular faces and opposite vertices, and 1 cutting horizontally through the middle).
* **Square-based Pyramid:** Has 4 planes of symmetry passing vertically through the apex (2 connecting opposite corners of the square base, and 2 connecting the midpoints of opposite sides of the base). The axis passing through the apex and the centre of the base has a rotational symmetry of order 4.

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Wait, there is one more plane. The plane that slices exactly halfway through the length of the rectangular faces, perpendicular to the triangular ends.
      2. So, 3 planes from the cross-section + 1 plane bisecting the length.
      3. **The total number of planes of symmetry is 4.**
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
