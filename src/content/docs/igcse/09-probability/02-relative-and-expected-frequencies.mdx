Probability can be approached in two ways: what we *expect* to happen mathematically (**theoretical probability**), and what *actually* happens when we perform an experiment (**experimental probability**).

<Aside type="note" title="Probability Terminology">
1. **Theoretical Probability:** What should happen in a perfect world. For a fair coin, the theoretical probability of getting Heads is exactly $0.5$.
2. **Relative Frequency:** Also known as experimental probability. This is what actually happens when you run an experiment. If you flip a coin 10 times and get 7 Heads, the relative frequency of Heads is $\frac{7}{10}$ or $0.7$.
3. **Expected Frequency:** Using probability to predict how many times an event will occur over a large number of trials or within a whole population.
</Aside>

---

## 1. Relative Frequency

When we perform an experiment (like spinning a spinner or dropping a piece of toast), we record the outcomes. The **relative frequency** is an estimate of the true probability based purely on those experimental results.

<Aside type="tip" title="Relative Frequency Formula">
$$
\text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{\text{Number of successful outcomes}}{\text{Total number of trials}}
$$
</Aside>

### The Law of Large Numbers

If you flip a coin 10 times, you might get 8 Heads. The relative frequency ($0.8$) is very different from the theoretical probability ($0.5$). However, if you flip that same coin 10,000 times, the relative frequency will settle down and get extremely close to $0.5$. 

<SteveTip title="More Trials = Better Estimate">
In exams, if you are asked how to make an experimental estimate more reliable, the answer is always: **"Perform more trials"** or **"Repeat the experiment more times."**
</SteveTip>

## 2. Fair, Bias, and Random

These three words have very specific mathematical meanings that frequently appear in IGCSE questions.

<Aside type="caution" title="Definitions">
* **Random:** An event is random if every single possible item or outcome has an **equal chance** of being selected.
* **Fair:** An object (like a dice or a coin) is fair if all of its faces/outcomes have an **equal theoretical probability** of occurring.
* **Bias:** An object is biased if the outcomes are **not equally likely**, usually due to a physical flaw, extra weight on one side, or uneven sections.
</Aside>

How do we test for bias? We use relative frequency! If we roll a dice 600 times, we expect each number to appear roughly 100 times. If the number six appears 450 times, we have strong experimental evidence that the dice is biased.

## 3. Expected Frequencies

If we know the probability of an event (either theoretical or from past experimental data), we can predict how many times we **expect** that event to happen over a certain number of trials.

<Aside type="tip" title="Expected Frequency Formula">
$$
\text{Expected Frequency} = \text{Probability of the event} \times \text{Total number of trials}
$$
</Aside>

**Example:** A factory produces 5000 lightbulbs. Historically, the probability of a lightbulb being defective is $0.02$. How many defective lightbulbs do we *expect* in this batch?

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Expected defects} &= 0.02 \times 5000 \\
&= 100 \text{ lightbulbs}
\end{aligned}
$$

<SteveTip title="To Round or Not To Round?">
Mathematically, an expected frequency does **NOT** need to be rounded to a whole number. It is an average expectation. For example, if you flip a biased coin 10 times and the expected number of Heads is $3.5$, you leave the answer as $3.5$. 

*However*, always read the question carefully! If the context or instructions explicitly ask for an integer (e.g., "Estimate the number of whole days it will rain"), you should round appropriately.
</SteveTip>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Relative Frequency from a Table">
    A spinner has four colours: Red, Blue, Green, and Yellow. Sarah spins the spinner multiple times. Her results are recorded in the table below.

    | Colour | Red | Blue | Green | Yellow |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | Frequency | 95 | 40 | 35 | 30 |

    a) Calculate the total number of times Sarah spun the spinner.

    b) Calculate the relative frequency of the spinner landing on Red.

    c) Do you think this spinner is fair? Give a mathematical reason for your answer.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Add all the frequencies together: $95 + 40 + 35 + 30 = 200$.
      2. The spinner was spun $200$ times.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the formula: Relative Frequency = (Successful trials) / (Total trials).
      2. For Red, there were $95$ successful trials out of $200$ total spins.
      3. Relative frequency of Red = $\frac{95}{200}$ or $0.475$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. To determine if it is fair, consider the theoretical probability. If fair, each of the 4 colours should have a probability of $\frac{1}{4}$ (or $0.25$).
      2. The relative frequency for Red ($0.475$) is almost double the expected $0.25$.
      3. Therefore, the spinner is **biased** towards Red and is **not fair**.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Expected Frequency">
    The probability that a special seed germinates and grows into a plant is $0.85$. 
    
    a) A farmer plants $400$ of these seeds. Calculate the number of seeds the farmer expects to germinate.

    b) The next month, the farmer plants $2000$ seeds. How many seeds are expected NOT to germinate?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Identify the probability: $P(\text{Germinates}) = 0.85$.
      2. Identify the total number of trials: $\text{Total} = 400$.
      3. Expected Frequency = Probability $\times$ Total trials.
      4. $\text{Expected} = 0.85 \times 400 = 340$.
      5. The farmer expects $340$ seeds to germinate.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. First, find the probability of a seed NOT germinating using the complement rule: $1 - 0.85 = 0.15$.
      2. Use the expected frequency formula for $2000$ trials: $0.15 \times 2000$.
      3. $\text{Expected not to germinate} = 300$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Combining Relative and Expected Frequencies">
    A quality control inspector tests 80 toys from a production line and finds that 3 of them have a manufacturing defect. 
    
    a) Write down the relative frequency of a toy having a defect.

    b) The factory produces $12,000$ toys in a week. Based on the inspector's sample, how many toys would you expect to be defective in that week?

    c) How many toys would you expect to be flawless (not defective) in that week?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the relative frequency formula: $\frac{\text{Successful outcomes}}{\text{Total trials}}$.
      2. Relative frequency = $\frac{3}{80}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Apply the experimental probability to the whole population ($12,000$ toys).
      2. Expected defects = $\frac{3}{80} \times 12000$.
      3. $\frac{3}{80} \times 12000 = 3 \times 150 = 450$.
      4. We expect $450$ toys to be defective.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Subtract the expected defective toys from the total number of toys.
      2. $12000 - 450 = 11550$.
      3. We expect $11,550$ toys to be flawless.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    John and Jane each perform an experiment by flipping a coin to see if it is fair. 
    
    * John flips the coin $20$ times and gets $14$ Heads.
    * Jane flips the same coin $2000$ times and gets $1050$ Heads.

    a) Calculate the relative frequency of Heads for John's experiment.

    b) Calculate the relative frequency of Heads for Jane's experiment.

    c) State whose experiment provides a more reliable estimate for the probability of getting Heads, and explain why.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. John's relative frequency = $\frac{14}{20}$.
      2. This simplifies to $\frac{7}{10}$ or $0.7$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Jane's relative frequency = $\frac{1050}{2000}$.
      2. This simplifies to $\frac{105}{200}$ or $0.525$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Jane's experiment is more reliable.
      2. According to the Law of Large Numbers, performing an experiment more times (more trials) provides a relative frequency that is much closer to the true theoretical probability.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A standard six-sided dice is rolled $600$ times. The results are shown below:

    | Number | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | Frequency | 102 | 98 | 105 | 95 | 45 | 155 |

    a) If the dice were perfectly fair, what is the expected frequency for each number?

    b) Calculate the relative frequency of rolling a 6 based on this experiment.

    c) Do you think the dice is fair or biased? Give a clear reason.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. A fair dice has a probability of $\frac{1}{6}$ for each number.
      2. Expected frequency = $\frac{1}{6} \times 600$.
      3. We would expect each number to appear exactly $100$ times.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. The number 6 appeared $155$ times out of $600$.
      2. Relative frequency = $\frac{155}{600}$.
      3. This simplifies to $\frac{31}{120}$ (or approximately $0.258$).
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. The dice is strongly likely to be **biased**.
      2. The number 6 appeared $155$ times, which is significantly higher than the expected $100$ times, while the number 5 appeared significantly lower ($45$ times).
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Financial Application">
    A charity sells $5000$ raffle tickets. The probability of any single ticket winning a $50\text{ GBP}$ prize is $0.015$.

    a) Calculate the expected number of winning tickets.

    b) Calculate the total amount of money, in $\text{ GBP}$, the charity expects to pay out in prize money.

    c) If the charity sells the tickets for $2\text{ GBP}$ each, what is their expected overall profit?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the expected frequency formula: Probability $\times$ Total trials.
      2. Expected winners = $0.015 \times 5000$.
      3. $0.015 \times 5000 = 75$. We expect $75$ winning tickets.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Multiply the expected number of winners by the prize amount.
      2. Expected payout = $75 \times 50\text{ GBP}$.
      3. The expected payout is $3750\text{ GBP}$.
    </Steps>

    **c)**
    <Steps>
      1. Calculate total income from ticket sales: $5000 \times 2\text{ GBP} = 10000\text{ GBP}$.
      2. Subtract the expected payout from the total income: $10000 - 3750$.
      3. The expected overall profit is $6250\text{ GBP}$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Working Backwards">
    In the month of April ($30$ days), it rained on $12$ days.
    
    a) Calculate the relative frequency of a day having rain in April. Give your answer as a fully simplified fraction.

    b) Assuming this relative frequency is a good estimate for the whole year ($365$ days), how many days would you expect it to rain in a full year?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    <Steps>
      1. Relative frequency = $\frac{\text{Days it rained}}{\text{Total days in April}}$.
      2. Relative frequency = $\frac{12}{30}$.
      3. Simplify the fraction by dividing top and bottom by 6: $\frac{2}{5}$.
    </Steps>

    **b)**
    <Steps>
      1. Use the expected frequency formula for the new total number of trials ($365$ days).
      2. Expected rainy days = $\frac{2}{5} \times 365$.
      3. Calculate: $365 \div 5 = 73$, then $73 \times 2 = 146$.
      4. You would expect it to rain on $146$ days in a full year.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
