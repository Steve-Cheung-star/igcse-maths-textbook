In the real world, we rarely look at just one event in isolation. We often want to find the probability of multiple things happening together, like flipping a coin *and* rolling a dice, or picking two consecutive winning lottery tickets.

<Aside type="note" title="The Golden Rules of Probability">
When dealing with combined events, ask yourself if you are dealing with an "AND" situation or an "OR" situation.

1. **The AND Rule (Multiplication):** For **independent events** (where one event does not affect the other), you multiply the probabilities.
   $$
   P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)
   $$

2. **The OR Rule (Addition):** For **mutually exclusive events** (events that cannot happen at the same time), you add the probabilities.
   $$
   P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)
   $$
</Aside>

## 2. Tree Diagrams: Replacement vs. No Replacement

Tree diagrams map out sequential events (one thing happening after another). 
* **Outcomes** are written at the very end of the branches.
* **Probabilities** are written on the branches themselves.
* To find a combined probability at the end of a path, **multiply** along the branches.

<Aside type="caution" title="The Replacement Trap">
* **With Replacement (Independent):** If you take an item from a bag and put it back, the probabilities for the second pick stay exactly the same.
* **Without Replacement (Dependent):** If you eat a sweet or keep a card, the total number of items left decreases by $1$. The probability denominators for the second branch MUST decrease by $1$.
</Aside>

## 3. The "At Least One" Trick (Using the Complement)

Sometimes calculating the probability of a combined event directly is exhausting because there are too many paths. A classic example is finding the probability of getting **"at least one"** of something.

Instead of calculating $P(1) + P(2) + P(3)$, it is much faster to use the complement rule:

$$
P(\text{At least one}) = 1 - P(\text{None})
$$

**Example:** You roll a fair 6-sided dice 3 times. What is the probability of rolling *at least one* six?
Instead of mapping out all 27 paths of a 3-tier tree diagram, just calculate the probability of getting **no sixes at all**:
* $P(\text{Not a 6}) = \frac{5}{6}$.
* $P(\text{No 6 on all three rolls}) = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{125}{216}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\text{At least one 6}) &= 1 - \frac{125}{216} \\
&= \frac{91}{216}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 4. Complex Conditional Events (Box to Box)

At the Extended level, you may encounter problems where an item is moved from one container to another *before* a second draw takes place. This alters the denominators AND the numerators of the second container!

**Example Scenario:**
* **Box A** contains 3 Red and 2 Blue balls.
* **Box B** contains 1 Red and 4 Blue balls.
* *A ball is drawn at random from Box A and placed into Box B. Then, a ball is drawn from Box B.* What is the probability that the final ball drawn from Box B is Blue?

This requires tracking two different alternate timelines for Box B:
1. **If a RED ball was moved from A to B:** Box B now has **2 Red and 4 Blue** (Total = 6). 
   Path Probability = $P(\text{Red from A}) \times P(\text{Blue from updated B}) = \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{4}{6} = \frac{12}{30}$.
2. **If a BLUE ball was moved from A to B:**
   Box B now has **1 Red and 5 Blue** (Total = 6).
   Path Probability = $P(\text{Blue from A}) \times P(\text{Blue from updated B}) = \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{10}{30}$.

To find the total probability of picking a Blue from Box B, we add the two valid paths together:
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\text{Blue from Box B}) &= \frac{12}{30} + \frac{10}{30} \\
&= \frac{22}{30} \\
&= \frac{11}{15}
\end{aligned}
$$

## 5. Venn Diagrams and Set Notation

We can also represent the probability of combined events using Venn diagrams and set notation.
* **Intersection (AND):** $P(A \cap B)$ is the probability of choosing something that belongs to BOTH event $A$ and event $B$ (the overlapping middle section).
* **Union (OR):** $P(A \cup B)$ is the probability of choosing something that belongs to event $A$, event $B$, or BOTH (the entirety of both circles).

    A bag contains $4$ red sweets and $6$ yellow sweets. Two sweets are selected at random **without replacement**.
    
    Calculate the probability that **at least one** of the sweets is red.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
      1. "At least one red" includes (Red, Red), (Red, Yellow), and (Yellow, Red). The only outcome it does *not* include is (Yellow, Yellow).
      2. It is faster to calculate $P(\text{None are red})$, which is $P(\text{Yellow, Yellow})$.
      3. First yellow sweet: $\frac{6}{10}$.
      4. Second yellow sweet (without replacement): $\frac{5}{9}$.
      5. Calculate $P(Y, Y)$:
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         P(Y, Y) &= \frac{6}{10} \times \frac{5}{9} \\
         &= \frac{30}{90} \\
         &= \frac{1}{3}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      6. Use the complement rule for "at least one":
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         P(\text{At least one red}) &= 1 - P(Y, Y) \\
         &= 1 - \frac{1}{3} \\
         &= \frac{2}{3}
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>

         $$
         \begin{aligned}

         &= \frac{22}{30} \\
         &= \frac{11}{15}
