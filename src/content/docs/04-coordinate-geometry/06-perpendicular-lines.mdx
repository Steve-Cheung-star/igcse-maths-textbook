## 1. The Negative Reciprocal Rule (Visualising Rise/Run)

Unlike parallel lines, perpendicular lines travel in entirely different directions. Look at the diagram above to understand how their gradients are connected.

If the **original line (Line 1)** has a gradient of $m_1 = \frac{a}{b}$ (meaning it goes **up $a$** for every **$b$ right**), then the **perpendicular line (Line 2)** must travel **right $a$** for every **down $b$**.

The gradient of the perpendicular line is:
$$
m_2 = \frac{-b}{a}
$$
This is the **negative reciprocal**. You flip the fraction and change the sign. Multiplying them always gives $-1$:
$$
m_1 \times m_2 = -1 \quad \rightarrow \quad \left(\frac{a}{b}\right) \times \left(-\frac{b}{a}\right) = -1
$$

<SteveTip title="Flip and Flip">
To find the perpendicular gradient, remember the two 'flips':
1.  Flip the **fraction** upside down.
2.  Flip the **sign** (positive $\leftrightarrow$ negative).
</SteveTip>

## 2. Identifying Perpendicular Gradients from Equations

You must ensure the equation is in the form $y = mx + c$ before you can identify the original gradient and calculate its negative reciprocal.

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the gradient of a line perpendicular to $2y = 3x + 1$.

1.  **Find the original gradient:** The equation is not in $y = mx + c$ form yet. Rearrange it:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \frac{2y}{2} &= \frac{3x + 1}{2} \\
    y &= \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{1}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The original gradient ($m_1$) is $\frac{3}{2}$.
2.  **Find the perpendicular gradient:** Flip $\frac{3}{2}$ and change the sign to negative.
    **Answer:** The perpendicular gradient ($m_2$) is $\boldsymbol{-\frac{2}{3}}$.

## 3. The Unique Perpendicular through a Point

For any given line and any given point ($P$) **not on that line**, there is **exactly one** unique perpendicular line that passes through $P$.

**Syllabus Example:** Find the equation of the line passing through $(-6, 4)$ which is perpendicular to the line $y = 3x - 2$.

<Steps>
1.  **Find the perpendicular gradient ($m_2$):** The original gradient is $3$. The perpendicular gradient is $m_2 = -\frac{1}{3}$.
2.  **Substitute into $y = mx + c$:** Use your perpendicular gradient and the given point $x = -6$, $y = 4$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    4 &= \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)(-6) + c \\
    4 &= 2 + c \\
    c &= \mathbf{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
3.  **Write the final equation:**
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 2}$
</Steps>

**Syllabus Example Walkthrough:**
Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the points $(-3, 8)$ and $(9, -2)$.

<Steps>
1.  **Find the Midpoint ($M$):** You *must* know where the middle is.
    $$
    M = \left( \frac{-3 + 9}{2}, \frac{8 + (-2)}{2} \right) = \left( \frac{6}{2}, \frac{6}{2} \right) = \mathbf{(3, 3)}
    $$
    *Our perpendicular bisector must pass through $(3, 3)$.*
2.  **Find the Original Gradient ($m_1$):**
    $$
    m_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{-2 - 8}{9 - (-3)} = \frac{-10}{12} = \mathbf{-\frac{5}{6}}
    $$
3.  **Find the Perpendicular Gradient ($m_2$):**
    $$
    m_2 = \mathbf{\frac{6}{5}}
    $$
4.  **Build the Equation:** Use $m = \frac{6}{5}$ and the midpoint $(3, 3)$. Substitute into $y = mx + c$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3 &= \left(\frac{6}{5}\right)(3) + c \quad \rightarrow \quad 3 = \frac{18}{5} + c \\
    c &= 3 - \frac{18}{5} = \frac{15}{5} - \frac{18}{5} \\
    c &= \mathbf{-\frac{3}{5}}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    **Answer:** $\boldsymbol{y = \frac{6}{5}x - \frac{3}{5}}$
</Steps>

This concept is used in optimization problems (e.g., finding the closest point on a road, a boundary, or a river).

**To find the shortest distance, you must:**
1.  Find the equation of the unique **perpendicular line** passing through the external point (Concept 3).
2.  Find the **point of intersection** ($I$) between the original line and your perpendicular line (by solving simultaneous equations, see Section 4.7).
3.  Calculate the **distance** between the original external point and the intersection point $I$ (using the Distance Formula, Section 4.3).

**The Steps:**

<Steps>
1.  **Find the gradient of the curve at $(4, 8)$:** This uses differentiation (Syllabus 8.1). The gradient of $y = x^2 - 8$ is calculated by its derivative $dy/dx = 2x$. At $x=4$, the gradient of the tangent is $2(4) = \mathbf{8}$.
2.  **Find the perpendicular gradient:** This is the normal gradient. $m = -\frac{1}{8}$.
3.  **Find the equation of the perpendicular line through $(4, 8)$:**
    $$
    y - 8 = -\frac{1}{8}(x - 4)
    $$
4.  **Find where this line cuts the x-axis:** We need to set $y=0$ in the line equation to find our 3rd vertex.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    0 - 8 &= -\frac{1}{8}(x - 4) \\
    -8 &= -\frac{1}{8}x + \frac{4}{8} \\
    -8.5 &= -0.125x \\
    x &= \frac{8.5}{0.125} = \mathbf{68}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The three vertices of our triangle are the origin $O(0, 0)$, the point on the curve $Q(4, 8)$, and the intersection $R(68, 0)$.
5.  **Calculate Triangle Area:**
    * **Base:** The distance on the x-axis from $O(0,0)$ to $R(68,0)$ is **$68$ units**.
    * **Height:** The shortest distance from Vertex $Q(4,8)$ to the base (the $x$-axis) is simply its perpendicular $y$-coordinate. **Height = $8$ units**.
    * **Area:**
    $$
    \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 68 \times 8 = 34 \times 8 = \mathbf{272 \text{ units}^2}
    $$
</Steps>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Area of Triangle synthesis">
    A straight line perpendicular to $3x + 4y = 12$ passes through the origin $(0, 0)$. 
    Find the area of the triangle formed by this perpendicular line, the origin, and the coordinate point where the original line $3x + 4y = 12$ intersects the $x$-axis.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    We need to solve for three vertices to find the Area.
    Vertex 1: Origin $(0, 0)$.
    
    Step 1: Find the original gradient.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    4y &= -3x + 12 \\
    y &= -\frac{3}{4}x + 3
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Gradient $m_1 = -\frac{3}{4}$.
    
    Step 2: Find the perpendicular line passing through the origin.
    Perpendicular gradient $m_2 = \frac{4}{3}$.
    Since it passes through $(0, 0)$, the equation is $y = \frac{4}{3}x$. (Vertex 1 and Vertex 2 are connected by this line).
    
    Step 3: Find Vertex 3 (where original line $3x + 4y = 12$ cuts the x-axis).
    Set $y=0$ in the original equation:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3x + 4(0) &= 12 \\
    3x &= 12 \\
    x &= 4
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Vertex 3 is $(4, 0)$.
    
    Step 4: Find Vertex 2 (intersection of original and perpendicular lines).
    We have $y = \frac{4}{3}x$. Substitute into $3x + 4y = 12$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3x + 4\left(\frac{4}{3}x\right) &= 12 \\
    3x + \frac{16}{3}x &= 12 \quad (\times 3) \\
    9x + 16x &= 36 \\
    25x &= 36 \rightarrow x = \frac{36}{25} \\
    y = \frac{4}{3}\left(\frac{36}{25}\right) &= \frac{4 \times 12}{25} = \frac{48}{25}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Vertex 2 is $\left(\frac{36}{25}, \frac{48}{25}\right)$.
    
    Step 5: Calculate Area. The triangle vertices are $O(0, 0)$, $V_2\left(\frac{36}{25}, \frac{48}{25}\right)$, and $V_3(4, 0)$.
    The base is on the x-axis from $(0, 0)$ to $(4, 0)$. Base = **$4$ units**.
    The height is the perpendicular distance from $V_2$ to the x-axis. Height = **$\frac{48}{25}$ units**.
    
    $$
    \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times 4 \times \frac{48}{25} = 2 \times \frac{48}{25} = \frac{96}{25} = \boldsymbol{3.84 \text{ units}^2}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
