## 1. Inquiry: Discovering the Shortcuts
**Inquiry Question:** *How do the rules for exponents emerge from simple multiplication?*

When we deal with monomials (single algebraic terms), we can use **Index Laws** to simplify expressions quickly. These rules are derived by expanding the exponents into their full multiplication form and counting the results.

---

## 2. Multiplication Law (Product Rule)
When multiplying terms with the **same base**, we add the exponents.
$$x^a \times x^b = x^{a+b}$$

**Investigation:**
Why does $5^2 \times 5^3 = 5^5$?
* $5^2 = 5 \times 5$
* $5^3 = 5 \times 5 \times 5$
* Total: $(5 \times 5) \times (5 \times 5 \times 5) = 5^5$.

---

## 3. Division Law (Quotient Rule)
When dividing terms with the **same base**, we subtract the exponents.
$$x^a \div x^b = x^{a-b}$$

**Example:** Simplify $y^7 \div y^3$.
* $7 - 3 = 4$
* **Result:** $y^4$.



---

## 4. Power of a Power Rule
When a power is raised to another power, we multiply the exponents.
$$(x^a)^b = x^{a \times b}$$

**Example:** Simplify $(3^2)^4$.
* $2 \times 4 = 8$
* **Result:** $3^8$.

### Q2: Dividing Variables
<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question">
    Simplify the following using the division law:
    a) $b^{10} \div b^2$
    b) $\frac{20w^8}{5w^3}$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution">
    a) $b^{10-2} = \mathbf{b^8}$
    b) $(20 \div 5) \times w^{8-3} = \mathbf{4w^5}$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
