## 1. Inquiry: Modeling the World
**Inquiry Questions:** *What does it mean to be a function? How do we model relations in real-life?* Linear functions allow us to visualize how one variable changes in relation to another. Whether it's the cost of a taxi ride or the speed of a car, these relationships can be plotted as straight lines on a **Cartesian Plane**.

---

## 2. Graphing from Patterns
A key objective is to represent linear patterns graphically. This involves three steps:
1.  **Table of Values:** Create a table showing the term number ($n$) and the term ($t$).
2.  **Ordered Pairs:** Treat each pair as a coordinate $(x, y)$, where $x$ is the position and $y$ is the value.
3.  **Plotting:** Plot these points on graph paper and connect them to form a line.



**Example:**
For the sequence $2, 3, 4, 5, 6 \dots$, the equation is $t = n + 1$.
* When $n=1, t=2 \rightarrow (1, 2)$
* When $n=2, t=3 \rightarrow (2, 3)$

---

## 3. Real-Life Linear Models
We use linear equations to describe realistic situations by identifying two components:
* **The Initial Value (y-intercept):** The starting point when $x = 0$.
* **The Rate of Change (gradient):** How much the value increases or decreases for every step forward.

**Sample Models from Outline:**
* **Gym Rental:** 40 HKD flat fee plus 2 HKD per person $\rightarrow y = 2x + 40$.
* **Shipping Package:** $50\text{g}$ for packaging plus $6\text{g}$ per flyer $\rightarrow M = 6d + 50$.

---

## 4. Distinguishing Linear vs. Non-Linear
Through investigation (often using graphing software), you must learn to distinguish a straight line from a curve. 
* **Linear:** The variable $x$ has no exponent (e.g., $y = 3x + 1$).
* **Non-Linear:** The variable $x$ is squared, cubed, or in the denominator (e.g., $y = x^2$).
