10.8 Cumulative Frequency Diagrams

When dealing with grouped continuous data, a cumulative frequency diagram (sometimes called an ogive) is an incredibly powerful visual tool. It plots the running total of frequencies against the data values, allowing us to estimate averages and the spread of the data.

1. Cumulative Frequency Tables and Diagrams

Section titled “1. Cumulative Frequency Tables and Diagrams”

To draw a cumulative frequency diagram, you first need to convert a standard frequency table into a cumulative frequency table by keeping a running total.

Example: Creating the Table

Section titled “Example: Creating the Table”

The table below shows the time taken (ttt minutes) by 80 students to complete a puzzle.

0<t≤100 < t \le 100<t≤1044
10<t≤2010 < t \le 2010<t≤20124 + 12 = 16
20<t≤3020 < t \le 3020<t≤302816 + 28 = 44
30<t≤4030 < t \le 4030<t≤402244 + 22 = 66
40<t≤5040 < t \le 5040<t≤501066 + 10 = 76

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Golden Rule of Plotting

When plotting your points, you must plot the cumulative frequency against the upper class boundary of the interval. For example, the first point is plotted at (10,4)(10, 4)(10,4), the second at (20,16)(20, 16)(20,16), and so on. Never plot against the midpoint!

Drawing the Diagram

Section titled “Drawing the Diagram”

Just like with scatter diagrams, the exam board has strict rules for drawing these:

  1. Plot the points using small, clear crosses (×\times×).
  2. Join the points with a smooth curve. Do not use a ruler to connect the points with straight lines!

2. Estimating the Median, Quartiles, and IQR

Section titled “2. Estimating the Median, Quartiles, and IQR”

Once your curve is drawn, you can use it to estimate key statistical values. To do this, you read across from the cumulative frequency (y-axis) to your curve, and then down to the data value (x-axis).

  • Total Frequency (nnn): The maximum y-value of your curve (in our example, n=80n = 80n=80).
  • Median (Q2Q_2Q2​): The value at 50%50\%50% of the total frequency. Read across from n÷2n \div 2n÷2.
  • Lower Quartile (Q1Q_1Q1​): The value at 25%25\%25% of the total frequency. Read across from n÷4n \div 4n÷4.
  • Upper Quartile (Q3Q_3Q3​): The value at 75%75\%75% of the total frequency. Read across from 3n÷43n \div 43n÷4.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The difference between the Upper and Lower Quartiles. IQR=Q3−Q1\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1IQR=Q3​−Q1​

What about Percentiles?

A percentile simply tells you the value below which a given percentage of data falls. For example, to find the 90th percentile for our 80 students, calculate 90%90\%90% of 808080 (0.9×80=720.9 \times 80 = 720.9×80=72). Read across from 727272 on the y-axis, and down to the x-axis.

Example: Reading the Curve

Section titled “Example: Reading the Curve”

Let’s use our graph of the 80 students to find the Median and IQR.

  1. Median: Half of 80 is 40. Read across from 40 on the y-axis, hit the curve, and go down. The median is roughly 28 minutes.
  2. Lower Quartile (Q1Q_1Q1​): A quarter of 80 is 20. Read across from 20 on the y-axis and down. Q1Q_1Q1​ is roughly 21.5 minutes.
  3. Upper Quartile (Q3Q_3Q3​): Three-quarters of 80 is 60. Read across from 60 on the y-axis and down. Q3Q_3Q3​ is roughly 37.5 minutes.
  4. IQR: Subtract Q1Q_1Q1​ from Q3Q_3Q3​. IQR=37.5−21.5=16 minutes\text{IQR} = 37.5 - 21.5 = 16 \text{ minutes}IQR=37.5−21.5=16 minutes

3. Estimating Probability

Section titled “3. Estimating Probability”

Because the y-axis gives us a running total of the data, we can also use cumulative frequency curves to estimate basic probabilities.

If you are asked to find the probability of a value being less than a certain amount, you simply read the cumulative frequency at that point and divide by the total number of items.

If you need the probability of a value being more than a certain amount, remember that the graph reads from the bottom up! You need to subtract the cumulative frequency reading from the total frequency first.

Example: Calculating Probability

Section titled “Example: Calculating Probability”

Using our puzzle times graph, what is the probability that a randomly selected student took more than 45 minutes to complete the puzzle?

  1. Find 45 minutes on the x-axis.
  2. Read a straight line up to the curve, and across to the y-axis. At 45 minutes, the cumulative frequency is roughly 72.
  3. This means 72 students took less than 45 minutes.
  4. To find the number who took more than 45 minutes, subtract from the total (80): 80−72=8 students80 - 72 = 8 \text{ students}80−72=8 students
  5. Write it as a probability: P(>45 mins)=880=110P(>45 \text{ mins}) = \frac{8}{80} = \frac{1}{10}P(>45 mins)=808​=101​ (or 10%)

Work backwards to find the values of a,b,ca, b, ca,b,c, and ddd.
