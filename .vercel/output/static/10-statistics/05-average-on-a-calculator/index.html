10.5 Averages on a Calculator

In Cambridge IGCSE 0607, your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC) is your most powerful tool for Paper 4 and Paper 6. When dealing with large sets of data or frequency tables, manually calculating averages is a massive waste of exam time and highly prone to arithmetic errors. You are expected to use your calculator’s statistical functions.

What Your Calculator Can Do

By entering your data into the calculator’s spreadsheet, it will instantly generate:

  • The Mean (xˉ\bar{x}xˉ)
  • The Total Sum (∑x\sum x∑x)
  • The Number of Values (nnn)
  • The Minimum, Lower Quartile (Q1Q_1Q1​), Median, Upper Quartile (Q3Q_3Q3​), and Maximum.

1. Entering Data into the GDC

Section titled “1. Entering Data into the GDC”

We will use the TI-Nspire as our primary example, as it is the most commonly used GDC for this syllabus. The process relies on the Lists & Spreadsheet application.

Step-by-Step for a Raw List of Data

Section titled “Step-by-Step for a Raw List of Data”

The simplest scenario is a raw, unorganised list of numbers. For example, the test scores of 7 students: 12,15,8,14,15,9,2012, 15, 8, 14, 15, 9, 2012,15,8,14,15,9,20.

  1. Open a new Lists & Spreadsheet document.
  2. Go to the very top row (above row 1) and name column A scores.
  3. Enter the 7 numbers straight down column A.
  4. Press Menu > 4: Statistics > 1: Stat Calculations > 1: One-Variable Statistics.
  5. The calculator asks for “Num of Lists”. Keep this as 1.
  6. A menu will pop up:
    • X1 List: Choose scores from the dropdown.
    • Frequency List: Leave this as 1. (This tells the calculator that each number in your list occurs exactly once).
  7. Press OK. The statistical results will appear in the next empty columns.

Step-by-Step for Discrete Data (with a Frequency Table)

Section titled “Step-by-Step for Discrete Data (with a Frequency Table)”

Often, data is collected in a frequency table to save space. Imagine a table showing the number of goals scored in 20 football matches.

Goals (xxx)Frequency (fff)
000333
111888
222666
333333
  1. Open a new Lists & Spreadsheet document.
  2. Go to the very top row and name column A goals and column B freq.
  3. Enter the data: type 0,1,2,30, 1, 2, 30,1,2,3 down column A, and 3,8,6,33, 8, 6, 33,8,6,3 down column B.
  4. Press Menu > 4: Statistics > 1: Stat Calculations > 1: One-Variable Statistics.
  5. Keep “Num of Lists” as 1 (we still only have one main variable, which is goals).
  6. A menu will pop up:
    • X1 List: Choose goals (click the arrow and select it from the dropdown).
    • Frequency List: Choose freq (this is crucial!).
  7. Press OK.

The 'Frequency List' Trap

If you leave the Frequency List as ‘1’ instead of changing it to your frequency column, the calculator will assume every goal amount only happened exactly once! Your mean will be completely wrong. Always double-check this setting depending on whether you are using a raw list or a frequency table.

Reading the Output

Section titled “Reading the Output”

Scroll down the results column. You need to recognise the calculator’s specific shorthand notation:

  • xˉ\bar{x}xˉ (x-bar): This is the Mean.
  • nnn: The total frequency (∑f\sum f∑f). Use this to check you haven’t missed any data!
  • MedianX: The Median.
  • Q1XQ_1XQ1​X: The Lower Quartile.
  • Q3XQ_3XQ3​X: The Upper Quartile.
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Finding the IQR

The calculator does not explicitly give you the Interquartile Range (IQR). However, it gives you Q1Q_1Q1​ and Q3Q_3Q3​. You must manually calculate the IQR by doing:

IQR=Q3X−Q1X\text{IQR} = Q_3X - Q_1XIQR=Q3​X−Q1​X

2. Estimating the Mean for Grouped Data

Section titled “2. Estimating the Mean for Grouped Data”

When data is grouped into classes (like 10<h≤2010 < h \le 2010<h≤20), you cannot type "10−2010 - 2010−20" into the calculator spreadsheet. The calculator only understands single numerical values.

To bypass this, you must calculate the midpoint of each class and type the midpoints into Column A.

Terminology: Midpoint vs. Mid-interval

In many IGCSE exam questions or textbooks, the “midpoint” is also referred to as the mid-interval value. They mean exactly the same thing: the precise middle of a given class width. For example, the mid-interval value of 10<h≤2010 < h \le 2010<h≤20 is 151515.

Step-by-Step for Grouped Continuous Data

Section titled “Step-by-Step for Grouped Continuous Data”

A botanist measures the heights (hhh cm) of 40 plants.

Height (hhh cm)Frequency (fff)Midpoint (xxx)
0<h≤100 < h \le 100<h≤10888555
10<h≤2010 < h \le 2010<h≤20202020151515
20<h≤3020 < h \le 3020<h≤30121212252525
  1. Open a new Lists & Spreadsheet document.
  2. Name column A midpoint and column B freq.
  3. Enter the midpoints (5,15,255, 15, 255,15,25) down column A.
  4. Enter the frequencies (8,20,128, 20, 128,20,12) down column B.
  5. Press Menu > 4: Statistics > 1: Stat Calculations > 1: One-Variable Statistics.
  6. Set X1 List to midpoint.
  7. Set Frequency List to freq.
  8. Press OK.

Look at the xˉ\bar{x}xˉ value. The calculator gives xˉ=16\bar{x} = 16xˉ=16. Therefore, the estimated mean is 16 cm16\text{ cm}16 cm.

Why is it an Estimate?

Remember from earlier sections: whenever you use grouped data, the mean is only an estimate. The calculator is doing the exact same ∑fx∑f\frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}∑f∑fx​ formula you learned to do manually, using the midpoints as assumptions for the exact values.

Use your GDC to find: a) The mean time b) The median time c) The interquartile range (IQR)

  1. Enter the 7 numbers straight down column A (name it times).
  2. Run 1-Var Stats. Set X1 List = times and leave Frequency List as 1.
  3. Mean (xˉ\bar{x}xˉ): 11.5711.5711.57 seconds (to 2 d.p.).
  4. Median (MedianX): 11.511.511.5 seconds.
  5. Quartiles: Q1X=11.2Q_1X = 11.2Q1​X=11.2, Q3X=12.0Q_3X = 12.0Q3​X=12.0.
  6. IQR: 12.0−11.2=0.812.0 - 11.2 = 0.812.0−11.2=0.8 seconds.
