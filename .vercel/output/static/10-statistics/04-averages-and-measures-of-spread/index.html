10.4 Averages and Measures of Spread

When analysing data, we usually want to find two things: a single value that represents the “centre” of the data (an average), and a value that tells us how stretched out or consistent the data is (a measure of spread).

The Three Averages

  • Mode: The most frequent value.
  • Median: The middle value when the data is in order.
  • Mean: The sum of all values divided by the total number of values.
Steve's Exam Wisdom

How to Remember Which is Which

It is incredibly common to mix these up in an exam! Use these tricks to remember:

  • mOde is the mOst frequent (both have the letter ‘o’).
  • medIan is the mIddle value (both have the letter ‘i’).
  • The Mean is just plain mean 😈 because I make you do the most arithmetic!

1. Averages for Individual Data

Section titled “1. Averages for Individual Data”

Let’s look at a raw list of data: 4,7,2,7,9,10,34, 7, 2, 7, 9, 10, 34,7,2,7,9,10,3.

Before doing anything with median or quartiles, always sort the data into ascending order: 2,3,4,7,7,9,102, 3, 4, 7, 7, 9, 102,3,4,7,7,9,10

The Mode

Section titled “The Mode”

The mode is the number that appears most often. In our list, the number 777 appears twice, while all others appear once.

  • Mode = 7

The Median

Section titled “The Median”

The median is the exact middle value. Cross off numbers from both ends until you reach the centre. 2,3,4,7,7,9,102, 3, 4, \mathbf{7}, 7, 9, 102,3,4,7,7,9,10

  • Median = 7

(If there are two middle numbers, add them together and divide by 2).

The Mean

Section titled “The Mean”

To find the mean (often represented by xˉ\bar{x}xˉ), add up all the numbers and divide by how many numbers there are.

Mean=2+3+4+7+7+9+107=427=6\text{Mean} = \frac{2 + 3 + 4 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 10}{7} = \frac{42}{7} = 6Mean=72+3+4+7+7+9+10​=742​=6

2. Measures of Spread: Range and Quartiles

Section titled “2. Measures of Spread: Range and Quartiles”

Averages tell you about the centre, but they don’t tell you how spread out the numbers are. For that, we use the range and the interquartile range (IQR).

Range vs. IQR

  • Range: The difference between the largest and smallest values. It is heavily affected by extreme outliers.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The spread of the middle 50% of the data. It ignores extreme outliers, making it a more reliable measure of consistency.

Calculating Quartiles

Section titled “Calculating Quartiles”

Quartiles split the data into four equal parts.

  1. Lower Quartile (Q1Q_1Q1​): The median of the lower half of the data.
  2. Median (Q2Q_2Q2​): The middle of the whole data set.
  3. Upper Quartile (Q3Q_3Q3​): The median of the upper half of the data.

Using our previous sorted list: 2,3,4,7,7,9,10\mathbf{2}, 3, \mathbf{4}, 7, \mathbf{7}, 9, \mathbf{10}2,3,4,7,7,9,10

  • Minimum = 222
  • Q1Q_1Q1​ (Lower Quartile) = 333 (middle of 2,3,42, 3, 42,3,4)
  • Median = 777
  • Q3Q_3Q3​ (Upper Quartile) = 999 (middle of 7,9,107, 9, 107,9,10)
  • Maximum = 101010
Range=Maximum−Minimum=10−2=8\text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum} = 10 - 2 = 8Range=Maximum−Minimum=10−2=8 Interquartile Range (IQR)=Q3−Q1=9−3=6\text{Interquartile} \ \text{Range} \ \text{(IQR)} = Q_3 - Q_1 = 9 - 3 = 6Interquartile Range (IQR)=Q3​−Q1​=9−3=6

4. Grouped Discrete Data

Section titled “4. Grouped Discrete Data”

When discrete data is collected in large amounts, it is often grouped into a frequency table. The data values (xxx) are exact numbers, and the frequency (fff) tells us how many times that exact number occurs.

The Mode

Section titled “The Mode”

To find the mode from a frequency table, simply look for the row with the highest frequency.

Calculating the Mean

Section titled “Calculating the Mean”

To find the mean, you must multiply each data value by its frequency (f×xf \times xf×x) to find the total for that row. Then, sum all the fxfxfx values and divide by the total frequency (∑f\sum f∑f).

Mean=∑fx∑f\text{Mean} = \frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}Mean=∑f∑fx​

Common Misconception

When dividing to find the mean of a frequency table, always divide by the Total Frequency (∑f\sum f∑f). A very common mistake is dividing by the number of rows in the table!

5. Grouped Continuous Data

Section titled “5. Grouped Continuous Data”

Continuous data is grouped into classes or intervals (e.g., 10<h≤2010 < h \le 2010<h≤20). Because we do not know the exact values inside each interval, we can only calculate an estimate of the mean.

The Modal Class

Section titled “The Modal Class”

We cannot find a single mode. Instead, we find the modal class (or modal group)—the interval with the highest frequency.

Estimating the Mean

Section titled “Estimating the Mean”

Because the exact values are unknown, we use the midpoint (xxx) of each class as a representative value for that entire group.

6. Working Backwards (Adding & Removing Data)

Section titled “6. Working Backwards (Adding & Removing Data)”

These questions test a deeper understanding of averages. They usually involve adding or removing data, or finding an unknown frequency.

Adding Data to the Mean

Section titled “Adding Data to the Mean”

The golden rule is to always calculate the TOTAL sum of the data first.

The Mean Formula Rearranged

Total Sum=Mean×Number of Values\text{Total} \ \text{Sum} = \text{Mean} \times \text{Number} \ \text{of} \ \text{Values}Total Sum=Mean×Number of Values

Example: Adding a Value A student’s mean score across 444 tests is 757575. What must they score on their 555th test to raise their mean to 808080?

  1. Find the total sum of the first 4 tests:

    4×75=3004 \times 75 = 3004×75=300

  2. Find the total sum needed for 5 tests to have a mean of 80:

    5×80=4005 \times 80 = 4005×80=400

  3. The 5th test must make up the difference:

    400−300=100400 - 300 = 100400−300=100

    They must score 100100100 on their 5th test.

Removing Data from the Mean

Section titled “Removing Data from the Mean”

The same logic applies when data is removed. Find the original total, subtract the removed value, and divide by the new number of values.

Example: Removing a Value A group of 666 friends has a mean height of 160 cm160 \text{ cm}160 cm. One friend, who is 180 cm180 \text{ cm}180 cm tall, leaves the group. Calculate the new mean height of the remaining 555 friends.

  1. Original total height of 6 friends:

    6×160=960 cm6 \times 160 = 960 \text{ cm}6×160=960 cm

  2. Subtract the friend who left:

    960−180=780 cm960 - 180 = 780 \text{ cm}960−180=780 cm

  3. Divide by the new number of friends (5):

    New Mean=7805=156 cm\text{New Mean} = \frac{780}{5} = 156 \text{ cm}New Mean=5780​=156 cm

Changing the Median and Mode

Section titled “Changing the Median and Mode”

Adding or removing extreme values often drastically shifts the mean, but its effect on the median and mode can be different.

Example: Consider the data: 2,4,4,5,82, 4, 4, 5, 82,4,4,5,8.

  • Mode = 444
  • Median = 444

If we add two new data points, 888 and 888, the new list is: 2,4,4,5,8,8,82, 4, 4, 5, 8, 8, 82,4,4,5,8,8,8.

  • The New Mode is 888 (it now appears three times).
  • The New Median is 555 (the new middle value).

A small company pays 6 employees 20,000ayear,andtheCEOispaid20,000 a year, and the CEO is paid 20,000ayear,andtheCEOispaid250,000 a year. The CEO claims the “average salary” is very high. Explain why the median would be a better average to use than the mean in this scenario.

  1. The CEO is using the mean. Because his salary ($250,000) is an extreme outlier, it drags the mean upwards dramatically, making it unrepresentative of what the standard employee actually earns.
  2. The median is a better average because it is not distorted by extreme outliers. It would correctly point to $20,000, which accurately represents the centre of the typical employees’ salaries.
