10.7 Scatter Diagrams

When we want to see if there is a relationship between two different variables (known as bivariate data), we plot them on a scatter diagram. For example, we might want to see if there is a link between the hours a student spends studying and the score they get on a test.

1. Understanding Correlation

Section titled “1. Understanding Correlation”

Correlation describes the type of relationship between the two variables. When you plot your points, the overall pattern they make tells you the correlation.

  • Positive Correlation: As one variable increases, the other variable also increases (e.g., Height and Shoe Size). The points slope upwards from left to right.
  • Negative Correlation: As one variable increases, the other variable decreases (e.g., Age of a car and its Value). The points slope downwards from left to right.
  • Zero Correlation: There is no relationship at all between the variables (e.g., Number of pets owned and Score on a maths test). The points are scattered randomly.

Strong vs. Weak

Correlation can also be described as strong or weak. If the points are clustered tightly together in a straight line, the correlation is strong. If the points follow a general trend but are widely spread out, the correlation is weak.

2. Drawing Scatter Diagrams

Section titled “2. Drawing Scatter Diagrams”

When asked to draw a scatter diagram in the exam, there are strict rules you must follow to secure your plotting marks.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Golden Rules of Plotting

  1. Use Crosses: You MUST plot points using small, neat crosses (×\times×). Do not use dots or filled circles! Examiners will deduct marks if your points are not clear crosses.
  2. Read the Scale Carefully: Do not blindly assume that one little square on the grid equals 111 unit. Common values for one small square are 1,2,5,0.1,0.2,1, 2, 5, 0.1, 0.2,1,2,5,0.1,0.2, or 0.50.50.5. Pro-tip: To find the exact value of one small square, locate two major number labels on the axis, find their difference (e.g., 40−20=2040 - 20 = 2040−20=20), count the number of small squares between them (e.g., 101010 squares), and divide the difference by the number of squares (20÷10=220 \div 10 = 220÷10=2 units per square). Be mindful of this!

3. The Line of Best Fit (By Eye)

Section titled “3. The Line of Best Fit (By Eye)”

A line of best fit (LOBF) is a straight line drawn through the center of the data points that best represents the trend.

If an exam question asks you to draw a line of best fit “by eye”, you must follow these four exact syllabus criteria:

  1. Find the Mean Point: You must calculate the mean of the xxx-values (xˉ\bar{x}xˉ) and the mean of the yyy-values (yˉ\bar{y}yˉ​).
  2. Plot the Mean Point: Plot the coordinate (xˉ,yˉ)(\bar{x}, \bar{y})(xˉ,yˉ​) on your scatter diagram.
  3. Draw a Single Ruled Line: Use a ruler! The line must pass perfectly through your plotted mean point.
  4. Balance the Points: Ensure the line extends across the full data set, and that there is a roughly even number of points on either side of the line over its entire length.

Using the Line of Best Fit

Section titled “Using the Line of Best Fit”

Once you have drawn the line of best fit, you can use it to estimate values.

Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

  • Interpolation: Estimating a value inside the range of your plotted data. This is generally reliable.
  • Extrapolation: Extending the line to estimate a value outside the range of your plotted data. This is unreliable because we don’t know if the trend continues beyond the data we have measured. Examiners love asking why an estimate is unreliable!

4. Linear Regression on a Calculator (GDC)

Section titled “4. Linear Regression on a Calculator (GDC)”

While drawing by eye is a useful skill, the IGCSE 0607 syllabus heavily tests your ability to use a Graphic Display Calculator to find the exact mathematical equation of the line of best fit. This is called the equation of linear regression, written in the form y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c.

Steps for the TI-Nspire

Section titled “Steps for the TI-Nspire”

Imagine you are given this data representing Hours Studied (xxx) vs. Test Score (yyy):

Hours (xxx)235689
Score (yyy)455570809095
  1. Open a new Lists & Spreadsheet document.
  2. Label the top of column A with your xxx-variable name (e.g., hours).
  3. Label the top of column B with your yyy-variable name (e.g., score).
  4. Enter all your data points down the columns. Ensure the pairs line up exactly across the rows!
  5. Press Menu > 4: Statistics > 1: Stat Calculations > 3: Linear Regression (mx+b).
  6. A box will pop up. Set your X List to hours and your Y List to score.
  7. Press OK.

The calculator will output a list of variables. The ones you need to write the equation are:

  • mmm: The gradient (slope) of the line.
  • bbb: The yyy-intercept (the syllabus writes this as ccc, but the calculator calls it bbb).

For the data above, the calculator gives m≈7.15m \approx 7.15m≈7.15 and b≈33.2b \approx 33.2b≈33.2, meaning your linear regression equation is:

y=7.15x+33.2y = 7.15x + 33.2y=7.15x+33.2

Section titled “Practice Questions”
  • 📝 Q1: Plotting and Scale
  • ✅ Solution 1

The scatter graph shows the heights and weights of some dogs. The x-axis (Height) has 1 large square representing 10 cm, split into 10 small squares. The y-axis (Weight) has 1 large square representing 5 kg, split into 10 small squares.

A new dog is 44 cm tall and weighs 21.5 kg. Explain exactly how many small squares along and up from the origin you would plot this cross.

  1. X-axis (Height): 10 small squares = 10 cm. Therefore, 1 small square = 10÷10=110 \div 10 = 110÷10=1 cm. To plot 44 cm, move 44 small squares along the x-axis.
  2. Y-axis (Weight): 10 small squares = 5 kg. Therefore, 1 small square = 5÷10=0.55 \div 10 = 0.55÷10=0.5 kg. To plot 21.5 kg, divide the weight by the value of one square: 21.5÷0.5=4321.5 \div 0.5 = 4321.5÷0.5=43. Move 43 small squares up the y-axis.
  3. Plot a small, neat cross (×\times×) at this exact intersection.

  • ✅ Solution 4

Using the data from Question 3, a teacher estimates the score of a student who studied for 151515 hours. State whether this estimate is reliable, and give a mathematical reason for your answer.

  1. Look at the original data table. The xxx-values (hours studied) range from 222 to 999.
  2. The value x=15x = 15x=15 is far outside the range of our given data.
  3. Answer: The estimate is unreliable.
  4. Reason: This is extrapolation. We cannot be sure that the linear trend continues beyond 9 hours (e.g., the student might get tired and their score might plateau).
