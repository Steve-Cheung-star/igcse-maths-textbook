8.2 Vectors in Two Dimensions

In mathematics, quantities are split into two categories. A scalar has only a magnitude (size), like temperature, mass, or speed. A vector has both a magnitude and a direction, like velocity or a translation from one point to another.

Vector Hierarchy

  1. Column Vectors: Represented as (xy)\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}(xy​), showing the exact horizontal and vertical steps to get from start to finish.
  2. Position Vectors: Vectors that specifically start from the origin (0,0)(0,0)(0,0).
  3. Displacement Vectors: Vectors that describe the journey between two specific points, denoted using the ray command, e.g., AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB.

1. Describing Vectors and Translations

Section titled “1. Describing Vectors and Translations”

A vector describes a journey. On a grid, we write this journey as a column vector:

(xy)\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}(xy​)
  • Top number (xxx): The horizontal movement. Positive means right; negative means left.
  • Bottom number (yyy): The vertical movement. Positive means up; negative means down.

Notation

Section titled “Notation”

Vectors can be written in three ways:

  • As a directed line segment between two points: AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB (starts at AAA, ends at BBB).
  • As a bold lowercase letter: a\mathbf{a}a (in your handwriting, you should underline it like a‾\underline{a}a​).
  • As a column vector: (43)\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}(43​).
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Points vs Vectors

Do not confuse coordinates with vectors! A coordinate (4,3)(4, 3)(4,3) tells you a specific location on the grid. A vector (43)\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}(43​) tells you a movement (go right 4, up 3), which can start from absolutely anywhere on the page.

In the diagram above:

  • a=AB→=(64)\mathbf{a} = \overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}a=AB=(64​) (Right 6, Up 4).
  • c=(−2−6)\mathbf{c} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ -6 \end{pmatrix}c=(−2−6​) (Left 2, Down 6).

2. Multiplying by a Scalar

Section titled “2. Multiplying by a Scalar”

A scalar is just a regular number. When you multiply a vector by a scalar, you are scaling its length.

Rules of Scalar Multiplication

  • Multiply by a number > 1: The vector gets longer.
  • Multiply by a fraction (between 0 and 1): The vector gets shorter.
  • Multiply by a negative number: The vector reverses its direction exactly 180∘180^\circ180∘.

Algebraically, you simply multiply both the top and bottom numbers of the column vector by the scalar:

3(2−1)=(3×23×−1)=(6−3)\begin{aligned} 3 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 \times 2 \\ 3 \times -1 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}3(2−1​)​=(3×23×−1​)=(6−3​)​
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Parallel Vectors

If one vector is a scalar multiple of another (e.g., a=(23)\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}a=(23​) and 3a=(69)3\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix}3a=(69​)), the two vectors are perfectly parallel. If the scalar is negative, they are parallel but point in opposite directions!

3. Vector Addition & Subtraction

Section titled “3. Vector Addition & Subtraction”

Vectors can be added and subtracted both algebraically (using column vectors) and geometrically (drawing them).

Algebraic Method

Section titled “Algebraic Method”

To add or subtract column vectors, simply apply the operation to the xxx values, and do the same for the yyy values.

  • Addition:
(35)+(2−1)=(3+25+(−1))=(54)\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 3 + 2 \\ 5 + (-1) \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}(35​)+(2−1​)​=(3+25+(−1)​)=(54​)​
  • Subtraction:
(42)−(−13)=(4−(−1)2−3)=(5−1)\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix} &= \begin{pmatrix} 4 - (-1) \\ 2 - 3 \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned}(42​)−(−13​)​=(4−(−1)2−3​)=(5−1​)​

Geometric Method (Head-to-Tail)

Section titled “Geometric Method (Head-to-Tail)”

To add vectors geometrically, you connect them head-to-tail.

  1. Draw the first vector.
  2. Start the tail of the second vector exactly at the head (arrow tip) of the first vector.
  3. The resultant vector (the answer) is the straight line drawn from the very start (the first tail) to the very end (the final head).

To subtract a vector, you add its negative. So, p−q\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{q}p−q is identical to p+(−q)\mathbf{p} + (-\mathbf{q})p+(−q). You draw p\mathbf{p}p, then draw q\mathbf{q}q but pointing it strictly 180∘180^\circ180∘ backwards!

  1. Find the horizontal and vertical journey from A(1,2)A(1, 2)A(1,2) to B(7,10)B(7, 10)B(7,10).
