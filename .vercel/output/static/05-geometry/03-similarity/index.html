5.3 Similarity

In geometry, it is crucial to distinguish between shapes that are congruent and shapes that are similar. Two shapes are said to be congruent if they have the exact same shape and the exact same size. Two shapes are said to be similar if they have the same shape but different sizes. Think of similarity as a mathematical enlargement or reduction; the shape can also be rotated or reflected, but its internal proportions and angles remain completely identical.

1. Proving Similarity in Triangles

Section titled “1. Proving Similarity in Triangles”

Before calculating missing lengths, you are often required to prove why two shapes are similar. For triangles, this simplifies into checking any of the following standard conditions:

  • AA (Angle-Angle) Similarity: If 2 pairs of angles are the same, the third pair must also be the same (since angles in a triangle sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘).
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side) Similarity: All 3 pairs of sides are in the exact same proportion.
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side) Similarity: 2 pairs of sides are proportional and the included angles between those sides are the same.
Steve's Exam Wisdom

State the Reason

Examiners will not award marks if you simply write “the angles are the same”. You must use the correct geometric vocabulary to justify your proof: state whether angles are alternate, corresponding, vertically opposite, or shared/common angles.

The Two Classic Triangle Configurations

Section titled “The Two Classic Triangle Configurations”

In IGCSE exams, similar triangles usually appear in one of two formats: the Nested Triangle or the Hourglass (Bowtie) Triangle. Both rely heavily on parallel line rules.

Configuration A: The Nested Triangle

Section titled “Configuration A: The Nested Triangle”

Here, a smaller triangle is drawn inside a larger one by drawing a line parallel to the base.

Triangle ADEADEADE is similar to triangle ABCABCABC (AA Similarity) because:

  1. Angle AAA is a common angle shared by both triangles.
  2. Angle ADEADEADE = Angle ABCABCABC (Corresponding angles, since line DEDEDE is parallel to line BCBCBC).

Configuration B: The Hourglass (Bowtie)

Section titled “Configuration B: The Hourglass (Bowtie)”

Here, two parallel lines form the bases, and the sides cross over each other, creating vertically opposite triangles.

Triangle PXQPXQPXQ is similar to triangle SXRSXRSXR (AA Similarity) because:

  1. Angle PXQPXQPXQ = Angle SXRSXRSXR (Vertically opposite angles).
  2. Angle QPXQPXQPX = Angle RSXRSXRSX (Alternate angles, as PQPQPQ is parallel to RSRSRS. Notice the “Z” shape mapping PPP to SSS!).

Match the Corresponding Sides Carefully

In the hourglass shape, the sides cross over. This means side PXPXPX corresponds to side SXSXSX (not RXRXRX), and side QXQXQX corresponds to side RXRXRX. Always trace the angles to find corresponding sides.

2. Calculating Lengths: Proportions and Linear Equations

Section titled “2. Calculating Lengths: Proportions and Linear Equations”

Once similarity is established, we use the Linear Scale Factor (LSF) to find missing lengths. A highly reliable method that minimises confusion is to always calculate the scale factor as a number greater than 1.

Linear Scale Factor (LSF)=Length of BIGGER shapeCorresponding length of SMALLER shape\begin{aligned} \text{Linear Scale Factor (LSF)} = \frac{\text{Length of BIGGER shape}}{\text{Corresponding length of SMALLER shape}} \end{aligned}Linear Scale Factor (LSF)=Corresponding length of SMALLER shapeLength of BIGGER shape​​
  • To find a BIGGER missing length: Take the smaller corresponding length and multiply by the LSF.
  • To find a SMALLER missing length: Take the bigger corresponding length and divide by the LSF.

At the Extended level, side lengths are often given as algebraic expressions, requiring you to set up and solve a linear equation using proportional ratios.

3. Relationships Between Lengths, Areas, and Volumes

Section titled “3. Relationships Between Lengths, Areas, and Volumes”

The most critical part of this topic is understanding how scaling a 1-dimensional length affects 2-dimensional areas and 3-dimensional volumes.

Anatomy of Scale Factors

When two solids are mathematically similar, their measurements scale based on their dimensions:

  • 1D - Linear Scale Factor (LSF): Used for length, width, height, perimeter, radius, and circumference.
  • 2D - Area Scale Factor (ASF): Area also applies to total surface area and cross-sectional area. ASF=(LSF)2\begin{aligned} \text{ASF} &= (\text{LSF})^2 \end{aligned}ASF​=(LSF)2​
  • 3D - Volume Scale Factor (VSF): Volume also applies to capacity, mass, and weight (assuming uniform density). VSF=(LSF)3\begin{aligned} \text{VSF} &= (\text{LSF})^3 \end{aligned}VSF​=(LSF)3​

Working Backwards Between Scale Factors

Section titled “Working Backwards Between Scale Factors”

You will frequently be given the areas or volumes of two similar shapes and asked to find a length. You cannot jump directly from Area to Volume. You must always return to the Linear Scale Factor first.

  • To find LSF from Area: LSF=ASF\text{LSF} = \sqrt{\text{ASF}}LSF=ASF​
  • To find LSF from Volume: LSF=VSF3\text{LSF} = \sqrt[3]{\text{VSF}}LSF=3VSF​
  • To convert Area to Volume: VSF=(ASF)3\text{VSF} = (\sqrt{\text{ASF}})^3VSF=(ASF​)3

Example 2: Working Backwards with Area Two similar rectangular badges are printed. The small badge has a width of 4 cm4\text{ cm}4 cm and an area of 12 cm212\text{ cm}^212 cm2. The large badge has an area of 108 cm2108\text{ cm}^2108 cm2. Find the width of the large badge.

  1. Find the Area Scale Factor (Big ÷\div÷ Small): ASF=10812ASF=9\begin{aligned} \text{ASF} &= \frac{108}{12} \\[1.5ex] \text{ASF} &= 9 \end{aligned}ASFASF​=12108​=9​
  2. Square root to find the Linear Scale Factor: LSF=9LSF=3\begin{aligned} \text{LSF} &= \sqrt{9} \\[1.5ex] \text{LSF} &= 3 \end{aligned}LSFLSF​=9​=3​
  3. We need the width of the larger badge, so we multiply the small width by the LSF: Large Width=4×3Large Width=12 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Large Width} &= 4 \times 3 \\[1.5ex] \text{Large Width} &= 12\text{ cm} \end{aligned}Large WidthLarge Width​=4×3=12 cm​

Answer: 12 cm12\text{ cm}12 cm

Section titled “Extended Application: Quadratics and 3D Objects”

Example 4: Areas leading to Quadratics Two mathematically similar triangles have a Linear Scale Factor of 222.
The area of the smaller triangle is given by the expression xxx.
The area of the larger triangle is given by the expression x2−12x^2 - 12x2−12.
Calculate the exact area of the smaller triangle.

  1. We know the Linear Scale Factor is 222.
  2. Therefore, the Area Scale Factor must be 22=42^2 = 422=4.
  3. This tells us that 4×(Small Area)=Large Area4 \times (\text{Small Area}) = \text{Large Area}4×(Small Area)=Large Area.
  4. Substitute the expressions to form an equation and solve: 4(x)=x2−120=x2−4x−120=(x−6)(x+2)\begin{aligned} 4(x) &= x^2 - 12 \\[1.5ex] 0 &= x^2 - 4x - 12 \\[1.5ex] 0 &= (x - 6)(x + 2) \end{aligned}4(x)00​=x2−12=x2−4x−12=(x−6)(x+2)​
  5. Solve for xxx: x=6orx=−2\begin{aligned} x = 6 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -2 \end{aligned}x=6orx=−2​
  6. Since an area cannot be negative, xxx must be 666. The area of the smaller triangle is 666.

Answer: 666
