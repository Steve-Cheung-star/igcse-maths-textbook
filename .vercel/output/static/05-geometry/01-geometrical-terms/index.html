5.1 Geometrical terms

Geometry has its own specific vocabulary. Understanding these terms is the foundation for solving complex spatial problems, proving theorems, and calculating areas and volumes.

1. Fundamental Terms

Section titled “1. Fundamental Terms”

Let’s start with the basic building blocks of space and how lines interact with each other.

  • Point: A specific location in space, marked with a dot or cross, and labelled with a capital letter (e.g., Point A). It has no size or dimension.
  • Line: A straight, continuous path that extends infinitely in both directions.
  • Plane: A perfectly flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely (like a flat sheet of paper).
  • Vertex (plural: Vertices): A point where two or more lines or edges meet (corners).
  • Parallel: Lines on the same plane that never meet, having the same gradient. Indicated by matching arrows on the lines.
  • Perpendicular: Two lines that intersect at exactly a right angle (90°).
  • Perpendicular Bisector: A line that cuts another line segment exactly in half at a 90° angle.
  • Bearing: An angle measured clockwise from North (000°), usually given as a three-digit number. Used for navigation.

2. Angles

Section titled “2. Angles”
  • Acute Angle: An angle strictly less than 90°.
  • Right Angle: An angle that is exactly 90°.
  • Obtuse Angle: An angle greater than 90° but strictly less than 180°.
  • Reflex Angle: An angle greater than 180° but strictly less than 360°.
  • Interior Angle: An angle inside a polygon.
  • Exterior Angle: An angle outside a polygon, created by extending one of its sides. (Interior + Exterior on the same vertex always = 180°).

3. Triangles

Section titled “3. Triangles”

A polygon with three edges and three vertices. The sum of interior angles is always 180°.

  • Equilateral: All 3 sides are equal in length, and all 3 interior angles are exactly 60°.
  • Isosceles: Has 2 equal sides. The two base angles opposite the equal sides are also equal.
  • Scalene: All 3 sides have different lengths, and all 3 angles are different.
  • Right-Angled: Contains exactly one 90° right angle. The longest side opposite the right angle is called the hypotenuse.

4. Special Quadrilaterals

Section titled “4. Special Quadrilaterals”

A polygon with four edges and four vertices. The sum of interior angles is always 360°. Pay close attention to the equal side notches (tick marks) and parallel arrows.

  • Square: A regular quadrilateral. All 4 sides equal length, all 4 angles are 90°.
  • Rectangle: Opposite sides are equal in length and parallel. All 4 angles are 90°.
  • Parallelogram: Opposite sides are parallel and equal in length. Opposite angles are equal.
  • Rhombus: A “pushed over” square. All 4 sides are equal in length. Opposite angles are equal. The diagonals bisect each other at a 90° angle.
  • Trapezium: Has exactly one pair of parallel sides.
  • Isosceles Trapezium: A trapezium where the non-parallel sides are equal in length, giving it a line of symmetry down the middle. Base angles are equal.
  • Kite: Has two pairs of adjacent (next to each other) sides that are equal in length. The diagonals intersect at a 90° angle.

5. Polygons

Section titled “5. Polygons”

A polygon is any closed 2D shape with straight sides.

  • Regular Polygon: All sides are equal in length, and all interior angles are equal.
  • Irregular Polygon: Sides and angles are of different sizes.

6. Vocabulary of a Circle

Section titled “6. Vocabulary of a Circle”

It is much easier to digest the parts of a circle when we split them into lines (lengths) and areas (regions).

Circle Lines and Points

Section titled “Circle Lines and Points”
  • Centre: The exact middle point. Every point on the circumference is an equal distance from the centre.
  • Radius (plural radii): A straight line from the centre to the circumference.
  • Diameter: A straight line passing exactly through the centre, touching the circumference at both ends. It is exactly twice the length of the radius (D=2rD = 2rD=2r).
  • Circumference: The distance all the way around the outside edge of the circle (its perimeter).
  • Chord: A straight line connecting any two points on the circumference (it does not have to pass through the centre).
  • Tangent: A straight line that touches the outside of the circle at exactly one point, never crossing inside.

Circle Areas and Arcs

Section titled “Circle Areas and Arcs”
  • Semicircle: Exactly half of a circle (created by a diameter).
  • Arc: A portion of the circumference. A minor arc is the shorter path between two points; a major arc is the longer path.
  • Sector: A region enclosed by two radii and an arc. Think of it like a slice of pizza or pie.
  • Segment: A region enclosed by a chord and an arc. The chord cuts the circle into a minor segment and a major segment.

7. Solids (3D Shapes)

Section titled “7. Solids (3D Shapes)”

Three-dimensional shapes possess volume and surface area.

  • Face: A flat 2D surface on a solid object.
  • Edge: The straight line segment where two faces meet.
  • Vertex: The corner point where edges meet.
  • Cube: 6 identical square faces. All edges are equal.
  • Cuboid: 6 rectangular faces (a 3D rectangle/box).
  • Prism: A solid with the exact same cross-section all the way through its length (e.g., a Toblerone bar is a triangular prism).
  • Cylinder: A specific type of prism with a circular cross-section.
  • Pyramid: A base with straight triangular faces that meet at a single top point (apex).
  • Tetrahedron: A specific pyramid with a triangular base (4 triangular faces in total).
  • Cone: A circular base tapering smoothly to a single point (apex).
  • Sphere: A perfectly round 3D object (like a ball). Every point on the surface is an equal distance from the centre.
  • Hemisphere: Exactly half of a sphere.

The Frustum

Section titled “The Frustum”

A frustum is the portion of a cone or pyramid that remains after its top (apex) has been cut off by a plane perfectly parallel to its base.
