5.5 Angles

Angles are the foundation of geometric reasoning. To succeed in IGCSE Maths, you must be able to confidently calculate unknown angles and provide rigorous mathematical reasons for your answers using the correct terminology.

1. Fundamental Angle Properties

Section titled “1. Fundamental Angle Properties”

Before dealing with complex shapes, you need to master the basic rules of angles. You must know these properties perfectly, as they will be the “reasons” you write down in the exam.

Three-Letter Notation

In IGCSE questions, angles are frequently named using three letters, such as angle ABCABCABC or ∠ABC\angle ABC∠ABC. The middle letter always represents the vertex (the point) where the angle is formed. The lines forming the angle are ABABAB and BCBCBC.

Key Angle Rules

Section titled “Key Angle Rules”
  • Angles on a straight line: Angles that meet at a point on a straight line always sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘.
  • Angles at a point: Angles that complete a full circle around a single point always sum to 360∘360^\circ360∘.
  • Vertically opposite angles: When two straight lines intersect, the angles opposite each other are exactly equal.

2. Angles in Parallel Lines

Section titled “2. Angles in Parallel Lines”

When a straight line (called a transversal) cuts across two or more parallel lines, it creates specific pairs of angles. Parallel lines are indicated by matching arrows on the lines.

Parallel Line Rules

  • Alternate angles are equal: They form a ‘Z’ shape. They are on opposite sides of the transversal and between the parallel lines.
  • Corresponding angles are equal: They form an ‘F’ shape. They are in the same relative position at each intersection.
  • Co-interior angles sum to 180°: They form a ‘C’ shape. They are on the same side of the transversal and between the parallel lines. These are also known as supplementary angles.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

Use the Correct Terminology

Examiners are extremely strict about terminology. If you write “Z-angle” or “F-angle” as your reason, you will score zero marks for the explanation. You must write “alternate angle”, “corresponding angle”, or “co-interior angles sum to 180°“.

3. Types of Triangles and their Angles

Section titled “3. Types of Triangles and their Angles”

A triangle is a 3-sided polygon. No matter the type of triangle, its internal angles will always sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘.

Exterior Angle Theorem

The exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the two opposite interior angles. This can act as a fantastic shortcut in complex exam questions!

Equilateral Triangles

Section titled “Equilateral Triangles”
  • All three sides are equal in length.
  • All three interior angles are exactly 60∘60^\circ60∘.
  • Lines of symmetry: 3.

Isosceles Triangles

Section titled “Isosceles Triangles”
  • Two sides are equal in length.
  • The angles opposite those equal sides (often called the “base angles”) are exactly equal.
  • Line of symmetry: 1.

4. Quadrilaterals and Concave Shapes

Section titled “4. Quadrilaterals and Concave Shapes”

A quadrilateral is any 4-sided shape. The sum of the interior angles of any quadrilateral is 360∘360^\circ360∘.

Convex vs. Concave

Section titled “Convex vs. Concave”

Most quadrilaterals you deal with (squares, rectangles, parallelograms, trapeziums, kites) are convex. This means all their interior angles are less than 180∘180^\circ180∘ and all vertices point outwards.

A concave quadrilateral (often resembling a dart or arrowhead) has one internal angle that is greater than 180∘180^\circ180∘ (a reflex angle). Its interior angles still sum to 360∘360^\circ360∘.

5. Angles in Polygons

Section titled “5. Angles in Polygons”

A polygon is any closed 2D shape with straight sides. Polygons can be regular (all sides and angles are equal) or irregular (sides and angles differ).

Interior and Exterior Angles

Section titled “Interior and Exterior Angles”
  • Interior Angle: The angle inside the polygon at a vertex.
  • Exterior Angle: The angle formed outside the polygon if you extend one of its sides in a straight line.

The Golden Rule of Polygon Angles

For absolutely any polygon (regular or irregular), at any given vertex:

Interior Angle+Exterior Angle=180∘\text{Interior Angle} + \text{Exterior Angle} = 180^\circInterior Angle+Exterior Angle=180∘

Calculating Angle Sums

Section titled “Calculating Angle Sums”

For a polygon with nnn sides:

  1. Sum of Interior Angles: Sum=(n−2)×180∘\text{Sum} = (n - 2) \times 180^\circSum=(n−2)×180∘ (This formula works because you can split an nnn-sided polygon into (n−2)(n - 2)(n−2) triangles).
  2. Sum of Exterior Angles: Sum=360∘\text{Sum} = 360^\circSum=360∘ (If you walk around the perimeter of any polygon, you will turn a full circle by the time you return to the start).

Regular Polygons

Section titled “Regular Polygons”

Because regular polygons share the total angle sum equally among all their vertices, you can use these powerful shortcuts:

  • One Exterior Angle: 360∘n\frac{360^\circ}{n}n360∘​
  • One Interior Angle: 180∘−Exterior Angle180^\circ - \text{Exterior Angle}180∘−Exterior Angle

Common Misconception

Many students try to find the interior angle of a regular polygon by using (n−2)×180n\frac{(n-2) \times 180}{n}n(n−2)×180​. While correct, this is prone to algebraic errors. It is almost always faster to find the exterior angle first (360n\frac{360}{n}n360​) and then subtract it from 180∘180^\circ180∘!

6. Structured Worked Examples

Section titled “6. Structured Worked Examples”

Examiners love to combine parallel lines, triangles, and straight lines into a single complex figure. You must build your answer step-by-step.

Example Task: In the diagram above, the top and bottom lines are parallel. An isosceles triangle rests on the bottom parallel line. A transversal line intersects both parallel lines and forms the left side of the triangle. The obtuse angle between the top parallel line and the transversal is 116∘116^\circ116∘. Find the internal angles xxx, yyy, and zzz, giving reasons.

Solution:

  1. Find yyy: The 116∘116^\circ116∘ angle and the interior angle bounded by the top parallel line and the transversal lie on a straight line. The acute angle inside the parallel lines at the top intersection is 180∘−116∘=64∘180^\circ - 116^\circ = 64^\circ180∘−116∘=64∘. Angle yyy and this 64∘64^\circ64∘ angle are alternate angles (forming a Z-shape). y=64∘y = 64^\circy=64∘ Reason: Angles on a straight line sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘, and alternate angles are equal.
  2. Find zzz: The triangle is isosceles (shown by the tick marks on the two sides). Therefore, the base angles are equal. z=y=64∘z = y = 64^\circz=y=64∘ Reason: Base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal.
  3. Find xxx: Angles in a triangle always sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘. x=180∘−(64∘+64∘)=180∘−128∘=52∘x = 180^\circ - (64^\circ + 64^\circ) = 180^\circ - 128^\circ = 52^\circx=180∘−(64∘+64∘)=180∘−128∘=52∘ Reason: Angles in a triangle sum to 180∘180^\circ180∘.

A triangle is drawn between two parallel lines. The triangle is isosceles. One of the base angles inside the triangle is 68∘68^\circ68∘. Calculate the value of the reflex angle ppp at the top vertex.

  1. The triangle is isosceles, so the other base angle is also 68∘68^\circ68∘.
  2. The internal angle of the triangle at the top vertex is: 180∘−(68∘+68∘)=180∘−136∘=44∘180^\circ - (68^\circ + 68^\circ) = 180^\circ - 136^\circ = 44^\circ180∘−(68∘+68∘)=180∘−136∘=44∘
  3. The internal angle and the reflex angle ppp form a full circle (360∘360^\circ360∘) around that point.
  4. Therefore, the reflex angle ppp is: p=360∘−44∘=316∘p = 360^\circ - 44^\circ = 316^\circp=360∘−44∘=316∘
