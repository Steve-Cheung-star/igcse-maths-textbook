6.4 Surface Area and Volume

Moving from 2D to 3D introduces two distinct measurements: Volume (the amount of 3D space inside an object) and Surface Area (the total 2D area of all the exposed outer surfaces).

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Formula Sheet is Your Friend

In the IGCSE Extended exam, you are given the formulas for the curved surface area of cylinders and cones, the surface area of spheres, and the volumes of prisms, pyramids, cylinders, cones, and spheres. You do not need to memorise them all, but you must know how to apply them and how to piece them together for compound shapes!

1. Prisms and Cylinders (The “Extruded” Shapes)

Section titled “1. Prisms and Cylinders (The “Extruded” Shapes)”

A prism is any 3D solid that has a perfectly uniform (constant) cross-section running all the way through its length. If you slice it like a loaf of bread, every slice looks exactly the same.

A cylinder is simply a prism with a circular cross-section.

General Prism Formulas

  • Volume: Volume=Area of cross-section×length\text{Volume} = \text{Area of cross-section} \times \text{length}Volume=Area of cross-section×length
  • Surface Area: Calculate the area of the two identical ends, and add the areas of all the rectangular sides connecting them. Total SA=2×(Area of cross-section)+(Perimeter of cross-section×length)\text{Total SA} = 2 \times (\text{Area of cross-section}) + (\text{Perimeter of cross-section} \times \text{length})Total SA=2×(Area of cross-section)+(Perimeter of cross-section×length)

Cylinder-Specific Formulas

While a cylinder follows the general rules of a prism, its circular nature gives it specific algebraic formulas that you should be comfortable with:

  • Base Area (Cross-section): πr2\pi r^2πr2
  • Curved Surface Area (CSA): 2πrh2\pi r h2πrh
  • Total Surface Area (TSA): 2πr2+2πrh2\pi r^2 + 2\pi r h2πr2+2πrh
  • Volume: πr2h\pi r^2 hπr2h

Example: Volume of a Cylinder

Section titled “Example: Volume of a Cylinder”

Calculate the exact volume of a cylinder with a radius of 6 cm6\text{ cm}6 cm and a height of 10 cm10\text{ cm}10 cm. Give your answer in terms of π\piπ.

  1. Identify the area of the base (a circle): Base Area=πr2=π×62=36π cm2\text{Base Area} = \pi r^2 = \pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi\text{ cm}^2Base Area=πr2=π×62=36π cm2
  2. Multiply by the height to find the volume: Volume=36π×10=360π cm3\text{Volume} = 36\pi \times 10 = 360\pi\text{ cm}^3Volume=36π×10=360π cm3

2. Pyramids and Cones (The “Pointy” Shapes)

Section titled “2. Pyramids and Cones (The “Pointy” Shapes)”

Any 3D shape that tapers from a flat base to a single point (an apex) has exactly one-third the volume of a prism with the same base and height. A cone is simply a pyramid with a circular base.

General Pyramid & Cone Volume

  • Volume: Volume=13×Base Area×Perpendicular Height\text{Volume} = \frac{1}{3} \times \text{Base Area} \times \text{Perpendicular Height}Volume=31​×Base Area×Perpendicular Height

The Square Pyramid

Section titled “The Square Pyramid”

A square pyramid has a square base. Its volume is 13×(x×x)×h\frac{1}{3} \times (x \times x) \times h31​×(x×x)×h, where xxx is the side length of the square base.

Cones and the Pythagoras Trap

Section titled “Cones and the Pythagoras Trap”

The surface area of a cone is made of two parts: the flat circular base and the curved surface wrapped around the side.

Height vs. Slant Height

For the volume, you must use the perpendicular height (hhh). For the curved surface area, you must use the slanted height (lll). Examiners will often give you one, and force you to calculate the other using Pythagoras’ Theorem:

l2=r2+h2l^2 = r^2 + h^2l2=r2+h2

Cone-Specific Formulas

Just like the cylinder, the circular properties of a cone mean it has precise formulas you need to use:

  • Base Area: πr2\pi r^2πr2
  • Curved Surface Area (CSA): πrl\pi r lπrl (where lll is the slant height)
  • Total Surface Area (TSA): πr2+πrl\pi r^2 + \pi r lπr2+πrl
  • Volume: 13πr2h\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h31​πr2h

3. Spheres and Hemispheres

Section titled “3. Spheres and Hemispheres”

Spheres are perfectly symmetrical 3D objects.

  • Volume of a Sphere: V=43πr3V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3V=34​πr3
  • Surface Area of a Sphere: SA=4πr2SA = 4 \pi r^2SA=4πr2

The Hemisphere Surface Area Trap

Section titled “The Hemisphere Surface Area Trap”

A hemisphere is exactly half of a sphere.

  • The volume is simply half the volume of a sphere: 23πr3\frac{2}{3} \pi r^332​πr3.
  • The surface area, however, is a classic exam trap! When you cut a sphere in half, you expose a brand new flat circular base.

Hemisphere Total Surface Area

Total SA of a solid hemisphere = Curved SA + Flat Base Area

Total SA=(2πr2)+(πr2)=3πr2\text{Total SA} = (2\pi r^2) + (\pi r^2) = 3\pi r^2Total SA=(2πr2)+(πr2)=3πr2

Always read the question carefully to check if the hemisphere is “solid” (needs the base area added) or “open/hollow” like a bowl (curved area only).

4. Advanced Concepts: Working Backwards & Melting Shapes

Section titled “4. Advanced Concepts: Working Backwards & Melting Shapes”

Examiners love to test your algebraic skills alongside your geometric knowledge. They will frequently give you the volume or surface area and ask you to work backwards to find a missing dimension (like the radius).

Another common scenario is conservation of volume, where one shape is melted down to form another. The key here is setting up an equation where Volumeold=Volumenew\text{Volume}_{\text{old}} = \text{Volume}_{\text{new}}Volumeold​=Volumenew​.

Example: Working Backwards

Section titled “Example: Working Backwards”

The surface area of a sphere is 144π cm2144\pi\text{ cm}^2144π cm2. Calculate its radius.

  1. Write down the surface area formula: SA=4πr2SA = 4\pi r^2SA=4πr2
  2. Substitute the known value: 144π=4πr2144\pi = 4\pi r^2144π=4πr2
  3. Cancel π\piπ from both sides: 144=4r2144 = 4r^2144=4r2
  4. Divide by 4 and square root: 36=r2  ⟹  r=6 cm36 = r^2 \implies r = 6\text{ cm}36=r2⟹r=6 cm

Example: Melting Shapes

Section titled “Example: Melting Shapes”

Three identical solid metal spheres, each with a radius of 2 cm2\text{ cm}2 cm, are melted down to form a single solid cone with a base radius of 4 cm4\text{ cm}4 cm. Calculate the perpendicular height of the cone.

  1. Calculate the total volume of the 3 spheres: Volspheres=3×(43πr3)=3×(43π×23)=4π×8=32π cm3\text{Vol}_{\text{spheres}} = 3 \times \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \right) = 3 \times \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi \times 2^3 \right) = 4 \pi \times 8 = 32\pi\text{ cm}^3Volspheres​=3×(34​πr3)=3×(34​π×23)=4π×8=32π cm3
  2. Set this equal to the volume formula of the cone: Volcone=13πr2h\text{Vol}_{\text{cone}} = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 hVolcone​=31​πr2h 32π=13π(4)2h32\pi = \frac{1}{3} \pi (4)^2 h32π=31​π(4)2h
  3. Solve for hhh: Cancel π\piπ and simplify: 32=13×16×h32 = \frac{1}{3} \times 16 \times h32=31​×16×h 32=16h332 = \frac{16h}{3}32=316h​ 96=16h  ⟹  h=6 cm96 = 16h \implies h = 6\text{ cm}96=16h⟹h=6 cm
