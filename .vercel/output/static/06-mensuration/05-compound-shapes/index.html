6.5 Compound Shapes and Parts of Shapes

In the real world, objects are rarely perfect, isolated spheres or basic cylinders. They are usually combinations of multiple shapes (compound shapes) or shapes that have had pieces removed (parts of shapes).

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Golden Rules of Compound Shapes

  1. Area and Volume are additive/subtractive: You can simply add the areas/volumes of the individual parts together, or subtract the missing piece from the whole.
  2. Perimeter and Surface Area are tricky: You cannot just add the perimeters or surface areas together. You must only calculate the exposed outer boundaries. Internal faces where shapes join are hidden and must be ignored!

1. 2D Compound Shapes and Parts of Shapes

Section titled “1. 2D Compound Shapes and Parts of Shapes”

A 2D compound shape is made by joining basic shapes (rectangles, triangles, semi-circles) together, or by cutting one out of another.

Perimeter

Section titled “Perimeter”

The perimeter is the total distance around the outside edge of the shape. If two shapes are joined, the shared internal line is no longer on the outside, so do not include it.

Area

Section titled “Area”

To find the area, either:

  • Add: Split the compound shape into simpler shapes, find their individual areas, and add them up.
  • Subtract: Find the area of the overall bounding shape and subtract the area of the missing “cut-out” section.

Example 1: A Shape with a Cut-out

Section titled “Example 1: A Shape with a Cut-out”

A rectangular metal plate measures 10 cm by 8 cm. A semi-circle with a diameter of 6 cm is cut out from one of the longer sides. Calculate the exact area and perimeter of the remaining plate.

  1. Area Calculation (Subtract):

    Area of full rectangle=10×8=80 cm2Area of full circle (r=3)=π×32=9πArea of semi-circle cut-out=12×9π=4.5π cm2Total Area=(80−4.5π) cm2\begin{aligned} \text{Area of full rectangle} &= 10 \times 8 \\ &= 80 \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Area of full circle } (r=3) &= \pi \times 3^2 \\ &= 9\pi \\ \text{Area of semi-circle cut-out} &= \frac{1}{2} \times 9\pi \\ &= 4.5\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Total Area} &= (80 - 4.5\pi) \text{ cm}^2 \end{aligned}Area of full rectangleArea of full circle (r=3)Area of semi-circle cut-outTotal Area​=10×8=80 cm2=π×32=9π=21​×9π=4.5π cm2=(80−4.5π) cm2​

  2. Perimeter Calculation (Add the outside edges): The semi-circle ate into the 10 cm side. The diameter is 6 cm, leaving 4 cm of straight edge on that top side (i.e., 2 cm on each side of the gap). Three full straight edges remain: 8 cm, 10 cm, and 8 cm.

    Curved edge (arc)=12×π×6=3π cmSum of straight edges=8+10+8+4=30 cmTotal Perimeter=(30+3π) cm\begin{aligned} \text{Curved edge (arc)} &= \frac{1}{2} \times \pi \times 6 \\ &= 3\pi \text{ cm} \\ \text{Sum of straight edges} &= 8 + 10 + 8 + 4 \\ &= 30 \text{ cm} \\ \text{Total Perimeter} &= (30 + 3\pi) \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Curved edge (arc)Sum of straight edgesTotal Perimeter​=21​×π×6=3π cm=8+10+8+4=30 cm=(30+3π) cm​

2. 3D Compound Solids and Parts of Solids

Section titled “2. 3D Compound Solids and Parts of Solids”

When dealing with 3D compound solids, the same principles apply, but surface area requires careful visualization.

Hidden Faces in Surface Area

If a cone is sitting on top of a cylinder, the flat circular base of the cone and the top circular face of the cylinder are touching. They are now inside the new solid. When calculating Total Surface Area, you must exclude these hidden faces and only calculate the exposed outer faces!

Example 2: Compound Solid Surface Area

Section titled “Example 2: Compound Solid Surface Area”

A solid is made by attaching a hemisphere of radius 5 cm to one end of a cylinder of radius 5 cm and height 12 cm. Calculate the exact total surface area.

  1. Identify the exposed faces: The solid has three exposed parts: the curved surface of the hemisphere, the curved surface of the cylinder, and the flat circular bottom of the cylinder.

  2. Calculate individual exposed areas:

    Curved area of hemisphere=12×4π(5)2=50π cm2Curved area of cylinder=2π(5)(12)=120π cm2Flat bottom base=π(5)2=25π cm2Total Surface Area=50π+120π+25π=195π cm2\begin{aligned} \text{Curved area of hemisphere} &= \frac{1}{2} \times 4\pi (5)^2 \\ &= 50\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Curved area of cylinder} &= 2\pi (5)(12) \\ &= 120\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Flat bottom base} &= \pi (5)^2 \\ &= 25\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Total Surface Area} &= 50\pi + 120\pi + 25\pi \\ &= 195\pi \text{ cm}^2 \end{aligned}Curved area of hemisphereCurved area of cylinderFlat bottom baseTotal Surface Area​=21​×4π(5)2=50π cm2=2π(5)(12)=120π cm2=π(5)2=25π cm2=50π+120π+25π=195π cm2​

3. The Frustum (Truncated Solids)

Section titled “3. The Frustum (Truncated Solids)”

A frustum is what remains when you slice the top off a cone or a pyramid with a cut parallel to the base. It looks like a bucket or a lampshade.

To find the volume or surface area of a frustum, treat it as a large cone with a smaller cone chopped off the top.

Vfrustum=Vlarge cone−Vsmall cone\begin{aligned} V_{\text{frustum}} &= V_{\text{large cone}} - V_{\text{small cone}} \end{aligned}Vfrustum​​=Vlarge cone​−Vsmall cone​​

Often, the exam question will not give you the height of the small removed cone. You must use similar triangles (scale factors) to find it.

Example 3: Volume of a Frustum

Section titled “Example 3: Volume of a Frustum”

A large cone has a base radius of 8 cm and a total height of 20 cm. A smaller cone is cut from the top parallel to the base, at a height of 10 cm from the bottom. Find the exact volume of the resulting frustum.

  1. Find the dimensions of the small (removed) cone:

    Height of small cone=20−10=10 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Height of small cone} &= 20 - 10 \\ &= 10 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Height of small cone​=20−10=10 cm​

  2. Use similar triangles to find the small cone’s radius (rrr):

    Ratio of heights=1020=12Radius of small cone (r)=8×12=4 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Ratio of heights} &= \frac{10}{20} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \\ \text{Radius of small cone } (r) &= 8 \times \frac{1}{2} \\ &= 4 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Ratio of heightsRadius of small cone (r)​=2010​=21​=8×21​=4 cm​

  3. Calculate the Volumes:

    Vlarge=13πR2H=13π(82)(20)=1280π3Vsmall=13πr2h=13π(42)(10)=160π3Vfrustum=1280π3−160π3=1120π3 cm3\begin{aligned} V_{\text{large}} &= \frac{1}{3}\pi R^2 H \\ &= \frac{1}{3}\pi (8^2)(20) \\ &= \frac{1280\pi}{3} \\ V_{\text{small}} &= \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \\ &= \frac{1}{3}\pi (4^2)(10) \\ &= \frac{160\pi}{3} \\ V_{\text{frustum}} &= \frac{1280\pi}{3} - \frac{160\pi}{3} \\ &= \frac{1120\pi}{3} \text{ cm}^3 \end{aligned}Vlarge​Vsmall​Vfrustum​​=31​πR2H=31​π(82)(20)=31280π​=31​πr2h=31​π(42)(10)=3160π​=31280π​−3160π​=31120π​ cm3​

A shape is formed by attaching an equilateral triangle of side length 6 cm to the top of a square of side length 6 cm. a) Calculate the exact perimeter of the shape. b) Calculate the area of the shape. Give your answer to 3 significant figures.

    Perimeter=(3×6)+(2×6)=18+12=30 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Perimeter} &= (3 \times 6) + (2 \times 6) \\ &= 18 + 12 \\ &= 30 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Perimeter​=(3×6)+(2×6)=18+12=30 cm​

  2. Part b - Area:

A solid wooden cylinder has a radius of 4 cm and a length of 10 cm. A cylindrical hole of radius 2 cm is drilled completely through the center of the wood, parallel to its length. Calculate the exact total surface area of the resulting object in terms of π\piπ.

  1. Identify the exposed surfaces:

    • The outer curved surface of the large cylinder.
    • The inner curved surface of the drilled hole (this is a newly exposed surface!).
    • The two flat ends (which are now rings/annuli, not solid circles).

  2. Calculate individual exposed areas:

    Outer Curved Area =2π(4)(10)=80π cm2Inner Curved Area =2π(2)(10)=40π cm2Area of one ring =π(42)−π(22)=16π−4π=12π cm2Area of both rings =2×12π=24π cm2Total Surface Area =80π+40π+24π=144π cm2\begin{aligned} \text{Outer Curved Area } &= 2\pi(4)(10) \\ &= 80\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Inner Curved Area } &= 2\pi(2)(10) \\ &= 40\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Area of one ring } &= \pi(4^2) - \pi(2^2) \\ &= 16\pi - 4\pi \\ &= 12\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Area of both rings } &= 2 \times 12\pi \\ &= 24\pi \text{ cm}^2 \\ \text{Total Surface Area } &= 80\pi + 40\pi + 24\pi \\ &= 144\pi \text{ cm}^2 \end{aligned}Outer Curved Area Inner Curved Area Area of one ring Area of both rings Total Surface Area ​=2π(4)(10)=80π cm2=2π(2)(10)=40π cm2=π(42)−π(22)=16π−4π=12π cm2=2×12π=24π cm2=80π+40π+24π=144π cm2​
