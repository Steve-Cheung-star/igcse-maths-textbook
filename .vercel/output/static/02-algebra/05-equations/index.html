2.5 Equations

An equation is a mathematical statement that two expressions are equal. Solving an equation means finding the value(s) of the unknown variable that make the statement true. This section covers everything from basic linear equations to complex algebraic fractions and using your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC).

1. Linear Equations

Section titled “1. Linear Equations”

Linear equations are equations where the highest power of the unknown variable is 111 (e.g., xxx, not x2x^2x2). The goal is to isolate the variable on one side of the equals sign using inverse operations.

Basic Linear Equations

Section titled “Basic Linear Equations”

Example 1: Solve 3x+4=103x + 4 = 103x+4=10

3x=10−43x=6x=2\begin{aligned} 3x &= 10 - 4 \\ 3x &= 6 \\ x &= 2 \end{aligned}3x3xx​=10−4=6=2​

Equations with Brackets

Section titled “Equations with Brackets”

Expand the brackets first, then collect like terms.

Example 2: Solve 5−2x=3(x+7)5 - 2x = 3(x + 7)5−2x=3(x+7)

5−2x=3x+21\begin{aligned} 5 - 2x &= 3x + 21 \end{aligned}5−2x​=3x+21​

Move all xxx terms to one side, and numbers to the other. To avoid negative coefficients, add 2x2x2x to both sides:

5−21=3x+2x−16=5xx=−165(or −3.2)\begin{aligned} 5 - 21 &= 3x + 2x \\ -16 &= 5x \\ x &= -\frac{16}{5} \quad \text{(or } -3.2\text{)} \end{aligned}5−21−16x​=3x+2x=5x=−516​(or −3.2)​

2. Fractional Equations

Section titled “2. Fractional Equations”

Equations with fractions require you to eliminate the denominators. You can do this by finding a common denominator or using cross-multiplication (if it is a single fraction equal to a single fraction).

Method 1: Cross-Multiplication

Section titled “Method 1: Cross-Multiplication”

Use this when you have one fraction strictly equal to another.

Example: Solve x2=2x+13\frac{x}{2} = \frac{2x + 1}{3}2x​=32x+1​

Multiply the numerator of one side by the denominator of the other:

3(x)=2(2x+1)3x=4x+2−2=x\begin{aligned} 3(x) &= 2(2x + 1) \\ 3x &= 4x + 2 \\ -2 &= x \end{aligned}3(x)3x−2​=2(2x+1)=4x+2=x​

Variables in the Denominator

The same cross-multiplication rule applies even if the variable is in the denominator!

Solve xx+2=4x−6\frac{x}{x + 2} = \frac{4}{x - 6}x+2x​=x−64​

x(x−6)=4(x+2)x2−6x=4x+8x2−10x−8=0(This forms a quadratic equation to solve later!)\begin{aligned} x(x - 6) &= 4(x + 2) \\ x^2 - 6x &= 4x + 8 \\ x^2 - 10x - 8 &= 0 \quad \text{(This forms a quadratic equation to solve later!)} \end{aligned}x(x−6)x2−6xx2−10x−8​=4(x+2)=4x+8=0(This forms a quadratic equation to solve later!)​

Method 2: Finding a Common Denominator

Section titled “Method 2: Finding a Common Denominator”

Use this when adding or subtracting multiple fractions in an equation.

Example: Solve x+23+2x−12=1\frac{x + 2}{3} + \frac{2x - 1}{2} = 13x+2​+22x−1​=1

The Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of 3 and 2 is 6. Multiply every single term in the equation by 6 to eliminate the fractions.

6×(x+23)+6×(2x−12)=6×(1)2(x+2)+3(2x−1)=62x+4+6x−3=68x+1=68x=5x=58\begin{aligned} 6 \times \left(\frac{x + 2}{3}\right) + 6 \times \left(\frac{2x - 1}{2}\right) &= 6 \times (1) \\ 2(x + 2) + 3(2x - 1) &= 6 \\ 2x + 4 + 6x - 3 &= 6 \\ 8x + 1 &= 6 \\ 8x &= 5 \\ x &= \frac{5}{8} \end{aligned}6×(3x+2​)+6×(22x−1​)2(x+2)+3(2x−1)2x+4+6x−38x+18xx​=6×(1)=6=6=6=5=85​​

3. Simultaneous Equations

Section titled “3. Simultaneous Equations”

Simultaneous equations involve two or more equations with two or more unknowns (usually xxx and yyy). You must find the pair of values that makes both equations true at the same time. The most common algebraic methods are Elimination and Substitution.

Example (Elimination): Solve the simultaneous equations:

  1. 3x+2y=123x + 2y = 123x+2y=12
  2. 4x−y=54x - y = 54x−y=5

Step 1: Match the coefficients of one variable. Let’s multiply equation (2) by 2 to match the yyy terms.

(2) ×2⇒8x−2y=10\times 2 \Rightarrow 8x - 2y = 10×2⇒8x−2y=10

Step 2: Add or subtract the equations to eliminate yyy.

(3x+2y)+(8x−2y)=12+1011x=22x=2\begin{aligned} (3x + 2y) + (8x - 2y) &= 12 + 10 \\ 11x &= 22 \\ x &= 2 \end{aligned}(3x+2y)+(8x−2y)11xx​=12+10=22=2​

Step 3: Substitute x=2x = 2x=2 back into either original equation to find yyy.

3(2)+2y=126+2y=122y=6y=3\begin{aligned} 3(2) + 2y &= 12 \\ 6 + 2y &= 12 \\ 2y &= 6 \\ y &= 3 \end{aligned}3(2)+2y6+2y2yy​=12=12=6=3​

Solution: x=2x = 2x=2, y=3y = 3y=3.

4. Quadratic Equations

Section titled “4. Quadratic Equations”

A quadratic equation takes the form ax2+bx+c=0ax^2 + bx + c = 0ax2+bx+c=0. Depending on the equation, you can solve it via factorisation, the quadratic formula, or your GDC.

Solving by Factorisation

Section titled “Solving by Factorisation”

If the expression factorises nicely, use the Zero Product Property (If ab=0ab = 0ab=0, then a=0a = 0a=0 or b=0b = 0b=0).

Example: Solve x2−5x+6=0x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0x2−5x+6=0

(x−2)(x−3)=0\begin{aligned} (x - 2)(x - 3) &= 0 \end{aligned}(x−2)(x−3)​=0​

Therefore, either x−2=0x - 2 = 0x−2=0 or x−3=0x - 3 = 0x−3=0.

Solution: x=2x = 2x=2 or x=3x = 3x=3.

The Quadratic Formula

Section titled “The Quadratic Formula”

If an equation does not factorise easily, use the quadratic formula (this is provided in your exam).

x=−b±b2−4ac2ax = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}x=2a−b±b2−4ac​​

Example: Solve 2x2−5x−4=02x^2 - 5x - 4 = 02x2−5x−4=0. Give your answers exact in surd form.

Step 1: Identify aaa, bbb, and ccc.

Here, a=2a = 2a=2, b=−5b = -5b=−5, and c=−4c = -4c=−4. (Always take the sign in front of the number!)

Step 2: Substitute carefully into the formula.

x=−(−5)±(−5)2−4(2)(−4)2(2)\begin{aligned} x &= \frac{-(-5) \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(2)(-4)}}{2(2)} \end{aligned}x​=2(2)−(−5)±(−5)2−4(2)(−4)​​​

Step 3: Simplify the discriminant (the part under the root) and denominator.

x=5±25−(−32)4x=5±25+324x=5±574\begin{aligned} x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 - (-32)}}{4} \\ x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 + 32}}{4} \\ x &= \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{57}}{4} \end{aligned}xxx​=45±25−(−32)​​=45±25+32​​=45±57​​​

Exact Surd Form

Sometimes the exam will ask for the exact answer. This means you must leave your answer containing the square root (surd form), rather than a rounded decimal like 3.123.123.12.

5. Changing the Subject of a Formula

Section titled “5. Changing the Subject of a Formula”

Changing the subject means rearranging a formula so that a different variable is isolated on its own on the left-hand side.

Dealing with Powers and Roots

Section titled “Dealing with Powers and Roots”

Use the inverse operation to remove powers or roots.

Example 1: Make xxx the subject of y=x+2y = \sqrt{x + 2}y=x+2​

y2=x+2(Square both sides)y2−2=x(Subtract 2)x=y2−2\begin{aligned} y^2 &= x + 2 \quad \text{(Square both sides)} \\ y^2 - 2 &= x \quad \text{(Subtract 2)} \\ x &= y^2 - 2 \end{aligned}y2y2−2x​=x+2(Square both sides)=x(Subtract 2)=y2−2​

The Subject Appears Twice (Extended)

Section titled “The Subject Appears Twice (Extended)”

If the letter you want to make the subject appears more than once, you must factorise to isolate it.

Example 2: Make xxx the subject of y=2x+1x−3y = \frac{2x + 1}{x - 3}y=x−32x+1​

y(x−3)=2x+1(Multiply by the denominator)yx−3y=2x+1(Expand the bracket)\begin{aligned} y(x - 3) &= 2x + 1 \quad \text{(Multiply by the denominator)} \\ yx - 3y &= 2x + 1 \quad \text{(Expand the bracket)} \end{aligned}y(x−3)yx−3y​=2x+1(Multiply by the denominator)=2x+1(Expand the bracket)​

Group all terms containing xxx on one side, and everything else on the other:

yx−2x=3y+1\begin{aligned} yx - 2x &= 3y + 1 \end{aligned}yx−2x​=3y+1​

Factorise out the xxx:

x(y−2)=3y+1x=3y+1y−2(Divide by the bracket)\begin{aligned} x(y - 2) &= 3y + 1 \\ x &= \frac{3y + 1}{y - 2} \quad \text{(Divide by the bracket)} \end{aligned}x(y−2)x​=3y+1=y−23y+1​(Divide by the bracket)​

6. Solving Equations with a GDC

Section titled “6. Solving Equations with a GDC”

For complex or “unfamiliar” equations, your Graphic Display Calculator is your best friend.

Example: Solve 2x−1=1x32x - 1 = \frac{1}{x^3}2x−1=x31​ using a GDC.

This is highly complex to solve algebraically, but very easy graphically.

Method 1: Intersection

  1. Enter y1=2x−1y_1 = 2x - 1y1​=2x−1
  2. Enter y2=1x3y_2 = \frac{1}{x^3}y2​=x31​
  3. Graph both functions.
  4. Use [Menu] → [Analyse Graph] → [Intersection] to find the xxx-coordinate where the two graphs cross.

Method 2: Finding Zeros (Roots)

  1. Rearrange the equation to equal zero: 2x−1−1x3=02x - 1 - \frac{1}{x^3} = 02x−1−x31​=0
  2. Enter this entire expression into y1y_1y1​.
  3. Graph the function.
  4. Use [Menu] → [Analyse Graph] → [Zero] to find the xxx-intercepts. The xxx-values are your solutions!
