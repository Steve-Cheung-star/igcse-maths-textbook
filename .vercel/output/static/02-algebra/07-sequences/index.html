2.7 Sequences

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers (or sometimes patterns/shapes) that follow a specific mathematical rule. Each number in the sequence is called a term.

We often use subscript notation to refer to specific terms. For a sequence named TTT:

  • T1T_1T1​ is the 1st term.
  • T2T_2T2​ is the 2nd term.
  • TnT_nTn​ is the nnnth term (a general formula for any term in the sequence, where nnn is the position).

1. Term-to-Term Rules

Section titled “1. Term-to-Term Rules”

A term-to-term rule tells you how to get from one term to the very next term. While easy to spot, term-to-term rules aren’t great if you want to find the 100th term, because you’d have to calculate all 99 terms before it!

Example: Find the next two terms and the term-to-term rule for the sequence: 5,9,13,17,…5, 9, 13, 17, \dots5,9,13,17,…

  • The pattern is adding 444 each time.
  • Term-to-term rule: Add 444.
  • Next two terms: 17+4=2117 + 4 = 2117+4=21, and 21+4=2521 + 4 = 2521+4=25.

2. Linear (Arithmetic) Sequences

Section titled “2. Linear (Arithmetic) Sequences”

A sequence is linear (or arithmetic) if it increases or decreases by a constant amount every time. This constant amount is called the first difference.

Finding the nnnth Term

Section titled “Finding the nnnth Term”

The nnnth term of a linear sequence always takes the form: Tn=dn+cT_n = dn + cTn​=dn+c Where ddd is the constant difference, and ccc is the “zeroth term” (the value that would come just before the 1st term).

Example 1: Increasing Sequence Find the nnnth term of the sequence: 7,10,13,16,19,…7, 10, 13, 16, 19, \dots7,10,13,16,19,…

Sequence (TnT_nTn​)710131619
1st Difference+3+3+3+3
  1. Find ddd: The sequence goes up by 3\color{#3b82f6}{3}3 each time, so the formula starts with 3n3n3n.
  2. Find ccc: To find the “0th term”, work backwards from the 1st term (777). 7−3=47 - 3 = 47−3=4
  3. Combine: The nnnth term is Tn=3n+4T_n = 3n + 4Tn​=3n+4.

Example 2: Decreasing Sequence Find the nnnth term of the sequence: 15,11,7,3,−1,…15, 11, 7, 3, -1, \dots15,11,7,3,−1,…

Sequence (TnT_nTn​)151173-1
1st Difference-4-4-4-4
  1. Find ddd: The sequence goes down by 4\color{#3b82f6}{4}4 each time. The difference is −4\color{#3b82f6}{-4}−4, so the formula starts with −4n-4n−4n.
  2. Find ccc: To find the “0th term”, do the opposite of the rule backwards: 15+4=1915 + 4 = 1915+4=19.
  3. Combine: The nnnth term is Tn=−4n+19T_n = -4n + 19Tn​=−4n+19.

Equivalent Expressions

It is very common (and often preferred because it looks neater) to write expressions starting with a positive number. −4n+19-4n + 19−4n+19 is mathematically identical to 19−4n19 - 4n19−4n. Both answers are perfectly correct!

3. Quadratic Sequences

Section titled “3. Quadratic Sequences”

A sequence is quadratic if the first difference changes, but the second difference is constant. The general formula is: Tn=an2+bn+cT_n = an^2 + bn + cTn​=an2+bn+c

The Algebraic Method

Section titled “The Algebraic Method”

We can find aaa, bbb, and ccc by memorising a system of three simple equations. We set these equations equal to the first number of each row in our differences table.

Example: Find the nnnth term for 4,11,22,37,56,…4, 11, 22, 37, 56, \dots4,11,22,37,56,…

Sequence411223756
1st Diff+7+11+15+19
2nd Diff+4+4+4

Match the first numbers of each row (working from the bottom upwards) to these equations:

2a=4(2nd Difference)3a+b=7(1st Difference)a+b+c=4(Sequence)\begin{aligned} \color{#ef4444} 2a &= \color{#ef4444} 4 \quad \text{(2nd Difference)} \\ \color{#3b82f6} 3a + b &= \color{#3b82f6} 7 \quad \text{(1st Difference)} \\ a + b + c &= 4 \quad \text{(Sequence)} \end{aligned}2a3a+ba+b+c​=4(2nd Difference)=7(1st Difference)=4(Sequence)​

Now, substitute your findings vertically to solve for the letters one by one:

Step 1: Solve for aaa

2a=4a=2\begin{aligned} \color{#ef4444} 2a &= 4 \\ a &= 2 \end{aligned}2aa​=4=2​

Step 2: Substitute aaa to solve for bbb

3(2)+b=76+b=7b=1\begin{aligned} \color{#3b82f6} 3(2) + b &= 7 \\ 6 + b &= 7 \\ b &= 1 \end{aligned}3(2)+b6+bb​=7=7=1​

Step 3: Substitute aaa and bbb to solve for ccc

2+1+c=43+c=4c=1\begin{aligned} 2 + 1 + c &= 4 \\ 3 + c &= 4 \\ c &= 1 \end{aligned}2+1+c3+cc​=4=4=1​

Step 4: Write the final formula Substitute your values for aaa, bbb, and ccc back into an2+bn+can^2 + bn + can2+bn+c: Tn=2n2+n+1T_n = 2n^2 + n + 1Tn​=2n2+n+1

4. Cubic Sequences

Section titled “4. Cubic Sequences”

A sequence is cubic if the third difference is constant. The general formula involves n3n^3n3: Tn=an3+bn2+cn+dT_n = an^3 + bn^2 + cn + dTn​=an3+bn2+cn+d

We use the same algebraic method as quadratics, but with four equations to match the four rows!

Example: Find the nnnth term for 3,10,29,66,…3, 10, 29, 66, \dots3,10,29,66,…

Sequence3102966
1st Diff+7+19+37
2nd Diff+12+18
3rd Diff+6

Match the first number of each row to the cubic sequence equations (working upwards):

6a=6(3rd Difference)12a+2b=12(2nd Difference)7a+3b+c=7(1st Difference)a+b+c+d=3(Sequence)\begin{aligned} \color{#a855f7} 6a &= \color{#a855f7} 6 \quad \text{(3rd Difference)} \\ \color{#ef4444} 12a + 2b &= \color{#ef4444} 12 \quad \text{(2nd Difference)} \\ \color{#3b82f6} 7a + 3b + c &= \color{#3b82f6} 7 \quad \text{(1st Difference)} \\ a + b + c + d &= 3 \quad \text{(Sequence)} \end{aligned}6a12a+2b7a+3b+ca+b+c+d​=6(3rd Difference)=12(2nd Difference)=7(1st Difference)=3(Sequence)​

Substitute vertically to find a,b,ca, b, ca,b,c, and ddd:

Step 1: Solve for aaa

6a=6a=1\begin{aligned} \color{#a855f7} 6a &= 6 \\ a &= 1 \end{aligned}6aa​=6=1​

Step 2: Substitute aaa to solve for bbb

12(1)+2b=1212+2b=122b=0b=0\begin{aligned} \color{#ef4444} 12(1) + 2b &= 12 \\ 12 + 2b &= 12 \\ 2b &= 0 \\ b &= 0 \end{aligned}12(1)+2b12+2b2bb​=12=12=0=0​

Step 3: Substitute aaa and bbb to solve for ccc

7(1)+3(0)+c=77+0+c=7c=0\begin{aligned} \color{#3b82f6} 7(1) + 3(0) + c &= 7 \\ 7 + 0 + c &= 7 \\ c &= 0 \end{aligned}7(1)+3(0)+c7+0+cc​=7=7=0​

Step 4: Substitute aaa, bbb, and ccc to solve for ddd

1+0+0+d=31+d=3d=2\begin{aligned} 1 + 0 + 0 + d &= 3 \\ 1 + d &= 3 \\ d &= 2 \end{aligned}1+0+0+d1+dd​=3=3=2​

Step 5: Write the final formula Tn=1n3+0n2+0n+2T_n = 1n^3 + 0n^2 + 0n + 2Tn​=1n3+0n2+0n+2 Tn=n3+2T_n = n^3 + 2Tn​=n3+2

5. Combining Sequences (Fractions)

Section titled “5. Combining Sequences (Fractions)”

Sometimes you will be given a rational sequence (a sequence of fractions). The trick here is not to divide them, but to completely separate the numerators (tops) and the denominators (bottoms). Treat them as two entirely independent sequences, figure out their formulas separately, and finally stack them back into a single fraction.

Example 1: Standard Rational Sequence

Section titled “Example 1: Standard Rational Sequence”

Find the nnnth term of the sequence: 32,56,712,920,…\frac{3}{2}, \frac{5}{6}, \frac{7}{12}, \frac{9}{20}, \dots23​,65​,127​,209​,…

Step 1: The Numerators (Tops) The top numbers are: 3,5,7,9,…3, 5, 7, 9, \dots3,5,7,9,… This is a linear sequence that increases by 222 each time (2n2n2n). The 0th term is 111. Top Formula: 2n+12n + 12n+1

Step 2: The Denominators (Bottoms) The bottom numbers are: 2,6,12,20,…2, 6, 12, 20, \dots2,6,12,20,…

  • 1st diff: +4,+6,+8+4, +6, +8+4,+6,+8
  • 2nd diff: +2+2+2 (It’s a quadratic sequence!)

Using our equations:

  • 2a=2  ⟹  a=1\color{#ef4444} 2a = 2 \implies a = 12a=2⟹a=1
  • 3a+b=4  ⟹  3(1)+b=4  ⟹  b=1\color{#3b82f6} 3a + b = 4 \implies 3(1) + b = 4 \implies b = 13a+b=4⟹3(1)+b=4⟹b=1
  • a+b+c=2  ⟹  1+1+c=2  ⟹  c=0a + b + c = 2 \implies 1 + 1 + c = 2 \implies c = 0a+b+c=2⟹1+1+c=2⟹c=0 Bottom Formula: 1n2+1n+01n^2 + 1n + 01n2+1n+0, which simplifies to n2+nn^2 + nn2+n.

Step 3: Recombine Stack your two formulas back into a fraction. Tn=2n+1n2+nT_n = \frac{2n + 1}{n^2 + n}Tn​=n2+n2n+1​

Example 2: Disguised (Simplified) Fractions

Section titled “Example 2: Disguised (Simplified) Fractions”

Sometimes, fractions are simplified by the examiner to hide the true underlying pattern. If a sequence of fractions seems to have a broken pattern—or contains random whole numbers—try “un-simplifying” them to reveal consistent differences.

Find the nnnth term of the sequence: 23,1,65,43,107,…\frac{2}{3}, 1, \frac{6}{5}, \frac{4}{3}, \frac{10}{7}, \dots32​,1,56​,34​,710​,…

This looks chaotic. Let’s convert the whole number 111 and the fraction 43\frac{4}{3}34​ into equivalent fractions that fit the flow of the other numerators and denominators.

  • T1=23T_1 = \frac{2}{3}T1​=32​
  • T2=1T_2 = 1T2​=1 (Let’s write this as 44\frac{4}{4}44​ to bridge the gap between 2 and 6)
  • T3=65T_3 = \frac{6}{5}T3​=56​
  • T4=43T_4 = \frac{4}{3}T4​=34​ (Multiply top and bottom by 2 to get 86\frac{8}{6}68​)
  • T5=107T_5 = \frac{10}{7}T5​=710​

Now look at our un-simplified sequence: 23,44,65,86,107,…\frac{2}{3}, \frac{4}{4}, \frac{6}{5}, \frac{8}{6}, \frac{10}{7}, \dots32​,44​,56​,68​,710​,…

The hidden patterns are now incredibly obvious!

  • Numerators: 2,4,6,8,10…2, 4, 6, 8, 10 \dots2,4,6,8,10… (Linear, going up by 222. Formula: 2n2n2n)
  • Denominators: 3,4,5,6,7…3, 4, 5, 6, 7 \dots3,4,5,6,7… (Linear, going up by 111. Formula: n+2n + 2n+2)

Final Recombined Formula: Tn=2nn+2T_n = \frac{2n}{n + 2}Tn​=n+22n​

6. Exponential (Geometric) Sequences

Section titled “6. Exponential (Geometric) Sequences”

In an exponential sequence, instead of adding or subtracting, you multiply or divide by a constant factor to get to the next term. This factor is called the common ratio (rrr).

The formula for the nnnth term is usually written as: Tn=a×rn−1T_n = a \times r^{n-1}Tn​=a×rn−1 (Where aaa is the 1st term, and rrr is the common ratio).

Why to the power of (n-1)?

To get to the 5th term, you don’t multiply by the ratio five times. You start at the 1st term and multiply by it four times. That’s why the power is n−1n-1n−1.

Example 1: Standard Growth 3,6,12,24,48,…3, 6, 12, 24, 48, \dots3,6,12,24,48,…

  • 1st term (aaa) = 333
  • Multiplier (rrr) = ×2\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times 2}×2
  • nnnth term: Tn=3×2n−1T_n = 3 \times \textcolor{#ea580c}{2}^{n-1}Tn​=3×2n−1

Example 2: Fractional Ratios (Decay) 80,40,20,10,…80, 40, 20, 10, \dots80,40,20,10,…

  • 1st term (aaa) = 808080
  • Multiplier (rrr) = ×12\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times \frac{1}{2}}×21​ (or 0.5\textcolor{#ea580c}{0.5}0.5)
  • nnnth term: Tn=80×(12)n−1T_n = 80 \times \left(\textcolor{#ea580c}{\frac{1}{2}}\right)^{n-1}Tn​=80×(21​)n−1

Example 3: Negative Ratios (Alternating Signs) 2,−6,18,−54,…2, -6, 18, -54, \dots2,−6,18,−54,… Notice how the signs flip-flop from positive to negative. This means you are multiplying by a negative number!

  • 1st term (aaa) = 222
  • Multiplier (rrr) = ×−3\textcolor{#ea580c}{\times -3}×−3
  • nnnth term: Tn=2×(−3)n−1T_n = 2 \times (\textcolor{#ea580c}{-3})^{n-1}Tn​=2×(−3)n−1 (Always put negative ratios in brackets so it is clear the power applies to the negative sign!)
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Simplifying using Index Laws

Sometimes, geometric sequences can be simplified further if the 1st term (aaa) and the ratio (rrr) share a common base. You can use index laws to combine them!

For example, look at the sequence: 5,25,125,625,…5, 25, 125, 625, \dots5,25,125,625,…

  • a=5a = 5a=5 and r=5r = \textcolor{#ea580c}{5}r=5.
  • The standard formula is: Tn=5×5n−1T_n = 5 \times \textcolor{#ea580c}{5}^{n-1}Tn​=5×5n−1

Since 555 is the same as 515^151, we are multiplying bases that are the same. We add the powers: 51×5n−1=51+n−1=5n5^1 \times 5^{n-1} = 5^{1 + n - 1} = 5^n51×5n−1=51+n−1=5n So, a much cleaner answer is simply Tn=5nT_n = 5^nTn​=5n.

7. Visual and Pattern Sequences

Section titled “7. Visual and Pattern Sequences”

In many exams (especially Paper 6 investigations), sequences are presented as a growing pattern of shapes, matchsticks, dots, or tiles. The goal is to translate the pictures into a table of numbers and then find the nnnth term.

Pattern Type A: Linear (Constant Addition)

Section titled “Pattern Type A: Linear (Constant Addition)”

A common pattern involves adding the same number of “sticks” to grow a shape.

Figure 1 (4 sticks)
Figure 2 (7 sticks)
Figure 3 (10 sticks)
  • Sequence: 4,7,10,…4, 7, 10, \dots4,7,10,…
  • Rule: It increases by 3\color{#3b82f6}{3}3 sticks each time (3n3n3n). Working backward, the 0th term is 111.
  • nnnth term: Tn=3n+1T_n = 3n + 1Tn​=3n+1

Pattern Type B: Quadratic (Growing Areas)

Section titled “Pattern Type B: Quadratic (Growing Areas)”

When a pattern grows in two dimensions (like forming larger rectangles or triangles out of dots), the sequence is usually quadratic.

Figure 1 (2 dots)
Figure 2 (6 dots)
Figure 3 (12 dots)
  • Sequence: 2,6,12,…2, 6, 12, \dots2,6,12,…
  • Analysis: Notice that Figure 1 is a 1×21 \times 21×2 rectangle. Figure 2 is a 2×32 \times 32×3 rectangle. Figure 3 is a 3×43 \times 43×4 rectangle.
  • nnnth term: A Figure nnn will always have dimensions n×(n+1)n \times (n+1)n×(n+1).
  • Formula: Tn=n(n+1)T_n = n(n+1)Tn​=n(n+1), which expands to Tn=n2+nT_n = n^2 + nTn​=n2+n.

Often, a single visual pattern generates two or more separate sequences, which you are asked to connect together. Let’s look at a courtyard pattern made of “Filled” (dark) tiles and “Outline” (light border) tiles.

Self-Checking in Investigations

Notice how the math perfectly aligns! If you add the pieces together, you get the whole: Filled Tiles + Outline Tiles = Total Tiles n2+(4n+4)=n2+4n+4n^2 + (4n + 4) = n^2 + 4n + 4n2+(4n+4)=n2+4n+4 And if you expand (n+2)2(n+2)^2(n+2)2, you get n2+4n+4n^2 + 4n + 4n2+4n+4. The formulas prove each other!
