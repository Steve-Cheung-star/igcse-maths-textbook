2.6 Inequalities

While an equation tells you that two expressions are exactly equal, an inequality tells you how two expressions compare in size. Instead of a single answer, solving an inequality usually gives you a range of possible values.

1. Inequalities on a Number Line

Section titled “1. Inequalities on a Number Line”

We use four main inequality symbols. When graphing these on a number line, we use different types of circles to show whether the value itself is included.

  • Strict Inequalities: Use an open circle (∘\circ∘)
    • <<< (Less than)
    • >>> (Greater than)
  • Inclusive Inequalities: Use a closed circle (∙\bullet∙)
    • ⩽\leqslant⩽ (Less than or equal to)
    • ⩾\geqslant⩾ (Greater than or equal to)

Examples

Section titled “Examples”

1. Representing a single inequality: x>2x > 2x>2 Draw an open circle at 222 and an arrow pointing to the right.

2. Representing a compound inequality: −3⩽x<1-3 \leqslant x < 1−3⩽x<1 This means xxx is between −3-3−3 and 111. It includes −3-3−3 but does not include 111.

  • Draw a closed circle at −3-3−3.
  • Draw an open circle at 111.
  • Connect the two circles with a line.

2. Solving Linear Inequalities

Section titled “2. Solving Linear Inequalities”

You can solve linear inequalities using the exact same methods you use for linear equations (expanding brackets, collecting like terms, moving variables to one side).

However, there is one crucial rule you must never forget:

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Golden Rule of Inequalities

If you multiply or divide an inequality by a negative number, you MUST flip the inequality sign!

For example, when dividing by −2-2−2:

−2x<10x>−5\begin{aligned} -2x &< 10 \\[1ex] x &> -5 \end{aligned}−2xx​<10>−5​

(Notice how the sign vertically flipped from <<< to >>>!)

Basic Linear Inequalities

Section titled “Basic Linear Inequalities”

Example 1: Solve 3x<2x+43x < 2x + 43x<2x+4

3x−2x<4x<4\begin{aligned} 3x - 2x &< 4 \\ x &< 4 \end{aligned}3x−2xx​<4<4​

Compound Inequalities

Section titled “Compound Inequalities”

Sometimes an inequality has three parts. Whatever you do to the middle, you must do to both the left and the right sides.

Example 2: Solve −3⩽3x−2<7-3 \leqslant 3x - 2 < 7−3⩽3x−2<7

Step 1: Add 2 to all three parts to isolate the xxx term in the middle.

−3+2⩽3x<7+2−1⩽3x<9\begin{aligned} -3 + 2 &\leqslant 3x < 7 + 2 \\ -1 &\leqslant 3x < 9 \end{aligned}−3+2−1​⩽3x<7+2⩽3x<9​

Step 2: Divide all three parts by 3.

−13⩽x<3\begin{aligned} -\frac{1}{3} &\leqslant x < 3 \end{aligned}−31​​⩽x<3​

3. Inequalities in Two Variables (Graphs)

Section titled “3. Inequalities in Two Variables (Graphs)”

Inequalities involving both xxx and yyy (like y>xy > xy>x or x+y⩽4x + y \leqslant 4x+y⩽4) represent an entire region on a Cartesian plane, rather than just a segment on a number line.

Drawing the Boundary Line

Section titled “Drawing the Boundary Line”

First, treat the inequality as an equation (e.g., y=xy = xy=x) and draw this line. The type of line you draw depends on the symbol:

  • Broken/Dashed lines (−−− ⁣−---\!-−−−−): Used for strict inequalities (<<< or >>>).
  • Solid lines (—): Used for inclusive inequalities (⩽\leqslant⩽ or ⩾\geqslant⩾).

Shading the Region

Section titled “Shading the Region”

Once the line is drawn, it splits the graph into two sides. You must shade one side.

IGCSE Shading Convention

In IGCSE Mathematics, the strict convention is to shade the UNWANTED region. This leaves the correct region “clear” or blank. Always double-check if the exam question says “shade the unwanted region” to be completely sure.

How to decide which side to shade (the “unwanted” side):

  1. Gradient-Intercept Form (y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c):
    • If the required region is y>mx+cy > mx + cy>mx+c, the wanted region is above the line. You should shade below the line.
    • If the required region is y<mx+cy < mx + cy<mx+c, the wanted region is below the line. You should shade above the line.
  2. Test a Point:
    • Pick a coordinate not on the line, usually (0,0)(0,0)(0,0) is easiest.
    • Substitute x=0,y=0x=0, y=0x=0,y=0 into the inequality.
    • If the statement is TRUE, (0,0)(0,0)(0,0) is in the wanted region. Shade the other side.
    • If the statement is FALSE, (0,0)(0,0)(0,0) is in the unwanted region. Shade the side containing (0,0)(0,0)(0,0).

Example: Graph the region y⩽4−xy \leqslant 4 - xy⩽4−x (or x+y⩽4x + y \leqslant 4x+y⩽4)

  1. Draw the line y=4−xy = 4 - xy=4−x. Because it is ⩽\leqslant⩽, use a solid line.
  2. Test (0,0)(0,0)(0,0): 0⩽4−0⇒0⩽40 \leqslant 4 - 0 \Rightarrow 0 \leqslant 40⩽4−0⇒0⩽4. This is TRUE.
  3. The origin (0,0)(0,0)(0,0) is in the wanted region. Therefore, shade the side away from the origin (above the line).

4. Advanced Graphing Examples

Section titled “4. Advanced Graphing Examples”

In exams, you must be comfortable mixing different boundary types and forms of equations. We use color-coded, semi-transparent shading here so that the coordinate grid remains visible, making it easier to read intercepts.

Example A: Drawing a Region from Inequalities

Section titled “Example A: Drawing a Region from Inequalities”

On a grid, leave clear the region RRR that satisfies the following three inequalities:

  1. x⩾2x \geqslant 2x⩾2 (Solid vertical line, Red)
  2. y⩾x+1y \geqslant x + 1y⩾x+1 (Solid diagonal line, Yellow)
  3. x+2y<14x + 2y < 14x+2y<14 (Dashed diagonal line, Blue)

Example B: Finding Inequalities from a Shaded Graph

Section titled “Example B: Finding Inequalities from a Shaded Graph”

Write down the three inequalities that define the unshaded region RRR below. Pay close attention to whether the boundaries are solid or dashed.

  1. The Horizontal Boundary (Red): The line is at y=5y = 5y=5. It is a solid line, and the clear region RRR is below it. ⇒y⩽5\Rightarrow \boldsymbol{y \leqslant 5}⇒y⩽5
  2. The Dashed Diagonal Boundary (Yellow): The line passes clearly through the coordinate intersections (2,0)(2, 0)(2,0) and (4,4)(4, 4)(4,4).
    • Gradient m=4−04−2=2m = \frac{4-0}{4-2} = 2m=4−24−0​=2.
    • Extending the line downwards, the yyy-intercept is at −4-4−4, making the equation y=2x−4y = 2x - 4y=2x−4.
    • It is dashed, and the clear region is to the left/above. Testing a point in RRR, like (3,3)(3, 3)(3,3): 3>2(3)−4  ⟹  3>23 > 2(3) - 4 \implies 3 > 23>2(3)−4⟹3>2 (True). ⇒y>2x−4\Rightarrow \boldsymbol{y > 2x - 4}⇒y>2x−4
  3. The Solid Diagonal Boundary (Blue): The line passes clearly through (0,3)(0, 3)(0,3) and (2,0)(2, 0)(2,0).
    • Using the general intercept form (xa+yb=1\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} = 1ax​+by​=1), we get x2+y3=1\frac{x}{2} + \frac{y}{3} = 12x​+3y​=1, which rearranges to 3x+2y=63x + 2y = 63x+2y=6.
    • It is solid, and the clear region is to the right/above. Testing a point in RRR, like (3,3)(3, 3)(3,3): 3(3)+2(3)=15⩾63(3) + 2(3) = 15 \geqslant 63(3)+2(3)=15⩾6 (True). ⇒3x+2y⩾6\Rightarrow \boldsymbol{3x + 2y \geqslant 6}⇒3x+2y⩾6

5. Solving Inequalities using a GDC

Section titled “5. Solving Inequalities using a GDC”

Your Graphic Display Calculator (GDC) can quickly find graphical regions or solve complex linear inequalities.

Graphing Inequality Regions:

  1. On the graph screen, delete the = sign in the f1(x)= entry line.
  2. A menu will appear allowing you to select ⩽,<,⩾,\leqslant, <, \geqslant,⩽,<,⩾, or >>>.
  3. Type your expression (e.g., f1(x) > 2x - 3).
  4. The calculator will automatically draw the correct line (dashed or solid) and shade the region. (Note: Calculators usually shade the wanted region by default, so remember to interpret this carefully against the IGCSE “shade unwanted” convention!)

Graphical Inequalities

Section titled “Graphical Inequalities”
  • 📝 Question 5
  • ✅ Solution 5

A region is defined by the three inequalities:

  1. x⩾1x \geqslant 1x⩾1
  2. y>2y > 2y>2
  3. x+y⩽6x + y \leqslant 6x+y⩽6

Describe the boundary lines you would draw, including whether they are solid or broken, and state which side you would shade.

Remember the convention is to shade the unwanted region:

1. x⩾1x \geqslant 1x⩾1

  • Draw a solid vertical line at x=1x = 1x=1.
  • The wanted region is to the right. Shade to the left (x<1\boldsymbol{x < 1}x<1).

2. y>2y > 2y>2

  • Draw a broken (dashed) horizontal line at y=2y = 2y=2.
  • The wanted region is above. Shade below (y⩽2\boldsymbol{y \leqslant 2}y⩽2).

3. x+y⩽6x + y \leqslant 6x+y⩽6

  • Draw a solid diagonal line passing through (0,6)(0,6)(0,6) and (6,0)(6,0)(6,0).
  • Test (0,0)(0,0)(0,0): 0+0⩽60 + 0 \leqslant 60+0⩽6 is TRUE. The origin is wanted.
  • Shade above/right of the line (x+y>6\boldsymbol{x + y > 6}x+y>6).
