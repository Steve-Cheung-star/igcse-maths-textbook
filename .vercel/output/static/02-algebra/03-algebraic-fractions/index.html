2.3 Algebraic Fractions

An algebraic fraction is a fraction that can be expressed in the form ab\frac{a}{b}ba​, where aaa and bbb are algebraic expressions (e.g., 4x+32x\frac{4x+3}{2x}2x4x+3​ or 3x2−5x+1x+3\frac{3x^2-5x+1}{x+3}x+33x2−5x+1​).

Algebraic fractions follow the exact same mathematical rules as numerical fractions. If you can simplify, add, subtract, multiply, and divide normal fractions, you already know how to handle algebraic ones. The only difference is that you are working with variables instead of just numbers.

1. Simplifying Algebraic Fractions

Section titled “1. Simplifying Algebraic Fractions”

Simplifying requires factoring out the Highest Common Factor (HCF) from the numerator and denominator and reducing appropriately. Do this before you attempt any complex multiplication or division!

Basic Simplification and Indices

Section titled “Basic Simplification and Indices”

When simplifying brackets with powers, use your index laws just like you would with single variables.

Example A: Constant Factors 8(x−4)2=4(x−4)\frac{8(x-4)}{2} = 4(x-4)28(x−4)​=4(x−4)

Steve's Exam Wisdom

No Need to Expand!

When you simplify a fraction and are left with brackets in the numerator or denominator, you usually do not need to expand them unless the question specifically asks for it. Leaving it as 4(x−4)4(x-4)4(x−4) is perfectly acceptable and safer!

Example B: Identical Brackets y−2y−2=1\frac{y-2}{y-2} = 1y−2y−2​=1

It is NOT 0!

A very common mistake when everything cancels out is to write 000 as the answer. Remember, any number (or expression) divided by itself is 111.

Example C: Cancelling with Indices

−4(x+10)56(x+10)8=−2(x+10)53(x+10)8(Simplify the numerical coefficients)=−23(x+10)3(Subtract the indices: 5−8=−3)\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{-4(x+10)^5}{6(x+10)^8} \\ &= \frac{-2(x+10)^5}{3(x+10)^8} \quad \text{(Simplify the numerical coefficients)} \\ &= \frac{-2}{3(x+10)^3} \quad \text{(Subtract the indices: } 5 - 8 = -3 \text{)} \end{aligned}​6(x+10)8−4(x+10)5​=3(x+10)8−2(x+10)5​(Simplify the numerical coefficients)=3(x+10)3−2​(Subtract the indices: 5−8=−3)​

Simplifying Rational Expressions (Extended)

Section titled “Simplifying Rational Expressions (Extended)”

Sometimes you are given a large, complex algebraic fraction. You must factorise fully first before cancelling anything.

The Illegal Cancellation

Students often see an expression like s2s+3\frac{s^2}{s+3}s+3s2​ and try to cancel the sss to get s3\frac{s}{3}3s​. This is mathematically illegal! You cannot cancel terms that are under addition or subtraction.

  • s2s+3≠s3\frac{s^2}{s+3} \neq \frac{s}{3}s+3s2​=3s​ ❌ (Addition prevents cancelling)
  • s2s×3=s3\frac{s^2}{s \times 3} = \frac{s}{3}s×3s2​=3s​ ✅ (Multiplication allows cancelling)

Example: Factorise and simplify x2−2xx2−5x+6\frac{x^2 - 2x}{x^2 - 5x + 6}x2−5x+6x2−2x​

Step 1: Factorise the numerator. The top expression has a common factor of xxx. x2−2x=x(x−2)x^2 - 2x = x(x - 2)x2−2x=x(x−2)

Step 2: Factorise the denominator. The bottom expression is a quadratic trinomial. We need numbers that multiply to +6+6+6 and add to −5-5−5. These are −2-2−2 and −3-3−3. x2−5x+6=(x−2)(x−3)x^2 - 5x + 6 = (x - 2)(x - 3)x2−5x+6=(x−2)(x−3)

Step 3: Rewrite and cancel common factors.

x2−2xx2−5x+6=x(x−2)(x−2)(x−3)=xx−3\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{x^2 - 2x}{x^2 - 5x + 6} \\ &= \frac{x\cancel{(x - 2)}}{\cancel{(x - 2)}(x - 3)} \\ &= \frac{x}{x - 3} \end{aligned}​x2−5x+6x2−2x​=(x−2)​(x−3)x(x−2)​​=x−3x​​

2. Multiplying and Dividing Algebraic Fractions

Section titled “2. Multiplying and Dividing Algebraic Fractions”

Multiplying and dividing are the easiest operations to perform on fractions because you do not need a common denominator.

Multiplication

Section titled “Multiplication”

Multiply the numerators together, and multiply the denominators together. Simplify the result if possible.

Anatomy of Fraction Multiplication

  • Top ×\times× Top: Multiply the coefficients and add the indices of matching variables.
  • Bottom ×\times× Bottom: Do the same for the denominators.
  • Simplify: Cancel any common factors between the final numerator and denominator.

Example: Calculate 3a4×9a10\frac{3a}{4} \times \frac{9a}{10}43a​×109a​

3a4×9a10=3a×9a4×10=27a240\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{3a}{4} \times \frac{9a}{10} \\ &= \frac{3a \times 9a}{4 \times 10} \\ &= \frac{27a^2}{40} \end{aligned}​43a​×109a​=4×103a×9a​=4027a2​​

Division

Section titled “Division”

Never divide fractions directly. Instead, use the Keep-Change-Flip method: keep the first fraction, change the division sign to multiplication, and flip the second fraction (find its reciprocal).

Example: Calculate 3a4÷9a10\frac{3a}{4} \div \frac{9a}{10}43a​÷109a​

3a4÷9a10=3a4×109a(Keep, Change, Flip)=30a36a\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{3a}{4} \div \frac{9a}{10} \\ &= \frac{3a}{4} \times \frac{10}{9a} \quad \text{(Keep, Change, Flip)} \\ &= \frac{30a}{36a} \end{aligned}​43a​÷109a​=43a​×9a10​(Keep, Change, Flip)=36a30a​​

Now, simplify the fraction. Both 303030 and 363636 are divisible by 666, and the aaa on the top and bottom cancel each other out.

=5×6a6×6a=56\begin{aligned} &= \frac{5 \times \cancel{6a}}{6 \times \cancel{6a}} \\ &= \frac{5}{6} \end{aligned}​=6×6a5×6a​=65​​

3. Adding and Subtracting Algebraic Fractions

Section titled “3. Adding and Subtracting Algebraic Fractions”

To add or subtract algebraic fractions, you must find a common denominator. This is usually the Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of the denominators.

graph TD
    Start[Look at the fractions] --> Q1{Are denominators the same?}
    Q1 -- Yes --> Add[Add/Subtract numerators directly]
    Q1 -- No --> Q2{Can you factorise denominators?}
    Q2 -- Yes --> Fact[Factorise to find the LCM easily]
    Q2 -- No --> Multiply[Multiply denominators together for LCM]
    Fact --> Equiv[Create equivalent fractions]
    Multiply --> Equiv
    Equiv --> Expand[Expand brackets on the top]
    Expand --> Combine[Combine over a single denominator]
    Combine --> Simplify[Collect like terms]

Basic Addition and Subtraction

Section titled “Basic Addition and Subtraction”

Example A: Express 2x+13−x+57\frac{2x + 1}{3} - \frac{x + 5}{7}32x+1​−7x+5​ as a single fraction. The common denominator for 333 and 777 is 212121. Multiply the top and bottom of each fraction to achieve this.

7(2x+1)21−3(x+5)21=14x+721−3x+1521\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{7(2x + 1)}{21} - \frac{3(x + 5)}{21} \\ &= \frac{14x + 7}{21} - \frac{3x + 15}{21} \end{aligned}​217(2x+1)​−213(x+5)​=2114x+7​−213x+15​​
Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Invisible Brackets

When subtracting a fraction with multiple terms in its numerator, treat that numerator as if it is wrapped in invisible brackets. The negative sign must distribute to every term inside! In the example above, you are subtracting positive 151515, which becomes −15-15−15.

=14x+7−3x−1521=11x−821\begin{aligned} &= \frac{14x + 7 - 3x - 15}{21} \\ &= \frac{11x - 8}{21} \end{aligned}​=2114x+7−3x−15​=2111x−8​​

Binomial Denominators (Extended)

Section titled “Binomial Denominators (Extended)”

When the denominators contain algebraic expressions, the LCM is usually the product of the two denominators. Leave the denominator factorised in your final answer!

Example B: Express 3x−1−2x+1\frac{3}{x - 1} - \frac{2}{x + 1}x−13​−x+12​ as a single fraction. The common denominator is (x−1)(x+1)(x - 1)(x + 1)(x−1)(x+1).

3(x+1)(x−1)(x+1)−2(x−1)(x−1)(x+1)=3x+3−(2x−2)(x−1)(x+1)=3x+3−2x+2(x−1)(x+1)=x+5(x−1)(x+1)\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{3(x + 1)}{(x - 1)(x + 1)} - \frac{2(x - 1)}{(x - 1)(x + 1)} \\ &= \frac{3x + 3 - (2x - 2)}{(x - 1)(x + 1)} \\ &= \frac{3x + 3 - 2x + 2}{(x - 1)(x + 1)} \\ &= \frac{x + 5}{(x - 1)(x + 1)} \end{aligned}​(x−1)(x+1)3(x+1)​−(x−1)(x+1)2(x−1)​=(x−1)(x+1)3x+3−(2x−2)​=(x−1)(x+1)3x+3−2x+2​=(x−1)(x+1)x+5​​

Factoring Denominators First (Extended)

Section titled “Factoring Denominators First (Extended)”

Sometimes, you can factorise one of the denominators to find a much simpler LCM.

Example C: Express 54x+12+3x+3\frac{5}{4x + 12} + \frac{3}{x + 3}4x+125​+x+33​ as a single fraction. Notice that 4x+124x + 124x+12 can be factorised into 4(x+3)4(x + 3)4(x+3). This means the LCM is just 4(x+3)4(x + 3)4(x+3)! We only need to multiply the second fraction by 44\frac{4}{4}44​.

54(x+3)+3×4(x+3)×4=54(x+3)+124(x+3)=174(x+3)\begin{aligned} &\quad \frac{5}{4(x + 3)} + \frac{3 \times 4}{(x + 3) \times 4} \\ &= \frac{5}{4(x + 3)} + \frac{12}{4(x + 3)} \\ &= \frac{17}{4(x + 3)} \end{aligned}​4(x+3)5​+(x+3)×43×4​=4(x+3)5​+4(x+3)12​=4(x+3)17​​
