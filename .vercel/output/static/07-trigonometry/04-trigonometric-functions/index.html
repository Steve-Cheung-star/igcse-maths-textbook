7.4 Trigonometric Functions

Up until now, we’ve looked at trigonometry inside triangles. But trigonometric functions can also be graphed on a coordinate plane. For IGCSE, you need to be able to sketch and interpret these graphs between 0∘0^\circ0∘ and 360∘360^\circ360∘, and use them to solve equations.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

Mode Check

Before you do any trigonometry in your exam, look at the top right of your TI-Nspire GDC screen. Make sure it says DEG. If it says RAD or GRAD, your graphs and equation answers will be completely wrong! You can usually just click on it with the touchpad to change it quickly.

1. The Trigonometric Graphs (0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘)

Section titled “1. The Trigonometric Graphs (0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘)”

All three main trigonometric graphs have a repeating pattern (they are periodic). You must memorize their general shapes, where they cross the axes, and their maximum/minimum values.

The Sine Graph: y=sin⁡xy = \sin xy=sinx

Section titled “The Sine Graph: y=sin⁡xy = \sin xy=sinx”
  • Starts at: (0,0)(0, 0)(0,0)
  • Maximum value: 111 (at 90∘90^\circ90∘)
  • Minimum value: −1-1−1 (at 270∘270^\circ270∘)
  • Roots (crosses x-axis): 0∘,180∘,360∘0^\circ, 180^\circ, 360^\circ0∘,180∘,360∘

The Cosine Graph: y=cos⁡xy = \cos xy=cosx

Section titled “The Cosine Graph: y=cos⁡xy = \cos xy=cosx”
  • Starts at: (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1) (It looks exactly like the sine wave, but shifted to the left by 90∘90^\circ90∘)
  • Maximum value: 111 (at 0∘0^\circ0∘ and 360∘360^\circ360∘)
  • Minimum value: −1-1−1 (at 180∘180^\circ180∘)
  • Roots (crosses x-axis): 90∘,270∘90^\circ, 270^\circ90∘,270∘

The Tangent Graph: y=tan⁡xy = \tan xy=tanx

Section titled “The Tangent Graph: y=tan⁡xy = \tan xy=tanx”
  • Starts at: (0,0)(0, 0)(0,0)
  • Maximum/Minimum: None (It stretches to infinity).
  • Roots (crosses x-axis): 0∘,180∘,360∘0^\circ, 180^\circ, 360^\circ0∘,180∘,360∘
  • Asymptotes: The graph never touches x=90∘x = 90^\circx=90∘ or x=270∘x = 270^\circx=270∘ (because tan⁡(90∘)\tan(90^\circ)tan(90∘) is undefined).

Misconception

We often talk about the “amplitude” (height) of sine and cosine waves. Tangent graphs do not have an amplitude. Because they stretch infinitely upwards and downwards, they have no maximum or minimum peak.

2. Solving Trigonometric Equations (0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘)

Section titled “2. Solving Trigonometric Equations (0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘)”

When you use the inverse trig functions (sin⁡−1\sin^{-1}sin−1, cos⁡−1\cos^{-1}cos−1, tan⁡−1\tan^{-1}tan−1) on your TI-Nspire GDC, it only gives you one answer. This is called the Principal Value (PV).

However, because the graphs go up and down and repeat themselves, there is almost always a second valid angle between 0∘0^\circ0∘ and 360∘360^\circ360∘. You find the second angle using the symmetry of the graphs (or the CAST diagram).

Principal Value (PV)

The Principal Value (PV) is simply the valid angle closest to zero. Sometimes, your TI-Nspire GDC will give you a negative angle as the PV. Don’t panic! Just apply the symmetry rules to that negative number to find the positive angles in your required range.

The Symmetry Rules for finding the second angle:

  • For sin⁡x\sin xsinx: Second angle =180∘−PV= 180^\circ - \text{PV}=180∘−PV
  • For cos⁡x\cos xcosx: Second angle =360∘−PV= 360^\circ - \text{PV}=360∘−PV
  • For tan⁡x\tan xtanx: Second angle =180∘+PV= 180^\circ + \text{PV}=180∘+PV

Example 1: Solving a basic Sine equation

Section titled “Example 1: Solving a basic Sine equation”

Solve sin⁡x=0.5\sin x = 0.5sinx=0.5 for 0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘.

  1. Find the Principal Value (PV) using your TI-Nspire GDC: x=sin⁡−1(0.5)x=30∘\begin{aligned} x &= \sin^{-1}(0.5) \\ x &= 30^\circ \end{aligned}xx​=sin−1(0.5)=30∘​
  2. Find the second value using the Sine symmetry rule: Second x=180∘−30∘x=150∘\begin{aligned} \text{Second } x &= 180^\circ - 30^\circ \\ x &= 150^\circ \end{aligned}Second xx​=180∘−30∘=150∘​
  3. State final answers: x=30∘x = 30^\circx=30∘ or 150∘150^\circ150∘

(Visual check: If you draw a horizontal line at y=0.5y = 0.5y=0.5 on the sine graph above, you will see it crosses the blue curve twice: once at 30∘30^\circ30∘ and once at 150∘150^\circ150∘!)

Example 2: Solving a rearranged Cosine equation

Section titled “Example 2: Solving a rearranged Cosine equation”

Solve 2cos⁡x+1=02 \cos x + 1 = 02cosx+1=0 for 0∘⩽x⩽360∘0^\circ \leqslant x \leqslant 360^\circ0∘⩽x⩽360∘.

  1. Rearrange the equation to isolate cos⁡x\cos xcosx: 2cos⁡x=−1cos⁡x=−0.5\begin{aligned} 2 \cos x &= -1 \\ \cos x &= -0.5 \end{aligned}2cosxcosx​=−1=−0.5​
  2. Find the Principal Value (PV): x=cos⁡−1(−0.5)x=120∘\begin{aligned} x &= \cos^{-1}(-0.5) \\ x &= 120^\circ \end{aligned}xx​=cos−1(−0.5)=120∘​
  3. Find the second value using the Cosine symmetry rule: Second x=360∘−120∘x=240∘\begin{aligned} \text{Second } x &= 360^\circ - 120^\circ \\ x &= 240^\circ \end{aligned}Second xx​=360∘−120∘=240∘​
  4. State final answers: x=120∘x = 120^\circx=120∘ or 240∘240^\circ240∘

3. The Unit Circle and the CAST Diagram

Section titled “3. The Unit Circle and the CAST Diagram”

While the symmetry rules (like 180∘−PV180^\circ - \text{PV}180∘−PV) are great to memorize, the Unit Circle and the CAST diagram explain exactly why those rules work and give you a foolproof visual tool for finding second angles.

The Unit Circle is simply a circle with a radius of 111, centered at the origin (0,0)(0,0)(0,0) on a coordinate grid. We measure angles starting from the positive x-axis and rotating anti-clockwise.

Because the radius is 111, any point on the edge of the circle has the coordinates (cos⁡θ,sin⁡θ)(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)(cosθ,sinθ).

  • The x-coordinate tells you the value of Cosine.
  • The y-coordinate tells you the value of Sine.
  • The slope of the line (yx\frac{y}{x}xy​) tells you the value of Tangent.

By looking at which coordinates are positive or negative in each of the four quadrants, we get the CAST Diagram.

How to use CAST:

Section titled “How to use CAST:”
  1. Find your Principal Value (PV) using your TI-Nspire GDC. Let’s call this base angle θ\thetaθ.
  2. Identify which quadrants your answer should be in. If the original equation was sin⁡x=−0.5\sin x = -0.5sinx=−0.5 (a negative number), Sine is negative in the T (3rd) and C (4th) quadrants.
  3. Apply the quadrant formulas to your base angle:
    • Quadrant 1 (A): θ\thetaθ
    • Quadrant 2 (S): 180∘−θ180^\circ - \theta180∘−θ
    • Quadrant 3 (T): 180∘+θ180^\circ + \theta180∘+θ
    • Quadrant 4 (C): 360∘−θ360^\circ - \theta360∘−θ

CAST vs Symmetry

You do not have to use both the symmetry rules and the CAST diagram. They are two different methods to achieve the exact same result. Pick the method that makes the most sense to you and stick with it!

4. Transformations: Amplitude and Period (Section 3.1)

Section titled “4. Transformations: Amplitude and Period (Section 3.1)”

In the Functions unit, you learn how multiplying parts of an equation stretches or squishes the graph. For trigonometry, these stretches have specific names: Amplitude and Period.

When a trigonometric function is written in the form y=asin⁡(bx)y = a \sin(bx)y=asin(bx) or y=acos⁡(bx)y = a \cos(bx)y=acos(bx):

1. Amplitude (aaa)

Section titled “1. Amplitude (aaa)”

The amplitude is the “height” of the wave from its center line to its peak.

  • A standard sine wave (y=sin⁡xy = \sin xy=sinx) has an amplitude of 111.
  • The multiplier aaa is a vertical stretch.
  • Formula: Amplitude=∣a∣\text{Amplitude} = |a|Amplitude=∣a∣ (Always positive, even if aaa is negative. A negative aaa just flips the graph upside down).

2. Period (bbb)

Section titled “2. Period (bbb)”

The period is how many degrees it takes for the graph to complete one full cycle before it starts repeating.

  • A standard sine wave completes one cycle in 360∘360^\circ360∘.
  • The multiplier bbb is a horizontal squash. It tells you how many full waves fit into 360∘360^\circ360∘.
  • Formula: Period=360∘b\text{Period} = \frac{360^\circ}{b}Period=b360∘​

Example: Sketching y=3sin⁡(2x)y = 3 \sin(2x)y=3sin(2x)

Section titled “Example: Sketching y=3sin⁡(2x)y = 3 \sin(2x)y=3sin(2x)”
  • Amplitude: The number in front is 333. The graph will peak at 333 and bottom out at −3-3−3.
  • Period: The multiplier inside is 222. Two full waves fit into 360∘360^\circ360∘. The length of one single wave is 360∘2=180∘\frac{360^\circ}{2} = 180^\circ2360∘​=180∘.
