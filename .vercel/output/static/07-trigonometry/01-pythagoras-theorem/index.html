7.1 Pythagoras' Theorem

Pythagoras’ theorem is a fundamental rule in geometry that relates the three sides of a right-angled triangle. It states that the square of the longest side (the hypotenuse) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two shorter sides.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Hypotenuse

The hypotenuse is always the longest side of a right-angled triangle, and it is always directly opposite the 90∘90^\circ90∘ right angle. Identifying the hypotenuse correctly is the most important step!

The formula is universally written as:

a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2

Where ccc is the hypotenuse, and aaa and bbb are the two shorter sides (legs).

1. Basic Pythagoras: Finding Missing Sides

Section titled “1. Basic Pythagoras: Finding Missing Sides”

Before moving to complex shapes or coordinate grids, you must master the two basic mechanics: finding the hypotenuse, and working backward to find a shorter side.

Example 1a: Finding the Hypotenuse

Section titled “Example 1a: Finding the Hypotenuse”

If you know the two shorter sides, you square them, add them together, and then square root the result to find the hypotenuse.

  1. Set up the equation: Let a=5a = 5a=5 and b=12b = 12b=12. The hypotenuse is ccc.

    52+122=c2\begin{aligned} 5^2 + 12^2 &= c^2 \end{aligned}52+122​=c2​

  2. Solve for ccc:

    25+144=c2169=c2c=169=13 cm\begin{aligned} 25 + 144 &= c^2 \\ 169 &= c^2 \\ c &= \sqrt{169} \\ &= 13 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}25+144169c​=c2=c2=169​=13 cm​

Example 1b: Finding a Shorter Side (A Leg)

Section titled “Example 1b: Finding a Shorter Side (A Leg)”

If you already know the hypotenuse and one shorter side, you must subtract the squares instead of adding them.

  1. Set up the equation: The hypotenuse is c=15c = 15c=15. Let a=9a = 9a=9 and the unknown side be b=xb = xb=x.

    92+x2=152\begin{aligned} 9^2 + x^2 &= 15^2 \end{aligned}92+x2​=152​

  2. Solve for xxx:

    81+x2=225x2=225−81x2=144x=144=12 cm\begin{aligned} 81 + x^2 &= 225 \\ x^2 &= 225 - 81 \\ x^2 &= 144 \\ x &= \sqrt{144} \\ &= 12 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}81+x2x2x2x​=225=225−81=144=144​=12 cm​

2. Pythagorean Triples (The “Cheat Codes”)

Section titled “2. Pythagorean Triples (The “Cheat Codes”)”

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three whole numbers that perfectly fit Pythagoras’ theorem. Memorizing the most common ones is a massive advantage for Paper 2 (where calculators are not allowed) and Paper 6 (where spotting number patterns is key to investigations).

If you recognize two sides of a right-angled triangle from a triple, you can instantly know the third side without doing any calculations!

The Essential Triples to Memorize:

  • 3, 4, 5
  • 5, 12, 13
  • 8, 15, 17
  • 7, 24, 25

Multiples of Triples

Any multiple of a Pythagorean triple is also a Pythagorean triple. For example, if you multiply the 3-4-5 triangle by 2, you get 6-8-10. If you multiply it by 10, you get 30-40-50. Notice that Example 1b above (9, 12, 15) is just the 3-4-5 triangle multiplied by 3!

3. Accuracy: Surds, 3 s.f., and Shared Edges

Section titled “3. Accuracy: Surds, 3 s.f., and Shared Edges”

In IGCSE Mathematics, you must leave your final answers to 3 significant figures (s.f.) unless the value is exact or the question specifies otherwise.

However, in multi-step questions with triangles that share an edge, never round your intermediate answers. If a side length is a long decimal, keep it in its exact surd form (e.g., 18\sqrt{18}18​) until the very final step. Rounding too early will create compounding errors that cost you accuracy marks.

Example 2: Triangles with a Shared Edge

Section titled “Example 2: Triangles with a Shared Edge”

Calculate the exact length of xxx, and give your final answer to 3 significant figures.

  1. Find the shared edge (hhh) exactly:

    32+32=h29+9=h218=h2h=18 cm\begin{aligned} 3^2 + 3^2 &= h^2 \\ 9 + 9 &= h^2 \\ 18 &= h^2 \\ h &= \sqrt{18} \text{ cm} \end{aligned}32+329+918h​=h2=h2=h2=18​ cm​

    (Do not write h=4.24h = 4.24h=4.24. Keep it as 18\sqrt{18}18​!)

  2. Use the exact surd to find xxx:

    x2+(18)2=102x2+18=100x2=100−18=82x=82≈9.05538...  ⟹  9.06 cm (to 3 s.f.)\begin{aligned} x^2 + (\sqrt{18})^2 &= 10^2 \\ x^2 + 18 &= 100 \\ x^2 &= 100 - 18 \\ &= 82 \\ x &= \sqrt{82} \\ &\approx 9.05538... \\ &\implies 9.06 \text{ cm (to 3 s.f.)} \end{aligned}x2+(18​)2x2+18x2x​=102=100=100−18=82=82​≈9.05538...⟹9.06 cm (to 3 s.f.)​

What happens if you round too early?

If you had rounded h=18h = \sqrt{18}h=18​ to 4.2 cm early on, your next step would be: x2+4.22=100  ⟹  x2+17.64=100  ⟹  x2=82.36  ⟹  x=9.075...x^2 + 4.2^2 = 100 \implies x^2 + 17.64 = 100 \implies x^2 = 82.36 \implies x = 9.075...x2+4.22=100⟹x2+17.64=100⟹x2=82.36⟹x=9.075... Your final answer would be 9.08 cm, which is mathematically incorrect and will lose you the final accuracy mark! Always keep surds.

4. Finding the Distance Between Two Points on a Grid

Section titled “4. Finding the Distance Between Two Points on a Grid”

You can use Pythagoras’ theorem to find the straight-line distance between any two coordinate points on a Cartesian grid. By drawing horizontal and vertical lines from the points, you can create a right-angled triangle where the distance between the points is the hypotenuse.

Example 3: Distance on a Coordinate Grid

Section titled “Example 3: Distance on a Coordinate Grid”

Find the distance between point A(2,3)A(2, 3)A(2,3) and point B(8,11)B(8, 11)B(8,11).

  1. Find the horizontal and vertical distances:

    Horizontal (a)=8−2=6Vertical (b)=11−3=8\begin{aligned} \text{Horizontal } (a) &= 8 - 2 \\ &= 6 \\ \text{Vertical } (b) &= 11 - 3 \\ &= 8 \end{aligned}Horizontal (a)Vertical (b)​=8−2=6=11−3=8​

  2. Apply Pythagoras’ theorem:

    a2+b2=c262+82=c236+64=c2100=c2c=100=10 units\begin{aligned} a^2 + b^2 &= c^2 \\ 6^2 + 8^2 &= c^2 \\ 36 + 64 &= c^2 \\ 100 &= c^2 \\ c &= \sqrt{100} \\ &= 10 \text{ units} \end{aligned}a2+b262+8236+64100c​=c2=c2=c2=c2=100​=10 units​

5. Pythagoras in Circles: Chords and Distances

Section titled “5. Pythagoras in Circles: Chords and Distances”

A chord is a straight line segment whose endpoints lie on the circle. Pythagoras’ theorem frequently appears in circle problems due to a very specific and important circle property.

Crucial Circle Property

A perpendicular line drawn from the centre of a circle to a chord will always cut that chord exactly in half (bisect it).

Drawing the radius from the centre to the end of the chord forms a right-angled triangle, allowing you to use Pythagoras’ theorem!

Example 4: Finding the Length of a Chord

Section titled “Example 4: Finding the Length of a Chord”

A circle has a radius of 13 cm. A chord is located at a perpendicular distance of 5 cm from the centre of the circle. Calculate the exact length of the chord.

  1. Set up the right-angled triangle: Let xxx be half the length of the chord. The hypotenuse is the radius (c=13c = 13c=13). The shorter side is the distance from the centre (a=5a = 5a=5).

    a2+x2=c252+x2=132\begin{aligned} a^2 + x^2 &= c^2 \\ 5^2 + x^2 &= 13^2 \end{aligned}a2+x252+x2​=c2=132​

  2. Solve for half the chord (xxx):

    25+x2=169x2=169−25=144x=144=12 cm\begin{aligned} 25 + x^2 &= 169 \\ x^2 &= 169 - 25 \\ &= 144 \\ x &= \sqrt{144} \\ &= 12 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}25+x2x2x​=169=169−25=144=144​=12 cm​

  3. Find the full chord length: Because the perpendicular bisects the chord, the full length is twice the value of xxx.

    Full Chord=2×12=24 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Full Chord} &= 2 \times 12 \\ &= 24 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Full Chord​=2×12=24 cm​

6. The Converse of Pythagoras’ Theorem

Section titled “6. The Converse of Pythagoras’ Theorem”

The converse of Pythagoras’ theorem works backwards to check if an unknown triangle has a right angle.

If you know the lengths of all three sides of a triangle, you can test if it is a right-angled triangle:

  • If a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2, then the triangle IS a right-angled triangle.
  • If a2+b2≠c2a^2 + b^2 \neq c^2a2+b2=c2, then the triangle IS NOT a right-angled triangle.

(Always ensure ccc is the longest side when setting up your test!)

Example 5: Checking for a Right Angle

Section titled “Example 5: Checking for a Right Angle”

A triangle has side lengths of 7 cm, 24 cm, and 25 cm. Is this a right-angled triangle?

  1. Identify the longest side: The longest side is 25 cm, so this must be ccc.
  2. Calculate the sum of the squares of the shorter sides: a2+b2=72+242=49+576=625\begin{aligned} a^2 + b^2 &= 7^2 + 24^2 \\ &= 49 + 576 \\ &= 625 \end{aligned}a2+b2​=72+242=49+576=625​
  3. Calculate the square of the longest side: c2=252=625\begin{aligned} c^2 &= 25^2 \\ &= 625 \end{aligned}c2​=252=625​
  4. Compare the results: 625=625\begin{aligned} 625 &= 625 \end{aligned}625​=625​ Since a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2, this is a right-angled triangle.

7. Classifying Triangles on a Grid

Section titled “7. Classifying Triangles on a Grid”

By calculating the distances between three coordinate points, we can determine the exact side lengths of a triangle. Once we have the side lengths, we can classify the triangle based on its properties:

  • Equilateral: All three sides are equal.
  • Isosceles: Exactly two sides are equal.
  • Scalene: All three sides are different lengths.
  • Right-angled: The sides satisfy a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2.

Example 6: Classifying by Coordinates

Section titled “Example 6: Classifying by Coordinates”

The points A(2,2)A(2, 2)A(2,2), B(5,6)B(5, 6)B(5,6), and C(8,2)C(8, 2)C(8,2) form a triangle. Find the exact lengths of the three sides and classify the triangle.

  1. Find the length of ABABAB using Pythagoras: The horizontal distance is 5−2=35 - 2 = 35−2=3. The vertical distance is 6−2=46 - 2 = 46−2=4.

    AB2=32+42=9+16=25AB=25=5 units\begin{aligned} AB^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\ &= 9 + 16 \\ &= 25 \\ AB &= \sqrt{25} \\ &= 5 \text{ units} \end{aligned}AB2AB​=32+42=9+16=25=25​=5 units​

  2. Find the length of BCBCBC using Pythagoras: The horizontal distance is 8−5=38 - 5 = 38−5=3. The vertical distance is 6−2=46 - 2 = 46−2=4.

    BC2=32+42=9+16=25BC=25=5 units\begin{aligned} BC^2 &= 3^2 + 4^2 \\ &= 9 + 16 \\ &= 25 \\ BC &= \sqrt{25} \\ &= 5 \text{ units} \end{aligned}BC2BC​=32+42=9+16=25=25​=5 units​

  3. Find the length of ACACAC (Horizontal Line): Because AAA and CCC are on the same yyy-level, we can just subtract their xxx-coordinates.

    AC=8−2=6 units\begin{aligned} AC &= 8 - 2 \\ &= 6 \text{ units} \end{aligned}AC​=8−2=6 units​

  4. Classify the Triangle: Because two sides are equal in length (AB=5AB = 5AB=5 and BC=5BC = 5BC=5), the triangle is Isosceles.

  2. Find the legs of the newly formed triangle: Horizontal leg=12 cm (same as bottom edge)Vertical leg=12−7=5 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Horizontal leg} &= 12 \text{ cm (same as bottom edge)} \\ \text{Vertical leg} &= 12 - 7 = 5 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Horizontal legVertical leg​=12 cm (same as bottom edge)=12−7=5 cm​
  3. Find the slant edge (hypotenuse ccc): 52+122=c225+144=c2169=c2c=169=13 cm\begin{aligned} 5^2 + 12^2 &= c^2 \\ 25 + 144 &= c^2 \\ 169 &= c^2 \\ c &= \sqrt{169} = 13 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}52+12225+144169c​=c2=c2=c2=169​=13 cm​
  4. Calculate total perimeter: Perimeter=12+7+12+13=44 cm\begin{aligned} \text{Perimeter} &= 12 + 7 + 12 + 13 \\ &= 44 \text{ cm} \end{aligned}Perimeter​=12+7+12+13=44 cm​
