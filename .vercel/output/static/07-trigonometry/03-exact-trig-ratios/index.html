7.3 Exact Trigonometric Values

In non-calculator papers, you are expected to know the exact values (in surd/fraction form) for specific “special” angles: 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90°.

Memorizing a table is one way to do it, but knowing how to build the table or where the values come from is a much safer strategy for an exam.

1. The “Special Triangles” Method (Understanding Why)

Section titled “1. The “Special Triangles” Method (Understanding Why)”

If you forget a value, you can instantly re-derive it by sketching one of two simple right-angled triangles.

Triangle 1: The 45° Triangle (Isosceles Right-Angled)

Section titled “Triangle 1: The 45° Triangle (Isosceles Right-Angled)”

Imagine a right-angled triangle where the two shorter sides are both exactly 1 unit long. Because it is isosceles, the two base angles must be equal: (180 - 90) ÷ 2 = 45°. By Pythagoras, the hypotenuse is 12+12=2\sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}12+12​=2​.

Using SOH CAH TOA on the 45° angle:

  • sin⁡(45∘)=12\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}sin(45∘)=2​1​
  • cos⁡(45∘)=12\cos(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}cos(45∘)=2​1​
  • tan⁡(45∘)=11=1\tan(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{1} = 1tan(45∘)=11​=1

Triangle 2: The 30°-60° Triangle (Equilateral Cut in Half)

Section titled “Triangle 2: The 30°-60° Triangle (Equilateral Cut in Half)”

Start with an equilateral triangle where all sides are 2 units and all angles are 60°. Drop a perpendicular line down the middle. This creates a right-angled triangle with a base of 1, a hypotenuse of 2, and a top angle of 30°. By Pythagoras, the height is 22−12=3\sqrt{2^2 - 1^2} = \sqrt{3}22−12​=3​.

Using SOH CAH TOA on the 30° angle:

  • sin⁡(30∘)=12\sin(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}sin(30∘)=21​
  • cos⁡(30∘)=32\cos(30^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}cos(30∘)=23​​
  • tan⁡(30∘)=13\tan(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}tan(30∘)=3​1​

Using SOH CAH TOA on the 60° angle:

  • sin⁡(60∘)=32\sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}sin(60∘)=23​​
  • cos⁡(60∘)=12\cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2}cos(60∘)=21​
  • tan⁡(60∘)=31=3\tan(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1} = \sqrt{3}tan(60∘)=13​​=3​

2. Rationalised vs. Unrationalised Forms

Section titled “2. Rationalised vs. Unrationalised Forms”

You will often see two different versions of the same exact value, depending on the textbook or marking scheme.

Mathematically, it is traditionally considered “messy” to leave a surd (square root) in the denominator of a fraction. To fix this, we rationalise the denominator by multiplying the top and bottom by that surd.

  • For 45°: 12×22=22\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} = \mathbf{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}2​1​×2​2​​=22​​
  • For 30°: 13×33=33\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \mathbf{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}}3​1​×3​3​​=33​​

Which one should you use?

For Cambridge IGCSE, both forms are completely acceptable and will score full marks unless the question specifically says “Give your answer with a rational denominator.” Don’t let the different formats confuse you—they are exactly the same number!

3. The “0-1-2-3-4” Table Generation Trick

Section titled “3. The “0-1-2-3-4” Table Generation Trick”

If you struggle with visualizing triangles, use this foolproof trick to generate the entire table from scratch the moment your exam starts.

  1. Write the angles: 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°.
  2. Write the numbers 0 to 4: Write 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 under the angles.
  3. Square root them all: 0,1,2,3,4\sqrt{0}, \sqrt{1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{4}0​,1​,2​,3​,4​.
  4. Divide them all by 2: This gives you the entire Sine row! (Simplify where possible: 4/2=2/2=1\sqrt{4}/2 = 2/2 = 14​/2=2/2=1).
  5. Reverse the row for Cosine: Just write the Sine row backwards.
  6. Divide Sin by Cos for Tangent: Because tan⁡(θ)=sin⁡(θ)cos⁡(θ)\tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}tan(θ)=cos(θ)sin(θ)​.

4. The Viral “Finger Method”

Section titled “4. The Viral “Finger Method””

You’ve likely seen this on social media. While it is purely a memorization gimmick and doesn’t teach you why the math works, it is undeniably useful for some students in a high-pressure exam scenario.

  1. Hold out your left hand, palm facing you.
  2. Assign angles to your fingers starting from the pinky: Pinky = 0°, Ring = 30°, Middle = 45°, Index = 60°, Thumb = 90°.
  3. Put down the finger for the angle you want.
  4. For Sine: Count the fingers BELOW the folded finger. Put that number in a square root and divide by 2. bottom fingers2\frac{\sqrt{\text{bottom fingers}}}{2}2bottom fingers​​
  5. For Cosine: Count the fingers ABOVE the folded finger. Put that number in a square root and divide by 2. top fingers2\frac{\sqrt{\text{top fingers}}}{2}2top fingers​​
  6. For Tangent: Put the square root of bottom fingers over the square root of top fingers. bottom fingerstop fingers\frac{\sqrt{\text{bottom fingers}}}{\sqrt{\text{top fingers}}}top fingers​bottom fingers​​

Example for sin(60°): Put down your index finger. There are 3 fingers below it. So, sin⁡(60∘)=32\sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}sin(60∘)=23​​.
