7.5 Non-Right-Angled Triangles (Trigonometry)

SOH CAH TOA and standard Pythagoras’ theorem are fantastic tools, but they only work for right-angled triangles. When a triangle doesn’t have a 90∘90^\circ90∘ angle, we need a different set of rules.

For IGCSE Extended, you need to master three key formulas: the Area of a Triangle, the Sine Rule, and the Cosine Rule.

Good News!

The main versions of these formulas are provided on the IGCSE formula sheet in your exam! You do not need to memorise them, but you do need to know when and how to use them.

The Golden Rule of Triangle Labelling

Section titled “The Golden Rule of Triangle Labelling”

Before using any of these formulas, you must label your triangle correctly.

  • Angles are labelled with capital letters (AAA, BBB, CCC).
  • Sides are labelled with lowercase letters (aaa, bbb, ccc).
  • Crucial detail: Side aaa is always exactly opposite angle AAA. Side bbb is opposite angle BBB, and so on.

1. Area of a Triangle

Section titled “1. Area of a Triangle”

To find the area of any triangle, you need two sides and the angle squished exactly between them (the “included” angle).

Formula:

Area=12absin⁡C\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin CArea=21​absinC
  • When to use it: When you have SAS (Side-Angle-Side).

The Proof

Section titled “The Proof”

Why does this work? Imagine dropping a perpendicular line (hhh) down from angle BBB to side bbb.

Using basic SOH CAH TOA on the left-hand right-angled triangle: sin⁡A=OppositeHypotenuse=hc\sin A = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{h}{c}sinA=HypotenuseOpposite​=ch​

Rearranging this gives us the height: h=csin⁡Ah = c \sin Ah=csinA

The normal area of a triangle is 12×base×height\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}21​×base×height.

Substitute our base (bbb) and our new height formula (csin⁡Ac \sin AcsinA): Area=12×b×(csin⁡A)=12bcsin⁡A\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times b \times (c \sin A) = \frac{1}{2}bc \sin AArea=21​×b×(csinA)=21​bcsinA

(This is the exact same formula as 12absin⁡C\frac{1}{2}ab \sin C21​absinC, just using different sides!)

2. The Sine Rule

Section titled “2. The Sine Rule”

The Sine Rule relates the lengths of the sides to the sines of their opposite angles.

Formulas: To find a side, put the sides on top:

asin⁡A=bsin⁡B=csin⁡C\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}sinAa​=sinBb​=sinCc​

To find an angle, flip it upside down (it makes the algebra easier):

sin⁡Aa=sin⁡Bb=sin⁡Cc\frac{\sin A}{a} = \frac{\sin B}{b} = \frac{\sin C}{c}asinA​=bsinB​=csinC​
  • When to use it: When you have a “Known Pair” (an angle and its opposite side) plus one other piece of information.

The Proof

Section titled “The Proof”

Using the exact same height hhh from the Area proof above, we can look at both sides of the triangle.

From the left triangle: h=csin⁡Ah = c \sin Ah=csinA

From the right triangle: h=asin⁡Ch = a \sin Ch=asinC

Since both expressions equal hhh, we can set them equal to each other: csin⁡A=asin⁡Cc \sin A = a \sin CcsinA=asinC

Divide both sides by (sin⁡Asin⁡C)(\sin A \sin C)(sinAsinC) to isolate the ratios: csin⁡C=asin⁡A\frac{c}{\sin C} = \frac{a}{\sin A}sinCc​=sinAa​

Section titled “Example: Finding a missing side”

In triangle PQRPQRPQR, angle P=65∘P = 65^\circP=65∘, angle R=40∘R = 40^\circR=40∘, and side p=12 cmp = 12\text{ cm}p=12 cm. Find the length of side rrr.

  2. Set up the equation (sides on top): rsin⁡(40∘)=12sin⁡(65∘)\frac{r}{\sin(40^\circ)} = \frac{12}{\sin(65^\circ)}sin(40∘)r​=sin(65∘)12​
  3. Rearrange and solve: r=12×sin⁡(40∘)sin⁡(65∘)r=7.713...0.906...r=8.51 cm (to 3 s.f.)\begin{aligned} r &= \frac{12 \times \sin(40^\circ)}{\sin(65^\circ)} \\ r &= \frac{7.713...}{0.906...} \\ r &= 8.51\text{ cm} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)} \end{aligned}rrr​=sin(65∘)12×sin(40∘)​=0.906...7.713...​=8.51 cm (to 3 s.f.)​

3. The Cosine Rule

Section titled “3. The Cosine Rule”

The Cosine Rule is basically a super-powered version of Pythagoras’ theorem that works for any angle.

Formulas: To find a side:

a2=b2+c2−2bccos⁡Aa^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos Aa2=b2+c2−2bccosA

To find an angle (this is just the above formula rearranged):

A=cos⁡−1(b2+c2−a22bc)A = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc}\right)A=cos−1(2bcb2+c2−a2​)
  • When to use it: When you do NOT have a known pair. Specifically, when you have SSS (all three sides) or SAS (two sides and the trapped angle).

The Proof

Section titled “The Proof”

We drop a perpendicular line hhh from the top vertex CCC. This splits the bottom base ccc into two parts: let’s call the left part xxx, making the right part c−xc - xc−x.

Using Pythagoras’ theorem on the right-hand right-angled triangle: a2=h2+(c−x)2a^2 = h^2 + (c - x)^2a2=h2+(c−x)2 a2=h2+c2−2cx+x2a^2 = h^2 + c^2 - 2cx + x^2a2=h2+c2−2cx+x2

Using Pythagoras on the left-hand triangle gives us: b2=h2+x2b^2 = h^2 + x^2b2=h2+x2

We can substitute b2b^2b2 into the equation above wherever we see (h2+x2)(h^2 + x^2)(h2+x2): a2=b2+c2−2cxa^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2cxa2=b2+c2−2cx

Finally, from the left triangle, basic trigonometry tells us: cos⁡A=xb  ⟹  x=bcos⁡A\cos A = \frac{x}{b} \implies x = b \cos AcosA=bx​⟹x=bcosA

Substitute that in for xxx: a2=b2+c2−2bccos⁡Aa^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos Aa2=b2+c2−2bccosA

Example: Finding a missing side

Section titled “Example: Finding a missing side”

In triangle XYZXYZXYZ, y=8 cmy = 8\text{ cm}y=8 cm, z=11 cmz = 11\text{ cm}z=11 cm, and the included angle X=115∘X = 115^\circX=115∘. Calculate the length of side xxx.

  1. Identify the rule: We have SAS, but we need a side. No known pairs exist. We must use the Cosine Rule.
  2. Substitute values: x2=82+112−2(8)(11)cos⁡(115∘)x2=64+121−176(−0.4226...)x2=185+74.38...x2=259.38...\begin{aligned} x^2 &= 8^2 + 11^2 - 2(8)(11)\cos(115^\circ) \\ x^2 &= 64 + 121 - 176(-0.4226...) \\ x^2 &= 185 + 74.38... \\ x^2 &= 259.38... \end{aligned}x2x2x2x2​=82+112−2(8)(11)cos(115∘)=64+121−176(−0.4226...)=185+74.38...=259.38...​
  3. Square root to find xxx: x=259.38...=16.1 cm (to 3 s.f.)x = \sqrt{259.38...} = 16.1\text{ cm} \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}x=259.38...​=16.1 cm (to 3 s.f.)

Obtuse Angles & Cosine

Unlike the Sine Rule, the Cosine Rule is perfectly well-behaved with obtuse angles. Notice in the example above that cos⁡(115∘)\cos(115^\circ)cos(115∘) naturally became a negative number, which correctly added length to the opposite side.

4. The Ambiguous Case of the Sine Rule

Section titled “4. The Ambiguous Case of the Sine Rule”

When you are given two sides and an angle that is not included (SSA), there are sometimes two completely different triangles that can be drawn with those exact measurements.

Imagine you are given angle A=30∘A = 30^\circA=30∘, side b=12 cmb = 12\text{ cm}b=12 cm, and side a=7 cma = 7\text{ cm}a=7 cm. Side aaa is like a pendulum swinging from the top vertex. It can strike the base in two different spots!

If you use the Sine Rule to find angle BBB, your calculator will only give you the acute angle at B1B_1B1​. If the question specifies that the angle is obtuse (meaning it’s the triangle formed by B2B_2B2​), you must manually find the second angle using the symmetry rule from the graphs:

Obtuse Angle=180∘−Calculator Angle\text{Obtuse Angle} = 180^\circ - \text{Calculator Angle}Obtuse Angle=180∘−Calculator Angle
Steve's Exam Wisdom

If you are using nSolve() on your TI-Nspire to find an angle with the Sine Rule, and the question specifies the angle is obtuse, you can tell the calculator to look in the right place by adding bounds! Type: nSolve(sin(x)/7 = sin(30)/12, x) | 90 < x < 180. This saves you doing the 180∘−x180^\circ - x180∘−x step manually.
