7.6 Pythagoras' Theorem and Trigonometry in 3D

Moving from 2D to 3D can feel intimidating because the diagrams look much more complicated. However, there is only one fundamental secret you need to know for this entire topic:

Every 3D problem is just a series of 2D problems hidden inside a 3D shape. Your main goal is to have “x-ray vision” to find the hidden right-angled triangles floating inside cuboids, pyramids, and prisms. Once you extract that flat 2D triangle, you just use standard Pythagoras and SOH CAH TOA!

1. 3D Pythagoras (The Shortcut)

Section titled “1. 3D Pythagoras (The Shortcut)”

If you need to find the longest diagonal stretching across an empty room (from the bottom-front-left corner to the top-back-right corner), you are finding the space diagonal of a cuboid.

You could do Pythagoras twice (once to find the diagonal across the floor, and once using that floor diagonal to go up to the ceiling), but there is a brilliant shortcut formula.

For a cuboid with length lll, width www, and height hhh, the space diagonal ddd is:

d=l2+w2+h2d = \sqrt{l^2 + w^2 + h^2}d=l2+w2+h2​

2. The Angle Between a Line and a Plane

Section titled “2. The Angle Between a Line and a Plane”

This is the most heavily tested concept in 3D trigonometry. A plane is just a flat surface (like the floor of a room or the base of a pyramid).

To find the angle between a sloped 3D line and a flat plane, you must find the line’s “shadow” (its mathematical name is the projection).

The 3-Step “Drop and Connect” Method:

  1. Look at the top point of your sloping line.
  2. Imagine dropping a rock straight down so it hits the plane perfectly vertically.
  3. Draw a line from where the rock landed back to the bottom of your sloping line. This is your “shadow”.

The angle between a line and a plane is always the angle tucked between the sloping line and its flat shadow.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

If you are struggling to visualize the “shadow” of a line, imagine shining a bright flashlight from the ceiling pointing straight down at the floor. The dark line cast onto the floor by your sloping 3D edge is exactly the shadow you need to draw your 2D triangle!

Sloping Edge vs. Sloping Face!

In pyramid questions, examiners love to trick you by asking for the angle between a sloping face (the flat triangular side) and the base, instead of a sloping edge.

The method is similar, but your flashlight casts a different shadow. The vertical drop goes to the center, but the shadow connects to the middle of the base edge, NOT to the corner! Always read carefully to see if they want the edge or the face.
