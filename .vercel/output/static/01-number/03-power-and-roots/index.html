1.3 Roots and Powers

Powers (indices) and roots are inverse operations. If a power “builds” a number, a root “breaks it down” to its original base. For IGCSE 0607, speed is your best friend—memorising the common values will save you precious minutes in Paper 2 (Non-Calculator).

1. Squares and Square Roots

Section titled “1. Squares and Square Roots”

A square number is the result of multiplying an integer by itself. x2=x×xx^2 = x \times xx2=x×x

The square root (x\sqrt{x}x​) asks: “What number, multiplied by itself, gives me xxx?”

Memorising the Big Squares

Struggling to remember the squares for 13, 14, and 15? Use this trick:

  • 132=16913^2 = \mathbf{169}132=169
  • 142=19614^2 = \mathbf{196}142=196 (Just swap the 6 and 9 from the 13 squared!)
  • 152=22515^2 = \mathbf{225}152=225 (Any number ending in 5 will always have a square that ends in 25).

2. Cubes and Cube Roots

Section titled “2. Cubes and Cube Roots”

A cube number is the result of multiplying a number by itself three times. x3=x×x×xx^3 = x \times x \times xx3=x×x×x

The cube root (x3\sqrt[3]{x}3x​) asks: “What number, multiplied by itself three times, gives me xxx?”

The IGCSE “Must-Know” List

Section titled “The IGCSE “Must-Know” List”

The syllabus specifically requires you to recall the cubes and roots of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 10:

  • 13=1  ⟹  13=11^3 = 1 \implies \sqrt[3]{1} = 113=1⟹31​=1
  • 23=8  ⟹  83=22^3 = 8 \implies \sqrt[3]{8} = 223=8⟹38​=2
  • 33=27  ⟹  273=33^3 = 27 \implies \sqrt[3]{27} = 333=27⟹327​=3
  • 43=64  ⟹  643=44^3 = 64 \implies \sqrt[3]{64} = 443=64⟹364​=4
  • 53=125  ⟹  1253=55^3 = 125 \implies \sqrt[3]{125} = 553=125⟹3125​=5
  • 103=1000  ⟹  10003=1010^3 = 1000 \implies \sqrt[3]{1000} = 10103=1000⟹31000​=10
Steve's Exam Wisdom

The 64 'Double Agent' Trap

Note that 64 is both a square number (828^282) and a cube number (434^343). Examiners love to use 64 in roots questions to see if you are paying attention to the little index number on the root symbol!

3. Calculating with Powers and Roots

Section titled “3. Calculating with Powers and Roots”

Other Powers

Section titled “Other Powers”

Beyond squares and cubes, you may see higher powers like 242^424 or 252^525.

  • Power of 4: 24=2×2×2×2=162^4 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 1624=2×2×2×2=16
  • Power of 5: 25=322^5 = 3225=32
Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Negative Base Trap: (-3)² vs -3²

Students lose marks here every single year! When raising negative numbers to a power, brackets matter:

  1. Even Powers of Negatives are Positive: (−3)2=−3×−3=9(-3)^2 = -3 \times -3 = 9(−3)2=−3×−3=9 (The negatives pair up and cancel out).

  2. Odd Powers of Negatives are Negative: (−3)3=−3×−3×−3=−27(-3)^3 = -3 \times -3 \times -3 = -27(−3)3=−3×−3×−3=−27

  3. The Calculator Trap: If you type -3^2 into your calculator without brackets, it reads it as “the negative version of 3 squared” and gives you -9. If you mean to square negative 3, you must use brackets: (-3)^2.

Order of Operations (BIDMAS/BEDMAS)

Section titled “Order of Operations (BIDMAS/BEDMAS)”

When roots are part of a larger calculation, treat the line of the root symbol like brackets. You must evaluate the entire value “under” the root sign before doing anything else.

Example: Work out 5×8+235 \times \sqrt{8 + 2^3}5×8+23​

  1. Resolve the power inside: 23=82^3 = 823=8
  2. Add inside the root: 8+8=168 + 8 = 168+8=16
  3. Find the root: 16=4\sqrt{16} = 416​=4
  4. Multiply: 5×4=205 \times 4 = 205×4=20

4. Advanced Indices (Extended Curriculum)

Section titled “4. Advanced Indices (Extended Curriculum)”

In the Extended curriculum, powers are not always positive whole numbers. You must know how to handle negative powers, fractions, and decimal bases.

Negative Indices

Section titled “Negative Indices”

A negative power indicates a reciprocal (flip it over). It does NOT make the final number negative! a−n=1ana^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}a−n=an1​

Steve's Exam Wisdom

The Negative Power Illusion

A negative power and a negative base do completely different things. Do not mix them up!

  • Standard: 3−2=132=193^{-2} = \frac{1}{3^2} = \frac{1}{9}3−2=321​=91​ (The power flips it).
  • Negative Base: (−3)−2=1(−3)2=19(-3)^{-2} = \frac{1}{(-3)^2} = \frac{1}{9}(−3)−2=(−3)21​=91​ (The power flips it, and the square makes the -3 positive).
  • No Brackets: −3−2=−(132)=−19-3^{-2} = -\left(\frac{1}{3^2}\right) = -\frac{1}{9}−3−2=−(321​)=−91​ (The power flips the 3, and the minus sign sits in front).

Fractional Indices

Section titled “Fractional Indices”

The denominator (bottom) of the fraction is the root, and the numerator (top) is the power. amn=(an)ma^{\frac{m}{n}} = (\sqrt[n]{a})^manm​=(na​)m

  • Example: 1634=(164)3=23=816^{\frac{3}{4}} = (\sqrt[4]{16})^3 = 2^3 = 81643​=(416​)3=23=8

Combined and Fractional Bases

Section titled “Combined and Fractional Bases”

Be careful when the “base” isn’t a whole integer, or when the index is both negative and a fraction.

  • Fractional Base: Raise both the top and bottom to the power. (23)3=2333=827\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^3 = \frac{2^3}{3^3} = \frac{8}{27}(32​)3=3323​=278​ If the power is negative, flip the fraction to make the power positive! (34)−2=(43)2=169\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{-2} = \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{16}{9}(43​)−2=(34​)2=916​

  • Decimal Base: Convert the decimal to a fraction first. It makes calculations much easier. 0.53=(12)3=180.5^3 = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{1}{8}0.53=(21​)3=81​
