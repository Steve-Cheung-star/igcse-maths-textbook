1.17 Radicals and Surds

1. What are Radicals and Surds?

Section titled “1. What are Radicals and Surds?”

In mathematics, a radical is any expression that uses a root symbol (like a square root, cube root, etc.).

A surd is a specific classification of a radical: it is a root that cannot be simplified into a rational number (a whole number or a clean fraction). In other words, a surd evaluates to an irrational number (a non-repeating, never-ending decimal).

In North America (Je suis Canadien 🇨🇦), we generally refer to all of these simply as radicals, but it is important to know the distinction:

  • 25\sqrt{25}25​ is a radical, but it evaluates to exactly 555. Therefore, it is a rational number, not a surd.
  • 20\sqrt{20}20​ is a radical, but it evaluates to ≈4.472...\approx 4.472...≈4.472.... Because it cannot be written as a clean fraction, it is an irrational radical, which is known as a surd.

The Number Hierarchy

All surds are radicals, but not all radicals are surds!

  • Real Numbers
    • Radicals (Any expression with a root: 4\sqrt{4}4​, 7\sqrt{7}7​, 83\sqrt[3]{8}38​)
      • Rational Radicals: Simplify cleanly into rational numbers (e.g., 4=2\sqrt{4} = 24​=2, 83=2\sqrt[3]{8} = 238​=2)
      • Surds: Irrational radicals that remain roots in their simplest form (e.g., 7≈2.645...\sqrt{7} \approx 2.645...7​≈2.645...)

Parts of a Radical

Section titled “Parts of a Radical”

Every radical expression is made up of three main components. For an expression like axna\sqrt[n]{x}anx​:

  • Coefficient (aaa): The number multiplying the radical on the outside.
  • Radical Symbol (x\sqrt{\phantom{x}}x​): The symbol indicating a root is being calculated.
  • Index / Degree (nnn): The small number sitting in the “v” of the radical symbol, indicating which root is being taken. If no index is written, it is mathematically assumed to be 222 (a square root).
  • Radicand (xxx): The number or expression underneath the radical symbol.

2. Converting: Entire and Mixed Radicals

Section titled “2. Converting: Entire and Mixed Radicals”

Radicals can be written in two forms:

  • Entire Radicals: The coefficient is 111 (everything is under the root), e.g., 20\sqrt{20}20​.
  • Mixed Radicals: There is a coefficient outside the root, e.g., 252\sqrt{5}25​.

Simplifying (Entire →\rightarrow→ Mixed)

Section titled “Simplifying (Entire →\rightarrow→ Mixed)”

To simplify a radical, find the largest perfect square factor of the radicand.

  1. Example: Simplify 72\sqrt{72}72​.
  2. Find the largest perfect square factor of 727272, which is 363636 (36×2=7236 \times 2 = 7236×2=72).
  3. Split the root: 36×2\sqrt{36} \times \sqrt{2}36​×2​.
  4. Evaluate the perfect square: 62\mathbf{6\sqrt{2}}62​.

”Unsimplifying” (Mixed →\rightarrow→ Entire)

Section titled “”Unsimplifying” (Mixed →\rightarrow→ Entire)”

Sometimes you need to reverse the process to compare the size of radicals. To bring a coefficient inside a square root, you must square it.

  1. Example: Convert 434\sqrt{3}43​ into an entire radical.
  2. Square the outside number and bring it under the root: 42×3\sqrt{4^2 \times 3}42×3​.
  3. Calculate: 16×3\sqrt{16 \times 3}16×3​.
  4. Final Answer: 48\mathbf{\sqrt{48}}48​.
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Exam Trick: Finding an Irrational Number Between Two Integers

A very common exam question asks you to name an irrational number between two standard integers (like 6 and 7).

To solve this, use the “unsimplifying” trick to turn the integers into entire radicals!

  1. Square both integers and put them under a root: 6=366 = \sqrt{36}6=36​ and 7=497 = \sqrt{49}7=49​.
  2. Any non-perfect square between 36 and 49 will be an irrational number between 6 and 7.
  3. Examples: 37\mathbf{\sqrt{37}}37​, 40\mathbf{\sqrt{40}}40​, or 48\mathbf{\sqrt{48}}48​. (Do not pick 36\sqrt{36}36​ or 49\sqrt{49}49​, as those are rational integers!)

3. Operations with Radicals

Section titled “3. Operations with Radicals”

Multiplication and Division

Section titled “Multiplication and Division”

When multiplying or dividing radicals, apply the operations to the coefficients together, and the radicands together. Keep “outside” numbers with “outside” numbers, and “inside” numbers with “inside” numbers.

  • Multiplication Rule: ab×cd=(a×c)b×da\sqrt{b} \times c\sqrt{d} = (a \times c)\sqrt{b \times d}ab​×cd​=(a×c)b×d​
  • Division Rule: abcd=(ac)bd\frac{a\sqrt{b}}{c\sqrt{d}} = \left(\frac{a}{c}\right)\sqrt{\frac{b}{d}}cd​ab​​=(ca​)db​​

Multiplying the Same Radical 2 or 3 Times

Section titled “Multiplying the Same Radical 2 or 3 Times”

A very common scenario is multiplying a radical by itself. Because a square root asks “what times itself equals this number?”, squaring a square root simply cancels the root out!

  • Two Times: x×x=x\sqrt{x} \times \sqrt{x} = xx​×x​=x.
    • Example: 7×7=7\sqrt{7} \times \sqrt{7} = \mathbf{7}7​×7​=7
  • Three Times: x×x×x=xx\sqrt{x} \times \sqrt{x} \times \sqrt{x} = x\sqrt{x}x​×x​×x​=xx​.
    • Example: 5×5×5=5×5=55\sqrt{5} \times \sqrt{5} \times \sqrt{5} = 5 \times \sqrt{5} = \mathbf{5\sqrt{5}}5​×5​×5​=5×5​=55​
  • With Coefficients: (ab)2=ab×ab=a2×b(a\sqrt{b})^2 = a\sqrt{b} \times a\sqrt{b} = a^2 \times b(ab​)2=ab​×ab​=a2×b.
    • Example: (32)2=32×(2)2=9×2=18(3\sqrt{2})^2 = 3^2 \times (\sqrt{2})^2 = 9 \times 2 = \mathbf{18}(32​)2=32×(2​)2=9×2=18

Common Misconceptions (Pythagoras Warning!)

Many students make these fatal errors, especially when doing Pythagorean theorem (a2+b2=c2\text{a}^2 + \text{b}^2 = \text{c}^2a2+b2=c2) questions:

  • ❌ WRONG: 2b=2b2\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{2b}2b​=2b​
    • RIGHT: The 222 outside is actually 4\sqrt{4}4​. So, 2b=4×b=4b2\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{b} = \mathbf{\sqrt{4b}}2b​=4​×b​=4b​.
  • ❌ WRONG: b2=b2\frac{\sqrt{b}}{2} = \sqrt{\frac{b}{2}}2b​​=2b​​
    • RIGHT: The 222 on the bottom is actually 4\sqrt{4}4​. So, b2=b4=b4\frac{\sqrt{b}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{b}}{\sqrt{4}} = \mathbf{\sqrt{\frac{b}{4}}}2b​​=4​b​​=4b​​.

Never mix an “outside” integer directly into an “inside” radicand without squaring it first!

Addition and Subtraction

Section titled “Addition and Subtraction”

You can only add or subtract radicals if they have the exact same radicand and index. Think of them like “like terms” in algebra (3x+2x=5x3x + 2x = 5x3x+2x=5x).

  • Rule: ac±bc=(a±b)ca\sqrt{c} \pm b\sqrt{c} = (a \pm b)\sqrt{c}ac​±bc​=(a±b)c​

Example: Add 320+553\sqrt{20} + 5\sqrt{5}320​+55​. You cannot add these immediately because the radicands are different. You must simplify first!

  1. Simplify 3203\sqrt{20}320​: 34×5=3(2)5=653\sqrt{4 \times 5} = 3(2)\sqrt{5} = 6\sqrt{5}34×5​=3(2)5​=65​.
  2. Rewrite the equation: 65+556\sqrt{5} + 5\sqrt{5}65​+55​.
  3. Add the coefficients: (6+5)5=115(6 + 5)\sqrt{5} = \mathbf{11\sqrt{5}}(6+5)5​=115​.

4. Expanding Brackets (FOIL)

Section titled “4. Expanding Brackets (FOIL)”

When dealing with binomials containing radicals, use the distributive property or FOIL (First, Outer, Inner, Last), just like you would with polynomials.

Example: Expand and simplify (3+25)(4−5)(3 + 2\sqrt{5})(4 - \sqrt{5})(3+25​)(4−5​).

  • First: 3×4=123 \times 4 = 123×4=12
  • Outer: 3×−5=−353 \times -\sqrt{5} = -3\sqrt{5}3×−5​=−35​
  • Inner: 25×4=852\sqrt{5} \times 4 = 8\sqrt{5}25​×4=85​
  • Last: 25×−5=−2(5)=−102\sqrt{5} \times -\sqrt{5} = -2(5) = -1025​×−5​=−2(5)=−10
  • Combine Like Terms: (12−10)+(−35+85)=2+55(12 - 10) + (-3\sqrt{5} + 8\sqrt{5}) = \mathbf{2 + 5\sqrt{5}}(12−10)+(−35​+85​)=2+55​.

5. Rationalising the Denominator

Section titled “5. Rationalising the Denominator”

It is a mathematical convention not to leave a radical in the denominator of a fraction. Rationalising is the process of eliminating the radical from the bottom.

Type 1: Single Radical Term

Section titled “Type 1: Single Radical Term”

Multiply the numerator and denominator by the radical in the denominator.

Example: Rationalise 723\frac{7}{2\sqrt{3}}23​7​.

  1. Multiply top and bottom by 3\sqrt{3}3​: 7×323×3\frac{7 \times \sqrt{3}}{2\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3}}23​×3​7×3​​.
  2. Simplify: 732(3)\frac{7\sqrt{3}}{2(3)}2(3)73​​.
  3. Final Answer: 736\mathbf{\frac{7\sqrt{3}}{6}}673​​.

Type 2: Two Radical Terms (Binomial Denominator)

Section titled “Type 2: Two Radical Terms (Binomial Denominator)”

Multiply the top and bottom by the conjugate of the denominator. The conjugate is the exact same expression, but with the opposite sign in the middle (a+b→a−ba + \sqrt{b} \rightarrow a - \sqrt{b}a+b​→a−b​). This creates a difference of squares, eliminating the radicals.

Example: Rationalise 57−2\frac{5}{\sqrt{7} - \sqrt{2}}7​−2​5​.

  1. Multiply by the conjugate 7+27+2\frac{\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2}}7​+2​7​+2​​.
  2. Top: 5(7+2)=57+525(\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2}) = 5\sqrt{7} + 5\sqrt{2}5(7​+2​)=57​+52​.
  3. Bottom: (7−2)(7+2)=(7)2−(2)2=7−2=5(\sqrt{7} - \sqrt{2})(\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2}) = (\sqrt{7})^2 - (\sqrt{2})^2 = 7 - 2 = 5(7​−2​)(7​+2​)=(7​)2−(2​)2=7−2=5.
  4. Result: 57+525\frac{5\sqrt{7} + 5\sqrt{2}}{5}557​+52​​.
  5. Factor and simplify: 5(7+2)5=7+2\frac{5(\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2})}{5} = \mathbf{\sqrt{7} + \sqrt{2}}55(7​+2​)​=7​+2​.
