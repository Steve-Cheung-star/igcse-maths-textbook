1.12 Percentages

Percentages are parts of 100. Whether you are calculating a store discount, a bank’s interest rate, or a business profit, the goal is to understand how a portion relates to the whole.

1. Finding a Percentage of a Quantity

Section titled “1. Finding a Percentage of a Quantity”

To find a percentage of an amount, convert the percentage to a decimal (the multiplier) and multiply.

  • Method: Divide the percentage by 100, then multiply by the quantity.
  • Example: Find 15% of £480.
    1. 15÷100=0.1515 \div 100 = 0.1515÷100=0.15
    2. 0.15×480=720.15 \times 480 = 720.15×480=72 Answer: £72

    "85% (The Rest: £408)" : 85

2. Expressing One Quantity as a Percentage of Another

Section titled “2. Expressing One Quantity as a Percentage of Another”

This is essentially “writing a test score.” You create a fraction representing the part over the whole, and then turn it into a percentage.

  • Formula: PartWhole×100\frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} \times 100WholePart​×100
  • Example: A student scores 34 out of 40 on a mental maths test. What is their percentage?
    1. 3440=0.85\frac{34}{40} = 0.854034​=0.85
    2. 0.85×100=850.85 \times 100 = 850.85×100=85 Answer: 85%

3. Percentage Increase and Decrease

Section titled “3. Percentage Increase and Decrease”

You can do this in two steps (find the percentage, then add/subtract), but using a multiplier is much faster and absolutely essential for harder “repeated change” questions.

The Multiplier Method

Section titled “The Multiplier Method”
  • Increase by 20%: The original is 100%. 100%+20%=120%100\% + 20\% = 120\%100%+20%=120%. As a decimal: 1.20
  • Decrease by 15%: The original is 100%. 100%−15%=85%100\% - 15\% = 85\%100%−15%=85%. As a decimal: 0.85

Example: A coat costs £120. It is reduced by 35% in a sale. Find the new price.

  1. Multiplier: 100%−35%=65%→0.65100\% - 35\% = 65\% \rightarrow 0.65100%−35%=65%→0.65.
  2. Calculation: 120×0.65=78120 \times 0.65 = 78120×0.65=78. Answer: £78

Chained (Repeated) Percentage Changes

Section titled “Chained (Repeated) Percentage Changes”

When an amount undergoes multiple percentage changes over time, you multiply the multipliers. You never just add or subtract the percentages themselves.

The Timeline of a Chained Change: Let’s say a population of 5000 increases by 10% in Year 1, and then decreases by 10% in Year 2.

flowchart LR
    A[Original: 5000] -- "× 1.10 (+10%)" --> B[Year 1: 5500]
    B -- "× 0.90 (-10%)" --> C[Year 2: 4950]
  • Fast Calculation: 5000×1.10×0.90=49505000 \times 1.10 \times 0.90 = 49505000×1.10×0.90=4950.

Pitfall: The '+10% then -10%' Trap

A massive misconception is thinking that increasing by 10% and then decreasing by 10% brings you back to the original starting number. It does NOT! The second percentage change (the decrease) is calculated on the new, larger amount, so it takes a bigger bite out of the total.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

Profit and Loss

“Profit” is just a fancy word for percentage increase. “Loss” is a percentage decrease. If a shopkeeper buys a phone for £200 and sells it for £250:

  1. Profit = £50.
  2. Percentage Profit = 50200×100=25%\frac{50}{200} \times 100 = 25\%20050​×100=25%.

4. Simple and Compound Interest

Section titled “4. Simple and Compound Interest”

This is a favorite Paper 4 topic. Note that formulas are not provided in the exam.

Simple Interest

Section titled “Simple Interest”

Interest is only calculated on the original amount. It stays exactly the same every single year. It is a linear sequence.

  • Formula: I=P×R×T100I = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}I=100P×R×T​ (where PPP = Principal/Original Amount, RRR = Rate, TTT = Time).

Compound Interest

Section titled “Compound Interest”

Interest is calculated on the new total each year (you earn interest on your interest). This creates exponential growth.

  • Formula: Total Amount=P×(Multiplier)T\text{Total Amount} = P \times (\text{Multiplier})^TTotal Amount=P×(Multiplier)T

Example: £2000 is invested at 4% per year compound interest for 3 years.

  1. Multiplier for 4% increase: 1.041.041.04
  2. Calculation: 2000×(1.04)3=2249.732000 \times (1.04)^3 = 2249.732000×(1.04)3=2249.73 Answer: £2249.73

Pitfall: Interest vs. Total Balance

Read the question carefully! Does it ask for the total amount in the bank or just the interest earned? If it asks for interest only: [Total Balance] - [Original Investment] = [Interest Earned].

5. Depreciation and Inflation

Section titled “5. Depreciation and Inflation”

These use exactly the same mathematical structure as compound interest, but they represent real-world values changing over time.

  • Depreciation (Value Goes Down): Used for cars, machinery, or technology. The multiplier is less than 1.
    • Example: A car worth £15,000 depreciates by 12% each year. Value after 5 years: 15,000×(0.88)515,000 \times (0.88)^515,000×(0.88)5.
  • Inflation (Value Goes Up): Used for the cost of living, groceries, or house prices. The multiplier is greater than 1.
    • Example: Bread costs £1.20. If annual inflation is 3%, what is the cost after 2 years? 1.20×(1.03)21.20 \times (1.03)^21.20×(1.03)2.

6. Solving for Unknowns (Advanced Extended)

Section titled “6. Solving for Unknowns (Advanced Extended)”

Sometimes the exam flips the compound interest question and asks you to find the Rate (RRR) or the Time (nnn).

Finding an Unknown Rate (The nth Root)

Section titled “Finding an Unknown Rate (The nth Root)”

If an investment of £500 grows to £600 in 4 years, what is the annual compound interest rate?

  1. Set up the equation: 500×x4=600500 \times x^4 = 600500×x4=600
  2. Isolate the multiplier (xxx): x4=600500=1.2x^4 = \frac{600}{500} = 1.2x4=500600​=1.2
  3. Use the nth root on your calculator: x=1.24≈1.0466x = \sqrt[4]{1.2} \approx 1.0466x=41.2​≈1.0466
  4. Convert multiplier back to a percentage: A multiplier of 1.04661.04661.0466 means a 4.66%4.66\%4.66% increase. Answer: 4.66%

Finding Unknown Time (Logarithms or Graphs)

Section titled “Finding Unknown Time (Logarithms or Graphs)”

If an investment of £1000 at 5% needs to reach £1500, how many years does it take?

  1. Set up the equation: 1000×(1.05)n=15001000 \times (1.05)^n = 15001000×(1.05)n=1500
  2. Isolate the power: 1.05n=1.51.05^n = 1.51.05n=1.5

Sneak Peek: Logs and Graphs

To solve for an exponent like nnn, we will eventually use Logarithms (Chapter 3). For now, you can solve this by using the Table function on your calculator (inputting y=1.05xy = 1.05^xy=1.05x) or by sketching the graph and finding where it crosses the line y=1.5y = 1.5y=1.5.

7. Reverse Percentages (The “Original Value” Trap)

Section titled “7. Reverse Percentages (The “Original Value” Trap)”

This is heavily tested in Paper 4. You are given the final value after a change has happened, and you are asked to find the original starting value.

Example: A laptop is sold for £540 after a 10% discount. Find the original price.

Pitfall: The 10% Illusion

Do not calculate 10% of £540 and add it on! The 10% discount was calculated based on the original (larger) price, not the sale price.

The Reverse Timeline Method

Section titled “The Reverse Timeline Method”

Think about how the price moved forward in time, and then reverse the mathematics to go backward in time.

flowchart LR
    subgraph Forward [1. The Forward Journey]
        direction LR
        A[Original Price: ?] -- "× 0.90" --> B[Sale Price: £540]
    end
    
    subgraph Reverse [2. The Reverse Journey]
        direction RL
        C[Sale Price: £540] -- "÷ 0.90" --> D[Original Price: £600]
    end
  • Calculation: 540÷0.90=600540 \div 0.90 = 600540÷0.90=600. Answer: £600
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Spotting the Reverse Question

Whenever you see phrases like “Original price,” “Before the increase,” “Cost price,” or “Prior to the sale,” you are dealing with a Reverse Percentage. Your first step should always be to set up a division: [Final Amount] ÷ [Multiplier] = [Original Amount]

  • ✅ Solution 8

An investment of £1200 is left in a compound interest account for 6 years. It grows to £1500. Calculate the annual percentage interest rate.

  1. Set up the equation: 1200×r6=15001200 \times r^6 = 15001200×r6=1500.

  3. Take the 6th root: r=1.256≈1.03789r = \sqrt[6]{1.25} \approx 1.03789r=61.25​≈1.03789.
  4. Convert multiplier back: A multiplier of 1.037891.037891.03789 is an increase of 3.789%3.789\%3.789%. Answer: 3.79% (to 3 sig figs)
