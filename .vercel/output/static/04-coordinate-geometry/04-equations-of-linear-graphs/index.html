4.4 Equations of linear graphs

Every straight line on a coordinate grid can be described by an algebraic equation. This equation is essentially a “rule” that connects the xxx-coordinate of every point on the line to its corresponding yyy-coordinate.

1. The Standard Form: y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c

Section titled “1. The Standard Form: y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c”

The most useful and common way to write the equation of a straight line is the gradient-intercept form.

y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c
  • m\boldsymbol{m}m represents the gradient (steepness). It tells you how many units the line goes up (or down) for every 111 unit it moves to the right.
  • c\boldsymbol{c}c represents the yyy-intercept. This is the exact value where the line crosses the vertical yyy-axis (where x=0x = 0x=0).

1.1 Finding the equation from a graph

Section titled “1.1 Finding the equation from a graph”

To find the equation of a line when looking at its graph, you need to identify ccc and calculate mmm.

Example Walkthrough:

  1. Find c\boldsymbol{c}c: Look at the yyy-axis. The line crosses at 111, so c=1\boldsymbol{c = 1}c=1.
  2. Find m\boldsymbol{m}m: Pick two easy-to-read points on the line, like (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1) and (4,3)(4, 3)(4,3). m=RiseRun=Change in yChange in x=24=0.5m = \frac{\text{Rise}}{\text{Run}} = \frac{\text{Change in } y}{\text{Change in } x} = \frac{2}{4} = \boldsymbol{0.5}m=RunRise​=Change in xChange in y​=42​=0.5
  3. Write the equation: Substitute mmm and ccc into y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c. Answer: y=0.5x+1\boldsymbol{y = 0.5x + 1}y=0.5x+1

(Note: Always give your final equation in a fully simplified form. Write y=0.5x+1y = 0.5x + 1y=0.5x+1 or y=12x+1y = \frac{1}{2}x + 1y=21​x+1, not y=24x+1y = \frac{2}{4}x + 1y=42​x+1.)

2. Finding an Equation Given the Gradient and a Point

Section titled “2. Finding an Equation Given the Gradient and a Point”

Often, you are given the gradient (mmm) and just one coordinate point that the line passes through. How you handle this depends on whether that point is the yyy-intercept or not.

Case A: The point is the yyy-intercept (0,c)(0, c)(0,c)

Section titled “Case A: The point is the yyy-intercept (0,c)(0, c)(0,c)”

If the given point has an xxx-coordinate of 000, it is the yyy-intercept. You have everything you need to write the equation immediately.

Example: A line has a gradient of −3-3−3 and passes through (0,5)(0, 5)(0,5).

  • m=−3m = -3m=−3
  • Because x=0x=0x=0, we know c=5c = 5c=5.
  • Answer: y=−3x+5\boldsymbol{y = -3x + 5}y=−3x+5

Case B: The point is NOT the yyy-intercept (x,y)(x, y)(x,y)

Section titled “Case B: The point is NOT the yyy-intercept (x,y)(x, y)(x,y)”

If the point is anywhere else, you must use algebra to solve for ccc. This is the absolute most important method to master for your exams!

Mastering the Gradient-Intercept Method

  1. Start with the standard formula: y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c
  2. Substitute your known gradient (mmm) into the equation.
  3. Substitute the xxx and yyy coordinates of your given point into the equation.
  4. Solve the resulting simple linear equation to find ccc.
  5. Rewrite the final equation with both mmm and ccc filled in.

Example: A line has a gradient of 444 and passes through the point (2,11)(2, 11)(2,11). Find its equation.

  1. Start with the formula: y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c
  2. Substitute the gradient: y=4x+cy = 4x + cy=4x+c
  3. Substitute the coordinates (x=2x = 2x=2, y=11y = 11y=11): 11=4(2)+c11=8+c\begin{aligned} 11 &= 4(2) + c \\ 11 &= 8 + c \end{aligned}1111​=4(2)+c=8+c​
  4. Solve for ccc: c=11−8c=3\begin{aligned} c &= 11 - 8 \\ c &= \mathbf{3} \end{aligned}cc​=11−8=3​
  5. Rewrite the final equation: Answer: y=4x+3\boldsymbol{y = 4x + 3}y=4x+3
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Check Your Work!

Once you find your final equation, plug the original xxx-coordinate back in. In the example above, if x=2x=2x=2, then y=4(2)+3=11y = 4(2) + 3 = 11y=4(2)+3=11. It matches the original point! This takes 5 seconds and guarantees you haven’t made an arithmetic mistake.

3. Horizontal and Vertical Lines

Section titled “3. Horizontal and Vertical Lines”

Lines that are perfectly flat or perfectly upright have special, very simple equations. They do not have an xxx and a yyy in the same equation.

  • Horizontal Lines (y=c\boldsymbol{y = c}y=c): Every point on this line has the exact same yyy-coordinate. The gradient is 000, so the "mxmxmx" part disappears.
  • Vertical Lines (x=k\boldsymbol{x = k}x=k): Every point on this line has the exact same xxx-coordinate. The gradient is “undefined” (infinitely steep), so we cannot use y=mx+cy=mx+cy=mx+c.

4. The General Form: ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c

Section titled “4. The General Form: ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c”

Sometimes, equations are written with both variables on the same side, like 3x+2y=123x + 2y = 123x+2y=12. This is often called the general form.

Common Misconception

Students frequently look at 3x+2y=123x + 2y = 123x+2y=12 and assume the gradient is 333 because it is the number attached to xxx. This is wrong! You can only read the gradient when yyy is completely by itself on one side of the equals sign.

4.1 Converting General Form to y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c

Section titled “4.1 Converting General Form to y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c”

To find the gradient and yyy-intercept, you must rearrange the equation to make yyy the subject.

Syllabus Example Walkthrough: Find the gradient and yyy-intercept of the graph with equation 5x+4y=85x + 4y = 85x+4y=8.

  1. Isolate the yyy term: Subtract 5x5x5x from both sides. 4y=−5x+84y = -5x + 84y=−5x+8
  2. Make yyy the subject: Divide everything by 444. y=−54x+84y = -\frac{5}{4}x + \frac{8}{4}y=−45​x+48​
  3. Simplify your fractions! (This is where many marks are lost). y=−54x+2(or y=−1.25x+2)y = -\frac{5}{4}x + 2 \quad (\text{or } y = -1.25x + 2)y=−45​x+2(or y=−1.25x+2)
  4. Extract your values: Answer: The gradient (mmm) is −54\boldsymbol{-\frac{5}{4}}−45​, and the yyy-intercept (ccc) is 2\boldsymbol{2}2.

Another Example (Watch the simplification): Find the gradient of the line 3x+6y=103x + 6y = 103x+6y=10.

6y=−3x+10y=−36x+106\begin{aligned} 6y &= -3x + 10 \\ y &= -\frac{3}{6}x + \frac{10}{6} \end{aligned}6yy​=−3x+10=−63​x+610​​

Wait! You must simplify the fraction −36-\frac{3}{6}−63​ to −12-\frac{1}{2}−21​. The final gradient is −12-\frac{1}{2}−21​.

4.2 Converting y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c into General Form

Section titled “4.2 Converting y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c into General Form”

Higher-tier questions may ask you to give your final equation in the form ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c where a,b,a, b,a,b, and ccc are integers (whole numbers). This means you need to get rid of any fractions.

Example Walkthrough: Rewrite the equation y=23x−4y = \frac{2}{3}x - 4y=32​x−4 in the form ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c, where a,b,ca, b, ca,b,c are integers.

  1. Clear the fraction: Multiply every single term by the denominator (333). (3)y=(3)23x−(3)43y=2x−12\begin{aligned} (3)y &= (3)\frac{2}{3}x - (3)4 \\ 3y &= 2x - 12 \end{aligned}(3)y3y​=(3)32​x−(3)4=2x−12​
  2. Rearrange the terms: Move the xxx and yyy terms to the same side. Subtract 2x2x2x from both sides: −2x+3y=−12\boldsymbol{-2x + 3y = -12}−2x+3y=−12 (Note: Writing 2x−3y=122x - 3y = 122x−3y=12 is also perfectly correct and means the exact same thing!)

5. Finding the Equation from Two Given Points

Section titled “5. Finding the Equation from Two Given Points”

If you aren’t given a graph, but instead are given two coordinate points, you can calculate the equation entirely algebraically by first finding the gradient, and then using Concept 2 (Case B).

Example Walkthrough: Find the fully simplified equation of the line passing through A(2,5)A(2, 5)A(2,5) and B(6,17)B(6, 17)B(6,17).

  1. Calculate the gradient (mmm): m=y2−y1x2−x1=17−56−2=124=3m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{17 - 5}{6 - 2} = \frac{12}{4} = \mathbf{3}m=x2​−x1​y2​−y1​​=6−217−5​=412​=3
  2. Substitute mmm and one point into y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c to find ccc: Using point A(2,5)A(2, 5)A(2,5) where x=2x = 2x=2 and y=5y = 5y=5: 5=(3)(2)+c5=6+cc=−1\begin{aligned} 5 &= (3)(2) + c \\ 5 &= 6 + c \\ c &= \mathbf{-1} \end{aligned}55c​=(3)(2)+c=6+c=−1​
  3. Write the final equation: Answer: y=3x−1\boldsymbol{y = 3x - 1}y=3x−1

6. * The Point-Gradient Form (Advanced Shortcut)

Section titled “6. * The Point-Gradient Form (Advanced Shortcut)”

While not strictly required in the basic IGCSE 0607 syllabus, the point-gradient form is an incredibly powerful tool. It is especially useful in higher-level maths (like finding the equation of a tangent line in calculus) because it lets you build the equation in a single step without having to solve for ccc first.

y−y1=m(x−x1)y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)y−y1​=m(x−x1​)

(Where mmm is the gradient, and (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1​,y1​) is the given coordinate point).

Connecting the “Three Forms”

Section titled “Connecting the “Three Forms””

Let’s look at how this single formula can easily transform into the other two major forms we’ve covered.

Question: A line has a gradient of 23\frac{2}{3}32​ and passes through the point (6,1)(6, 1)(6,1). Find its equation.

Step 1: Point-Gradient Form (The Setup) Drop m=23m = \frac{2}{3}m=32​, x1=6x_1 = 6x1​=6, and y1=1y_1 = 1y1​=1 directly into the formula:

y−1=23(x−6)y - 1 = \frac{2}{3}(x - 6)y−1=32​(x−6)

(You are technically done building the line right here! But exams usually want it simplified into a specific format.)

Step 2: Gradient-Intercept Form (y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c) To get here, just expand the bracket and make yyy the subject:

y−1=23x−4(expanded the bracket)y=23x−3(added 1 to both sides)\begin{aligned} y - 1 &= \frac{2}{3}x - 4 \quad \text{(expanded the bracket)} \\ y &= \frac{2}{3}x - 3 \quad \text{(added 1 to both sides)} \end{aligned}y−1y​=32​x−4(expanded the bracket)=32​x−3(added 1 to both sides)​

Step 3: General Form (ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c with integers) Take your y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c answer, clear the fraction by multiplying everything by 333, and rearrange:

3y=2x−9−2x+3y=−9\begin{aligned} 3y &= 2x - 9 \\ \boldsymbol{-2x + 3y} &\boldsymbol{= -9} \end{aligned}3y−2x+3y​=2x−9=−9​
