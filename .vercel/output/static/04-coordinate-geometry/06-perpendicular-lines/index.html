4.6 Perpendicular lines

When two straight lines are perpendicular, they intersect at exactly a right angle (90∘90^\circ90∘).

1. The Negative Reciprocal Rule (Visualising Rise/Run)

Section titled “1. The Negative Reciprocal Rule (Visualising Rise/Run)”

Unlike parallel lines, perpendicular lines travel in entirely different directions. Look at the diagram above to understand how their gradients are connected.

If the original line (Line 1) has a gradient of m1=abm_1 = \frac{a}{b}m1​=ba​ (meaning it goes up aaa for every bbb right), then the perpendicular line (Line 2) must travel right aaa for every down bbb.

The gradient of the perpendicular line is:

m2=−bam_2 = \frac{-b}{a}m2​=a−b​

This is the negative reciprocal. You flip the fraction and change the sign. Multiplying them always gives −1-1−1:

m1×m2=−1(ab)×(−ba)=−1\begin{aligned} m_1 \times m_2 &= -1 \\ \left(\frac{a}{b}\right) \times \left(-\frac{b}{a}\right) &= -1 \end{aligned}m1​×m2​(ba​)×(−ab​)​=−1=−1​
Steve's Exam Wisdom

Flip and Flip

To find the perpendicular gradient, remember the two ‘flips’:

  1. Flip the fraction upside down.
  2. Flip the sign (positive ↔\leftrightarrow↔ negative).

2. Identifying Perpendicular Gradients from Equations

Section titled “2. Identifying Perpendicular Gradients from Equations”

You must ensure the equation is in the form y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c before you can identify the original gradient and calculate its negative reciprocal.

Syllabus Example Walkthrough: Find the gradient of a line perpendicular to 2y=3x+12y = 3x + 12y=3x+1.

  1. Find the original gradient: The equation is not in y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c form yet. Rearrange it:

    2y2=3x+12y=32x+12\begin{aligned} \frac{2y}{2} &= \frac{3x + 1}{2} \\ y &= \frac{3}{2}x + \frac{1}{2} \end{aligned}22y​y​=23x+1​=23​x+21​​

    The original gradient (m1m_1m1​) is 32\frac{3}{2}23​.

  2. Find the perpendicular gradient: Flip 32\frac{3}{2}23​ and change the sign to negative.

    Answer: The perpendicular gradient (m2m_2m2​) is −23\boldsymbol{-\frac{2}{3}}−32​.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

Speed Trick: The Standard Form Shortcut

If you are given an equation in standard form ax+by=cax + by = cax+by=c, you don’t actually need to rearrange it to y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c!

The perpendicular line will always take the form bx−ay=kbx - ay = kbx−ay=k (or −bx+ay=k-bx + ay = k−bx+ay=k).

If you need the line to pass through a specific point (p,q)(p, q)(p,q), simply substitute x=px = px=p and y=qy = qy=q into your new form to find kkk.

b(p)−a(q)=kb(p) - a(q) = kb(p)−a(q)=k

3. The Unique Perpendicular through a Point

Section titled “3. The Unique Perpendicular through a Point”

For any given line and any given point (PPP) not on that line, there is exactly one unique perpendicular line that passes through PPP.

Syllabus Example: Find the equation of the line passing through (−6,4)(-6, 4)(−6,4) which is perpendicular to the line y=3x−2y = 3x - 2y=3x−2.

  1. Find the perpendicular gradient (m2m_2m2​): The original gradient is 333. The perpendicular gradient is m2=−13m_2 = -\frac{1}{3}m2​=−31​.

  2. Substitute into y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c: Use your perpendicular gradient and the given point x=−6x = -6x=−6, y=4y = 4y=4.

    4=(−13)(−6)+c4=2+cc=2\begin{aligned} 4 &= \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)(-6) + c \\ 4 &= 2 + c \\ c &= \mathbf{2} \end{aligned}44c​=(−31​)(−6)+c=2+c=2​

  3. Write the final equation:

    Answer: y=−13x+2\boldsymbol{y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 2}y=−31​x+2

4. The Perpendicular Bisector

Section titled “4. The Perpendicular Bisector”

This is a classic Extended-tier question. A perpendicular bisector is a line that cuts exactly through the midpoint of a line segment connecting two points, at a perfect 90∘90^\circ90∘ angle.

Syllabus Example Walkthrough: Find the equation of the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the points (−3,8)(-3, 8)(−3,8) and (9,−2)(9, -2)(9,−2).

  1. Find the Midpoint (MMM): You must know where the middle is.

    M=(−3+92,8+(−2)2)=(62,62)=(3,3)M = \left( \frac{-3 + 9}{2}, \frac{8 + (-2)}{2} \right) = \left( \frac{6}{2}, \frac{6}{2} \right) = \mathbf{(3, 3)}M=(2−3+9​,28+(−2)​)=(26​,26​)=(3,3)

    Our perpendicular bisector must pass through (3,3)(3, 3)(3,3).

  2. Find the Original Gradient (m1m_1m1​):

    m1=y2−y1x2−x1=−2−89−(−3)=−1012=−56m_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{-2 - 8}{9 - (-3)} = \frac{-10}{12} = \mathbf{-\frac{5}{6}}m1​=x2​−x1​y2​−y1​​=9−(−3)−2−8​=12−10​=−65​

  3. Find the Perpendicular Gradient (m2m_2m2​):

    m2=65m_2 = \mathbf{\frac{6}{5}}m2​=56​

  4. Build the Equation: Use m=65m = \frac{6}{5}m=56​ and the midpoint (3,3)(3, 3)(3,3). Substitute into y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c:

    3=(65)(3)+c3=185+cc=3−185c=155−185c=−35\begin{aligned} 3 &= \left(\frac{6}{5}\right)(3) + c \\ 3 &= \frac{18}{5} + c \\ c &= 3 - \frac{18}{5} \\ c &= \frac{15}{5} - \frac{18}{5} \\ c &= \mathbf{-\frac{3}{5}} \end{aligned}33ccc​=(56​)(3)+c=518​+c=3−518​=515​−518​=−53​​

    Answer: y=65x−35\boldsymbol{y = \frac{6}{5}x - \frac{3}{5}}y=56​x−53​

5. Shortest Distance (Perpendicular Distance)

Section titled “5. Shortest Distance (Perpendicular Distance)”

In geometry, the shortest distance between an external point and a straight line is always the perpendicular distance.

This concept is used in optimization problems (e.g., finding the closest point on a road, a boundary, or a river).

To find the shortest distance, you must:

  1. Find the equation of the unique perpendicular line passing through the external point (Concept 3).
  2. Find the point of intersection (III) between the original line and your perpendicular line (by solving simultaneous equations, see Section 4.7).
  3. Calculate the distance between the original external point and the intersection point III (using the Distance Formula, Section 4.3).

6. Synthesis: Area of a Scalene Triangle

Section titled “6. Synthesis: Area of a Scalene Triangle”

This is a high-difficulty application that brings together multiple skills from Coordinate Geometry.

The Problem: Find the exact area of the scalene triangle defined by the vertices A(1,1)A(1, 1)A(1,1), B(7,4)B(7, 4)B(7,4), and C(3,8)C(3, 8)C(3,8) by calculating the shortest distance from CCC to the base ABABAB.

The Steps:

  1. Find the equation of the base line ABABAB: Gradient of AB=4−17−1=36=12AB = \frac{4 - 1}{7 - 1} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}AB=7−14−1​=63​=21​.

    Using A(1,1)A(1, 1)A(1,1) in y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c:

    1=12(1)+cc=12\begin{aligned} 1 &= \frac{1}{2}(1) + c \\ c &= \frac{1}{2} \end{aligned}1c​=21​(1)+c=21​​

    Equation of ABABAB: y=0.5x+0.5y = 0.5x + 0.5y=0.5x+0.5

  2. Find the equation of the perpendicular height from CCC:

    The perpendicular gradient is the negative reciprocal of 12\frac{1}{2}21​, which is −2-2−2.

    Using point C(3,8)C(3, 8)C(3,8) in y=mx+cy = mx + cy=mx+c:

    8=−2(3)+c8=−6+cc=14\begin{aligned} 8 &= -2(3) + c \\ 8 &= -6 + c \\ c &= 14 \end{aligned}88c​=−2(3)+c=−6+c=14​

    Equation of perpendicular: y=−2x+14y = -2x + 14y=−2x+14

  3. Find the intersection point (III) to get the height’s end point:

    Set the two line equations equal to each other:

    0.5x+0.5=−2x+142.5x=13.5x=5.4\begin{aligned} 0.5x + 0.5 &= -2x + 14 \\ 2.5x &= 13.5 \\ x &= \mathbf{5.4} \end{aligned}0.5x+0.52.5xx​=−2x+14=13.5=5.4​

    Substitute xxx back in to find yyy:

    y=−2(5.4)+14y=3.2\begin{aligned} y &= -2(5.4) + 14 \\ y &= \mathbf{3.2} \end{aligned}yy​=−2(5.4)+14=3.2​

    The intersection is I(5.4,3.2)I(5.4, 3.2)I(5.4,3.2).

  4. Calculate lengths using the Distance Formula:

    Base Length (ABABAB):

    AB=(7−1)2+(4−1)2=36+9=45\begin{aligned} AB &= \sqrt{(7 - 1)^2 + (4 - 1)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{36 + 9} \\ &= \mathbf{\sqrt{45}} \end{aligned}AB​=(7−1)2+(4−1)2​=36+9​=45​​

    Perpendicular Height (CICICI):

    CI=(5.4−3)2+(3.2−8)2=(2.4)2+(−4.8)2=5.76+23.04=28.8\begin{aligned} CI &= \sqrt{(5.4 - 3)^2 + (3.2 - 8)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{(2.4)^2 + (-4.8)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{5.76 + 23.04} \\ &= \mathbf{\sqrt{28.8}} \end{aligned}CI​=(5.4−3)2+(3.2−8)2​=(2.4)2+(−4.8)2​=5.76+23.04​=28.8​​

  5. Calculate Final Area:

    Area=12×Base×Height=12×45×28.8=12×1296=12×36=18 units2\begin{aligned} \text{Area} &= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Base} \times \text{Height} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \times \sqrt{45} \times \sqrt{28.8} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \times \sqrt{1296} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \times 36 \\ &= \mathbf{18 \text{ units}^2} \end{aligned}Area​=21​×Base×Height=21​×45​×28.8​=21​×1296​=21​×36=18 units2​
