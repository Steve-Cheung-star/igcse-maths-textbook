4.2 Gradient of linear graphs

The gradient (often represented by the letter mmm) measures the steepness and direction of a straight line.

Before calculating any numbers, you should always look at the graph to determine what kind of gradient you are dealing with. We read graphs from left to right, just like reading a book.

1. Finding the Gradient from a Graph (Rise over Run)

Section titled “1. Finding the Gradient from a Graph (Rise over Run)”

If you are given a graph of a straight line, you can find its gradient by drawing a right-angled triangle between two points on the line. We use the phrase “Rise over Run”:

m=Vertical Change (Rise)Horizontal Change (Run)m = \frac{\text{Vertical Change (Rise)}}{\text{Horizontal Change (Run)}}m=Horizontal Change (Run)Vertical Change (Rise)​

Visual Example: Positive Gradient

Section titled “Visual Example: Positive Gradient”

How to calculate visually: Pick two points where the line crosses grid corners perfectly (AAA and BBB).

  1. Count the spaces horizontally for Run (move right →\rightarrow→ positive). Run=8\text{Run} = 8Run=8.
  2. Count the spaces vertically for Rise (move up →\rightarrow→ positive). Rise=4\text{Rise} = 4Rise=4.
  3. Calculate the gradient: m=48=0.5m = \frac{4}{8} = 0.5m=84​=0.5.

2. Calculating the Gradient from Two Coordinates

Section titled “2. Calculating the Gradient from Two Coordinates”

Often, you won’t have a graph. You will just be given the coordinates of two points, such as (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1​,y1​) and (x2,y2)(x_2, y_2)(x2​,y2​). We calculate the “Rise over Run” using algebra.

The Gradient Formula:

m=y2−y1x2−x1m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}m=x2​−x1​y2​−y1​​

Crucial Gradient Rules

  • Simplifying: A gradient is just a fraction. Always simplify it! If you calculate 42\frac{4}{2}24​, write your final answer as 222. If you get 36\frac{3}{6}63​, simplify it to 12\frac{1}{2}21​.
  • The Floating Negative: If your gradient is negative, it doesn’t matter where the minus sign lives: −34=−34=3−4-\frac{3}{4} = \frac{-3}{4} = \frac{3}{-4}−43​=4−3​=−43​. Usually, we place it neatly in front of the fraction.
  • Working Backwards: The formula works perfectly backwards too: m=y1−y2x1−x2m = \frac{y_1 - y_2}{x_1 - x_2}m=x1​−x2​y1​−y2​​. Just never mix the order. If you start with point 1 on top, you MUST start with point 1 on the bottom!
  • Subtracting Negatives: Be incredibly careful when subtracting a negative coordinate. Remember that minus a minus becomes a plus: 5−(−3)=5+3=85 - (-3) = 5 + 3 = 85−(−3)=5+3=8.

Visualising Formula Application: Negative Gradient Example

Section titled “Visualising Formula Application: Negative Gradient Example”

This visual example shows why the maths works for a negative (downhill) slope and how to carefully align your calculation to avoid errors.

Find the gradient of the line passing through P(−3,4)P(-3, 4)P(−3,4) and Q(2,−1)Q(2, -1)Q(2,−1).

Calculation steps: Let (x1,y1)=(−3,4)(x_1, y_1) = (-3, 4)(x1​,y1​)=(−3,4) and (x2,y2)=(2,−1)(x_2, y_2) = (2, -1)(x2​,y2​)=(2,−1).

m=−1−42−(−3)m=−52+3m=−55m=−1\begin{aligned} m &= \frac{-1 - 4}{2 - (-3)} \\ m &= \frac{-5}{2 + 3} \\ m &= \frac{-5}{5} \\ \mathbf{m} &\mathbf{= -1} \end{aligned}mmmm​=2−(−3)−1−4​=2+3−5​=5−5​=−1​

Pick two points that cross grid corners perfectly. Point A(0,1)A(0, 1)A(0,1) is the y-intercept. Let’s pick another clear corner, B(3,10)B(3, 10)B(3,10).

  1. Count Run from AAA to BBB (move right 3): Run=3\text{Run} = 3Run=3.
  2. Count Rise from AAA to BBB (move up from 1 to 10): Rise=9\text{Rise} = 9Rise=9.

m=RiseRun=93=3m = \frac{\text{Rise}}{\text{Run}} = \frac{9}{3} = 3m=RunRise​=39​=3
