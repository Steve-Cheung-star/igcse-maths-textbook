4.3 Length and midpoint

When we look at a straight line drawn between two points on a coordinate grid, we are looking at a line segment. Because it has a definite start and end, we can measure exactly how long it is, find the exact coordinate of its middle, and even split it algebraically into specific fractional ratios.

1. Calculating the Length of a Line Segment

Section titled “1. Calculating the Length of a Line Segment”

To find the distance between two points, we don’t need a ruler—we need Pythagoras’ theorem.

If you draw a right-angled triangle between your two points, the horizontal distance is your “run”, the vertical distance is your “rise”, and the line segment itself is the hypotenuse.

The Distance Formula

Section titled “The Distance Formula”

In mathematics, the Greek letter delta (Δ\DeltaΔ) is used to represent the “change in” or “difference”. Therefore, Δx\Delta xΔx is the horizontal distance (x2−x1x_2 - x_1x2​−x1​) and Δy\Delta yΔy is the vertical distance (y2−y1y_2 - y_1y2​−y1​).

By applying Pythagoras (a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2) directly to the coordinates, we get the official distance formula:

d=(Δx)2+(Δy)2=(x2−x1)2+(y2−y1)2d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2} = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}d=(Δx)2+(Δy)2​=(x2​−x1​)2+(y2​−y1​)2​

Standard Example Walkthrough: Calculate the length of the line joining A(1,2)A(1, 2)A(1,2) and B(7,5)B(7, 5)B(7,5).

  1. Find the change in xxx: Δx=7−1=6\Delta x = 7 - 1 = 6Δx=7−1=6
  2. Find the change in yyy: Δy=5−2=3\Delta y = 5 - 2 = 3Δy=5−2=3
  3. Apply the distance formula:
d2=62+32d2=36+9d2=45d=45≈6.71 (to 3 sig figs)\begin{aligned} d^2 &= 6^2 + 3^2 \\ d^2 &= 36 + 9 \\ d^2 &= 45 \\ d &= \sqrt{45} \approx 6.71 \text{ (to 3 sig figs)} \end{aligned}d2d2d2d​=62+32=36+9=45=45​≈6.71 (to 3 sig figs)​

1.1 Harder Problems: Missing Coordinates & Quadratics

Section titled “1.1 Harder Problems: Missing Coordinates & Quadratics”

In higher-tier exams, you might be given the length of the line and asked to find an unknown coordinate. This usually requires forming and solving a quadratic equation.

Example Walkthrough: The distance between the points P(2,5)P(2, 5)P(2,5) and Q(x,1)Q(x, 1)Q(x,1) is exactly 555 units. Find the possible values of xxx.

  1. Substitute what you know into the distance formula:
52=(x−2)2+(1−5)225=(x−2)2+(−4)225=(x−2)2+16\begin{aligned} 5^2 &= (x - 2)^2 + (1 - 5)^2 \\ 25 &= (x - 2)^2 + (-4)^2 \\ 25 &= (x - 2)^2 + 16 \end{aligned}522525​=(x−2)2+(1−5)2=(x−2)2+(−4)2=(x−2)2+16​
  1. Isolate the bracket by subtracting 16 from both sides:
9=(x−2)2\begin{aligned} 9 &= (x - 2)^2 \end{aligned}9​=(x−2)2​
  1. Square root both sides. Crucial step: Remember that a square root has both a positive and negative answer!
±3=x−2\begin{aligned} \pm 3 &= x - 2 \end{aligned}±3​=x−2​
  1. Solve for the two possible values of xxx:
x=2+3orx=2−3x=5orx=−1\begin{aligned} x &= 2 + 3 \quad \text{or} \quad x = 2 - 3 \\ \boldsymbol{x} &\boldsymbol{= 5} \quad \text{or} \quad \boldsymbol{x = -1} \end{aligned}xx​=2+3orx=2−3=5orx=−1​

(Why two answers? Because from point P, a line of length 5 could stretch downwards to the right, landing at x=5x=5x=5, or downwards to the left, landing at x=−1x=-1x=−1.)

2. Finding the Midpoint of a Line Segment

Section titled “2. Finding the Midpoint of a Line Segment”

The midpoint is the exact centre of a line segment. To find it, you simply need to find the average of the xxx-coordinates and the average of the yyy-coordinates.

The Midpoint Formula

Section titled “The Midpoint Formula”
M=(x1+x22,y1+y22)M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)M=(2x1​+x2​​,2y1​+y2​​)

Standard Example Walkthrough: Find the midpoint of the line connecting (−4,2)(-4, 2)(−4,2) and (6,−4)(6, -4)(6,−4).

x-midpoint=−4+62=22=1y-midpoint=2+(−4)2=−22=−1\begin{aligned} x\text{-midpoint} &= \frac{-4 + 6}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \\ y\text{-midpoint} &= \frac{2 + (-4)}{2} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1 \end{aligned}x-midpointy-midpoint​=2−4+6​=22​=1=22+(−4)​=2−2​=−1​

Answer: M(1,−1)\boldsymbol{M(1, -1)}M(1,−1).

2.1 Harder Problems: Solving Linear Equations

Section titled “2.1 Harder Problems: Solving Linear Equations”

Sometimes the coordinates themselves contain unknown letters (algebraic terms), and you are given the final midpoint. You must split the problem into two separate linear equations.

Example Walkthrough: The midpoint of the line segment joining A(2a,5)A(2a, 5)A(2a,5) and B(10,3b)B(10, 3b)B(10,3b) is the point M(8,1)M(8, 1)M(8,1). Find the values of aaa and bbb.

Step 1: Equate the x-components

2a+102=82a+10=162a=6a=3\begin{aligned} \frac{2a + 10}{2} &= 8 \\ 2a + 10 &= 16 \\ 2a &= 6 \\ \boldsymbol{a} &\boldsymbol{= 3} \end{aligned}22a+10​2a+102aa​=8=16=6=3​

Step 2: Equate the y-components

5+3b2=15+3b=23b=−3b=−1\begin{aligned} \frac{5 + 3b}{2} &= 1 \\ 5 + 3b &= 2 \\ 3b &= -3 \\ \boldsymbol{b} &\boldsymbol{= -1} \end{aligned}25+3b​5+3b3bb​=1=2=−3=−1​

3. Finding a Missing Endpoint

Section titled “3. Finding a Missing Endpoint”

A common exam question gives you one endpoint and the midpoint, and asks you to deduce the other endpoint. You can solve this algebraically, or visually using “vectors” (the stepping method).

Question: The midpoint of a segment ABABAB is M(4,3)M(4, 3)M(4,3). If AAA is (1,7)(1, 7)(1,7), find the coordinates of BBB.

Method 1: The “Vector / Jump” Method (Recommended)

Section titled “Method 1: The “Vector / Jump” Method (Recommended)”

Because the midpoint is exactly halfway, whatever journey you take from AAA to get to MMM, you must repeat exactly to get from MMM to BBB.

  1. Find the jump from A to M:
    • Δx\Delta xΔx: From 111 to 444 is a jump of +3+3+3.
    • Δy\Delta yΔy: From 777 to 333 is a jump of −4-4−4.
  2. Apply the exact same jump from M to B:
    • xxx-coordinate: 4+3=74 + 3 = \mathbf{7}4+3=7
    • yyy-coordinate: 3−4=−13 - 4 = \mathbf{-1}3−4=−1
  3. Answer: B(7,−1)\boldsymbol{B(7, -1)}B(7,−1).

Method 2: The Algebraic Method

Section titled “Method 2: The Algebraic Method”

If you prefer formulas, set up the midpoint formula with point BBB as unknown (x,y)(x, y)(x,y).

1+x2=4⇒1+x=8⇒x=77+y2=3⇒7+y=6⇒y=−1\begin{aligned} \frac{1 + x}{2} &= 4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 1 + x = 8 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol{x = 7} \\ \frac{7 + y}{2} &= 3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 7 + y = 6 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol{y = -1} \end{aligned}21+x​27+y​​=4⇒1+x=8⇒x=7=3⇒7+y=6⇒y=−1​

4. Dividing a Line Segment in a Given Ratio (m:nm:nm:n)

Section titled “4. Dividing a Line Segment in a Given Ratio (m:nm:nm:n)”

Sometimes you need to find a specific point PPP that divides a line segment ABABAB into a specific ratio, like 2:32:32:3, rather than just splitting it exactly in half (1:11:11:1).

There are two main ways to solve these problems: using logic and fractional steps, or using the formal Section Formula (often called the “Criss-Cross” method).

Method 1: The Fractional Steps Method

Section titled “Method 1: The Fractional Steps Method”

This method builds on the visual vector jumps we used for finding endpoints.

Example Walkthrough: Find the coordinates of the point PPP which divides the line segment joining A(2,1)A(2, 1)A(2,1) and B(12,16)B(12, 16)B(12,16) in the ratio 2:32:32:3.

  1. Find the total number of parts: 2+3=5 parts total2 + 3 = \mathbf{5 \text{ parts total}}2+3=5 parts total. This means point PPP is exactly 25\frac{2}{5}52​ of the way along the line from AAA to BBB.

  2. Find the total distance (Δx\Delta xΔx and Δy\Delta yΔy) from A to B: Δx=12−2=10\Delta x = 12 - 2 = 10Δx=12−2=10 Δy=16−1=15\Delta y = 16 - 1 = 15Δy=16−1=15

  3. Calculate the fraction of that distance: x-move=25×10=4x\text{-move} = \frac{2}{5} \times 10 = 4x-move=52​×10=4 y-move=25×15=6y\text{-move} = \frac{2}{5} \times 15 = 6y-move=52​×15=6

  4. Add these moves onto the starting point A(2,1)A(2, 1)A(2,1): x-coordinate:2+4=6x\text{-coordinate}: 2 + 4 = \mathbf{6}x-coordinate:2+4=6 y-coordinate:1+6=7y\text{-coordinate}: 1 + 6 = \mathbf{7}y-coordinate:1+6=7

Answer: The point PPP is (6,7)\boldsymbol{(6, 7)}(6,7).

Method 2: The Section Formula (Criss-Cross Method)

Section titled “Method 2: The Section Formula (Criss-Cross Method)”

If you prefer pure algebra, you can use the formula below. It is often called the “Criss-Cross” method because the first part of the ratio (mmm) multiplies the coordinates of the second point (BBB), and the second part of the ratio (nnn) multiplies the coordinates of the first point (AAA).

P=(mx2+nx1m+n,my2+ny1m+n)P = \left( \frac{mx_2 + nx_1}{m+n}, \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m+n} \right)P=(m+nmx2​+nx1​​,m+nmy2​+ny1​​)

Using the exact same example (A(2,1)A(2, 1)A(2,1), B(12,16)B(12, 16)B(12,16), ratio 2:32:32:3):

  1. Identify variables: (x1,y1)=(2,1)(x_1, y_1)=(2, 1)(x1​,y1​)=(2,1), (x2,y2)=(12,16)(x_2, y_2)=(12, 16)(x2​,y2​)=(12,16), m=2m=2m=2, n=3n=3n=3.
  2. Set up the xxx and yyy calculations: xP=mx2+nx1m+n=2(12)+3(2)2+3=24+65=305=6yP=my2+ny1m+n=2(16)+3(1)2+3=32+35=355=7\begin{aligned} x_P &= \frac{m x_2 + n x_1}{m+n} = \frac{2(12) + 3(2)}{2+3} = \frac{24 + 6}{5} = \frac{30}{5} = \boldsymbol{6} \\ y_P &= \frac{m y_2 + n y_1}{m+n} = \frac{2(16) + 3(1)}{2+3} = \frac{32 + 3}{5} = \frac{35}{5} = \boldsymbol{7} \end{aligned}xP​yP​​=m+nmx2​+nx1​​=2+32(12)+3(2)​=524+6​=530​=6=m+nmy2​+ny1​​=2+32(16)+3(1)​=532+3​=535​=7​

Answer: The point PPP is (6,7)\boldsymbol{(6, 7)}(6,7).
