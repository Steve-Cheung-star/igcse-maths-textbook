9.2 Relative and Expected Frequencies

Probability can be approached in two ways: what we expect to happen mathematically (theoretical probability), and what actually happens when we perform an experiment (experimental probability).

Probability Terminology

  1. Theoretical Probability: What should happen in a perfect world. For a fair coin, the theoretical probability of getting Heads is exactly 0.50.50.5.
  2. Relative Frequency: Also known as experimental probability. This is what actually happens when you run an experiment. If you flip a coin 10 times and get 7 Heads, the relative frequency of Heads is 710\frac{7}{10}107​ or 0.70.70.7.
  3. Expected Frequency: Using probability to predict how many times an event will occur over a large number of trials or within a whole population.

1. Relative Frequency

Section titled “1. Relative Frequency”

When we perform an experiment (like spinning a spinner or dropping a piece of toast), we record the outcomes. The relative frequency is an estimate of the true probability based purely on those experimental results.

Relative Frequency Formula

Relative Frequency=Number of successful trialsTotal number of trials\text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{\text{Number of successful trials}}{\text{Total number of trials}}Relative Frequency=Total number of trialsNumber of successful trials​

The Law of Large Numbers

Section titled “The Law of Large Numbers”

If you flip a coin 10 times, you might get 8 Heads. The relative frequency (0.80.80.8) is very different from the theoretical probability (0.50.50.5). However, if you flip that same coin 10,000 times, the relative frequency will settle down and get extremely close to 0.50.50.5.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

More Trials = Better Estimate

In exams, if you are asked how to make an experimental estimate more reliable, the answer is always: “Perform more trials” or “Repeat the experiment more times.”

2. Fair, Bias, and Random

Section titled “2. Fair, Bias, and Random”

These three words have very specific mathematical meanings that frequently appear in IGCSE questions.

Definitions

  • Random: An event is random if every single possible item or outcome has an equal chance of being selected.
  • Fair: An object (like a dice or a coin) is fair if all of its faces/outcomes have an equal theoretical probability of occurring.
  • Bias: An object is biased if the outcomes are not equally likely, usually due to a physical flaw, extra weight on one side, or uneven sections.

How do we test for bias? We use relative frequency! If we roll a dice 600 times, we expect each number to appear roughly 100 times. If the number six appears 450 times, we have strong experimental evidence that the dice is biased.

3. Expected Frequencies

Section titled “3. Expected Frequencies”

If we know the probability of an event (either theoretical or from past experimental data), we can predict how many times we expect that event to happen over a certain number of trials.

Expected Frequency Formula

Expected Frequency=Probability of the event×Total number of trials\text{Expected Frequency} = \text{Probability of the event} \times \text{Total number of trials}Expected Frequency=Probability of the event×Total number of trials

Example: A factory produces 5000 lightbulbs. Historically, the probability of a lightbulb being defective is 0.020.020.02. How many defective lightbulbs do we expect in this batch?

Expected defects=0.02×5000=100 lightbulbs\begin{aligned} \text{Expected defects} &= 0.02 \times 5000 \\ &= 100 \text{ lightbulbs} \end{aligned}Expected defects​=0.02×5000=100 lightbulbs​
Steve's Exam Wisdom

To Round or Not To Round?

Mathematically, an expected frequency does NOT need to be rounded to a whole number. It is an average expectation. For example, if you flip a biased coin 10 times and the expected number of Heads is 3.53.53.5, you leave the answer as 3.53.53.5.

However, always read the question carefully! If the context or instructions explicitly ask for an integer (e.g., “Estimate the number of whole days it will rain”), you should round appropriately.

  2. Do you think this spinner is fair? Give a reason for your answer.

  2. For Red, there were 959595 successful trials out of 200200200 total spins.

A quality control inspector tests 80 toys from a production line and finds that 3 of them have a manufacturing defect.

The factory produces 12,000 toys in a week. Based on the inspector’s sample, how many toys would you expect to be defective in that week?

  2. P(Defect)=380P(\text{Defect}) = \frac{3}{80}P(Defect)=803​.
  3. Next, apply this probability to the whole population (12,00012,00012,000 toys) to find the expected frequency.
  4. Expected defects = P(Defect)×Total productionP(\text{Defect}) \times \text{Total production}P(Defect)×Total production. Expected defects=380×12000=3×150=450\begin{aligned} \text{Expected defects} &= \frac{3}{80} \times 12000 \\ &= 3 \times 150 \\ &= 450 \end{aligned}Expected defects​=803​×12000=3×150=450​
