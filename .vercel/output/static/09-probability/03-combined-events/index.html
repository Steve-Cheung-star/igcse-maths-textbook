9.3 Probability of Combined Events

In the real world, we rarely look at just one event in isolation. We often want to find the probability of multiple things happening together, like flipping a coin and rolling a dice, or picking two consecutive winning lottery tickets.

The Golden Rules of Probability

When dealing with combined events, ask yourself if you are dealing with an “AND” situation or an “OR” situation.

  1. The AND Rule (Multiplication): For independent events (where one event does not affect the other), you multiply the probabilities.

    P(A and B)=P(A)×P(B)P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)P(A and B)=P(A)×P(B)

  2. The OR Rule (Addition): For mutually exclusive events (events that cannot happen at the same time), you add the probabilities.

    P(A or B)=P(A)+P(B)P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)P(A or B)=P(A)+P(B)

1. Sample Space Diagrams

Section titled “1. Sample Space Diagrams”

A sample space diagram is a 2D grid used to show all the possible outcomes of two events happening at the same time. It is highly visual and guarantees you won’t miss any combinations!

Example: Two fair 4-sided spinners, each numbered 1 to 4, are spun. The two numbers landed on are added together. Find the probability of scoring exactly 6.

Looking at the sample space diagram above, there are 4×4=164 \times 4 = 164×4=16 total possible outcomes. By counting the circled 666s in the grid, we can easily see there are exactly 333 ways to score a 6.

P(Score of 6)=316\begin{aligned} P(\text{Score of 6}) &= \frac{3}{16} \end{aligned}P(Score of 6)​=163​​

2. Tree Diagrams: Replacement vs. No Replacement

Section titled “2. Tree Diagrams: Replacement vs. No Replacement”

Tree diagrams map out sequential events (one thing happening after another).

  • Outcomes are written at the very end of the branches.
  • Probabilities are written on the branches themselves.
  • To find a combined probability at the end of a path, multiply along the branches.

The Replacement Trap

  • With Replacement (Independent): If you take an item from a bag and put it back, the probabilities for the second pick stay exactly the same.
  • Without Replacement (Dependent): If you eat a sweet or keep a card, the total number of items left decreases by 111. The probability denominators for the second branch MUST decrease by 111.

3. The “At Least One” Trick (Using the Complement)

Section titled “3. The “At Least One” Trick (Using the Complement)”

Sometimes calculating the probability of a combined event directly is exhausting because there are too many paths. A classic example is finding the probability of getting “at least one” of something.

Instead of calculating P(1)+P(2)+P(3)P(1) + P(2) + P(3)P(1)+P(2)+P(3), it is much faster to use the complement rule:

P(At least one)=1−P(None)P(\text{At least one}) = 1 - P(\text{None})P(At least one)=1−P(None)

Example: You roll a fair 6-sided dice 3 times. What is the probability of rolling at least one six? Instead of mapping out all 27 paths of a 3-tier tree diagram, just calculate the probability of getting no sixes at all:

  • P(Not a 6)=56P(\text{Not a 6}) = \frac{5}{6}P(Not a 6)=65​.
  • P(No 6 on all three rolls)=56×56×56=125216P(\text{No 6 on all three rolls}) = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{125}{216}P(No 6 on all three rolls)=65​×65​×65​=216125​.
P(At least one 6)=1−125216=91216\begin{aligned} P(\text{At least one 6}) &= 1 - \frac{125}{216} \\ &= \frac{91}{216} \end{aligned}P(At least one 6)​=1−216125​=21691​​

4. Complex Conditional Events (Box to Box)

Section titled “4. Complex Conditional Events (Box to Box)”

At the Extended level, you may encounter problems where an item is moved from one container to another before a second draw takes place. This alters the denominators AND the numerators of the second container!

Example Scenario:

  • Box A contains 3 Red and 2 Blue balls.
  • Box B contains 1 Red and 4 Blue balls.
  • A ball is drawn at random from Box A and placed into Box B. Then, a ball is drawn from Box B. What is the probability that the final ball drawn from Box B is Blue?

This requires tracking two different alternate timelines for Box B:

  1. If a RED ball was moved from A to B: Box B now has 2 Red and 4 Blue (Total = 6). Path Probability = P(Red from A)×P(Blue from updated B)=35×46=1230P(\text{Red from A}) \times P(\text{Blue from updated B}) = \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{4}{6} = \frac{12}{30}P(Red from A)×P(Blue from updated B)=53​×64​=3012​.
  2. If a BLUE ball was moved from A to B: Box B now has 1 Red and 5 Blue (Total = 6). Path Probability = P(Blue from A)×P(Blue from updated B)=25×56=1030P(\text{Blue from A}) \times P(\text{Blue from updated B}) = \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{10}{30}P(Blue from A)×P(Blue from updated B)=52​×65​=3010​.

To find the total probability of picking a Blue from Box B, we add the two valid paths together:

P(Blue from Box B)=1230+1030=2230=1115\begin{aligned} P(\text{Blue from Box B}) &= \frac{12}{30} + \frac{10}{30} \\ &= \frac{22}{30} \\ &= \frac{11}{15} \end{aligned}P(Blue from Box B)​=3012​+3010​=3022​=1511​​

5. Venn Diagrams and Set Notation

Section titled “5. Venn Diagrams and Set Notation”

We can also represent the probability of combined events using Venn diagrams and set notation.

  • Intersection (AND): P(A∩B)P(A \cap B)P(A∩B) is the probability of choosing something that belongs to BOTH event AAA and event BBB (the overlapping middle section).
  • Union (OR): P(A∪B)P(A \cup B)P(A∪B) is the probability of choosing something that belongs to event AAA, event BBB, or BOTH (the entirety of both circles).

A bag contains 444 red sweets and 666 yellow sweets. Two sweets are selected at random without replacement.

Calculate the probability that at least one of the sweets is red.

  1. “At least one red” includes (Red, Red), (Red, Yellow), and (Yellow, Red). The only outcome it does not include is (Yellow, Yellow).
  2. It is faster to calculate P(None are red)P(\text{None are red})P(None are red), which is P(Yellow, Yellow)P(\text{Yellow, Yellow})P(Yellow, Yellow).
  3. First yellow sweet: 610\frac{6}{10}106​.
  4. Second yellow sweet (without replacement): 59\frac{5}{9}95​.
  5. Calculate P(Y,Y)P(Y, Y)P(Y,Y): P(Y,Y)=610×59=3090=13\begin{aligned} P(Y, Y) &= \frac{6}{10} \times \frac{5}{9} \\ &= \frac{30}{90} \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \end{aligned}P(Y,Y)​=106​×95​=9030​=31​​
  6. Use the complement rule for “at least one”: P(At least one red)=1−P(Y,Y)=1−13=23\begin{aligned} P(\text{At least one red}) &= 1 - P(Y, Y) \\ &= 1 - \frac{1}{3} \\ &= \frac{2}{3} \end{aligned}P(At least one red)​=1−P(Y,Y)=1−31​=32​​

In a group of 404040 tourists:

  • 242424 speak Spanish (SSS)
  • 181818 speak French (FFF)
  • 666 speak neither Spanish nor French.

A tourist is chosen at random. Find P(S∩F)P(S \cap F)P(S∩F).

  1. First, find how many tourists speak at least one of the languages by subtracting the “neither” group from the universal set: 40−6=3440 - 6 = 3440−6=34.
  2. The simple sum of the Spanish speakers and French speakers is 24+18=4224 + 18 = 4224+18=42.
  3. Because 424242 is greater than the 343434 tourists available, the difference must be the people who were counted twice (the intersection).
  4. Number of people who speak both =42−34=8= 42 - 34 = 8=42−34=8. This means n(S∩F)=8n(S \cap F) = 8n(S∩F)=8.
  5. The probability of picking someone from the intersection out of the whole group is 840\frac{8}{40}408​. P(S∩F)=840=15\begin{aligned} P(S \cap F) &= \frac{8}{40} \\ &= \frac{1}{5} \end{aligned}P(S∩F)​=408​=51​​

Box X contains 222 white tokens and 333 black tokens. Box Y contains 444 white tokens and 111 black token.

A token is selected at random from Box X and moved into Box Y. Then, a token is selected at random from Box Y.

Calculate the probability that the token selected from Box Y is white.

  1. There are two ways the final token can be white. We must track both paths and add them together.
  2. Path 1: White from X, then White from Y
    • P(White from X)=25P(\text{White from X}) = \frac{2}{5}P(White from X)=52​
    • Box Y now gains a white token. It has 555 White and 111 Black (666 total).
    • P(White from updated Y)=56P(\text{White from updated Y}) = \frac{5}{6}P(White from updated Y)=65​
    • Path 1 Probability =25×56=1030= \frac{2}{5} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{10}{30}=52​×65​=3010​
  3. Path 2: Black from X, then White from Y
    • P(Black from X)=35P(\text{Black from X}) = \frac{3}{5}P(Black from X)=53​
    • Box Y now gains a black token. It has 444 White and 222 Black (666 total).
    • P(White from updated Y)=46P(\text{White from updated Y}) = \frac{4}{6}P(White from updated Y)=64​
    • Path 2 Probability =35×46=1230= \frac{3}{5} \times \frac{4}{6} = \frac{12}{30}=53​×64​=3012​
  4. Add the valid paths together: P(White from Box Y)=1030+1230=2230=1115\begin{aligned} P(\text{White from Box Y}) &= \frac{10}{30} + \frac{12}{30} \\ &= \frac{22}{30} \\ &= \frac{11}{15} \end{aligned}P(White from Box Y)​=3010​+3012​=3022​=1511​​
