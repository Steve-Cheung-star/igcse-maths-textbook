3.7 The Logarithmic Function

Just as subtraction is the opposite of addition, and division is the opposite of multiplication, logarithms are the mathematical opposite of exponentials.

When you have an equation like 10x=100010^x = 100010x=1000, it is easy to see that x=3x = 3x=3. But what if the equation is 10x=5010^x = 5010x=50? We need a mathematical tool to “undo” the exponent and solve for xxx. That tool is the logarithm.

A Brief History of the Logarithm

Section titled “A Brief History of the Logarithm”

Before calculators existed, scientists and astronomers spent months doing tedious long multiplication by hand. In the late 16th century, a Scottish mathematician named John Napier spent over 20 years manually calculating millions of geometric ratios. In 1614, he published his invention: logarithms. Napier’s brilliant system allowed mathematicians to turn horrific multiplication problems into simple addition problems, changing the course of science forever!

1. The Inverse Relationship

Section titled “1. The Inverse Relationship”

A logarithm simply asks the question: “What power do I need to raise the base to, in order to get this number?”

The fundamental rule you must memorize is the conversion between exponential form and logarithmic form: y=ax  ⟺  x=log⁡ayy = a^x \iff x = \log_a yy=ax⟺x=loga​y

Here, aaa is the base. Notice that the base of the exponent becomes the base of the logarithm.

2. The Cancellation Properties

Section titled “2. The Cancellation Properties”

Because logarithms and exponentials are inverse functions, they effectively “cancel” each other out when nested together. These two cancellation properties are incredibly useful for solving algebraic equations:

  1. Logarithm of an Exponential: log⁡a(ax)=x\log_a(a^x) = xloga​(ax)=x (Example: log⁡10(107)=7\log_{10}(10^7) = 7log10​(107)=7)

  2. Exponential of a Logarithm: alog⁡a(x)=xa^{\log_a(x)} = xaloga​(x)=x (Example: 10log⁡10(45)=4510^{\log_{10}(45)} = 4510log10​(45)=45)

3. The Logarithmic Graph

Section titled “3. The Logarithmic Graph”

Because logarithmic functions are the exact inverse of exponential functions, their graphs are mirror images of each other, reflected perfectly across the diagonal line y=xy = xy=x.

Let’s look at the graphs of y=10xy = 10^xy=10x and its inverse, y=log⁡10xy = \log_{10} xy=log10​x:

Key properties to notice:

  • The exponential graph has a horizontal asymptote at y=0y = 0y=0 (the x-axis) and a y-intercept at (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1).
  • The logarithmic graph has a vertical asymptote at x=0x = 0x=0 (the y-axis) and an x-intercept at (1,0)(1, 0)(1,0). Notice how it dives steeply downwards as xxx approaches zero.
  • You cannot take the logarithm of zero or a negative number. The domain is strictly x>0x > 0x>0.

4. Solving Exponential Equations

Section titled “4. Solving Exponential Equations”

When the unknown variable is trapped in the power (e.g., ax=ba^x = bax=b), you can solve it by taking the logarithm (base 10) of both sides.

Using the rules of logarithms, the power xxx drops down to the front, giving us the standard solution formula: ax=b  ⟹  x=log⁡blog⁡aa^x = b \implies x = \frac{\log b}{\log a}ax=b⟹x=logalogb​

Example Walkthrough

Solve for xxx: 3x=453^x = 453x=45

  1. Apply the formula: x=log⁡45log⁡3x = \frac{\log 45}{\log 3}x=log3log45​
  2. Type this into your GDC (remembering to input base 10!).
  3. x≈3.46x \approx 3.46x≈3.46 (to 3 significant figures).

5. Real-World Contexts: Growth and Decay

Section titled “5. Real-World Contexts: Growth and Decay”

Logarithms are incredibly useful in finance and science when you need to figure out how long something takes to grow or decay.

Compound Interest (Growth)

Section titled “Compound Interest (Growth)”

If you want to know how many years it will take for an investment to reach a certain value, you are solving an exponential equation.

Example: 500 dollars is invested at a compound interest rate of 4% per year. How many full years will it take for the investment to exceed 800 dollars?

  1. Set up the compound interest formula: 500×(1+4100)n=800500 \times \left(1 + \frac{4}{100}\right)^n = 800500×(1+1004​)n=800
  2. Divide by 500: 1.04n=1.61.04^n = 1.61.04n=1.6
  3. Use the logarithm rule: n=log⁡1.6log⁡1.04n = \frac{\log 1.6}{\log 1.04}n=log1.04log1.6​
  4. Calculate: n≈12.0003n \approx 12.0003n≈12.0003
  5. Since it asks for full years to exceed 800, the answer is 13 years.

Depreciation (Decay)

Section titled “Depreciation (Decay)”

The exact same logic applies when things lose value over time.

Example: A new car is bought for 25,000 dollars. It depreciates (loses value) at a rate of 15% per year. How many years will it take for its value to drop to exactly 10,000 dollars?

  1. Set up the decay formula (using subtraction instead of addition): 25000×(1−15100)n=1000025000 \times \left(1 - \frac{15}{100}\right)^n = 1000025000×(1−10015​)n=10000
  2. Simplify the bracket and divide by 25,000: 0.85n=0.40.85^n = 0.40.85n=0.4
  3. Use the logarithm rule: n=log⁡0.4log⁡0.85n = \frac{\log 0.4}{\log 0.85}n=log0.85log0.4​
  4. Calculate: n≈5.64n \approx 5.64n≈5.64
  5. Answer: It will take 5.64 years.

6. Solving Log Equations from a Graph

Section titled “6. Solving Log Equations from a Graph”

Sometimes an exam question will give you a complicated logarithmic or exponential equation to solve, like 10x−1=5x+210^{x - 1} = 5x + 210x−1=5x+2. Instead of using algebra, you should use your GDC.

Treat both sides of the equation as separate functions and plot them:

  • f1(x)=10x−1f_1(x) = 10^{x - 1}f1​(x)=10x−1
  • f2(x)=5x+2f_2(x) = 5x + 2f2​(x)=5x+2

Use Menu →\rightarrow→ Analyze Graph →\rightarrow→ Intersection on your calculator to find the point where the two lines cross. The xxx-coordinate of this intersection (x≈2.10x \approx 2.10x≈2.10) is the solution to your equation!
