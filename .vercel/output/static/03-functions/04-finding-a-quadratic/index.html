3.4 Finding a Quadratic Function

In previous chapters, you learned how to extract key features from a given quadratic equation. In this section, we work in reverse: you will act as a mathematical detective, using visual clues from a graph (like its turning point or where it crosses the axes) to figure out its original equation.

1. Using the Vertex Form

Section titled “1. Using the Vertex Form”

Every quadratic function can be written in vertex form: y=a(x−h)2+ky = a(x - h)^2 + ky=a(x−h)2+k

This form is incredibly powerful because the coordinates of the vertex (the turning point) are exactly (h,k)(h, k)(h,k).

Finding the Equation Given the Vertex and One Point

Section titled “Finding the Equation Given the Vertex and One Point”

If you are given the vertex and exactly one other coordinate on the curve, you can find the full equation.

Example: A parabola has a vertex at (2,1)(2, 1)(2,1) and passes through the point (0,5)(0, 5)(0,5). Find its equation.

  1. Substitute the vertex (h,k)(h, k)(h,k): Here, h=2h = 2h=2 and k=1k = 1k=1. y=a(x−2)2+1y = a(x - 2)^2 + 1y=a(x−2)2+1
  2. Substitute the other point (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) to find aaa: The point is (0,5)(0, 5)(0,5), so plug in x=0x = 0x=0 and y=5y = 5y=5. 5=a(0−2)2+15=a(−2)2+15=4a+14=4aa=1\begin{aligned} 5 &= a(0 - 2)^2 + 1 \\ 5 &= a(-2)^2 + 1 \\ 5 &= 4a + 1 \\ 4 &= 4a \\ a &= 1 \end{aligned}5554a​=a(0−2)2+1=a(−2)2+1=4a+1=4a=1​
  3. Write the final equation: y=1(x−2)2+1  ⟹  y=(x−2)2+1y = 1(x - 2)^2 + 1 \implies y = (x - 2)^2 + 1y=1(x−2)2+1⟹y=(x−2)2+1

The Negative Sign Trap

Notice the minus sign in y=a(x−h)2+ky = a(x - h)^2 + ky=a(x−h)2+k. If your vertex has a negative x-coordinate, say (−3,4)(-3, 4)(−3,4), the equation becomes y=a(x−(−3))2+4y = a(x - (-3))^2 + 4y=a(x−(−3))2+4, which simplifies to y=a(x+3)2+4y = a(x + 3)^2 + 4y=a(x+3)2+4. The sign inside the bracket always looks like the opposite of the vertex’s x-coordinate!

2. Using the x-intercepts Form

Section titled “2. Using the x-intercepts Form”

If a graph crosses the x-axis, those crossing points are called x-intercepts or roots. If you know both roots, let’s call them ppp and qqq, you can build the quadratic using the factored form: y=a(x−p)(x−q)y = a(x - p)(x - q)y=a(x−p)(x−q)

Finding the Equation Given the x-intercepts and One Point

Section titled “Finding the Equation Given the x-intercepts and One Point”

If you are given the two roots and one extra coordinate on the curve, use the factored form.

Example: A quadratic graph crosses the x-axis at x=−1x = -1x=−1 and x=3x = 3x=3. It passes through the point (1,−4)(1, -4)(1,−4). Find its equation.

  1. Substitute the roots ppp and qqq: Here, p=−1p = -1p=−1 and q=3q = 3q=3. y=a(x−(−1))(x−3)y=a(x+1)(x−3)\begin{aligned} y &= a(x - (-1))(x - 3) \\ y &= a(x + 1)(x - 3) \end{aligned}yy​=a(x−(−1))(x−3)=a(x+1)(x−3)​
  2. Substitute the other point (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) to find aaa: The point is (1,−4)(1, -4)(1,−4), so plug in x=1x = 1x=1 and y=−4y = -4y=−4. −4=a(1+1)(1−3)−4=a(2)(−2)−4=−4aa=1\begin{aligned} -4 &= a(1 + 1)(1 - 3) \\ -4 &= a(2)(-2) \\ -4 &= -4a \\ a &= 1 \end{aligned}−4−4−4a​=a(1+1)(1−3)=a(2)(−2)=−4a=1​
  3. Write the final equation: y=1(x+1)(x−3)  ⟹  y=(x+1)(x−3)y = 1(x + 1)(x - 3) \implies y = (x + 1)(x - 3)y=1(x+1)(x−3)⟹y=(x+1)(x−3) (You can expand this to y=x2−2x−3y = x^2 - 2x - 3y=x2−2x−3 if the question asks for the form ax2+bx+cax^2 + bx + cax2+bx+c.)

3. The Special Case: When a=1a = 1a=1

Section titled “3. The Special Case: When a=1a = 1a=1”

Often, an exam question will explicitly tell you that the function is of the form y=x2+bx+cy = x^2 + bx + cy=x2+bx+c. Because there is no number written in front of the x2x^2x2, this immediately tells you that a=1a = 1a=1.

This saves you half the work! You do not need a secondary point to substitute. You simply plug your vertex (h,k)(h, k)(h,k) into y=(x−h)2+ky = (x - h)^2 + ky=(x−h)2+k, or your roots into y=(x−p)(x−q)y = (x - p)(x - q)y=(x−p)(x−q), and expand the brackets to find your final answer.

Steve's Exam Wisdom

Expanding Vertex Form Quickly

If a question tells you y=x2+bx+cy = x^2 + bx + cy=x2+bx+c has a vertex at (4,−3)(4, -3)(4,−3), write y=(x−4)2−3y = (x - 4)^2 - 3y=(x−4)2−3. Expand carefully using (A−B)2=A2−2AB+B2(A - B)^2 = A^2 - 2AB + B^2(A−B)2=A2−2AB+B2:

y=(x2−8x+16)−3y=x2−8x+13\begin{aligned} y &= (x^2 - 8x + 16) - 3 \\ y &= x^2 - 8x + 13 \end{aligned}yy​=(x2−8x+16)−3=x2−8x+13​

You have successfully found the coefficients without doing any messy simultaneous equations!

The turning point of a quadratic graph is (3,4)(3, 4)(3,4). The graph crosses the y-axis at (0,−14)(0, -14)(0,−14). Find the equation of the quadratic in the form y=a(x−h)2+ky = a(x - h)^2 + ky=a(x−h)2+k.

  1. The vertex is (3,4)(3, 4)(3,4), so h=3h = 3h=3 and k=4k = 4k=4. y=a(x−3)2+4y = a(x - 3)^2 + 4y=a(x−3)2+4
  2. The graph passes through (0,−14)(0, -14)(0,−14). Substitute x=0,y=−14x=0, y=-14x=0,y=−14: −14=a(0−3)2+4−14=9a+4−18=9aa=−2\begin{aligned} -14 &= a(0 - 3)^2 + 4 \\ -14 &= 9a + 4 \\ -18 &= 9a \\ a &= -2 \end{aligned}−14−14−18a​=a(0−3)2+4=9a+4=9a=−2​
  3. The equation is: y=−2(x−3)2+4y = -2(x - 3)^2 + 4y=−2(x−3)2+4
