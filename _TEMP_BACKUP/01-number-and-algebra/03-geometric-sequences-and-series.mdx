While arithmetic sequences grow by a constant addition, **geometric sequences** grow by a constant multiplication. This multiplicative nature leads to exponential growth or decay. 

In the AA SL course, mastering geometric sequences requires strong algebraic skills, particularly the use of index laws and logarithms. You will apply these concepts to model dynamic, real-world phenomena such as the spread of disease, salary increases, and population growth.

## 1. Geometric Sequences (The $n^{\text{th}}$ Term)

A geometric sequence is a sequence of numbers where the ratio of any term to the previous term is strictly constant. We call this the **common ratio**, denoted by $r$.

* If $r > 1$, the sequence exhibits exponential growth.
* If $0 < r < 1$, the sequence exhibits exponential decay.
* If $r$ is negative, the sequence oscillates between positive and negative values.

<FormulaBox title="1.3 The nth term of a geometric sequence">
$$
u_n = u_1 r^{n-1}
$$
</FormulaBox>

Where:
* $u_n$ is the value of the $n^{\text{th}}$ term.
* $u_1$ is the first term.
* $r$ is the common ratio, found by calculating $u_2 \div u_1$ or $u_n \div u_{n-1}$.
* $n$ is the position of the term.

## 2. Finite Geometric Series & Sigma Notation

A geometric **series** is the sum of the terms of a geometric sequence. Because the terms grow (or shrink) exponentially, the sums can become very large very quickly. 

The formula booklet provides two equivalent versions of the sum formula.

<FormulaBox title="1.3 The sum of n terms of a finite geometric sequence">
$$
S_n = \frac{u_1(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} = \frac{u_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}, \quad r \neq 1
$$
</FormulaBox>

<SteveTip title="Which version of the formula should I use?">
Both formulae will give you the exact same answer. However, you can make your algebra much easier to manage without a calculator (Paper 1) by choosing strategically:
* Use the first version (with $r^n - 1$) when your ratio $r > 1$. This prevents the denominator from becoming negative.
* Use the second version (with $1 - r^n$) when your ratio $r < 1$. This keeps the denominator positive!
</SteveTip>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Finding a Specific Term">
    Consider the geometric sequence: $5, 15, 45, 135, \dots$
    
    a) Write down the value of the first term, $u_1$, and the common ratio, $r$.
    
    b) Find the exact value of the $10^{\text{th}}$ term.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **Part a:**
    The first term is:
    $$
    u_1 = 5
    $$
    Calculate $r$ by dividing the second term by the first:
    $$
    r = \frac{15}{5} = 3
    $$
    
    **Part b:**
    Use the $n^{\text{th}}$ term formula with $n = 10$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u_n &= u_1 r^{n-1} \\
    u_{10} &= 5(3)^{10-1} \\
    u_{10} &= 5(3)^9 \\
    u_{10} &= 5(19683) = 98415
    \end{aligned}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Negative Ratios (Oscillating Sequences)">
    A geometric sequence begins $8, -4, 2, -1, \dots$
    
    a) Find the common ratio.
    
    b) Find the $8^{\text{th}}$ term as an exact fraction.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **Part a:**
    Divide the second term by the first:
    $$
    r = \frac{-4}{8} = -\frac{1}{2}
    $$

    **Part b:**
    Substitute into the formula, ensuring you place the negative ratio in brackets:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u_8 &= 8 \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^{8-1} \\
    u_8 &= 8 \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^7
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Since the power (7) is odd, the result will be negative:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u_8 &= 8 \left(-\frac{1}{128}\right) \\
    u_8 &= -\frac{8}{128} = -\frac{1}{16}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Summing a Geometric Series">
    Find the sum of the first $12$ terms of the geometric sequence $2, 6, 18, 54, \dots$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    Identify the parameters: $u_1 = 2$, $r = 3$, and $n = 12$.
    Because $r > 1$, we will use the first version of the sum formula:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    S_n &= \frac{u_1(r^n - 1)}{r - 1} \\
    S_{12} &= \frac{2(3^{12} - 1)}{3 - 1} \\
    S_{12} &= \frac{2(531441 - 1)}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Notice the 2 in the numerator and denominator cancel out perfectly:
    $$
    S_{12} = 531440
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Exam-Style Sigma Notation">
    Evaluate the following series. You must show the first term and common ratio clearly.
    $$
    \sum_{k=1}^{8} 4(0.5)^{k-1}
    $$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
    1. **Generate the first few terms** to confirm it is geometric and identify $u_1$ and $r$.
       When $k = 1$:
       $$ u_1 = 4(0.5)^0 = 4(1) = 4 $$
       When $k = 2$:
       $$ u_2 = 4(0.5)^1 = 2 $$
    2. **Identify parameters:** This is a geometric series with $u_1 = 4$ and $r = 0.5$. There are $8 - 1 + 1 = 8$ terms.
    3. **Apply the sum formula:** Because $r < 1$, use the second version of the formula:
       $$
       \begin{aligned}
       S_n &= \frac{u_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r} \\
       S_8 &= \frac{4(1 - 0.5^8)}{1 - 0.5} \\
       S_8 &= \frac{4(1 - 0.00390625)}{0.5}
       \end{aligned}
       $$
    4. **Simplify:** Dividing by $0.5$ is the same as multiplying by 2:
       $$
       S_8 = 8(0.99609375) = 7.96875
       $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Application - Population Growth">
    A biologist is studying a bacterial infection. The initial population of bacteria is estimated to be $400$. The population increases by $15\%$ every hour.
    
    a) Write down the common ratio, $r$, for this geometric sequence.
    
    b) Calculate the expected bacterial population after $10$ full hours have elapsed.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **Part a:**
    A $15\%$ increase means the new population is $115\%$ of the previous hour. Therefore, the multiplier (common ratio) is:
    $$
    r = 1.15
    $$

    **Part b:**
    We want the population *after* 10 hours. 
    * Initial (Time 0) = $u_1 = 400$
    * After 1 hour = $u_2$
    * After 10 hours = $u_{11}$
    
    Using the $n^{\text{th}}$ term formula where $n = 11$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u_{11} &= 400(1.15)^{11-1} \\
    u_{11} &= 400(1.15)^{10} \\
    u_{11} &\approx 1618.22
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Therefore, the population is approximately 1618 bacteria.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Solving for n using Logarithms (Paper 2)">
    A geometric sequence has a first term of 7 and a common ratio of 2.
    Find the smallest value of $n$ for which the $n^{\text{th}}$ term exceeds $10,000$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    We need to set up an inequality: $u_n > 10000$.
    $$
    7(2)^{n-1} > 10000
    $$
    Divide by 7:
    $$
    2^{n-1} > 1428.57...
    $$
    To solve for an unknown exponent, take the natural logarithm ($\ln$) of both sides:
    $$
    \ln(2^{n-1}) > \ln(1428.57...)
    $$
    Use the power rule of logarithms to bring the exponent down:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    (n-1) \ln(2) &> \ln(1428.57...) \\
    n - 1 &> \frac{\ln(1428.57...)}{\ln(2)} \\
    n - 1 &> 10.48... \\
    n &> 11.48...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Since $n$ must be a positive integer, the smallest number of terms is:
    $$
    n = 12
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Simultaneous Equations (Finding r)">
    The $2^{\text{nd}}$ term of a geometric sequence is 12 and the $5^{\text{th}}$ term is 324.
    Given that all terms in the sequence are positive, find the common ratio $r$ and the first term $u_1$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    Set up two equations using the $n^{\text{th}}$ term formula:
    $$
    u_1 r^1 = 12 \quad \text{--- (Equation 1)}
    $$
    $$
    u_1 r^4 = 324 \quad \text{--- (Equation 2)}
    $$
    Unlike arithmetic sequences where we subtract, for geometric sequences we **divide** the equations to eliminate $u_1$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \frac{u_1 r^4}{u_1 r} &= \frac{324}{12} \\
    r^3 &= 27
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Cube root both sides:
    $$
    r = 3
    $$
    Substitute $r = 3$ back into Equation 1 to find $u_1$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    u_1(3) &= 12 \\
    u_1 &= 4
    \end{aligned}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Algebraic Geometric Sequence (Show That)">
    The first three terms of a geometric sequence are $x - 3$, $x + 1$, and $3x - 1$.
    Given that $x > 0$, find the value of $x$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    In a geometric sequence, the ratio between consecutive terms must be equal. Therefore:
    $$
    \frac{u_2}{u_1} = \frac{u_3}{u_2}
    $$
    Substitute the algebraic expressions:
    $$
    \frac{x + 1}{x - 3} = \frac{3x - 1}{x + 1}
    $$
    Cross-multiply to clear the fractions:
    $$
    (x + 1)(x + 1) = (3x - 1)(x - 3)
    $$
    Expand both sides:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x^2 + 2x + 1 &= 3x^2 - 9x - x + 3 \\
    x^2 + 2x + 1 &= 3x^2 - 10x + 3
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Bring all terms to one side to form a quadratic equation equal to zero:
    $$
    2x^2 - 12x + 2 = 0
    $$
    Divide by 2 to simplify:
    $$
    x^2 - 6x + 1 = 0
    $$
    This quadratic does not factorise cleanly, so use the quadratic formula:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x &= \frac{-(-6) \pm \sqrt{(-6)^2 - 4(1)(1)}}{2(1)} \\
    x &= \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{36 - 4}}{2} \\
    x &= \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{32}}{2}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Simplify the surd ($\sqrt{32} = 4\sqrt{2}$):
    $$
    x = \frac{6 \pm 4\sqrt{2}}{2} = 3 \pm 2\sqrt{2}
    $$
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
