Does studying more hours lead to higher test scores? Does a country's GDP correlate with its life expectancy? By analysing **bivariate data**, we can identify relationships, measure their strength, and create mathematical models for making predictions.

## 1. Scatter Diagrams and Linear Correlation

A **scatter diagram** is the most effective way to visually represent bivariate data. Each pair of coordinates $(x, y)$ is plotted as a single point. By looking at the overall pattern of the points, we can determine the type of relationship, or **correlation**, that exists.

When plotting a line of best fit "by eye", it should follow the general trend of the data and must always pass through the **mean point**, denoted as $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. The mean point is found by calculating the mean of all $x$-values and the mean of all $y$-values.

### Types of Correlation
* **Positive Correlation:** As $x$ increases, $y$ increases (line slopes upwards).
* **Negative Correlation:** As $x$ increases, $y$ decreases (line slopes downwards).
* **Zero Correlation:** No visible linear pattern.

## 2. Pearson's Product-Moment Correlation Coefficient ($r$)

We need a mathematical way to quantify the strength and direction of a linear relationship. This is where **Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient ($r$)** comes in. The value of $r$ always falls between $-1$ and $1$.

### Interpreting the Strength of $r$
While there is no universal mathematical agreement on the exact boundaries for "weak," "moderate," and "strong," the IB generally accepts the following scale (using the absolute value, $\lvert r \rvert$):

* $0.00 \le \lvert r \rvert < 0.25$: **Very Weak / No Correlation**
* $0.25 \le \lvert r \rvert < 0.50$: **Weak**
* $0.50 \le \lvert r \rvert < 0.75$: **Moderate**
* $0.75 \le \lvert r \rvert \le 1.00$: **Strong**

<Aside type="caution" title="Context Matters!">
  These boundaries are just a guide! In experimental physics, an $r$-value of $0.85$ might be considered weakly correlated because laboratory conditions demand precision. Conversely, in sociology, human behaviour is so erratic that an $r$-value of $0.45$ might be considered a remarkably strong correlation.
</Aside>

### The Impact of Outliers
Pearson's $r$ is highly sensitive to **outliers** (anomalous data points that do not fit the general trend). A single severe outlier can make a strong linear relationship appear moderate or weak. This is why it is critical to observe the scatter diagram visually; if an outlier is clearly the result of a measurement error, it should be removed before calculating $r$ or generating a regression model.

## 3. The Linear Regression Line ($y$ on $x$)

If the scatter diagram and Pearson's $r$ indicate a correlation, we can construct a mathematical model. The **regression line of $y$ on $x$** is the line that minimises the sum of the squares of the vertical distances from each data point to the line (the residuals).

The equation takes the form:
$$ y = ax + b $$

* **$a$ (Gradient/Slope):** The average change in $y$ for every one-unit increase in $x$.
* **$b$ ($y$-intercept):** The predicted value of $y$ when $x = 0$.

### Reliability of Predictions

We use the regression line of $y$ on $x$ to **predict** the value of $y$ for a given value of $x$. The reliability of this prediction depends on two main factors:

1. **The strength of the correlation:** Predictions are only considered reliable if the correlation is **strong** ($\lvert r \rvert \ge 0.75$). If the model is weak or moderate, the data points are too scattered from the line for the prediction to be trustworthy.
2. **Interpolation vs Extrapolation:**
   * **Interpolation:** Making predictions within the domain of your original data. (Generally reliable).
   * **Extrapolation:** Making predictions outside the domain of your original data. (Unreliable, as we do not know if the trend continues).

<Aside type="danger" title="Predicting x from y">
  The regression line of $y$ on $x$ is specifically calculated to minimise the *vertical* distances from the points to the line. Therefore, **you cannot reliably substitute a $y$-value into the $y$ on $x$ regression equation to predict an $x$-value**.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Linear Regression
**Question:** The temperature ($x$ in °C) and the number of hot chocolate drinks sold ($y$) at a cafe are recorded over $5$ days.

| Temperature, $x$ (°C) | $5$ | $8$ | $12$ | $15$ | $18$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Hot Chocolates, $y$ | $90$ | $75$ | $60$ | $45$ | $30$ |

**a)** Using your GDC, find the value of Pearson's $r$ and categorise its strength.
**b)** Find the equation of the regression line of $y$ on $x$.
**c)** Predict the number of hot chocolates sold if the temperature is $10$°C.
**d)** Discuss the reliability of your prediction in part (c).

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Enter data and calculate using GDC.**
     Entering the table into the TI-Nspire and running `Linear Regression (mx+b)` gives:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     r &= -0.999 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)} \\
     a &= -4.53 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)} \\
     b &= 113 \text{ (to 3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     Since $r = -0.999$, this is a **strong negative** correlation.
  2. **Write the regression equation.**
     $$ y = -4.53x + 113 $$
  3. **Substitute $x = 10$ into the equation.**
     Using the unrounded values on your calculator:
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= -4.5329...(10) + 112.58... \\
     &= 67.251... \approx 67.3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The cafe would expect to sell approximately $67$ hot chocolates.
  4. **Evaluate reliability.**
     The prediction is highly reliable because the correlation is very strong ($\lvert r \rvert > 0.75$) and it is an example of interpolation ($10$°C is within the given data range of $5$°C to $18$°C).
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Extrapolation Dangers">
    A company models its production volume ($x$, in hundreds of units) against operating costs ($y$, in $1000 \text{ USD}$) using the equation $y = 3.5x + 10$. The model was built using production volumes between $2$ and $15$ (hundreds of units). The correlation is $r = 0.88$.
    
    **a)** Predict the operating costs if they produce $5000$ units (i.e., $x = 50$).
    
    **b)** Explain why the prediction made in part (a) is highly unreliable, despite the $r$-value being strong.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. **Substitute $x = 50$ into the equation.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 3.5(50) + 10 \\
     &= 175 + 10 \\
     &= 185
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The predicted cost is $185,000 \text{ USD}$.
</Steps>
    
    **b)** This prediction is highly unreliable because it involves **extrapolation**. $x = 50$ is far outside the measured data range of $2 \le x \le 15$. The linear trend is unlikely to continue indefinitely; for massive production increases, they might need to build a new factory, which would cause costs to jump non-linearly.
  </TabItem>
