When we calculate probabilities, we often receive new information that changes the likelihood of an event occurring. This is the essence of **conditional probability**. If we know that a certain event has already happened, we are no longer looking at the entire original sample space; instead, we restrict our focus to a smaller, "reduced" sample space.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: Medicine, TOK & Interdisciplinary Knowledge">
  **Medical Studies:** Conditional probability is the mathematical foundation of diagnostic testing. When assessing risk factors, doctors don't just look at the overall probability of a disease $\text{P}(D)$. They look at the conditional probability given a symptom $\text{P}(D|\text{Symptom})$ or a positive test result $\text{P}(D|\text{Test Positive})$. 
  
  **Theory of Knowledge (TOK):** The applications of probability stretch across medicine, finance, sociology, and physics. Does organising knowledge into separate "silos" limit our ability to understand complex, interconnected global issues?
</Aside>

## 1. Conditional Probability Formula

The probability of event $A$ occurring, **given that** event $B$ has already occurred, is written as $\text{P}(A|B)$. 

When $B$ has occurred, $B$ becomes our new sample space. The only way $A$ can now occur is if the outcome is in the intersection of $A$ and $B$.

$$ \text{P}(A|B) = \dfrac{\text{P}(A \cap B)}{\text{P}(B)} $$
*(where $\text{P}(B) > 0$)*

By rearranging this formula, we get the **general multiplication rule**:
$$ \text{P}(A \cap B) = \text{P}(B)\text{P}(A|B) $$

### Worked Example: Using the Formula
**Question:** In a group of students, the probability that a student studies History is $0.6$. The probability that a student studies both History and Geography is $0.45$. Given that a randomly selected student studies History, find the probability that they also study Geography.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Define the events and state known probabilities.**
     Let $H$ be studying History, and $G$ be studying Geography.
     $\text{P}(H) = 0.6$
     $\text{P}(H \cap G) = 0.45$
  2. **Set up the conditional probability formula.**
     We are looking for $\text{P}(G|H)$.
     $$ \text{P}(G|H) = \dfrac{\text{P}(G \cap H)}{\text{P}(H)} $$
  3. **Substitute and solve.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(G|H) &= \dfrac{0.45}{0.6} \\
     &= 0.75
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 2. Independent Events

Two events are **independent** if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other occurring. 

Formally, $A$ and $B$ are independent if:
$$ \text{P}(A|B) = \text{P}(A) $$
This means knowing $B$ happened didn't change the chance of $A$ happening. Furthermore, if $A$ is independent of $B$, it is also independent of $B$ not happening ($B'$). Therefore, for independent events:
$$ \text{P}(A|B) = \text{P}(A) = \text{P}(A|B') $$

### Testing for Independence

Because $\text{P}(A|B) = \dfrac{\text{P}(A \cap B)}{\text{P}(B)}$, substituting $\text{P}(A)$ for $\text{P}(A|B)$ gives us the standard mathematical test for independence:
Two events $A$ and $B$ are independent **if and only if**:
$$ \text{P}(A \cap B) = \text{P}(A) \times \text{P}(B) $$

<Aside type="danger" title="Independent vs. Mutually Exclusive">
  Students often confuse **Independent** events with **Mutually Exclusive** events. They are completely different concepts!
  * **Independent:** One event doesn't affect the probability of the other. Test: $\text{P}(A \cap B) = \text{P}(A)\text{P}(B)$.
  * **Mutually Exclusive:** The two events cannot happen at the same time. Test: $\text{P}(A \cap B) = 0$.
  In fact, if two events (with probabilities $> 0$) are mutually exclusive, they *cannot* be independent! (Knowing one happened means the other definitely didn't).
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Testing Independence
**Question:** Events $X$ and $Y$ are such that $\text{P}(X) = 0.4$, $\text{P}(Y) = 0.5$, and $\text{P}(X \cup Y) = 0.7$. Determine whether events $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. **Find the intersection $\text{P}(X \cap Y)$.**
     Using the addition rule: $\text{P}(X \cup Y) = \text{P}(X) + \text{P}(Y) - \text{P}(X \cap Y)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0.7 &= 0.4 + 0.5 - \text{P}(X \cap Y) \\
     0.7 &= 0.9 - \text{P}(X \cap Y) \\
     \text{P}(X \cap Y) &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. **Calculate the product of the individual probabilities.**
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(X) \times \text{P}(Y) &= 0.4 \times 0.5 \\
     &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. **Compare and conclude.**
     Since $\text{P}(X \cap Y) = \text{P}(X) \times \text{P}(Y)$, the events $X$ and $Y$ are independent.
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Solving for k (Union Constraints)">
    Events $A$ and $B$ are such that $\text{P}(A) = 0.4$, $\text{P}(B) = k$, and $\text{P}(A \cup B) = 0.7$.  
    Find the value of $k$ given that:  
    **a)** $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive.  
    **b)** $A$ and $B$ are independent.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive, then $\text{P}(A \cap B) = 0$.
  2. Substitute into the addition rule: $\text{P}(A \cup B) = \text{P}(A) + \text{P}(B) - \text{P}(A \cap B)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0.7 &= 0.4 + k - 0 \\
     k &= 0.7 - 0.4 \\
     k &= 0.3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. If $A$ and $B$ are independent, then $\text{P}(A \cap B) = \text{P}(A) \times \text{P}(B) = 0.4k$.
  2. Substitute into the addition rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A \cup B) &= \text{P}(A) + \text{P}(B) - \text{P}(A \cap B) \\
     0.7 &= 0.4 + k - 0.4k \\
     0.7 - 0.4 &= k(1 - 0.4) \\
     0.3 &= 0.6k \\
     k &= \dfrac{0.3}{0.6} = 0.5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Solving for k (Intersection Constraints)">
    Events $C$ and $D$ are such that $\text{P}(C) = k$, $\text{P}(D) = 2k$, and $\text{P}(C \cap D) = 0.18$.  
    **a)** Find the exact value of $k$, given that $C$ and $D$ are independent events.  
    **b)** Hence, find $\text{P}(C \cup D)$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Because $C$ and $D$ are independent, we use the property $\text{P}(C \cap D) = \text{P}(C)\text{P}(D)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0.18 &= k \times 2k \\
     0.18 &= 2k^2 \\
     k^2 &= 0.09
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $k$. Since a probability must be non-negative, we reject $k = -0.3$.
     $$ k = 0.3 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Determine the individual probabilities: $\text{P}(C) = 0.3$ and $\text{P}(D) = 2(0.3) = 0.6$.
  2. Substitute into the addition rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(C \cup D) &= \text{P}(C) + \text{P}(D) - \text{P}(C \cap D) \\
     &= 0.3 + 0.6 - 0.18 \\
     &= 0.9 - 0.18 \\
     &= 0.72
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Formula Manipulation">
    Events $A$ and $B$ are such that $\text{P}(A) = 0.55$, $\text{P}(B) = 0.4$, and $\text{P}(A \cup B) = 0.75$.  
    **a)** Find $\text{P}(A \cap B)$.  
    **b)** Find $\text{P}(A|B)$.  
    **c)** Find $\text{P}(B|A)$.  
    **d)** State, with a mathematical reason, whether $A$ and $B$ are independent.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the addition rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A \cup B) &= \text{P}(A) + \text{P}(B) - \text{P}(A \cap B) \\
     0.75 &= 0.55 + 0.4 - \text{P}(A \cap B) \\
     \text{P}(A \cap B) &= 0.95 - 0.75 \\
     \text{P}(A \cap B) &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the conditional probability formula.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A|B) &= \dfrac{\text{P}(A \cap B)}{\text{P}(B)} \\
     &= \dfrac{0.2}{0.4} \\
     &= 0.5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the conditional probability formula.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(B|A) &= \dfrac{\text{P}(B \cap A)}{\text{P}(A)} \\
     &= \dfrac{0.2}{0.55} \\
     &= \dfrac{20}{55} = \dfrac{4}{11} \approx 0.364 \text{ (3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **d)**
<Steps>
  1. Check if $\text{P}(A \cap B) = \text{P}(A)\text{P}(B)$ or if $\text{P}(A|B) = \text{P}(A)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A)\text{P}(B) &= 0.55 \times 0.4 = 0.22
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Since $\text{P}(A \cap B) = 0.2$ and $\text{P}(A)\text{P}(B) = 0.22$, they are not equal. Therefore, $A$ and $B$ are **not independent**.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Conditional Probability from a Table">
    A company surveys 120 employees about their preferred working environment. The results are categorised by department.
    
    | | Remote | Hybrid | Office | **Total** |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **IT** | 25 | 15 | 5 | **45** |
    | **Sales** | 10 | 30 | 35 | **75** |
    | **Total** | **35** | **45** | **40** | **120** |
    
    A randomly selected employee is chosen.  
    **a)** Find the probability that the employee prefers Hybrid work.  
    **b)** Given that the employee is in IT, find the probability that they prefer Remote work.  
    **c)** Given that the employee prefers the Office, find the probability that they are in Sales.  
    **d)** Are the events "Prefers Remote" and "Is in IT" independent? Justify your answer.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    Let $H$, $R$, $O$ represent Hybrid, Remote, Office. Let $I$, $S$ represent IT, Sales.
    
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Read the total for Hybrid from the table.
     $$ \text{P}(H) = \dfrac{45}{120} = \dfrac{3}{8} = 0.375 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. We want $\text{P}(R|I)$. The "given that" restricts our total to only the IT row (Total = 45).
  2. The number of IT employees who prefer Remote is 25.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(R|I) &= \dfrac{\text{P}(R \cap I)}{\text{P}(I)} \\
     &= \dfrac{25/120}{45/120} = \dfrac{25}{45} = \dfrac{5}{9} \approx 0.556 \text{ (3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. We want $\text{P}(S|O)$. The restricted sample space is the Office column (Total = 40).
  2. The number of Office workers in Sales is 35.
     $$ \text{P}(S|O) = \dfrac{35}{40} = \dfrac{7}{8} = 0.875 $$
</Steps>
    **d)**
<Steps>
  1. We can test if $\text{P}(R|I) = \text{P}(R)$.
  2. From (b), $\text{P}(R|I) = \dfrac{5}{9} \approx 0.556$.
  3. Overall probability of Remote: $\text{P}(R) = \dfrac{35}{120} = \dfrac{7}{24} \approx 0.292$.
  4. Since $\text{P}(R|I) \neq \text{P}(R)$, the events are **not independent**.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Medical Diagnosis Context">
    A rare disease affects 2% of a population. A diagnostic test is developed. 
    If a person has the disease, the test will be positive 95% of the time. 
    If a person does not have the disease, the test will falsely return a positive result 4% of the time.  
    **a)** Draw a clearly labelled tree diagram to represent this information.  
    **b)** Find the probability that a randomly chosen person tests positive.  
    **c)** Given that a person tests positive, find the probability that they actually have the disease.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    Let $D$ be having the disease, and $D'$ be not having the disease. Let $T^+$ be a positive test, and $T^-$ be a negative test.
    
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. First branches: Disease ($D$) = 0.02, No Disease ($D'$) = 0.98.
  2. Second branches from $D$: Positive ($T^+$) = 0.95, Negative ($T^-$) = 0.05.
  3. Second branches from $D'$: Positive ($T^+$) = 0.04, Negative ($T^-$) = 0.96.
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. A positive test can happen two ways: $D \cap T^+$ or $D' \cap T^+$.
  2. Sum the probabilities of these two paths.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(T^+) &= \text{P}(D \cap T^+) + \text{P}(D' \cap T^+) \\
     &= (0.02 \times 0.95) + (0.98 \times 0.04) \\
     &= 0.019 + 0.0392 \\
     &= 0.0582
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. We want $\text{P}(D|T^+)$.
     $$ \text{P}(D|T^+) = \dfrac{\text{P}(D \cap T^+)}{\text{P}(T^+)} $$
  2. Substitute the values calculated in part (b).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(D|T^+) &= \dfrac{0.019}{0.0582} \\
     &\approx 0.326 \text{ (3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     *(Note for students: Despite testing positive, there is only a 32.6% chance the person actually has the disease. This is a classic medical statistics counter-intuitive result!)*
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Venn Diagram Complements and Conditionals">
    A group of 80 students were asked if they like Algebra ($A$) or Geometry ($G$). 
    45 like Algebra, 35 like Geometry, and 15 like neither.  
    **a)** Find the number of students who like both Algebra and Geometry.  
    **b)** Draw a fully labelled Venn diagram representing this information.  
    **c)** Find $\text{P}(A \cap G')$.  
    **d)** Find $\text{P}(G | A')$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Let $x$ be the number of students who like both.
     The number who like at least one is $80 - 15 = 65$.
  2. Use the addition rule for sets: $n(A \cup G) = n(A) + n(G) - n(A \cap G)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     65 &= 45 + 35 - x \\
     65 &= 80 - x \\
     x &= 15
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Draw the universal set containing 80.
  2. Intersection ($A \cap G$) = 15.
  3. Algebra only ($A \cap G'$) = $45 - 15 = 30$.
  4. Geometry only ($G \cap A'$) = $35 - 15 = 20$.
  5. Outside both ($(A \cup G)'$) = 15.
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. $\text{P}(A \cap G')$ is the probability of liking Algebra but NOT Geometry (Algebra only).
     $$ \text{P}(A \cap G') = \dfrac{30}{80} = \dfrac{3}{8} = 0.375 $$
</Steps>
    **d)**
<Steps>
  1. We want $\text{P}(G | A')$. The "given that" condition is $A'$ (students who do NOT like Algebra).
  2. Find the total number of students in $A'$ (the new sample space).
     $n(A') = 20 \text{ (Geometry only)} + 15 \text{ (Neither)} = 35$.
  3. Out of those 35 students, how many like Geometry? Only the 20 who are in $G \cap A'$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(G | A') &= \dfrac{n(G \cap A')}{n(A')} \\
     &= \dfrac{20}{35} = \dfrac{4}{7} \approx 0.571 \text{ (3 s.f.)}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Conceptual Independence Test">
    A standard fair six-sided die is rolled. 
    Let $E$ be the event that the number rolled is even.
    Let $M$ be the event that the number rolled is a multiple of 3.  
    Determine, with mathematical justification, whether events $E$ and $M$ are independent.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
<Steps>
  1. List the sample space and the outcomes for each event.
     $U = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$
     $E = \{2, 4, 6\} \implies \text{P}(E) = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2}$
     $M = \{3, 6\} \implies \text{P}(M) = \dfrac{2}{6} = \dfrac{1}{3}$
  2. Find the intersection $E \cap M$.
     The only number both even and a multiple of 3 is $6$.
     $E \cap M = \{6\} \implies \text{P}(E \cap M) = \dfrac{1}{6}$
  3. Test for independence: does $\text{P}(E \cap M) = \text{P}(E) \times \text{P}(M)$?
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(E) \times \text{P}(M) &= \dfrac{1}{2} \times \dfrac{1}{3} \\
     &= \dfrac{1}{6}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Since $\text{P}(E \cap M) = \text{P}(E) \times \text{P}(M)$, the events $E$ and $M$ are **independent**.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Manipulating Conditionals">
    For two events $S$ and $T$, it is given that $\text{P}(S) = 0.4$, $\text{P}(T|S) = 0.3$, and $\text{P}(S|T) = 0.6$.  
    **a)** Find $\text{P}(S \cap T)$.  
    **b)** Find $\text{P}(T)$.  
    **c)** Are $S$ and $T$ mutually exclusive? Justify your answer.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the multiplication rule.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(S \cap T) &= \text{P}(S)\text{P}(T|S) \\
     &= 0.4 \times 0.3 \\
     &= 0.12
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Use the conditional probability formula for $\text{P}(S|T)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(S|T) &= \dfrac{\text{P}(S \cap T)}{\text{P}(T)} \\
     0.6 &= \dfrac{0.12}{\text{P}(T)} \\
     \text{P}(T) &= \dfrac{0.12}{0.6} \\
     &= 0.2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Mutually exclusive events cannot occur at the same time, meaning their intersection probability must be $0$.
  2. We found $\text{P}(S \cap T) = 0.12$.
  3. Since $0.12 \neq 0$, events $S$ and $T$ are **not mutually exclusive**.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q9: A Casino Game Context">
    A casino offers a card game. Event $A$ is drawing an Ace, and Event $K$ is drawing a King. Players must pay 10.00 USD to play.
    Suppose the casino uses a rigged deck such that $\text{P}(A) = 0.15$ and $\text{P}(K) = 0.20$. You are told that $A$ and $K$ are mutually exclusive in this single draw.  
    **a)** State $\text{P}(A \cap K)$.  
    **b)** Find the conditional probability $\text{P}(A | K')$.  
    **c)** Explain in words what $\text{P}(A | K')$ means in this context.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 9">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Because $A$ and $K$ are mutually exclusive (you cannot draw a card that is both an Ace and a King simultaneously).
     $$ \text{P}(A \cap K) = 0 $$
</Steps>
    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. We want $\text{P}(A | K')$. Use the conditional formula:
     $$ \text{P}(A | K') = \dfrac{\text{P}(A \cap K')}{\text{P}(K')} $$
  2. Find $\text{P}(K')$.
     $$ \text{P}(K') = 1 - 0.20 = 0.80 $$
  3. Find $\text{P}(A \cap K')$. Since $A$ and $K$ are mutually exclusive, all of event $A$ happens entirely outside of $K$. Thus, $A \cap K' = A$.
     $$ \text{P}(A \cap K') = \text{P}(A) = 0.15 $$
  4. Calculate the conditional probability.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{P}(A | K') &= \dfrac{0.15}{0.80} \\
     &= 0.1875
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. It represents the probability of drawing an Ace, **given that** the card drawn was not a King.
  2. Because we know a King was not drawn, the total pool of possible outcomes is smaller, making the likelihood of drawing an Ace slightly higher (from 0.15 to 0.1875).
</Steps>
