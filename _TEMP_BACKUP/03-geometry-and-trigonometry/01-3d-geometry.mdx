## 1. 3D Coordinate Geometry

To define a point in 3D space, we add a third axis, the $z$-axis, which is perpendicular to both the $x$ and $y$ axes. A coordinate is written as an ordered triplet: $(x, y, z)$.

### Distance in 3D Space
The 3D distance formula is a direct extension of Pythagoras' theorem applied twice. It calculates the direct straight-line length (the space diagonal) between two points.

<FormulaBox title="Distance between two points (x₁, y₁, z₁) and (x₂, y₂, z₂)">
$$ d = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2 + (z_1 - z_2)^2} $$
</FormulaBox>

### The Midpoint in 3D
The midpoint is simply the average of the $x$-coordinates, the average of the $y$-coordinates, and the average of the $z$-coordinates.

<FormulaBox title="Coordinates of the midpoint of a line segment">
$$ \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}, \frac{z_1 + z_2}{2} \right) $$
</FormulaBox>

## 2. Volumes and Surface Areas of 3D Solids

When tackling 3D solids, it is crucial to distinguish between the **perpendicular height** ($h$) and the **slant height** ($l$).

### Pyramids
A right pyramid has its apex directly above the centre of its base. The base is typically a square or a rectangle in SL problems. Always calculate the area of the base ($A$) first.

<FormulaBox title="Volume of a right-pyramid">
$$ V = \frac{1}{3}Ah $$
</FormulaBox>

**Hidden Right Triangles:** There are two main right triangles to look for in a square-based pyramid:
1.  Using the **slant height of a face**: Formed by the vertical height, half the base width, and the face's slant height.
2.  Using a **slant edge**: Formed by the vertical height, half the base diagonal, and the slant edge.

### Cones
A right cone has its apex directly above the centre of its circular base. 

<FormulaBox title="Volume of a right cone">
$$ V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h $$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Area of the curved surface of a cone">
$$ A = \pi rl $$
</FormulaBox>

*(Note: The curved surface area formula does not include the circular base. If the cone is solid and closed, the total surface area is $\pi r l + \pi r^2$).*

**Hidden Right Triangle:** The radius ($r$), perpendicular height ($h$), and slant height ($l$) always form a right-angled triangle where **$l^2 = r^2 + h^2$**.

### Spheres and Hemispheres
A perfectly round geometrical 3D object. A hemisphere is exactly half of a sphere.

<FormulaBox title="Volume of a sphere">
$$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 $$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="Surface area of a sphere">
$$ A = 4\pi r^2 $$
</FormulaBox>

**Hemisphere Properties:**
* Volume = $\frac{2}{3}\pi r^3$.
* Curved Surface Area = $2\pi r^2$.
* *Total* Surface Area (if solid/closed) = $2\pi r^2$ (curved) + $\pi r^2$ (flat circular base) = **$3\pi r^2$**.

<SteveTip title="Composite Solids">
Many exam questions combine these shapes (e.g., a cone resting on a cylinder, or a hemisphere on top of a cone). 
1. **Volumes** are strictly additive. Total Volume = Volume A + Volume B.
2. **Surface Areas** are tricky. You must subtract the hidden faces where the two shapes join. Do not blindly add the total surface areas together!
</SteveTip>

## 3. 3D Trigonometry and Angles

The SL syllabus specifies that you only need to use **right-angled trigonometry** (SOH CAH TOA, Pythagoras) for 3D shapes. The challenge is not the trigonometry itself, but *visualising* and extracting the correct 2D triangle from the 3D space.

### Angle Between a Line and a Plane
To find the angle between a line and a plane (like a space diagonal hitting the base of a cuboid):
1.  Imagine a light shining directly down onto the plane.
2.  Drop a **perpendicular** from the top of the line to the plane.
3.  Draw the **projection** (the "shadow") of the line on the plane.
4.  The angle required is the angle between the original line and its projection.

<Aside type="caution" title="Common Pitfall: Identifying the Correct Angle">
In a square-based pyramid, distinguishing between two specific angles is vital:
* The angle between a **slant edge** and the base (uses the half-diagonal of the base).
* The angle between a **triangular face** and the base (uses half the width of the base).
Always draw the 2D cross-section flat on your page before applying trigonometry!
</Aside>

    Consider the points $A(-2, 5, 1)$ and $B(4, -3, 6)$.
    
    a) Find the exact distance between $A$ and $B$.  
    b) Find the coordinates of $M$, the midpoint of the line segment $AB$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** Use the 3D distance formula:
    $$ d = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2 + (z_1 - z_2)^2} $$
    $$ d = \sqrt{(-2 - 4)^2 + (5 - (-3))^2 + (1 - 6)^2} $$
    $$ d = \sqrt{(-6)^2 + (8)^2 + (-5)^2} $$
    $$ d = \sqrt{36 + 64 + 25} $$
    $$ d = \sqrt{125} $$
    The exact distance is $5\sqrt{5}$ units.

    **b)** Use the 3D midpoint formula:
    $$ M = \left( \frac{-2 + 4}{2}, \frac{5 - 3}{2}, \frac{1 + 6}{2} \right) $$
    $$ M = \left( \frac{2}{2}, \frac{2}{2}, \frac{7}{2} \right) $$
    The midpoint is $(1, 1, 3.5)$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Finding an Unknown Coordinate">
    The distance between point $P(1, 2, z)$ and point $Q(4, -2, 5)$ is exactly 13 units. 
    Given that $z > 0$, find the value of $z$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    Set up the distance formula equal to 13:
    $$ \sqrt{(1 - 4)^2 + (2 - (-2))^2 + (z - 5)^2} = 13 $$
    
    Square both sides to remove the square root:
    $$ (-3)^2 + (4)^2 + (z - 5)^2 = 169 $$
    $$ 9 + 16 + (z - 5)^2 = 169 $$
    $$ 25 + (z - 5)^2 = 169 $$
    $$ (z - 5)^2 = 144 $$
    
    Take the square root of both sides (remember the $\pm$):
    $$ z - 5 = \pm 12 $$
    $$ z = 5 \pm 12 $$
    
    This gives two possible values: $z = 17$ or $z = -7$.
    Since we are given that $z > 0$, the only valid solution is $z = 17$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Sphere and Hemisphere Fundamentals">
    A solid sphere has a radius of 6 cm.
    
    a) Calculate the exact volume of the sphere.  
    b) The sphere is cut exactly in half to form two solid hemispheres. Calculate the total surface area of *one* of these hemispheres. Give your answer in terms of $\pi$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Volume of a sphere:
    $$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 $$
    $$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi (6)^3 $$
    $$ V = \frac{4}{3}\pi (216) $$
    $$ V = 4 \times 72 \pi $$
    The exact volume is $288\pi$ cm³.

    **b)** A solid hemisphere has two surfaces: the curved bowl and the flat circular base.
    $$ \text{Curved Surface Area} = \frac{1}{2}(4\pi r^2) = 2\pi r^2 $$
    $$ \text{Base Area} = \pi r^2 $$
    $$ \text{Total Surface Area} = 3\pi r^2 $$
    
    Substitute $r = 6$:
    $$ \text{Total SA} = 3\pi (6)^2 $$
    $$ \text{Total SA} = 3\pi (36) $$
    The total surface area is $108\pi$ cm².
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: The Right Cone">
    A right cone has a base radius of 5 cm and a perpendicular height of 12 cm.
    
    a) Find the slant height of the cone.  
    b) Calculate the total surface area of the solid cone to 1 decimal place.  
    c) Calculate the exact volume of the cone.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** The radius, perpendicular height, and slant height form a right-angled triangle.
    $$ l^2 = r^2 + h^2 $$
    $$ l^2 = 5^2 + 12^2 $$
    $$ l^2 = 25 + 144 = 169 $$
    $$ l = 13 $$
    The slant height is 13 cm.

    **b)** Total Surface Area = Base Area + Curved Surface Area
    $$ \text{Total SA} = \pi r^2 + \pi rl $$
    $$ \text{Total SA} = \pi(5)^2 + \pi(5)(13) $$
    $$ \text{Total SA} = 25\pi + 65\pi = 90\pi $$
    $$ 90\pi \approx 282.743... $$
    The total surface area is 282.7 cm².

    **c)** Volume of a cone:
    $$ V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h $$
    $$ V = \frac{1}{3}\pi (5)^2 (12) $$
    $$ V = \frac{1}{3}\pi (25) (12) $$
    $$ V = 100\pi $$
    The exact volume is $100\pi$ cm³.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Right Square-Based Pyramid">
    A right pyramid has a square base with sides of length 10 cm. The slant height of each triangular face is 13 cm.
    
    a) Find the perpendicular height of the pyramid.  
    b) Calculate the volume of the pyramid.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)** Visualize the right-angled triangle formed by the perpendicular height ($h$), the slant height ($l = 13$), and a line running from the centre of the base to the middle of an edge.
    Since the base is 10 cm wide, the distance from the centre to the edge is 5 cm.
    
    Using Pythagoras:
    $$ h^2 + 5^2 = 13^2 $$
    $$ h^2 + 25 = 169 $$
    $$ h^2 = 144 $$
    $$ h = 12 $$
    The perpendicular height is 12 cm.

    **b)** Volume of a pyramid = $\frac{1}{3}Ah$.
    The base is a square: $A = 10 \times 10 = 100$.
    $$ V = \frac{1}{3}(100)(12) $$
    $$ V = 400 $$
    The volume is 400 cm³.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Composite Solid (Ice Cream Cone)">
    A composite solid consists of a solid hemisphere placed perfectly on top of a solid right cone. 
    The radius of both the hemisphere and the cone is 4 cm. The total height of the composite solid is 19 cm.
    
    a) Find the perpendicular height of the cone.  
    b) Calculate the exact total volume of the composite solid.  
    c) Calculate the exact total exposed surface area of the solid.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)** The total height is made up of the height of the cone plus the radius of the hemisphere on top.
    $$ \text{Total Height} = h_{\text{cone}} + r $$
    $$ 19 = h_{\text{cone}} + 4 $$
    $$ h_{\text{cone}} = 15 $$
    The perpendicular height of the cone is 15 cm.

    **b)** Total Volume = Volume of Cone + Volume of Hemisphere
    $$ V_{\text{cone}} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi (4)^2 (15) = 80\pi $$
    $$ V_{\text{hemisphere}} = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{2}{3}\pi (4)^3 = \frac{128\pi}{3} $$
    $$ \text{Total Volume} = 80\pi + \frac{128\pi}{3} = \frac{240\pi}{3} + \frac{128\pi}{3} = \frac{368\pi}{3} $$
    The total volume is exactly $\frac{368\pi}{3}$ cm³ (or approx 385.4 cm³).

    **c)** The exposed surface area consists of the curved area of the hemisphere and the curved area of the cone. The flat circular bases are joined internally and are *hidden*.
    
    First, find the slant height ($l$) of the cone:
    $$ l^2 = 4^2 + 15^2 = 16 + 225 = 241 $$
    $$ l = \sqrt{241} $$
    
    $$ \text{Exposed SA} = \text{Curved SA}_{\text{hemisphere}} + \text{Curved SA}_{\text{cone}} $$
    $$ \text{Exposed SA} = 2\pi r^2 + \pi rl $$
    $$ \text{Exposed SA} = 2\pi(4)^2 + \pi(4)(\sqrt{241}) $$
    $$ \text{Exposed SA} = 32\pi + 4\pi\sqrt{241} $$
    The exact total surface area is $32\pi + 4\pi\sqrt{241}$ cm².
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Melting and Reformation (Conservation of Volume)">
    A solid metal cylinder has a radius of 8 cm and a height of 20 cm. 
    It is melted down completely and recast into identical smaller solid spheres, each with a radius of 2 cm.
    
    *(Note: Volume of a cylinder = $\pi r^2 h$)*
    
    Assuming no metal is lost in the process, find the maximum number of full spheres that can be cast.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    First, find the total volume of metal available from the cylinder:
    $$ V_{\text{cylinder}} = \pi r^2 h $$
    $$ V_{\text{cylinder}} = \pi (8)^2 (20) $$
    $$ V_{\text{cylinder}} = \pi (64) (20) = 1280\pi $$
    The volume is $1280\pi$ cm³.
    
    Next, find the volume of one small sphere:
    $$ V_{\text{sphere}} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 $$
    $$ V_{\text{sphere}} = \frac{4}{3}\pi (2)^3 $$
    $$ V_{\text{sphere}} = \frac{32\pi}{3} $$
    The volume of one sphere is $\frac{32\pi}{3}$ cm³.
    
    Divide the total volume by the volume of one sphere to find the quantity:
    $$ \text{Number of spheres} = \frac{1280\pi}{\frac{32\pi}{3}} $$
    The $\pi$ cancels out.
    $$ \text{Number} = 1280 \times \frac{3}{32} $$
    $$ \text{Number} = 40 \times 3 = 120 $$
    
    Exactly 120 spheres can be cast.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Real-World Application (Finance & Architecture)">
    An architectural firm is designing a roof in the shape of a solid hemisphere with a diameter of 24 m. 
    The curved exterior surface of the roof needs to be painted with a special weather-resistant coating.
    
    The coating costs 45 EUR per square metre.
    
    Calculate the total cost of the coating required to paint the roof. Give your answer to the nearest EUR.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    First, establish the radius. Diameter = 24 m, so $r = 12$ m.
    
    We only need to paint the *curved exterior*, so we use the curved surface area formula for a hemisphere:
    $$ A = 2\pi r^2 $$

  <TabItem label="📝 Q15: Complex Composite Constraints">
    A manufacturer needs to design a solid lead plumb bob consisting of a cylinder with a cone attached to the bottom and a hemisphere attached to the top. 
    * All three shapes have the same radius, $r$.
    * The height of the cylinder is $3r$.
    * The total volume of the entire plumb bob must be exactly 50 cm³.
    * The perpendicular height of the cone portion is equal to its diameter.
    
    Calculate the exact radius $r$ required for this design.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 15">
    We must construct an algebraic expression for the total volume in terms of $r$.
    
    1.  **Hemisphere (top):** $V_H = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3$
    2.  **Cylinder (middle):** The height is $3r$.
        $$ V_{Cyl} = \pi r^2 h = \pi r^2 (3r) = 3\pi r^3 $$
    3.  **Cone (bottom):** The height is equal to its diameter, so $h = 2r$.
        $$ V_{Cone} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 (2r) = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 $$
    
    Combine them for the total volume:
    $$ V_{Total} = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 + 3\pi r^3 + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 $$
    $$ V_{Total} = \left(\frac{2}{3} + \frac{9}{3} + \frac{2}{3}\right)\pi r^3 $$
    $$ V_{Total} = \frac{13}{3}\pi r^3 $$
    
    Set this equal to the required total volume of 50:
    $$ \frac{13}{3}\pi r^3 = 50 $$
    $$ \pi r^3 = \frac{150}{13} $$
    $$ r^3 = \frac{150}{13\pi} $$
    $$ r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{150}{13\pi}} $$
    
    The exact radius is $\sqrt[3]{\frac{150}{13\pi}}$ cm (approx 1.54 cm).
  </TabItem>
