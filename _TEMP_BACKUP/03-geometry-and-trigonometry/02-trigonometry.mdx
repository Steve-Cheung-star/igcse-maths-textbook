## 1. Right-Angled Trigonometry

Before applying advanced rules, always check if your triangle contains a right angle ($90^\circ$). If it does, you can rely on the foundational tools of Pythagoras and SOH CAH TOA, which are often faster and simpler.

* **Pythagoras' Theorem:** $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$
* **SOH:** $\sin(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}$
* **CAH:** $\cos(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}$
* **TOA:** $\tan(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}$

## 2. The Sine and Cosine Rules

When a triangle does not have a right angle, we must use the Sine and Cosine rules. 

**Standard Naming Convention:** Angles are labelled with capital letters (e.g., $A, B, C$), and the side directly opposite each angle is labelled with the corresponding lowercase letter (e.g., $a, b, c$).

### The Sine Rule
Use the Sine Rule when you know a "matching pair" (an angle and the side directly opposite it), plus one other piece of information.

<FormulaBox title="The sine rule">
$$ \dfrac{a}{\sin A} = \dfrac{b}{\sin B} = \dfrac{c}{\sin C} $$
</FormulaBox>

*Note: If you are trying to find an unknown angle, it is algebraically much easier to flip the entire formula upside down so the unknown $\sin(A)$ is in the numerator! (e.g., $\frac{\sin A}{a} = \frac{\sin B}{b}$).*

### The Cosine Rule
Use the Cosine Rule when you do not have a matching pair. This occurs in two specific scenarios:
1.  **SAS (Side-Angle-Side):** You know two sides and the angle sandwiched between them.
2.  **SSS (Side-Side-Side):** You know all three sides and want to find an angle.

<FormulaBox title="The cosine rule (for finding a side)">
$$ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C $$
</FormulaBox>

<FormulaBox title="The cosine rule (rearranged for finding an angle)">
$$ \cos C = \dfrac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab} $$
</FormulaBox>

## 3. Area of a Triangle

You no longer need to know the perpendicular height to find the area of a triangle. If you know two sides and the included angle (SAS), you can find the area instantly.

<FormulaBox title="Area of a triangle">
$$ A = \dfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C $$
</FormulaBox>

<SteveTip title="GDC Mastery: Radians vs. Degrees">
The most common mistake in IB Mathematics is having your calculator in the wrong angle mode. 
* In **Geometry & Trigonometry** (Topic 3), you will almost exclusively use **Degrees**.
* In **Calculus** (Topic 5), you will exclusively use **Radians**.
Always check the top right corner of your TI-Nspire screen before starting an exam. Click it with your touchpad to toggle instantly between DEG and RAD.

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Direct Application">
    In triangle $ABC$, the length of $AB$ is $8 \text{ cm}$, $BC$ is $11 \text{ cm}$, and the angle $ABC$ is $65^\circ$.
    
    a) Calculate the length of side $AC$.  
    b) Calculate the size of angle $BAC$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)** We have two sides and the included angle (SAS), so we use the Cosine Rule to find the opposite side ($AC$, which we will call $b$).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    b^2 &= a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos B \\
    b^2 &= 11^2 + 8^2 - 2(11)(8)\cos(65^\circ) \\
    b^2 &= 121 + 64 - 176(0.4226...) \\
    b^2 &= 185 - 74.381... \\
    b^2 &= 110.618... \\
    b &= \sqrt{110.618...} \approx 10.517...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The length of $AC$ is **$10.5 \text{ cm}$** (to 3 s.f.).

    **b)** We now have a matching pair (angle $B = 65^\circ$ and side $b = 10.517$). We can use the Sine Rule to find angle $A$ (which is $\angle BAC$). Let side $a = 11$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \dfrac{\sin A}{a} &= \dfrac{\sin B}{b} \\
    \dfrac{\sin A}{11} &= \dfrac{\sin 65^\circ}{10.517...} \\
    \sin A &= 11 \times \left( \dfrac{\sin 65^\circ}{10.517...} \right) \\
    \sin A &\approx 0.9479... \\
    A &= \sin^{-1}(0.9479...) \approx 71.42...^\circ
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The angle $BAC$ is **$71.4^\circ$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Working Backwards with Area">
    In triangle $PQR$, the length of $PQ$ is $12 \text{ cm}$ and the angle $QPR$ is $40^\circ$. 
    The total area of the triangle is exactly $45 \text{ cm}^2$.
    
    a) Find the exact length of side $PR$.  
    b) Calculate the length of side $QR$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)** Let the length of $PR$ be $x$. We use the area formula, knowing it requires two sides and the included angle.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Area} &= \dfrac{1}{2}bc\sin P \\
    45 &= \dfrac{1}{2}(x)(12)\sin(40^\circ) \\
    45 &= 6x\sin(40^\circ) \\
    x &= \dfrac{45}{6\sin(40^\circ)} \\
    x &= \dfrac{45}{3.8567...} \approx 11.667...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The length of $PR$ is **$11.7 \text{ cm}$** (to 3 s.f.).

    **b)** Now we have SAS ($PQ = 12, PR = 11.667, \angle P = 40^\circ$). We use the Cosine Rule to find the opposite side, $QR$ (or $p$).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    p^2 &= q^2 + r^2 - 2qr\cos P \\
    p^2 &= 11.667^2 + 12^2 - 2(11.667)(12)\cos(40^\circ) \\
    p^2 &= 136.14 + 144 - 280.01\cos(40^\circ) \\
    p^2 &= 280.14 - 214.49... \\
    p^2 &= 65.65... \\
    p &= \sqrt{65.65...} \approx 8.102...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The length of $QR$ is **$8.10 \text{ cm}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Economics Context (Land Valuation)">
    A developer intends to purchase a triangular plot of land. Two boundaries of the land measure $40 \text{ m}$ and $60 \text{ m}$, and the angle enclosed between these two boundaries is $110^\circ$.
    
    The land is valued at a flat rate of **150 EUR** per square metre.
    
    a) Calculate the area of the land.  
    b) Calculate the total cost of purchasing the plot. Give your answer to the nearest EUR.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** Use the area formula with the two given sides and the included angle.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    A &= \dfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C \\
    A &= \dfrac{1}{2}(40)(60)\sin(110^\circ) \\
    A &= 1200 \times 0.9396... \\
    A &\approx 1127.63...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The area is **$1128 \text{ m}^2$** (to 4 s.f.).

    **b)** Multiply the unrounded area by the cost per square metre.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{Total Cost} &= 1127.631... \times 150 \\
    \text{Total Cost} &= 169144.66...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    To the nearest whole number, the cost is **169145 EUR**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: SSS and the Largest Angle">
    The three sides of a triangular metal plate measure $7 \text{ cm}$, $9 \text{ cm}$, and $14 \text{ cm}$.
    
    Identify the location of the largest angle in the triangle, and calculate its size in degrees.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **Identify:** A fundamental rule of geometry states that the largest interior angle is always directly opposite the longest side. Therefore, the largest angle is opposite the $14 \text{ cm}$ side.

    **Calculate:** We have all three sides (SSS), so we use the rearranged Cosine Rule. Let $c = 14$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \cos C &= \dfrac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab} \\
    \cos C &= \dfrac{7^2 + 9^2 - 14^2}{2(7)(9)} \\
    \cos C &= \dfrac{49 + 81 - 196}{126} \\
    \cos C &= \dfrac{-66}{126} = -0.5238... \\
    C &= \cos^{-1}(-0.5238...) \approx 121.58...^\circ
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The largest angle is **$121.6^\circ$** (to 3 s.f.). *(Note: A negative cosine value guarantees an obtuse angle, which visually confirms it is the largest!).*
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Navigation and Bearings">
    A ship sails from Port $A$ on a bearing of $040^\circ$ for a distance of $20 \text{ km}$ to reach a buoy at $B$. 
    It then turns and sails from the buoy on a bearing of $150^\circ$ for a distance of $35 \text{ km}$ to reach Port $C$.
    
    Calculate the direct distance between Port $A$ and Port $C$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    *Bearings are always measured clockwise from the North line.*
    
    **Step 1: Sketch and find the interior angle at $B$.**
    * Draw a North line straight up from $A$. The line $AB$ branches off at $40^\circ$.
    * Draw a North line straight up from $B$. The line $BC$ branches off at $150^\circ$.
    * Using parallel North lines, the interior angle at $B$ consists of two parts. The angle "looking back" at $A$ (co-interior with $40^\circ$) is $180^\circ - 40^\circ = 140^\circ$.
    * Since angles around the point $B$ must sum to $360^\circ$, the interior angle inside the triangle is:
        $$ \angle ABC = 360^\circ - 140^\circ - 150^\circ = 70^\circ $$

    **Step 2: Use the Cosine Rule.**
    We now have a triangle with sides $20$, $35$, and an included angle of $70^\circ$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    b^2 &= a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos B \\
    b^2 &= 35^2 + 20^2 - 2(35)(20)\cos(70^\circ) \\
    b^2 &= 1225 + 400 - 1400(0.3420...) \\
    b^2 &= 1625 - 478.82... \\
    b^2 &= 1146.17... \\
    b &= \sqrt{1146.17...} \approx 33.85...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The direct distance from Port $A$ to Port $C$ is **$33.9 \text{ km}$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Angles of Elevation (Two Observers)">
    Two observers, Alice and Bob, are standing on the same side of a tall vertical communications tower, on flat ground. Alice is standing $50 \text{ m}$ directly behind Bob.
    
    The angle of elevation to the top of the tower from Alice is $25^\circ$, and from Bob is $40^\circ$.
    
    Calculate the exact height of the tower.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    Let the top of the tower be $T$, the base be $D$, Bob's position be $B$, and Alice's position be $A$.
    The distance $AB = 50 \text{ m}$.
    
    **Method: Use the non-right triangle $\Delta ABT$ first.**
    1.  The angle $\angle TBD$ is $40^\circ$. The angles on a straight line add to $180^\circ$, so the interior angle $\angle TBA = 180^\circ - 40^\circ = 140^\circ$.
    2.  The angles in triangle $\Delta ABT$ must sum to $180^\circ$:
        $$ \angle ATB = 180^\circ - 140^\circ - 25^\circ = 15^\circ $$
    3.  We now have a matching pair in $\Delta ABT$: angle $15^\circ$ and opposite side $AB = 50$. We can use the Sine Rule to find the hypotenuse $BT$.
        $$
        \begin{aligned}
        \dfrac{BT}{\sin(25^\circ)} &= \dfrac{50}{\sin(15^\circ)} \\
        BT &= \dfrac{50\sin(25^\circ)}{\sin(15^\circ)} \\
        BT &\approx 81.65... \text{ m}
        \end{aligned}
        $$
    
    **Method: Now use the right-angled triangle $\Delta TBD$.**
    We have the hypotenuse $BT = 81.65$, and the angle $40^\circ$. We want the opposite side (the height, $h$).
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \sin(40^\circ) &= \dfrac{h}{81.65...} \\
    h &= 81.65... \times \sin(40^\circ) \\
    h &\approx 52.48...
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The height of the tower is **$52.5 \text{ m}$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Composite Figures (Quadrilaterals)">
    A plot of land is in the shape of a quadrilateral $ABCD$. A fence runs diagonally across the plot from $A$ to $C$.
    In triangle $ABC$: $AB = 5 \text{ km}$, $BC = 8 \text{ km}$, and angle $ABC = 100^\circ$.
    In triangle $ADC$: $AD = 6 \text{ km}$ and $DC = 7 \text{ km}$.
    
    Calculate the size of angle $ADC$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    This requires a two-step process. The diagonal $AC$ is the shared side between both triangles.
    
    **Step 1: Find the length of $AC$ using triangle $ABC$.**
    We have SAS, so we use the Cosine Rule.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    AC^2 &= 5^2 + 8^2 - 2(5)(8)\cos(100^\circ) \\
    AC^2 &= 25 + 64 - 80(-0.1736...) \\
    AC^2 &= 89 + 13.89... = 102.89... \\
    AC &= \sqrt{102.89...} \approx 10.143... \text{ km}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **Step 2: Find angle $ADC$ using triangle $ADC$.**
    We now have all three sides of $\Delta ADC$ ($AD=6, DC=7, AC=10.143$). We use the rearranged Cosine Rule. Let side $c$ be the diagonal $AC$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \cos D &= \dfrac{6^2 + 7^2 - (10.143...)^2}{2(6)(7)} \\
    \cos D &= \dfrac{36 + 49 - 102.89...}{84} \\
    \cos D &= \dfrac{-17.89...}{84} \approx -0.213... \\
    D &= \cos^{-1}(-0.213...) \approx 102.29...^\circ
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The angle $ADC$ is **$102^\circ$** (to 3 s.f.).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Perimeter and Proportions">
    A triangular garden $PQR$ has one fence boundary $PQ = 10 \text{ m}$. 
    The angle at corner $P$ is $50^\circ$, and the angle at corner $Q$ is $60^\circ$.
    
    Calculate the total perimeter of the garden.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    To find the perimeter, we must calculate the lengths of the two missing sides, $PR$ and $QR$.
    
    **Step 1: Find the missing angle.**
    Angles in a triangle sum to $180^\circ$.
    $$ \angle R = 180^\circ - 50^\circ - 60^\circ = 70^\circ $$
    
    We now have a matching pair: $\angle R = 70^\circ$ and its opposite side $PQ = 10 \text{ m}$.
    
    **Step 2: Use the Sine Rule to find $QR$ (side $p$).**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \dfrac{p}{\sin 50^\circ} &= \dfrac{10}{\sin 70^\circ} \\
    p &= 10 \times \left( \dfrac{\sin 50^\circ}{\sin 70^\circ} \right) \\
    p &\approx 8.15... \text{ m}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **Step 3: Use the Sine Rule to find $PR$ (side $q$).**
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \dfrac{q}{\sin 60^\circ} &= \dfrac{10}{\sin 70^\circ} \\
    q &= 10 \times \left( \dfrac{\sin 60^\circ}{\sin 70^\circ} \right) \\
    q &\approx 9.22... \text{ m}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **Step 4: Calculate total perimeter.**
    $$ \text{Perimeter} = 10 + 8.15... + 9.22... = 27.37... $$
    The total perimeter is **$27.4 \text{ m}$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
