description: Explore the second derivative, learn to interpret the relationship between a function and its derivatives graphically, and understand concavity.
---

Once we have found the derivative of a function, we have a new mathematical object: the gradient function, $f'(x)$. Because this is itself a function, we can differentiate it again. This process yields the **second derivative**, which tells us the *rate of change of the rate of change*. While the first derivative tells us whether a function is increasing or decreasing, the second derivative tells us about the curvature, or **concavity**, of the graph.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: Simple Harmonic Motion">
  **Links to Physics:** The second derivative is the cornerstone of kinematics and dynamics. If $s(t)$ represents the displacement of an object, $s'(t)$ is its velocity, and $s''(t)$ is its acceleration. In Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)—such as a pendulum swinging or a mass on a spring—the acceleration is directly proportional to the negative of the displacement. This is elegantly summarised by the differential equation $s''(t) = -\omega^2 s(t)$.
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Aim 8: Economics and Marginal Analysis">
  In economics, businesses analyse cost functions $C(x)$, where $x$ is the number of units produced. The first derivative, $C'(x)$, represents the *marginal cost* (the cost of producing one additional unit). The second derivative, $C''(x)$, tells us how the marginal cost is changing. If $C''(x) > 0$, the cost of producing each additional unit is increasing, often indicating diminishing returns. For instance, if production costs are scaling rapidly, intervention may be required to keep costs below 500 EUR per batch.
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="Theory of Knowledge: Mathematical Constructs">
  Does the second derivative "exist" in nature, or is it merely a human mathematical construct we impose on motion? When we feel the "jerk" of a car accelerating, we are physically experiencing a third derivative. How do our physical senses map to abstract calculus concepts?
</Aside>

<Aside type="note" title="International Mindedness: The Calculus Wars">
  The use of different notations—Leibniz $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}$ (German), Newton $\ddot{y}$ (English), and Lagrange $f''(x)$ (Italian-French)—reflects the highly international, yet historically fiercely competitive, development of calculus across Europe in the 17th and 18th centuries.
</Aside>

## 1. Notation and Calculation

Calculating the second derivative is simply a matter of applying your differentiation rules (Power, Chain, Product, Quotient) twice in succession. 

* **Lagrange Notation:** If the original function is $f(x)$, the first derivative is $f'(x)$, and the second derivative is written as **$f''(x)$**.
* **Leibniz Notation:** If $y$ is given in terms of $x$, the first derivative is $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}$, and the second derivative is written as **$\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}$**. 

<Aside type="caution" title="Leibniz Notation Nuance">
  Be careful with the placement of the superscripts in Leibniz notation. It is $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}$, which conceptually means $\dfrac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x} \left( \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} \right)$. It is **not** $\left(\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x}\right)^2$, which would be squaring the first derivative.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Finding the Second Derivative
**Question:** Given $y = x^4 \ln x$, find $\dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2}$.  
**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Find the first derivative using the product rule, where $u = x^4$ and $v = \ln x$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} &= 4x^3 \ln x + x^4 \left(\dfrac{1}{x}\right) \\
     &= 4x^3 \ln x + x^3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Differentiate again to find the second derivative.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2} &= \left[ 12x^2 \ln x + 4x^3 \left(\dfrac{1}{x}\right) \right] + 3x^2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Simplify the resulting expression.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \dfrac{\text{d}^2y}{\text{d}x^2} &= 12x^2 \ln x + 4x^2 + 3x^2 \\
     &= 12x^2 \ln x + 7x^2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

### Connecting the Behaviours
The key to mastering these graphical problems is memorising what a specific feature on one graph implies for the others.

* **When $f(x)$ has a local maximum or minimum:** The gradient is zero, so $f'(x) = 0$. (Notice the red dots align vertically).
* **When $f(x)$ is increasing:** The gradient is positive, so the graph of $f'(x)$ is above the $x$-axis.
* **When $f(x)$ is decreasing:** The gradient is negative, so the graph of $f'(x)$ is below the $x$-axis.

### Concavity and $f''(x)$
The second derivative determines the "bending" of the original curve.
* **Concave Up ($f''(x) > 0$):** The curve of $f(x)$ is shaped like a smile $\cup$. The gradients are increasing.
* **Concave Down ($f''(x) < 0$):** The curve of $f(x)$ is shaped like a frown $\cap$. The gradients are decreasing.
* **Point of Inflection ($f''(x) = 0$):** This is where the curve changes from concave up to concave down, or vice versa. (Notice the yellow dots align vertically: inflection on $f(x)$ matches the turning point on $f'(x)$ and the root on $f''(x)$).

<Aside type="danger" title="A Common Misconception">
  Just because $f''(x) = 0$ does **not** guarantee a point of inflection. The sign of $f''(x)$ must actually change across that point. Think of $f(x) = x^4$. At $x=0$, $f''(0) = 0$, but the graph is concave up everywhere, so $x=0$ is a minimum, not a point of inflection.
</Aside>

### Worked Example: Interpreting Graphs
**Question:** The graph of the derivative $y = f'(x)$ is a parabola opening upwards with $x$-intercepts at $x = -2$ and $x = 4$. State the $x$-coordinates of any turning points on $f(x)$ and determine their nature.  
**Solution:**

<Steps>
  1. Identify turning points of $f(x)$ by looking for where $f'(x) = 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Turning points are at } x &= -2 \text{ and } x = 4.
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Analyse the behaviour of $f'(x)$ around $x = -2$. $f'(x)$ goes from positive to negative.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) \text{ changes from } + \text{ to } - \implies \text{Local Maximum at } x &= -2.
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Analyse the behaviour of $f'(x)$ around $x = 4$. $f'(x)$ goes from negative to positive.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f'(x) \text{ changes from } - \text{ to } + \implies \text{Local Minimum at } x &= 4.
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q10: Reverse Engineering from Derivatives">
    The graph of the second derivative of a function $f(x)$ is given by the line $f''(x) = 4x - 8$. The first derivative $f'(x)$ has a stationary point at $x = 2$.  
    **a)** Determine the interval where the graph of $f(x)$ is concave down.  
    **b)** Given that $f'(x)$ has an $x$-intercept at $x = 0$, find the $x$-coordinate of another turning point of $f(x)$ by analysing the symmetry of $f'(x)$.  
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 10">
    **a)**

<Steps>
      1. Concave down implies $f''(x) < 0$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         4x - 8 &< 0 \\
         4x &< 8 \\
         x &< 2
         \end{aligned}
         $$
    </Steps>  
    **b)**

<Steps>
      1. Since $f''(x)$ is a linear function with a root at $x=2$, $f'(x)$ must be a quadratic function (a parabola) with its vertex at $x=2$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{The axis of symmetry for } f'(x) \text{ is } x &= 2.
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      2. We are given one root of $f'(x)$ at $x=0$. Due to the symmetry of a parabola, the other root must be equidistant from the vertex $x=2$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{Distance from root to vertex} &= 2 - 0 = 2 \\
         \text{Other root} &= 2 + 2 = 4
         \end{aligned}
         $$
      3. Turning points of $f(x)$ occur where $f'(x) = 0$.
         $$
         \begin{aligned}
         \text{The other turning point is at } x &= 4.
