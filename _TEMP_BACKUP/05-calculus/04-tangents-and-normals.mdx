Now that we can find the gradient of a curve at any point using differentiation, we can use this information to construct the equations of specific straight lines associated with the curve. The two most important lines are the **tangent** and the **normal**.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: Other Subjects and TOK">
  **Links to other subjects:** * **Physics:** The tangent to a displacement-time graph gives the instantaneous velocity. In optics, the **normal** line is crucial; the laws of reflection and refraction (Snell's Law) are always measured relative to the normal to the surface. Furthermore, electric field lines are always normal to equipotential surfaces.
  * **Economics:** The concept of price elasticity of demand relies heavily on the gradient of the demand curve at a specific point, essentially using the tangent to analyse how consumer behaviour responds to marginal price changes.

## 1. Tangents and Normals Defined

* **Tangent:** A straight line that "just touches" the curve at a specific point, $P$. It has the exact same gradient as the curve at that point.
* **Normal:** A straight line that passes through the exact same point $P$, but is **perpendicular** (at $90^{\circ}$) to the tangent line.

## 2. Analytic Approach: Finding the Equation

Because both the tangent and the normal are straight lines, we use the standard equation of a straight line:
$$ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) $$
To find this equation, you always need two pieces of information:
1.  **A point $(x_1, y_1)$** on the line.
2.  **The gradient $m$** of the line.

### Finding the Equation of a Tangent
The gradient of the tangent, $m_T$, is exactly equal to the value of the derivative at that specific $x$-coordinate.
$$ m_T = f'(x_1) $$

### Worked Example: Tangent Line
**Question:** Find the equation of the tangent to the curve $y = x^2 - 4x + 5$ at the point where $x = 3$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Find the $y$-coordinate of the point by substituting $x = 3$ into the original equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= (3)^2 - 4(3) + 5 \\
     y &= 9 - 12 + 5 \\
     y &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The point is $(3, 2)$.
  2. Find the derivative $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ to get the general gradient function.
     $$ \begin{aligned} \dfrac{dy}{dx} &= 2x - 4 \end{aligned} $$
  3. Substitute $x = 3$ into the derivative to find the tangent gradient ($m_T$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_T &= 2(3) - 4 \\
     m_T &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Use the straight line formula $y - y_1 = m_T(x - x_1)$ with the point $(3, 2)$ and gradient $m_T = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - 2 &= 2(x - 3) \\
     y - 2 &= 2x - 6 \\
     y &= 2x - 4
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

---

### Finding the Equation of a Normal
Because the normal is perpendicular to the tangent, its gradient ($m_N$) is the **negative reciprocal** of the tangent's gradient.
$$ m_N = -\dfrac{1}{m_T} $$
*(Recall from prior studies: perpendicular gradients multiply to give $-1$, so $m_T \times m_N = -1$).*

### Worked Example: Normal Line
**Question:** Find the equation of the normal to the curve $f(x) = 2x^3 - 5x$ at the point $(1, -3)$. Give your answer in the form $ax + by + c = 0$, where $a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. The point $(1, -3)$ is already given. We need the gradient of the tangent first. Differentiate the function.
     $$ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= 6x^2 - 5 \end{aligned} $$
  2. Substitute $x = 1$ into $f'(x)$ to find $m_T$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_T &= 6(1)^2 - 5 \\
     m_T &= 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Calculate the gradient of the normal, $m_N$, by taking the negative reciprocal.
     $$ \begin{aligned} m_N &= -\dfrac{1}{1} = -1 \end{aligned} $$
  4. Use $y - y_1 = m_N(x - x_1)$ to find the equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - (-3) &= -1(x - 1) \\
     y + 3 &= -x + 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  5. Rearrange into the required format $ax + by + c = 0$.
     $$ \begin{aligned} x + y + 2 &= 0 \end{aligned} $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="danger" title="Common Pitfall: Confusing the Original and Derivative">
  A very frequent exam error is substituting the $x$-value into the *derivative* to find the $y$-coordinate, or substituting the $x$-value into the *original function* to find the gradient. 
  **Rule of thumb:**
  * Original function $f(x)$ $\rightarrow$ gives you position (coordinates).
  * Derivative function $f'(x)$ $\rightarrow$ gives you steepness (gradient).
</Aside>

## 3. Using Technology (TI-Nspire)

In Paper 2 exams, you are expected to use your Graphic Display Calculator to bypass tedious algebra when asked for the equation of a tangent or normal.

<SteveTip title="GDC Skills: Instant Equations">
  On the TI-Nspire Calculator page, you can find equations in seconds:
  * **For a Tangent:** Press `Menu` > `4: Calculus` > `9: Tangent Line`. 
      Syntax: `tangentLine(function, variable, value)`. 
      Example: `tangentLine(x^2 - 4x + 5, x, 3)` returns `2x - 4`.
  * **For a Normal:** Press `Menu` > `4: Calculus` > `A: Normal Line`.
      Syntax: `normalLine(function, variable, value)`.
      Example: `normalLine(2x^3 - 5x, x, 1)` returns `-x - 2`. *(Note: the GDC writes $y = -x - 2$, so you may need to rearrange manually if the question asks for $ax+by+c=0$ form).*
</SteveTip>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q1: Basic Tangent">
    Consider the function $f(x) = x^2 + 6x - 2$.  
    **a)** Find $f'(x)$.  
    **b)** Find the gradient of the tangent to the curve at $x = -1$.  
    **c)** Find the equation of the tangent at $x = -1$, in the form $y = mx + c$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
    $$ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= 2x + 6 \end{aligned} $$

    **b)**
    Substitute $x = -1$ into the derivative.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    f'(-1) &= 2(-1) + 6 \\
    &= 4
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Find the $y$-coordinate by substituting $x = -1$ into the original function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(-1) &= (-1)^2 + 6(-1) - 2 \\
     &= 1 - 6 - 2 \\
     &= -7
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The point is $(-1, -7)$.
  2. Use the point-gradient formula with $m = 4$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - (-7) &= 4(x - (-1)) \\
     y + 7 &= 4(x + 1) \\
     y &= 4x - 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Basic Normal">
    A curve has the equation $y = x^3 - 2x^2 + 4$.  
    **a)** Find the coordinates of the point on the curve where $x = 2$.  
    **b)** Find the equation of the normal to the curve at $x = 2$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
    Substitute $x = 2$ into the original equation.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    y &= (2)^3 - 2(2)^2 + 4 \\
    y &= 8 - 8 + 4 \\
    y &= 4
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The coordinates are $(2, 4)$.

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Find the derivative to determine the gradient of the tangent.
     $$ \begin{aligned} \dfrac{dy}{dx} &= 3x^2 - 4x \end{aligned} $$
  2. Find $m_T$ at $x = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_T &= 3(2)^2 - 4(2) \\
     m_T &= 12 - 8 = 4
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Find the gradient of the normal, $m_N$.
     $$ \begin{aligned} m_N &= -\dfrac{1}{4} \end{aligned} $$
  4. Use the straight line equation with point $(2, 4)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - 4 &= -\dfrac{1}{4}(x - 2) \\
     y &= -\dfrac{1}{4}x + \dfrac{1}{2} + 4 \\
     y &= -\dfrac{1}{4}x + \dfrac{9}{2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Parallel Tangents">
    The curve $C$ is given by $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{3}x^3 - x^2 - 2x + 5$.  
    **a)** Find $f'(x)$.  
    **b)** Find the $x$-coordinates of the points on $C$ where the tangent is parallel to the line $y = 6x - 1$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)**
    $$ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= x^2 - 2x - 2 \end{aligned} $$

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. The line $y = 6x - 1$ has a gradient of $6$. Parallel tangents must also have a gradient of $6$.
  2. Set the derivative equal to $6$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x^2 - 2x - 2 &= 6 \\
     x^2 - 2x - 8 &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Factorise and solve for $x$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     (x - 4)(x + 2) &= 0 \\
     x &= 4 \text{ and } x = -2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q4: Intersection with Axes">
    Find the equation of the tangent to the curve $y = 5 - \dfrac{4}{x}$ at the point where $x = 2$. Then, find the coordinates of the point where this tangent intersects the $x$-axis.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
<Steps>
  1. Find the $y$-coordinate at $x = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 5 - \dfrac{4}{2} = 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     Point is $(2, 3)$.
  2. Rewrite the function with a negative index and differentiate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= 5 - 4x^{-1} \\
     \dfrac{dy}{dx} &= 4x^{-2} = \dfrac{4}{x^2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Find the gradient $m_T$ at $x = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_T &= \dfrac{4}{2^2} = \dfrac{4}{4} = 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Find the equation of the tangent.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - 3 &= 1(x - 2) \\
     y &= x + 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  5. The tangent intersects the $x$-axis when $y = 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0 &= x + 1 \\
     x &= -1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The coordinates are $(-1, 0)$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Q5: Economics Application">
    A company's daily profit, $P$ in USD, from selling $x$ smartwatches is modelled by $P(x) = -0.1x^2 + 40x - 1500$.  
    **a)** Find the equation of the tangent line to the profit curve when $x = 100$ watches are sold.  
    **b)** In economic terms, what does the gradient of this tangent line represent?
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Find the profit ($y$-coordinate) when $x = 100$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P(100) &= -0.1(100)^2 + 40(100) - 1500 \\
     &= -1000 + 4000 - 1500 \\
     &= 1500 \text{ USD}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     Point is $(100, 1500)$.
  2. Differentiate to find $P'(x)$.
     $$ \begin{aligned} P'(x) &= -0.2x + 40 \end{aligned} $$
  3. Evaluate $P'(100)$ to find the gradient $m_T$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     m_T &= -0.2(100) + 40 \\
     m_T &= -20 + 40 = 20
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Write the equation of the tangent.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     P - 1500 &= 20(x - 100) \\
     P - 1500 &= 20x - 2000 \\
     P &= 20x - 500
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
    The gradient of the tangent line ($20$ USD/watch) represents the **marginal profit**. It is the approximate additional profit the company will make from selling one additional smartwatch (the 101st watch).
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Normal at x-intercept">
    Let $g(x) = x^2 - 9$.  
    **a)** Find the positive $x$-intercept of the graph of $g$.  
    **b)** Find the equation of the normal to the graph of $g$ at this $x$-intercept.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. The $x$-intercept occurs where $g(x) = 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x^2 - 9 &= 0 \\
     x^2 &= 9 \\
     x &= 3 \text{ or } x = -3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. The positive $x$-intercept is $x = 3$. The point is $(3, 0)$.
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Differentiate to find the gradient function.
     $$ \begin{aligned} g'(x) &= 2x \end{aligned} $$
  2. Find the gradient of the tangent at $x = 3$.
     $$ \begin{aligned} m_T &= 2(3) = 6 \end{aligned} $$
  3. Find the gradient of the normal.
     $$ \begin{aligned} m_N &= -\dfrac{1}{6} \end{aligned} $$
  4. Form the equation using point $(3, 0)$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y - 0 &= -\dfrac{1}{6}(x - 3) \\
     y &= -\dfrac{1}{6}x + \dfrac{1}{2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q7: Unknown Coefficients">
    The curve $y = ax^2 + bx$ has a tangent at the point $(1, 4)$ with the equation $y = 6x - 2$.  
    **a)** Use the fact that $(1, 4)$ lies on the curve to write down an equation in terms of $a$ and $b$.  
    **b)** Find the values of $a$ and $b$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)**
    Substitute $x = 1$ and $y = 4$ into the curve equation.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    4 &= a(1)^2 + b(1) \\
    a + b &= 4
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. The gradient of the tangent line $y = 6x - 2$ is $6$. This means the derivative at $x = 1$ must equal $6$.
  2. Differentiate the curve equation.
     $$ \begin{aligned} \dfrac{dy}{dx} &= 2ax + b \end{aligned} $$
  3. Substitute $x = 1$ and set the derivative equal to $6$.
     $$ \begin{aligned} 2a(1) + b &= 6 \\ 2a + b &= 6 \end{aligned} $$
  4. Solve the system of simultaneous equations from part (a) and step 3.
     Equation 1: $a + b = 4 \Rightarrow b = 4 - a$
     Equation 2: $2a + b = 6$
  5. Substitute Equation 1 into Equation 2.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2a + (4 - a) &= 6 \\
     a + 4 &= 6 \\
     a &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  6. Substitute $a = 2$ back into Equation 1 to find $b$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     2 + b &= 4 \\
     b &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q8: Physics Context">
    A particle moves such that its displacement $s$ metres from an origin at time $t$ seconds is given by $s(t) = t^3 - 2t^2 + t$.  
    **a)** Find the velocity of the particle at $t = 2$ seconds.  
    **b)** Find the equation of the tangent to the displacement-time graph at $t = 2$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Velocity is the derivative of displacement ($v = \dfrac{ds}{dt}$).
     $$ \begin{aligned} v(t) &= 3t^2 - 4t + 1 \end{aligned} $$
  2. Substitute $t = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     v(2) &= 3(2)^2 - 4(2) + 1 \\
     &= 12 - 8 + 1 \\
     &= 5 \text{ m/s}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Find the coordinates of the point at $t = 2$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s(2) &= (2)^3 - 2(2)^2 + 2 \\
     &= 8 - 8 + 2 \\
     &= 2
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The point is $(2, 2)$ on the $t$-$s$ plane.
  2. The gradient of the tangent is the velocity we found in part (a), so $m = 5$.
  3. Form the equation using variables $s$ and $t$ (instead of $y$ and $x$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s - 2 &= 5(t - 2) \\
     s - 2 &= 5t - 10 \\
     s &= 5t - 8
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
