Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. If differentiation tells us the rate of change (the gradient) of a function, integration allows us to work backwards from the rate of change to find the original function itself. This process is formally known as **anti-differentiation**. Furthermore, integration is a powerful tool used to calculate the exact area enclosed by curves.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections: Other Subjects and TOK">
  **Links to other subjects:** * **Physics and SEHS (Sports, Exercise and Health Science):** The relationships between displacement, velocity, and acceleration are defined by calculus. Just as the gradient of a displacement-time graph gives velocity, the area under a velocity-time graph gives the total displacement. We use integration to move from acceleration $\rightarrow$ velocity $\rightarrow$ displacement.

## 1. Indefinite Integrals (Anti-differentiation)

When we differentiate $f(x) = x^3$, we get $f'(x) = 3x^2$. 
Therefore, if we integrate $3x^2$, we should get back to $x^3$. 

However, notice that differentiating $y = x^3 + 4$ or $y = x^3 - 10$ also gives exactly $3x^2$. Because the derivative of any constant is zero, we must always add a **constant of integration, $C$**, when performing an indefinite integral to account for this unknown original constant.

<FormulaBox title="Integral of $x^n$">
$$ \int x^n \mathrm d x = \dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C, n \neq -1 $$
</FormulaBox>

### The General Rule
To integrate a term of the form $ax^n$:
1. **Increase** the power by $1$.
2. **Divide** by the new power.
3. **Add $C$** (if there are no limits of integration).

### Worked Example: Indefinite Integration
**Question:** Find the indefinite integral $\int \left( 4x^3 - \dfrac{2}{x^2} + 5 \right) \text{d}x$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Rewrite all terms with index notation so the power rule can be applied.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \int \left( 4x^3 - 2x^{-2} + 5 \right) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate each term separately by adding $1$ to the power and dividing by the new power.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \dfrac{4x^4}{4} - \dfrac{2x^{-1}}{-1} + 5x + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Simplify the coefficients and return to positive indices if preferred.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= x^4 + 2x^{-1} + 5x + C \\
     &= x^4 + \dfrac{2}{x} + 5x + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="danger" title="Common Pitfall: Forgetting 'C'">
  A very common error in exams is forgetting to write $+ C$ after evaluating an indefinite integral. Without it, your answer represents only *one* possible function, rather than the entire family of anti-derivatives.
</Aside>

## 2. Finding the Constant of Integration

If we are given a **boundary condition** (a specific point $(x, y)$ that lies on the original curve), we can calculate the exact value of the constant term $C$.

### Worked Example: Finding a Specific Function
**Question:** The gradient function of a curve is given by $\dfrac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = 3x^2 + x$. The curve passes through the point $(1, 10)$. Find the equation of the curve.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Set up the indefinite integral to find the general equation for $y$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= \int (3x^2 + x) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Perform the integration, remembering to include $+ C$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= \dfrac{3x^3}{3} + \dfrac{x^2}{2} + C \\
     y &= x^3 + 0.5x^2 + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute the boundary condition $x = 1, y = 10$ into the equation to solve for $C$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     10 &= (1)^3 + 0.5(1)^2 + C \\
     10 &= 1 + 0.5 + C \\
     10 &= 1.5 + C \\
     C &= 8.5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Write the final specific equation.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     y &= x^3 + 0.5x^2 + 8.5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

## 3. Definite Integrals and Area Under a Curve

A **definite integral** has limits (boundaries) indicating the start and end $x$-values. We evaluate the integral at the upper limit and subtract the value of the integral at the lower limit. **The $+C$ cancels out during this subtraction, so we do not write it for definite integrals.**

The most significant application of the definite integral is finding the precise geometrical area between a curve and the $x$-axis.

<FormulaBox title="Area between a curve">
$$ A = \int_a^b y \, \mathrm d x $$
</FormulaBox>

### Worked Example: Definite Integral
**Question:** Calculate the exact area of the region enclosed by the curve $y = 3x^2 + 4$, the $x$-axis, and the vertical lines $x = 2$ and $x = 6$.

**Solution:**
<Steps>
  1. Write the correct definite integral expression before calculating.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Area} &= \int_{2}^{6} (3x^2 + 4) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate the function inside square brackets with limits written on the right side.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \left[ \dfrac{3x^3}{3} + 4x \right]_{2}^{6} \\
     &= \left[ x^3 + 4x \right]_{2}^{6}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute the upper limit ($6$), then subtract the expression evaluated at the lower limit ($2$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \left( (6)^3 + 4(6) \right) - \left( (2)^3 + 4(2) \right) \\
     &= (216 + 24) - (8 + 8) \\
     &= 240 - 16 \\
     &= 224 \text{ square units}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="Important Syllabus Note">
  The syllabus explicitly states: *Students are expected to first write a correct expression before calculating the area, for example $\int_{2}^{6} (3x^2 + 4) \text{d}x$.* Do not jump straight to the numerical answer, even if you are using a calculator!
</Aside>

    Find the following indefinite integrals:  
    **a)** $\int (6x^5 - 2x) \text{d}x$  
    **b)** $\int \left( \dfrac{3}{x^4} + 7 \right) \text{d}x$  
    **c)** $\int (2x - 1)(x + 3) \text{d}x$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Integrate each term and add $C$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \int (6x^5 - 2x) \text{d}x &= \dfrac{6x^6}{6} - \dfrac{2x^2}{2} + C \\
     &= x^6 - x^2 + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Rewrite with a negative index first.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \int (3x^{-4} + 7) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \dfrac{3x^{-3}}{-3} + 7x + C \\
     &= -x^{-3} + 7x + C \\
     &= -\dfrac{1}{x^3} + 7x + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **c)**
<Steps>
  1. Expand the brackets first; we cannot integrate a product directly at this level.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     (2x - 1)(x + 3) &= 2x^2 + 6x - x - 3 \\
     &= 2x^2 + 5x - 3
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate the expanded polynomial.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \int (2x^2 + 5x - 3) \text{d}x &= \dfrac{2x^3}{3} + \dfrac{5x^2}{2} - 3x + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q2: Finding the Constant">
    A curve has a gradient function given by $f'(x) = 4x^3 - 6x$. The curve passes through the point $(2, 5)$.  
    **a)** Find $f(x)$.  
    **b)** Find the $y$-intercept of the curve.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Set up the integral.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(x) &= \int (4x^3 - 6x) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(x) &= \dfrac{4x^4}{4} - \dfrac{6x^2}{2} + C \\
     f(x) &= x^4 - 3x^2 + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute the given point $(2, 5)$ to find $C$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     5 &= (2)^4 - 3(2)^2 + C \\
     5 &= 16 - 12 + C \\
     5 &= 4 + C \\
     C &= 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. State the final function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(x) &= x^4 - 3x^2 + 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. The $y$-intercept occurs where $x = 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     f(0) &= (0)^4 - 3(0)^2 + 1 \\
     &= 1
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     The $y$-intercept is $(0, 1)$.
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q3: Basic Definite Integral">
    Evaluate the following definite integral algebraically (without a GDC):  
    $\int_{-1}^{2} (3x^2 - 4x + 2) \text{d}x$
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
<Steps>
  1. Find the anti-derivative in square brackets.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \int_{-1}^{2} (3x^2 - 4x + 2) \text{d}x &= \left[ \dfrac{3x^3}{3} - \dfrac{4x^2}{2} + 2x \right]_{-1}^{2} \\
     &= \left[ x^3 - 2x^2 + 2x \right]_{-1}^{2}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Substitute the upper limit ($2$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Upper} &= (2)^3 - 2(2)^2 + 2(2) \\
     &= 8 - 8 + 4 \\
     &= 4
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Substitute the lower limit ($-1$).
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Lower} &= (-1)^3 - 2(-1)^2 + 2(-1) \\
     &= -1 - 2(1) - 2 \\
     &= -1 - 2 - 2 \\
     &= -5
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  4. Subtract the lower result from the upper result.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Value} &= 4 - (-5) \\
     &= 9
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

    Consider the function $f(x) = 5 - x^2$.  
    **a)** Write down the integral expression for the area enclosed by the graph of $f$, the $x$-axis, and the vertical lines $x = 0$ and $x = 2$.  
    **b)** Calculate the exact area of this region algebraically.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">

<Steps>
  1. Integrate the function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}

     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Evaluate at limits.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \left( 5(2) - \dfrac{(2)^3}{3} \right) - \left( 5(0) - \dfrac{(0)^3}{3} \right) \\
     &= \left( 10 - \dfrac{8}{3} \right) - (0) \\
     &= \dfrac{30}{3} - \dfrac{8}{3} \\
     &= \dfrac{22}{3} \text{ square units}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    Let $g(x) = -x^2 + 4x$.  
    **a)** Find the $x$-intercepts of the graph of $g$.  
    **b)** Hence, find the total area enclosed by the curve of $g$ and the $x$-axis.

  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Set $g(x) = 0$ to find the intercepts.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     -x^2 + 4x &= 0 \\
     -x(x - 4) &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Solve for $x$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     x = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad x = 4
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Set up the integral using the $x$-intercepts as the limits.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     \text{Area} &= \int_{0}^{4} (-x^2 + 4x) \text{d}x
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Integrate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \left[ -\dfrac{x^3}{3} + \dfrac{4x^2}{2} \right]_{0}^{4} \\
     &= \left[ -\dfrac{x^3}{3} + 2x^2 \right]_{0}^{4}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Evaluate.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     &= \left( -\dfrac{(4)^3}{3} + 2(4)^2 \right) - (0) \\
     &= -\dfrac{64}{3} + 32 \\
     &= -\dfrac{64}{3} + \dfrac{96}{3} \\
     &= \dfrac{32}{3} \text{ square units}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

  <TabItem label="📝 Q6: Kinematics (Physics Connection)">
    A runner's velocity $v$ (in $\text{m/s}$) at time $t$ seconds is given by $v(t) = 0.6t^2 - 2t + 4$.  
    The runner's displacement $s$ from the start line is $0 \text{ m}$ when $t = 0$.  
    **a)** Find an expression for the displacement $s(t)$ at time $t$.  
    **b)** Find the runner's total displacement after $5$ seconds.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Displacement is the integral of velocity: $s(t) = \int v(t) \text{d}t$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s(t) &= \int (0.6t^2 - 2t + 4) \text{d}t \\
     s(t) &= \dfrac{0.6t^3}{3} - \dfrac{2t^2}{2} + 4t + C \\
     s(t) &= 0.2t^3 - t^2 + 4t + C
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  2. Use boundary condition: when $t = 0, s = 0$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     0 &= 0.2(0)^3 - (0)^2 + 4(0) + C \\
     C &= 0
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. Write the final expression.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s(t) &= 0.2t^3 - t^2 + 4t
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $t = 5$ into the displacement formula.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     s(5) &= 0.2(5)^3 - (5)^2 + 4(5) \\
     &= 0.2(125) - 25 + 20 \\
     &= 25 - 25 + 20 \\
     &= 20 \text{ m}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
  </TabItem>

    A manufacturer determines that their marginal cost function (the rate of change of total cost with respect to the number of units produced, $x$) is modelled by $MC(x) = 0.03x^2 - 0.4x + 15$ in USD per unit. The fixed cost (cost when $0$ units are produced) is $500 \text{ USD}$.  
    **a)** Find the total cost function, $C(x)$.  
    **b)** Calculate the total cost of producing $10$ units.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **a)**
<Steps>
  1. Total cost is the integral of marginal cost.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     C(x) &= \int (0.03x^2 - 0.4x + 15) \text{d}x \\
     C(x) &= \dfrac{0.03x^3}{3} - \dfrac{0.4x^2}{2} + 15x + K \\
     C(x) &= 0.01x^3 - 0.2x^2 + 15x + K
     \end{aligned}
     $$
     *(Note: we used $K$ as the constant to avoid confusion with Cost $C$)*
  2. Use the fixed cost as the boundary condition: $C(0) = 500$.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     500 &= 0.01(0)^3 - 0.2(0)^2 + 15(0) + K \\
     K &= 500
     \end{aligned}
     $$
  3. State the final function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     C(x) &= 0.01x^3 - 0.2x^2 + 15x + 500
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>

    **b)**
<Steps>
  1. Substitute $x = 10$ into the total cost function.
     $$
     \begin{aligned}
     C(10) &= 0.01(10)^3 - 0.2(10)^2 + 15(10) + 500 \\
     &= 0.01(1000) - 0.2(100) + 150 + 500 \\
     &= 10 - 20 + 150 + 500 \\
     &= 640 \text{ USD}
     \end{aligned}
     $$
</Steps>
