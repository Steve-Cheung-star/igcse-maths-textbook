Functions are rarely static; they are dynamically moved, flipped, and resized to model changing conditions. By applying specific algebraic changes to a base function $f(x)$, we can transform its graph. This is a vital skill for modelling real-world phenomena, from shifting economic curves to altering the amplitude of physical waves.

<Aside type="note" title="Connections">
**Cross-curricular Links:** In Economics, a change in consumer income might result in a horizontal translation of the demand curve. In Physics, the vertical stretch of a sine wave represents an increase in amplitude (such as a louder sound), while a horizontal stretch changes the period of simple harmonic motion.
</Aside>

---

## 1. Translations and Reflections

Translations move the entire graph without changing its fundamental shape, while reflections "flip" the graph symmetrically across a coordinate axis.

### Translations
* **Vertical Translation:** $y = f(x) + b$  
    Shifts the graph **up** by $b$ units (or down if $b$ is negative).
* **Horizontal Translation:** $y = f(x - a)$  
    Shifts the graph **right** by $a$ units. 

<Aside type="caution" title="Counter-intuitive Horizontal Shifts">
Horizontal translations often catch students off guard. The transformation $f(x - 3)$ moves the graph $3$ units to the **right**, whereas $f(x + 3)$ moves it $3$ units to the **left**. Always think: "What value of $x$ makes the inside of the bracket zero?"
</Aside>

### Reflections
* **Reflection in the $x$-axis:** $y = -f(x)$  
    The $y$-values change sign. The graph flips upside down.
* **Reflection in the $y$-axis:** $y = f(-x)$  
    The $x$-values change sign. The graph flips horizontally across the $y$-axis.

## 2. Stretches

Stretches physically deform the graph, pulling it away from or pushing it towards the coordinate axes.

* **Vertical Stretch:** $y = p f(x)$  
    The graph is stretched vertically by a **scale factor of $p$**. 
    * Points on the $x$-axis (where $y = 0$) remain invariant (fixed in place).
* **Horizontal Stretch:** $y = f(qx)$  
    The graph is stretched horizontally by a **scale factor of $\frac{1}{q}$**.
    * Points on the $y$-axis (where $x = 0$) remain invariant.

<Aside type="danger" title="Horizontal Scale Factors">
Just like horizontal translations, horizontal stretches are the inverse of what you might expect. The transformation $f(2x)$ represents a horizontal compression by a scale factor of $\frac{1}{2}$. It makes the graph twice as "thin".
</Aside>

---

## 3. Composite Transformations and Order

When multiple transformations are applied to a base function, the **order of execution is strictly important**. As a general rule of thumb, follow the standard order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS):

1.  **Horizontal Transformations** (work inside the brackets first).
2.  **Stretches and Reflections** (multiplication).
3.  **Vertical Translations** (addition/subtraction outside the function).

*(Note: In the SL syllabus, you are not required to perform complex grouped horizontal transformations like $f(ax + b)$ in a single step, but you must be able to read composite vertical transformations accurately).*

## Technology: Using the GDC for Transformations

<SteveTip title="TI-Nspire Tips for Graphing Transformations">
You can use your GDC to quickly verify how a transformation behaves without re-typing the entire equation.
1. Open a **Graph** page and enter your base function, e.g., $f_1(x) = x^2 - 4x$.
2. To graph a transformation, press **[TAB]** to open the entry line for $f_2(x)$.
3. Press the **[VAR]** key, select $f_1$, and type your transformation directly into it. 
   * Example: Type `-2 * f1(x + 3) + 1`
4. The GDC will automatically plot the complex transformed curve, saving you from expanding the algebra manually!
</SteveTip>

    Describe the sequence of four geometric transformations that have been applied to $f(x)$ to obtain $g(x)$.

    1.  **Horizontal Translation:** $(x - 2)$ indicates a translation of **$2$ units to the right**.
    2.  **Reflection:** The negative sign indicates a **reflection in the $x$-axis**.
    3.  **Vertical Stretch:** The coefficient $3$ indicates a **vertical stretch with a scale factor of $3$**.
    4.  **Vertical Translation:** $+4$ indicates a translation of **$4$ units upwards**.
  </TabItem>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Transforming Coordinates">
    A continuous function $f(x)$ has a local minimum at the coordinate point $(-4, 10)$. 
    
    Find the exact coordinates of this local minimum after the function undergoes the transformation $g(x) = \dfrac{1}{2}f(2x) - 5$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    Apply the transformations algebraically to the point $(x, y) = (-4, 10)$.

    **1. Horizontal Stretch:** $f(2x)$ means a horizontal stretch by a scale factor of $\frac{1}{2}$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x_{new} &= -4 \times \dfrac{1}{2} \\
    x_{new} &= -2
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **2. Vertical Stretch:** $\frac{1}{2}f(\dots)$ means a vertical stretch by a scale factor of $\frac{1}{2}$.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    y_{temp} &= 10 \times \dfrac{1}{2} \\
    y_{temp} &= 5
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    **3. Vertical Translation:** $- 5$ means a translation of $5$ units down.
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    y_{new} &= 5 - 5 \\
    y_{new} &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    The new coordinates of the local minimum are **$(-2, 0)$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Transforming Asymptotes">
    The exponential function $f(x) = e^x$ has a horizontal asymptote at $y = 0$.
    
    a) Write down the equation of the horizontal asymptote for the transformed function $g(x) = e^{x+3} - 7$.  
    b) Explain why the horizontal translation does not affect the equation of the horizontal asymptote.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    **a)** The function $g(x)$ is formed by translating $f(x)$ left by $3$ units and down by $7$ units. 
    The vertical translation shifts the asymptote down by $7$.
    The new horizontal asymptote is **$y = -7$**.

    **b)** A horizontal translation shifts the graph left or right along the $x$-axis. Since a horizontal asymptote is a horizontal line extending infinitely in both $x$-directions, sliding it left or right maps the line perfectly back onto itself. Only vertical transformations (stretches or translations) alter the $y$-value of a horizontal line.
  </TabItem>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Real-World Application (Economics)">
    A company models its weekly profit function for manufacturing $x$ units of a product as $P(x) = 15x - 0.1x^2$, where $P(x)$ is the profit in EUR.
    
    Due to a new factory lease, the company's fixed overhead costs increase by **250 EUR** per week. 
    
    a) Write down the new profit function, $P_{new}(x)$, resulting from this increased cost.  
    b) Describe this change as a geometric transformation of the original profit curve.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    **a)** An increase in fixed overhead costs means the overall profit decreases by a flat $250$ EUR regardless of how many units are produced. We subtract $250$ from the entire function.
    
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    P_{new}(x) &= P(x) - 250 \\
    P_{new}(x) &= 15x - 0.1x^2 - 250
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    
    The new profit function is **$P_{new}(x) = -0.1x^2 + 15x - 250$**.

    **b)** Because a constant was subtracted from the outside of the function, this represents a **vertical translation of $250$ units downwards**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Real-World Application (Physics)">
    A sound wave is initially modelled by the function $h(t) = \sin(t)$.
    An amplifier is used to increase the volume (amplitude) by a factor of $4$, and a synthesizer increases the pitch (frequency), which compresses the wave horizontally by a scale factor of $\frac{1}{3}$.
    
    Write down the new function $g(t)$ that models the altered sound wave.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    1.  **Amplitude Increase:** This is a vertical stretch by a scale factor of $4$. The function becomes $4 \sin(t)$.
    2.  **Horizontal Compression:** A scale factor of $\frac{1}{3}$ requires a coefficient of $3$ inside the function bracket. The function becomes $4 \sin(3t)$.
    
    The new wave model is **$g(t) = 4 \sin(3t)$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: Constructing Composite Equations">
    Let $f(x) = x^3$. The graph of $f$ is translated **$5$ units to the left** and then **reflected in the $y$-axis** to form the graph of $g(x)$.
    
    Write down the equation of $g(x)$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    We must apply the transformations strictly step-by-step.

    **Step 1: Translate $5$ units to the left.**
    Replace $x$ with $(x + 5)$.
    $$ y = (x + 5)^3 $$

    **Step 2: Reflect in the $y$-axis.**
    Replace $x$ with $(-x)$.
    $$ y = (-x + 5)^3 $$

    The resulting equation is **$g(x) = (-x + 5)^3$**.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Roots and Algebraic Verification">
    Consider the function $f(x) = x^2 - 9$.
    
    a) Find the exact roots (x-intercepts) of $f(x)$.  
    b) A new function is defined as $g(x) = -f(x - 2)$. Find the roots of $g(x)$ algebraically.  
    c) Outline how you could verify your roots for $g(x)$ using a GDC.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    **a)** Set $f(x) = 0$:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x^2 - 9 &= 0 \\
    x^2 &= 9 \\
    x &= \pm 3
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The roots of $f(x)$ are **$x = -3$** and **$x = 3$**.

    **b)** First, construct the equation for $g(x)$.
    Substitute $(x - 2)$ into $f$, then make the whole thing negative:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    g(x) &= -((x - 2)^2 - 9) \\
    g(x) &= -(x^2 - 4x + 4 - 9) \\
    g(x) &= -(x^2 - 4x - 5) \\
    g(x) &= -x^2 + 4x + 5
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Set $g(x) = 0$ to find the roots and factorise:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    -x^2 + 4x + 5 &= 0 \\
    x^2 - 4x - 5 &= 0 \\
    (x - 5)(x + 1) &= 0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    The roots of $g(x)$ are **$x = -1$** and **$x = 5$**.

    *(Shortcut Check: The original roots were $-3$ and $3$. A translation $2$ units right changes them to $-1$ and $5$. The subsequent reflection in the $x$-axis doesn't move points already on the $x$-axis, so the roots remain $-1$ and $5$.)*

    **c)** To verify using the GDC:
    1. Open a **Graph** page.
    2. Enter $f_1(x) = -((x - 2)^2 - 9)$.
    3. Press **[Menu] > 6: Analyze Graph > 1: Zero**.
    4. Highlight the regions where the graph crosses the $x$-axis to confirm the values $-1$ and $5$.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Reverse Engineering Transformations">
    The base graph $f(x) = \ln(x)$ is transformed to yield the graph of $h(x) = 2\ln(x + 1) - 6$.
    
    A student claims the graph was first translated downwards by $6$, then stretched vertically by $2$, and finally moved left by $1$.
    
    Is the student correct? Justify your reasoning mathematically.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    **No, the student is incorrect.**

    If we follow the student's claimed order of operations algebraically starting from $f(x)$:
    1.  Translate downwards by $6$: $y = \ln(x) - 6$
    2.  Stretch vertically by $2$: $y = 2(\ln(x) - 6) = 2\ln(x) - 12$
    3.  Move left by $1$: $y = 2\ln(x + 1) - 12$
    
    This results in $y = 2\ln(x + 1) - 12$, which does not match the actual target function $h(x) = 2\ln(x + 1) - 6$.

    **The correct order is:**
    1.  Translate left by $1$: $\ln(x + 1)$
    2.  Stretch vertically by a scale factor of $2$: $2\ln(x + 1)$
    3.  Translate down by $6$: $2\ln(x + 1) - 6$
    
    This demonstrates why executing vertical stretches before vertical translations (following standard BODMAS rules) is vital.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
