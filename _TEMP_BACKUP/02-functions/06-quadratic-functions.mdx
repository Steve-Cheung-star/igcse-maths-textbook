Quadratic functions represent one of the most important families of curves in mathematics. Unlike straight lines, quadratics accelerate; their rate of change is constantly changing, forming a perfect, symmetrical U-shape known as a **parabola**. From the arc of a thrown basketball to the reflective surface of a satellite dish, quadratics model the physical world with astonishing accuracy.

## 1. The Anatomy of a Parabola

Every quadratic graph (parabola) has a few defining geometric features:
* **The Vertex:** The absolute maximum or minimum turning point of the curve.
* **The Axis of Symmetry:** A perfectly vertical line that passes straight through the vertex, cutting the parabola into two mirrored halves.
* **The $y$-intercept:** Where the curve crosses the $y$-axis (always exactly one).
* **The $x$-intercepts (Roots/Zeros):** Where the curve crosses the $x$-axis (can be two, one, or none).

## 2. The Three Forms of a Quadratic

To fully master quadratics, you must be able to move fluidly between three different algebraic forms. Each form is a different lens that highlights a specific geometric feature of the parabola.

### 1. Standard Form
$$
f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c
$$
This is the default expanded form. 
* The constant $c$ reveals the **$y$-intercept** at $(0, c)$.
* If $a > 0$, the parabola opens **upwards** (like a smile $\cup$).
* If $a < 0$, the parabola opens **downwards** (like a frown $\cap$).

<FormulaBox title="2.6 Axis of symmetry of the graph of a quadratic function">

If $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \Rightarrow$ axis of symmetry is $x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$

</FormulaBox>

### 2. Factored Form (Intercept Form)
$$
f(x) = a(x - p)(x - q)
$$
This form is built by factorising the quadratic. 
* It instantly reveals the **$x$-intercepts**, which are located at $(p, 0)$ and $(q, 0)$.
* Because a parabola is perfectly symmetrical, the axis of symmetry lies exactly halfway between the two roots: $x = \dfrac{p + q}{2}$.

### 3. Vertex Form
$$
f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k
$$
This form is achieved by "completing the square".
* It instantly reveals the coordinates of the **vertex** as $(h, k)$.
* The axis of symmetry is simply the vertical line $x = h$.

<Aside type="danger" title="Common Pitfall: The negative sign in Vertex Form">
Pay close attention to the minus sign inside the brackets of $a(x - h)^2 + k$. 
If your equation is $f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 + 5$, the vertex is $(3, 5)$. 
If your equation is $f(x) = 2(x + 3)^2 + 5$, the $x$-coordinate is negative, so the vertex is $(-3, 5)$.
</Aside>

## 3. Converting Between Forms

The IB will frequently ask you to change a quadratic from one form to another. 

**Standard to Factored:** Factorise the quadratic expression.
**Standard to Vertex:** Use the method of "completing the square" (or find the vertex using $x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$ and substitute it back in to find $y$).
**Vertex/Factored to Standard:** Simply expand the brackets and simplify.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="Worked Example: Converting to Vertex Form">
    Express the quadratic $f(x) = 2x^2 - 12x + 11$ in the form $a(x - h)^2 + k$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Algebraic Method (Completing the Square)">
    <Steps>
    1. **Factor out $a$ from the first two terms only:**
       $$ f(x) = 2(x^2 - 6x) + 11 $$
    2. **Complete the square inside the bracket:** Take half of the $x$-coefficient ($-6 \to -3$), square it ($9$), and add/subtract it inside.
       $$ f(x) = 2(x^2 - 6x + 9 - 9) + 11 $$
    3. **Rewrite the perfect square and kick out the extra term:** Remember to multiply the $-9$ by the $2$ on the outside!
       $$ f(x) = 2((x - 3)^2 - 9) + 11 $$
       $$ f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 - 18 + 11 $$
    4. **Simplify the constants:**
       $$ f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 - 7 $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Formula Method (The Shortcut)">
    <Steps>
    1. **Identify $a, b, c$:**
       $a = 2$, $b = -12$, $c = 11$.
    2. **Find the axis of symmetry ($h$):**
       $$ h = -\dfrac{b}{2a} = -\dfrac{-12}{2(2)} = \dfrac{12}{4} = 3 $$
    3. **Substitute $x = 3$ back into $f(x)$ to find the $y$-coordinate of the vertex ($k$):**
       $$ k = f(3) = 2(3)^2 - 12(3) + 11 $$
       $$ k = 18 - 36 + 11 = -7 $$
    4. **Construct the vertex form:** We know $a = 2$, $h = 3$, $k = -7$.
       $$ f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k $$
       $$ f(x) = 2(x - 3)^2 - 7 $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Practice Questions

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 1: Expanding to Standard Form">
    A quadratic function is given by $f(x) = -3(x + 2)^2 + 15$.
    
    Express $f(x)$ in the form $ax^2 + bx + c$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 1">
    <Steps>
    1. **Expand the perfect square first:** Remember BEDMAS/BIDMAS. Do not multiply the $-3$ in until the exponent is resolved.
       $$ f(x) = -3(x^2 + 4x + 4) + 15 $$
    2. **Distribute the $-3$:**
       $$ f(x) = -3x^2 - 12x - 12 + 15 $$
    3. **Simplify the constants:**
       $$ f(x) = -3x^2 - 12x + 3 $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 2: Finding the Vertex">
    Consider the function $g(x) = -x^2 + 8x - 5$.
    
    Find the coordinates of the absolute maximum point of this function.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 2">
    <Steps>
    1. **Recognise the request:** The "absolute maximum point" of a downward-facing parabola ($a = -1$) is its vertex.
    2. **Use the axis of symmetry formula:** Here, $a = -1$ and $b = 8$.
       $$ x = -\dfrac{b}{2a} = -\dfrac{8}{2(-1)} = \dfrac{-8}{-2} = 4 $$
       The $x$-coordinate of the vertex is $4$.
    3. **Find the $y$-coordinate:** Substitute $x = 4$ into $g(x)$.
       $$ g(4) = -(4)^2 + 8(4) - 5 $$
       $$ g(4) = -16 + 32 - 5 $$
       $$ g(4) = 11 $$
    4. **Final Answer:** The coordinates of the maximum point are **$(4, 11)$**.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 3: Building from Intercepts">
    A parabola passes through the $x$-axis at the points $(-2, 0)$ and $(5, 0)$. 
    It also crosses the $y$-axis at $(0, -30)$.
    
    Find the equation of the parabola, giving your answer in the form $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 3">
    <Steps>
    1. **Use the Factored Form:** We know the roots are $p = -2$ and $q = 5$.
       $$ f(x) = a(x - (-2))(x - 5) $$
       $$ f(x) = a(x + 2)(x - 5) $$
    2. **Find the stretch factor $a$:** Use the known coordinate $(0, -30)$. Substitute $x = 0$ and $y = -30$.
       $$ -30 = a(0 + 2)(0 - 5) $$
       $$ -30 = a(2)(-5) $$
       $$ -30 = -10a $$
       $$ a = 3 $$
    3. **Substitute $a$ back in and expand:**
       $$ f(x) = 3(x + 2)(x - 5) $$
       $$ f(x) = 3(x^2 - 5x + 2x - 10) $$
       $$ f(x) = 3(x^2 - 3x - 10) $$
       $$ f(x) = 3x^2 - 9x - 30 $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 4: Unknowns in Vertex Form">
    A quadratic function is given by $f(x) = a(x - h)^2 + k$. 
    The vertex of the graph is $(-3, 4)$, and the graph passes through the point $(-1, 12)$.
    
    Find the values of $a$, $h$, and $k$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 4">
    <Steps>
    1. **Input the vertex:** The vertex is $(h, k)$, so $h = -3$ and $k = 4$.
       Substitute these into the vertex form:
       $$ f(x) = a(x - (-3))^2 + 4 $$
       $$ f(x) = a(x + 3)^2 + 4 $$
    2. **Find $a$:** Substitute the coordinate $x = -1, y = 12$ to solve for $a$.
       $$ 12 = a(-1 + 3)^2 + 4 $$
       $$ 12 = a(2)^2 + 4 $$
       $$ 12 = 4a + 4 $$
       $$ 8 = 4a $$
       $$ a = 2 $$
    3. **Final Answer:** $a = 2$, $h = -3$, $k = 4$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 5: Intersection with a Line">
    Find the exact coordinates of the points of intersection between the parabola $y = x^2 - 3x - 4$ and the line $y = 2x + 2$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 5">
    <Steps>
    1. **Set the equations equal to each other:**
       $$ x^2 - 3x - 4 = 2x + 2 $$
    2. **Rearrange into a standard quadratic equal to zero:** Subtract $2x$ and $2$ from both sides.
       $$ x^2 - 5x - 6 = 0 $$
    3. **Factorise to solve for $x$:** Find two numbers that multiply to $-6$ and add to $-5$.
       $$ (x - 6)(x + 1) = 0 $$
       So, $x = 6$ or $x = -1$.
    4. **Find the corresponding $y$-coordinates:** Substitute the $x$-values back into the linear equation (it's much easier than the quadratic).
       For $x = 6$: $y = 2(6) + 2 = 14$
       For $x = -1$: $y = 2(-1) + 2 = 0$
    5. **State the coordinates:** The points of intersection are **$(6, 14)$** and **$(-1, 0)$**.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 6: The Unknown Axis">
    The quadratic function $f(x) = -2x^2 + px + 7$ has an axis of symmetry at $x = 3$.
    
    Find the exact value of $p$.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 6">
    <Steps>
    1. **State the formula:** The axis of symmetry is $x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$.
    2. **Substitute known values:** We know $x = 3$, $a = -2$, and $b = p$.
       $$ 3 = -\dfrac{p}{2(-2)} $$
    3. **Solve for $p$:**
       $$ 3 = -\dfrac{p}{-4} $$
       $$ 3 = \dfrac{p}{4} $$
       $$ p = 12 $$
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 7: Kinematics Context">
    A toy rocket is launched from a platform. Its height above the ground, $h$ metres, at time $t$ seconds, is modelled by the function:
    $$ h(t) = -4.9t^2 + 14.7t + 2 $$
    
    **a)** State the height of the platform.
    
    **b)** Calculate the maximum height reached by the rocket.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 7">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a:** The height of the platform is the initial height, which occurs at $t = 0$. 
       This is the $y$-intercept ($c$-value) of the standard form.
       Height = $2$ metres.
    2. **Part b:** The maximum height occurs at the vertex of the parabola. First, find the time it takes to reach the maximum height using the axis of symmetry formula:
       $$ t = -\dfrac{b}{2a} = -\dfrac{14.7}{2(-4.9)} = -\dfrac{14.7}{-9.8} = 1.5 \text{ seconds} $$
    3. **Find the height:** Substitute $t = 1.5$ back into $h(t)$.
       $$ h(1.5) = -4.9(1.5)^2 + 14.7(1.5) + 2 $$
       $$ h(1.5) = -4.9(2.25) + 22.05 + 2 $$
       $$ h(1.5) = -11.025 + 22.05 + 2 = 13.025 $$
       The maximum height is $13.025$ metres.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="📝 Question 8: Optimisation (Classic Extension)">
    A farmer wants to enclose a rectangular garden using a straight stone wall for one side and $60$ metres of wire fencing for the other three sides.
    
    Let the width of the garden extending away from the wall be $x$ metres.
    
    **a)** Show that the area of the garden, $A$, can be written as $A(x) = 60x - 2x^2$.
    
    **b)** Find the dimensions of the garden that will maximise the enclosed area.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="✅ Solution 8">
    <Steps>
    1. **Part a:** If the two widths are $x$, they use up $2x$ metres of fencing. 
       The length running parallel to the wall will be whatever fencing is left over: $L = 60 - 2x$.
       Area of a rectangle is width $\times$ length:
       $$ A(x) = x(60 - 2x) $$
       $$ A(x) = 60x - 2x^2 $$
       *(Note: This is a downward-facing quadratic, meaning it has a maximum peak!)*
    2. **Part b:** To find the maximum area, find the $x$-coordinate of the vertex of $A(x) = -2x^2 + 60x$.
       $$ x = -\dfrac{b}{2a} = -\dfrac{60}{2(-2)} = \dfrac{-60}{-4} = 15 $$
       The width that maximises area is $x = 15$ metres.
    3. **Find the other dimension:** The length is $60 - 2(15) = 30$ metres.
    4. **Final Answer:** The dimensions are $15\text{m}$ by $30\text{m}$.
    </Steps>
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>
